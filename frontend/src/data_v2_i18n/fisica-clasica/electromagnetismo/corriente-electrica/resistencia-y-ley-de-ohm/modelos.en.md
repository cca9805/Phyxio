## Ideal model

The most basic and universally used model in physics education is that of the ideal ohmic component. In this simplified scenario, it is assumed that the [[resistencia]] of the material is a perfect constant that remains invariable regardless of external conditions. This model postulates that charge flow responds instantaneously and linearly to any variation in the applied potential. It is an extremely powerful abstraction allowing quick circuit mesh analysis and preliminary design of low-power electronic systems where deviations from linearity are small enough to be ignored in first-order calculations.

## Hypotheses

For the ideal model to be applicable, the following fundamental hypotheses must be met:
- **Absolute linearity**: The relationship between [[tension]] and [[intensidad]] is assumed to be a perfect straight line passing through the origin (0,0) of the Cartesian plane, implying no threshold [[tension]] to start conduction.
- **Isotropy and structural homogeneity**: Resistive material properties are assumed to be identical in all spatial directions and there are no local variations in impurity density or crystal structure that could deviate charge flow unpredictably.
- **Total thermal independence**: The model completely ignores heat generated by [[intensidad]] flow (Joule effect). It is assumed that the cooling system is infinitely efficient or that dissipated power is so low it does not produce a temperature increase capable of altering material resistivity.
- **Pure steady state**: No time delay effects, parasitic capacitances, or inductances associated with component geometry are considered, treating the electrical response as purely resistive and simultaneous with the stimulus.

## Quantitative validity domain

Applying the linear ohmic model is physically acceptable as long as [[intensidad]] density remains below the material's saturation limits. As a general engineering rule, the model is considered valid if the conductor temperature does not vary by more than 10 % relative to the ambient reference temperature (normally 20 or 25 degrees Celsius). In terms of electric field intensity, for common metals like copper, the model is robust for fields below 10⁵ V/m. Furthermore, in terms of frequency, the model is applicable as long as the conductor radius is significantly smaller than the skin depth, which for copper at 50 Hz is about 8.5 mm.

## Model failure signals

There are clear indicators that the ideal model has ceased to be representative of physical reality:
- **Observable thermal drift**: Measured [[intensidad]] intensity [[intensidad]] with precision instruments begins to gradually decrease even though source [[tension]] [[tension]] is kept rigorously constant. This indicates that generated heat is increasing ionic thermal agitation, raising actual [[resistencia]].
- **Proportionality discrepancy**: When collecting data sweeping different voltages, it is observed that when doubling [[tension]], the resulting [[intensidad]] increases by a factor significantly different from two. This is typical in bulb filaments or in semiconductors operating near their power limits.
- **Scale inconsistency in low impedance**: When calculating extremely low resistances (order of microohms), the ideal model usually fails by not including terminal contact [[resistencia]], which at those scales can be the dominant error component.

## Extended or alternative model

When the ideal model error exceeds the acceptable tolerance margin for a design (generally set at 5 %), the engineer must transition toward higher physical fidelity models. The extended electrothermal model incorporates [[resistencia]] dependence on temperature using a thermal coefficient alpha. On the other hand, for components showing intrinsically non-linear behavior, such as semiconductor diodes, static [[resistencia]] is abandoned in favor of the dynamic [[resistencia]] or small-signal model, which defines opposition as the derivative of [[tension]] with respect to [[intensidad]] at a specific working point. In high-frequency applications, the distributed parameter model of transmission lines is used.

## Operational comparison

Choosing between the ideal model and its extended versions is a balance exercise between simplicity and precision. The ideal model allows solving complex systems of hundreds of nodes in milliseconds, being ideal for the logical design and rapid prototyping phase. However, for final validation of power systems, electric motors, or high-frequency electronics, using the ideal model is dangerous and can lead to catastrophic failures due to overheating. The professional must be able to identify the point where the precision gain of the extended model offsets the increased calculation complexity, always ensuring system thermal safety is guaranteed above the mathematical elegance of the original Ohm's Law.
