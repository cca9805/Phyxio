## Historical problem

By the mid-nineteenth century, physics had well-verified experimental laws for static electricity and magnetism: the laws of Gauss, Biot–Savart, and Faraday described fields produced by charges at rest or moving slowly. However, nobody knew whether electricity and magnetism could combine to produce something completely new when fields varied rapidly with time. The unanswered question was: can [[campo_electrico]] and [[campo_magnetico]] exist and propagate without any material source, travelling through free space? Maxwell was the one who turned that question into an equation and then into a quantitative prediction.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The obstacle was not only mathematical. The original Ampère's law, which related the magnetic field to the current density, was inconsistent in dynamic situations: applied to a charging capacitor, it gave contradictory results depending on the integration surface chosen. The limiting paradigm was deeper: physicists of the era, including the action-at-a-distance school of Weber and Neumann, conceived electromagnetic interactions as instantaneous forces between particles, without any "field" that propagates. The idea that a varying electric field could act as a source of the magnetic field — even in vacuum, with no moving charges — was conceptually alien and had no direct experimental basis.

## What changed

Maxwell resolved the inconsistency of Ampère's law by adding a new term: the displacement current, proportional to the time derivative of the electric field. It was not a directly measurable quantity; it was a purely theoretical correction, motivated by the requirement of mathematical consistency. With that term, the four equations formed a closed, consistent system. In 1865, Maxwell combined the four equations algebraically, eliminating the cross fields, and obtained a second-order differential equation for [[campo_electrico]] and [[campo_magnetico]] separately, in the canonical form of the scalar wave equation. The propagation speed that emerged was the reciprocal of the square root of the product [[mu0]]·[[epsilon0]], and its numerical value matched the [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] measured experimentally by Fizeau (1849) and Foucault (1862) with remarkable precision. Maxwell concluded that light is an electromagnetic wave.

## Impact on physics

Maxwell's derivation unified three previously separate branches: electricity, magnetism, and optics. Where there had been three sets of phenomena with their own laws, there was now a single system of four equations. The impact was twofold: conceptual and technological. Conceptually, it demonstrated that the electromagnetic field is a real physical entity that carries energy and can propagate without material support, displacing the action-at-a-distance paradigm. Technologically, it opened the way for Heinrich Hertz, who in 1886–1888 generated and detected radiofrequency electromagnetic waves in the laboratory, measuring [[omega]] and [[numero_de_onda]] and verifying that their ratio was [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]]. Hertz's demonstration was the starting point for radio, television, and all twentieth-century wireless communications.

## Connection with modern physics

Maxwell's wave equation presented a deep problem: the speed [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] that appeared in it was independent of the observer's motion, in contradiction with Galilean transformations. In 1905, Einstein resolved the contradiction not by modifying Maxwell's equations but by abandoning Newtonian mechanics. The invariance of [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] for all inertial observers became the second postulate of special relativity, and the electromagnetic wave equation turned out to be exactly invariant under Lorentz transformations. Maxwell had unknowingly written the first relativistic equation in physics. Today, [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] is not a measured quantity but a defining constant of the International System of Units since 1983, and the metre is defined in terms of it. The electromagnetic wave equation remains the core of quantum electrodynamics (QED), the most precise theory physics has ever produced.
