## Historical problem

In the mid-19th century, the laws of electricity and magnetism were a set of empirical formulas (Coulomb, Ampère, Faraday) that were not connected. The greatest mystery was the nature of light: it was known to be a wave, but it was thought to require a mechanical material medium (the "ether") to propagate, much like sound needs air.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty lay in accepting that vacuum could possess physical properties. Newton's physics was based on "action at a distance", but Maxwell proposed "action mediated by fields". Scientists of the time struggled to imagine how an electrical oscillation could be transformed into a magnetic one without solid support.

## What changed

James Clerk Maxwell introduced the displacement current and synthesized differential equations. By solving them for vacuum, he discovered a wave solution whose speed depended on [[epsilon0]] and [[mu0]]. By calculating that value, he obtained exactly 300,000 km/s. His interpretation was revolutionary: "light consists in transverse undulations of the same medium which is the cause of electric and magnetic phenomena".

## Impact on physics

This discovery unified optics, electricity, and magnetism into a single branch. It was the first great unification of physics. Furthermore, by predicting that the speed [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] was a property of the vacuum, he laid the foundations for Einstein to develop relativity, by proving that the speed of light was absolute and independent of the observer.

## Connection with modern physics

Today, Maxwell's interpretation is the basis for gauge field theories. The idea that the field transports energy and momentum is fundamental in quantum mechanics, where energy flow is interpreted as photon motion. The "Maxwell constant" [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] is now recognized as the speed limit of all causality in the universe.
