The path towards modern energy efficiency has shifted from a purely mechanical view of equipment to a systemic and biological understanding of the human habitat.

## Historical problem

For much of the 20th century, climate control was evaluated solely by the nominal power of the equipment (how many kilowatts a boiler was capable of delivering). There was no metric that related the heat delivered to the electrical consumption on a seasonal basis. Buildings were constructed with a "cheap energy" mentality, where the lack of insulation was simply compensated for by installing more powerful machines, regardless of the resulting energy waste.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the lack of distinction between the efficiency of a motor and the efficiency of a heating system. The critical interaction between the building envelope, the outdoor climate, and the generator's behavior was ignored. This fragmented view led to unrealistic savings expectations and equipment oversizing, which ironically reduced their operational efficiency by forcing them to work in short on and off cycles.

## What changed

The energy crisis of the 1970s and subsequent concern about climate change forced the adoption of seasonal indicators such as [[rendimiento_estacional_scop]]. The fundamental change was to stop looking at efficiency as a "point on a map" (a laboratory test at 7 °C) and start looking at it as a "complete movie" (a weighted average of the entire winter). It was recognized that the house is an open system in constant exchange with the environment.

## Impact on physics

This evolution allowed for the development of regulations such as the Technical Building Code (CTE) in Spain. Building physics shifted from being a minor branch of architecture to a central discipline of energy engineering, integrating thermodynamics, heat transfer, and dynamic systems analysis in a rigorous way.

## Connection with modern physics

Today, thanks to continuous monitoring and the Internet of Things (IoT), efficiency has become a living metric. Modern systems are not only efficient by design, but by operation, adjusting their power in real time according to weather predictions and occupancy. We have moved from passive efficiency to **adaptive efficiency**, connecting with the principles of optimal control and complex systems in current physics.
