# Climate control

## General idea of the topic
Domestic climate control represents one of the most critical and
energy-demanding applications in modern households, governed by rigorous
thermodynamic principles. This topic analyses and compares the two primary
strategies for electrically driven heat transfer: direct resistive
conversion and mechanical heat pumping.

Through the study of these technologies we examine how physics defines
the upper limit of efficiency and determines the real resource consumption
required to maintain indoor comfort under changing environmental
conditions.

## What you will truly understand
- The fundamental difference between the direct generation of resistive heat (with an insurmountable physical limit of 100% efficiency) and the mechanical transport of heat, which overcomes this apparent boundary.
- How a compression refrigeration cycle operates to extract heat from a cold source and deliver it to a hot one through mechanical work.
- The critical distinction between localized coefficient of performance (COP/EER) measured under fixed laboratory conditions and seasonal performance metrics (SCOP/SEER) that average real annual behavior.
- The exact quantitative relationship between a home's external thermal demand and the electrical energy consumption of the equipment.
- The economic and carbon footprint impact of different climate control technologies based on the balance of demanded power versus consumed energy.

## Recommended learning route
1. **Resistive heating**: The Joule effect applied to direct heating, nominal power, and theoretical maximum efficiency.
2. **Heat pump**: Principles of the compression thermodynamic cycle, Coefficient of Performance (COP), and energy transport.
3. **Seasonal efficiency**: Operating losses in extreme climates, temperature variability, and the annual seasonal performance standard (SCOP).

## Physical criterion you must acquire
You will learn to compare thermal systems not by their commercial power input or marketing claims, but through energy balances based on the first and second laws of thermodynamics. You will develop the technical criteria to link the indoor-outdoor temperature difference with the degradation of the equipment's COP, and you will understand why the thermal resistance of the home's envelope directly conditions the final electricity bill.

## Connection with real problems
- Technically selecting the optimal heating system for a home based on local outdoor temperature climate curves.
- Projecting real monthly savings and payback periods when replacing combustion boilers or resistive heaters with high-efficiency heat pumps.
- Diagnosing anomalous behavior in air conditioning or heating systems when operating outside their physical outdoor temperature limits.
- Reducing overall electrical consumption in heating by analyzing the thermal coupling factor between the climate control equipment and the building envelope.

## Final goal of the topic
To empower the student to choose and size domestic climate control strategies, achieving an optimal balance between thermal comfort, operational safety, and annual economic cost supported by thermodynamic models.
