﻿# Applications


## 1. Spot heating of briefly used rooms

Resistive heating is the optimal solution for rooms with short and unpredictable occupancy periods, such as a bathroom, a converted storage room used as a study, or a conditioned garage. In these cases, the low installation cost and immediate heat response compensate for the higher cost per kWh compared to a heat pump, which would require many more hours of use to recover the initial investment.

Dominant variable: ** [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] — the cost-effectiveness of resistive heating improves dramatically when daily [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] is low (< 2 h), reducing total [[consumo_electrico]] to manageable values.

Validity limit: ** This application loses cost-effectiveness when daily use exceeds 4–5 hours for more than 60 consecutive days. Beyond that threshold, the accumulated cost of an alternative solution with [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] > 2 begins to offset the installation investment.

## 2. Temporary supplement during extreme cold peaks

Primary climate control systems (heat pumps, central boilers) are sized to cover demand under normal conditions. During extreme cold episodes that exceed the primary system's design capacity, a portable resistive emitter can cover the temporary thermal deficit without needing to oversize the fixed installation.

Dominant variable: ** [[potencia_del_emisor]] — in this context, [[potencia_del_emisor]] is selected to cover the difference between peak demand and the maximum capacity of the primary system.

Validity limit: ** The application is valid only for peaks lasting less than 72 consecutive hours and with [[potencia_del_emisor]] that does not compromise the contracted power limit. If peaks are recurring (more than 10 days/year), the primary system sizing should be reviewed.

## 3. Baseline reference for energy audits

The resistive [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] = 1 is the canonical reference point for any comparison of heating technologies. When conducting a residential energy audit or evaluating the feasibility of installing a heat pump, the "all resistive" scenario with [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] = 1 establishes the upper limit of electrical consumption and the lower limit of cost-effectiveness that any other technology must exceed to justify its investment.

Dominant variable: ** Seasonal [[consumo_electrico]] calculated with [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] , which acts as the denominator in the potential savings comparison.

Validity limit: ** Resistive heating as a baseline is valid when alternative systems offer the same [[calor_util]] under the same comfort conditions. If the alternative system cannot maintain the setpoint temperature on the coldest days, the comparison is not direct.

## 4. Heating in seasonally occupied homes

Second homes, country houses, or mountain cabins with fewer than 30 days of concentrated annual use find resistive heating their best economic solution. Zero installation cost (a portable radiator suffices), no maintenance, and ease of use make the higher energy cost irrelevant given the low total annual operating hours.

Dominant variable: ** Total annual [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] — if cumulative annual [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] is below 200 hours, resistive heating competes favorably against any system with higher [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] but greater initial investment.

Validity limit: ** When seasonal use concentrates in weeks with extreme temperatures (mountain in winter), the required [[potencia_del_emisor]] may be high and simultaneity with other appliances must be verified against the available electrical supply, which in remote locations may be limited.

## 5. Experimental verification of the E = P·t model

Resistive heating is the ideal pedagogical system for teaching and experimentally verifying the linear relationship between [[consumo_electrico]], [[potencia_del_emisor]], and [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] using portable consumption meters. The perfect linearity between [[consumo_electrico]], [[potencia_del_emisor]], and [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] allows laboratory experiments to be designed where students measure real [[consumo_electrico]] and compare it to the calculated value, obtaining model validation with errors typically below 3%.

Dominant variable: ** Measured [[consumo_electrico]] versus calculated [[consumo_electrico]] — the discrepancy between the two quantifies the deviation from the ideal model.

Validity limit: ** Experimental verification is valid for emitters with stable [[conversion efficiency|eta]] and without automatic power control. Emitters with variable power or inverter modulation do not verify the simple linear relationship and require an extended version of the experiment.

## Cross-cutting considerations

The common denominator across all of the above applications is the need to accurately know the real [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]], not the connection time. In any context where resistive heating is applied, the effective [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] is the variable with the greatest impact on the final [[consumo_electrico]] and therefore on the system's cost-effectiveness. The [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] = 1 guarantees system predictability; what varies between scenarios is always the building's thermal demand and usage habits, which translate directly into variations in [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]].

The five applications share another structural feature: in all of them, the economic break-even analysis depends on the ratio between the annual accumulated [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] and the installation cost differential. The lower the annual [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]], the more competitive resistive heating becomes relative to higher-performance alternatives. This explains why resistive heating survives in the market despite its inferior [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]]: its total lifecycle cost is genuinely lower in low-use scenarios.

From a teaching perspective, resistive heating's perfect linearity makes it the ideal entry point for any course on energy and domestic consumption. The formula connecting [[consumo_electrico]], [[potencia_del_emisor]], and [[tiempo_efectivo_de_uso]] is the simplest possible version of the energy-power-time relationship, with no correction factors or nonlinear terms. Mastering this model gives students the quantitative foundation to evaluate all other heating technologies, since every alternative — heat pump, gas boiler, district heating — can be compared against the resistive [[coeficiente_de_rendimiento]] = 1 baseline.

The [[calor_util]] delivered is physically identical regardless of whether resistive or pump technology is used: the room reaches the same temperature, the occupant feels the same comfort. What differs is the [[consumo_electrico]] required to achieve that [[calor_util]]. This framing — same output, different input — is the correct way to compare heating technologies, and resistive heating's transparency makes that comparison unambiguous and pedagogically powerful.
