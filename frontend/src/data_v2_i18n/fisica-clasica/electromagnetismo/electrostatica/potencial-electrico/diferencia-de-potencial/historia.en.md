## Historical problem

At the end of the eighteenth century, physicists could produce and accumulate electric charges using electrostatic machines, but lacked a quantitative framework for describing the energetic effect of those charges on others. The Leyden jar stored electric charge, and it was known that a more charged jar produced a more intense spark, but no quantity existed to systematically relate the amount of stored charge to its capacity to do work.

The central problem was conceptual: mechanical work had been well understood since Newton, but there was no way to connect it to electricity. How could one quantify the "effort" of the electric field to move a charge? How could one compare the "electrical intensity" of two different sources?

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main difficulty lay in the fact that electrical phenomena were described in terms of "electric fluid" (Franklin, 1747) or two fluids (Dufay). Within this conceptual framework, potential difference had no natural interpretation. The analogy with hydraulic pressure existed in embryonic form but had not been formalised mathematically.

Another obstacle was the absence of reproducible measuring instruments. Cavendish's experiments (around 1773) on the capacitance of conductors already implicitly contained the concept of potential, but Cavendish did not publish his results and his work remained unknown until Maxwell recovered it in 1879.

## What changed

Alessandro Volta built the first voltaic pile in 1800, a source of continuous potential difference. This device forced the scientific community to precisely define the quantity the pile generated. Volta introduced the term "tension" or "electromotive force" to describe the pile's capacity to drive charges through a circuit.

The decisive step was taken by Georg Simon Ohm in 1827, who formulated his law relating current to potential difference through resistance. Although Ohm was initially rejected by the German scientific community, his work established potential difference as an operationally defined quantity: what a voltmeter measures between two points of a circuit.

Formalisation as a field quantity came with the work of Carl Friedrich Gauss and, above all, William Thomson (Lord Kelvin), who in the 1850s established the relation between electric potential and mechanical work in fully rigorous terms, unifying static and dynamic electricity.

## Impact on physics

Potential difference became the bridge quantity between field physics (Faraday, Maxwell) and electrical engineering (Edison, Tesla). Maxwell's 1865 formulation integrated potential difference into the field equation formalism, showing that the scalar potential was only one part of the general electromagnetic potential.

The practical impact was immediate: designing electrical networks, transformers and motors requires computing potential differences with precision. The war of currents between Edison (direct current) and Tesla/Westinghouse (alternating current) was largely a dispute about which potential difference—and at what distance—was safer and more efficient for energy transmission.

## Connection with modern physics

In quantum electrodynamics, potential difference is generalised to the four-potential, which transforms covariantly under relativistic reference-frame changes. The classical scalar potential is the temporal component of this four-vector. The gauge invariance of the potential—the fact that only potential differences, not absolute values, have physical meaning—becomes the organising principle of all quantum field theory.

In solid-state physics, the potential difference between two materials in contact (contact or Volta potential) determines the properties of p-n junctions in diodes and transistors, the foundation of all modern electronics. The electrochemical potential, which extends electric potential difference to systems with chemical concentration gradients, is fundamental in batteries, fuel cells and cell biology.
