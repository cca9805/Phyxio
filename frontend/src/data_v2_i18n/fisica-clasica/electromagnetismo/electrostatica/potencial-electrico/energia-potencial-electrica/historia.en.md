## Historical problem

The question that motivated the development of the concept of electric potential energy was not abstract: it was the practical question of how much work must be done to move a charge in an electric field, and whether that work depends on the path taken or only on the starting and ending points. At the end of the eighteenth century, when Coulomb established his law of forces between charges and physicists began to study electric fields systematically, no conceptual framework existed to describe the energy stored in charge configurations. The force at each point could be calculated, but there was no direct way to find a charge's speed after travelling a path without laboriously integrating that force step by step.

The problem was especially acute because the electric force changes direction and magnitude along the path in a non-trivial way. For curved trajectories in non-uniform fields, integration was practically impossible with available methods. Physics needed a state quantity that captured all the energetic information of the charge's position without depending on the history of its motion.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The principal obstacle was the absence of the concept of the field as an autonomous physical entity. For physicists of Coulomb's and Franklin's era, electric interaction was action at a distance: one charge exerted a force on another with nothing mediating the intervening space. In this conceptual framework, speaking of "energy stored in the field" made no sense, because the field did not exist as a real entity: only forces between charges existed.

The second difficulty was mathematical in nature. The conservation theorem for conservative forces—stating that work depends only on the path's endpoints—was not formulated in general terms. Although Leibniz and his contemporaries had developed the concept of vis viva (kinetic energy), the idea of a scalar potential energy function from which the force could be derived was a genuinely new concept. Without that formalism, demonstrating that the work of the electric field is path-independent required an explicit calculation for each path, making the concept difficult to generalize.

## What changed

The key step was taken by Siméon Denis Poisson in the first decade of the nineteenth century, when he formalized the concept of the potential function for Newton's gravitational field and extended it to electrostatics. Poisson showed that for forces decaying as the inverse square of distance, there exists a scalar function—the potential—whose negative derivative in any direction gives the force component in that direction. This meant all the field's information was encapsulated in a single number per point in space: the potential.

George Green independently developed equivalent mathematical tools through what are now called Green's functions and Green's theorem, relating the potential to the charge distributions that generate it. Green's 1828 publication was seminal, though it went unnoticed for years until Lord Kelvin rediscovered it. The combined result was a complete formalism where the potential energy of a charge in a field was fully determined by the field's scalar potential, without needing to specify any trajectory.

## Impact on physics

The potential formalism transformed electrostatics from a collection of case-by-case force calculations into a discipline with a coherent mathematical architecture. Instead of working with force vectors at each point, physicists could work with a scalar function and recover the force by taking the gradient. This simplification was not merely computational: it revealed a deep structure of nature, the existence of conserved quantities associated with fields that can be derived from scalar potentials.

The potential formalism was the prototype Maxwell used when developing his theory of electromagnetism. The extension from the static electric potential to the dynamic electromagnetic potential (including the magnetic vector potential) was conceptually possible precisely because the scalar formalism was already established. Without electric potential energy as a consolidated concept, Maxwell's theory would have been far harder to formulate.

The importance extends beyond electrostatics: the same potential function formalism appears in quantum mechanics (the Coulomb potential in the Schrödinger equation defines the energy levels of the hydrogen atom), in statistical physics (the Coulomb potential energy between ions determines the thermodynamics of electrolytes), and in general relativity (where the Newtonian potential is the weak-field approximation of the metric tensor).

## Connection with modern physics

Electric potential energy is the conceptual foundation of electrochemistry and all battery technology. When a battery delivers current, it converts electric potential difference (and therefore [[energia_potencial_electrica]]) into chemical energy or mechanical work. The voltage of an electrochemical cell is nothing other than the difference in [[energia_potencial_electrica]] per unit charge between the two electrodes, determined by the redox reactions occurring at each.

In particle physics, the energy of particles in accelerators is commonly measured in electronvolts (eV), a unit defined directly as the potential energy gained or lost by an electron crossing a potential difference of 1 volt. This unit, which combines the concept of [[energia_potencial_electrica]] with the elementary charge, spans all scales of physics: from conduction electrons in metals (tenths of eV) to protons in the Large Hadron Collider (TeV). The ubiquity of the electronvolt in modern physics is testimony that electric potential energy is not a historical concept but an active pillar of the scientific edifice.
