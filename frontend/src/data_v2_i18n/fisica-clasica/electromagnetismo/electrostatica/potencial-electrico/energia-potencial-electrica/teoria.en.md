## Conceptual context

Electric potential energy is the concept that connects the geometry of a system of charges to the energy it can release or absorb when that geometry changes. Just as an object raised above the ground stores gravitational potential energy that can convert to kinetic energy as it falls, a charge placed in an electric field stores potential energy that can convert to motion or work.

This leaf belongs to the electric potential node and builds directly on potential difference. While potential difference describes a property of the electric field that is independent of any test charge, electric potential energy describes the energy that a specific charge has within that field. The distinction between these two quantities is one of the most frequent sources of confusion in electrostatics and is the central axis of this leaf.

Understanding electric potential energy allows solving a fundamental class of problems: predicting the speed a charge acquires when it moves between two field points, using only energy conservation without needing to know the force at every instant.

## 🟢 Essential level

**Electric potential energy** [[energia_potencial_electrica]] is the energy stored by a charge [[carga_de_prueba]] because it is placed in an electric field. When the charge moves to a position more favorable to the field, that energy converts to kinetic energy. When it moves to an unfavorable position, energy must be supplied from outside.

The intuitive picture is direct: place a positive charge near another positive charge. Since both repel, energy is stored in that configuration. Release them, and the repulsion accelerates them—potential energy converts to motion. Conversely, to bring two same-sign charges closer, you must push against the repulsion, and the work you do is stored as the system's potential energy.

The standard reference convention sets [[energia_potencial_electrica]] to zero when the charges are infinitely separated. Any charge system with finite separation generally has non-zero potential energy.

## 🔴 Structural level

The deepest structure of electric potential energy rests on the fact that the electric field is **conservative**. This means the work done by the field when moving a charge between any two points is independent of the path taken: it depends only on the starting and ending points. This property is what makes it possible to define potential energy as a function of the system's state, not of its history.

Path independence has profound consequences for problem solving. There is no need to know the force at every point along the path; it suffices to evaluate [[energia_potencial_electrica]] at the initial and final states. This is why potential energy enables elegant solutions to problems that would be intractable if one tried to integrate the force along an arbitrary trajectory.

The relationship between potential energy and the electric force also reveals the architecture of the field. In one dimension, the force component on a charge along a given direction equals the negative of the derivative of [[energia_potencial_electrica]] with respect to the coordinate in that direction. This means the force always points in the direction where [[energia_potencial_electrica]] decreases most rapidly. A free positive charge, driven solely by the field, will always move toward regions of lower [[energia_potencial_electrica]], i.e., toward regions of lower [[potencial_electrico_en_el_punto_a]] (field lines run from higher to lower potential). A free negative charge does the opposite.

The inverse-distance dependence of Coulomb interaction energy on [[r_12]] has important structural consequences. Unlike gravitational energy—always negative—electric potential energy can be positive or negative depending on charge signs. This enables bound systems (opposite-sign pairs with negative [[energia_potencial_electrica]]) and potential barriers (regions where positive [[energia_potencial_electrica]] decelerates the charge).

The superposition principle extends the two-charge formula to any number of charges by summing over all pairs; for continuous distributions this sum becomes an integral, leading to the concept of electric field energy density.

## Deep physical interpretation

The sign of [[energia_potencial_electrica]] is the first physical reading that must be made after calculating it. A positive value indicates a configuration with available energy: if left to evolve freely, the system can release energy. A negative value signals a bound state: the system requires external energy to reach infinite separation. The absolute value of [[energia_potencial_electrica]] when negative is called the binding energy and measures the stability of the configuration.

[[variacion_de_energia_potencial_electrica]] equals the work done by an external agent moving the charge at constant speed. An agent moving [[carga_de_prueba]] toward higher potential does positive work that increases [[energia_potencial_electrica]]; the field does positive work in the opposite direction. Also, [[energia_potencial_electrica]] is always a system property: the interaction energy between [[carga_de_prueba]] and the field of the rest of the system, not something [[carga_de_prueba]] carries intrinsically.

## Order of magnitude

Typical scales of [[energia_potencial_electrica]] span many orders of magnitude depending on context. In atomic and molecular physics, the interaction energy between the electron and proton in the hydrogen atom is approximately −13.6 eV, equivalent to about −2.2×10⁻¹⁸ J. This tiny amount of energy is nonetheless sufficient to keep the electron bound to the nucleus.

## Personalized resolution method

To calculate [[energia_potencial_electrica]] for a charge [[carga_de_prueba]] in a field of known potential [[potencial_electrico_en_el_punto_a]]:

1. Identify which charge's energy is sought and what the external field potential is at that charge's position.
2. Apply the fundamental relation, carefully checking the signs of [[carga_de_prueba]] and [[potencial_electrico_en_el_punto_a]].
3. The result in joules is the energy stored in the interaction.

To calculate the interaction energy between two point charges [[q_1]] and [[q_2]]:

1. Measure or calculate the separation distance [[r_12]].
2. Insert the values with their correct signs into the Coulomb energy formula.
3. Interpret the sign of the result: positive means repulsion, negative means attraction.

To apply energy conservation when a charge moves between two points:
1. Calculate [[energia_potencial_electrica]] in the initial state and in the final state.
2. The change [[variacion_de_energia_potencial_electrica]] equals the negative of the work done by the electric field.
3. If initial and final kinetic energies are known, the difference in [[energia_potencial_electrica]] gives the net work of all non-electric agents.

## Special cases and extended example

**Charge inside a conductor**: inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field is zero and the potential is constant. Therefore, [[energia_potencial_electrica]] of any charge placed at any interior point of the conductor is the same. A small charge moving inside the conductor experiences no change in [[energia_potencial_electrica]] and the field does no work on it—consistent with the zero interior field.

**Negative charge in a positive potential region**: if [[carga_de_prueba]] is negative and [[potencial_electrico_en_el_punto_a]] is positive, [[energia_potencial_electrica]] is negative. The charge tends to move toward regions of higher potential (unlike positive charges, which move toward lower potential). This asymmetry is fundamental in electron motion in circuits and in semiconductor physics.

**Two equal charges approaching each other**: as [[r_12]] between two same-sign charges decreases, [[energia_potencial_electrica]] increases monotonically. All that increase in energy comes from the work done by the external agent that brought them closer. If that agent is removed at some point, the repulsion converts the stored potential energy into kinetic energy: the charges accelerate away until practically all the initial [[energia_potencial_electrica]] has become kinetic energy.

## Real student questions

**Why can [[energia_potencial_electrica]] be negative?**

The system is more stable than the reference (charges at infinity). Negative [[energia_potencial_electrica]] violates no principle: separating the system to the reference requires supplying exactly that amount of energy.

**Why does the energy not depend on the path if the force varies from point to point?**

Because the electric field is conservative: work depends only on the endpoints. The field derives from a scalar potential, which mathematically guarantees this property.

**If I double the distance [[r_12]], what happens to [[energia_potencial_electrica]]?**

Doubling [[r_12]] reduces [[energia_potencial_electrica]] by half: direct consequence of the inverse-distance dependence. The energy decays more gently than the Coulomb force (which falls off as the square of the distance), with important implications for system stability.

**Is [[energia_potencial_electrica]] a property of the charge or of the field?**

A system property: the interaction between [[carga_de_prueba]] and the field of the rest of the system. It belongs neither to [[carga_de_prueba]] alone (which acquires different [[energia_potencial_electrica]] at different points) nor to the field alone (which exists without any test charge).

## Cross-cutting connections and study paths

Electric potential energy connects directly to [potential difference](leaf:fisica-clasica/electromagnetismo/electrostatica/potencial-electrico/diferencia-de-potencial), which is [[variacion_de_energia_potencial_electrica]] divided by the test charge. Mastering this leaf is essential to understand why potential difference is the most useful operational concept in circuits: it eliminates dependence on [[carga_de_prueba]] and gives a pure characteristic of the field.

Looking ahead, electric potential energy is the key to understanding energy stored in capacitors, where the energy is proportional to the square of the applied voltage. It is also the entry point to solid-state physics: the binding energy between ions in a crystal lattice is calculated by summing Coulomb interaction energies over all pairs.

The connection to classical mechanics through conservation of total energy (kinetic plus electric potential) is the bridge that allows analyzing particle accelerators, cathode ray tubes, and the Bohr atom without solving differential equations of motion.

## Final synthesis

Electric potential energy [[energia_potencial_electrica]] quantifies the energy stored in the interaction between a charge and its surrounding electric field. Its calculation requires knowing the potential [[potencial_electrico_en_el_punto_a]] at the charge's position—or the system geometry for direct charge-to-charge interaction—and demands rigorous attention to signs, which determine whether the interaction is attractive or repulsive and whether the energy is available to be released or must be supplied from outside.
