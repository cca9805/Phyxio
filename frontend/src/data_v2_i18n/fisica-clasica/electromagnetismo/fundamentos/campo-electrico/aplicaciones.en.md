# Applications

## 1. Oscilloscope screens and cathode ray tubes

In classic analog oscilloscopes and cathode ray tubes (CRTs), an electron beam is accelerated and laterally deflected by conductive plates between which a nearly uniform electric field is established. Applying a potential difference between the plates creates a field that exerts a force [[fuerza_electrica]] on the beam electrons proportional to the electron charge multiplied by [[campo_electrico]].

The field between the plates acts as a continuous force perpendicular to the initial direction of motion, curving the electron trajectory and shifting the impact point on the screen. Image precision depends directly on controlling the intensity and direction of [[campo_electrico]] with high accuracy. Without the concept of the field as a quantifiable vector quantity, it would be impossible to calculate the deflection before building the device.

Dominant variable: the electric field strength [[campo_electrico]] between the deflecting plates, which determines the magnitude of the lateral deflection of the beam.
Validity limit: the uniform field model between the plates is valid as long as the plate spacing is much smaller than their length, and as long as the electron velocity is below 10% of the speed of light.

## 2. Electroscope and static charge measurement

The gold-leaf electroscope is the oldest and conceptually most transparent electric field detector. When a charged object approaches the instrument's electrode, the field generated by the charge displaces the free electrons of the metal, inducing an opposite charge on the electrode and a same-sign charge on the leaves. The leaves, with equal sign, repel each other and visibly separate.

The angular separation of the leaves is a qualitative measure of the intensity of [[campo_electrico]] in the electrode region. The phenomenon directly illustrates that the field exists as a property of space before the test charge (the leaves) is placed there: the field of the external object reorganizes the free charges of the instrument. This is the most direct experimental demonstration that [[campo_electrico]] is independent of the test charge in the limit where it does not disturb the source.

Dominant variable: the electric field [[campo_electrico]] generated by the external charge, which determines the charge redistribution in the instrument and the magnitude of the leaf separation.
Validity limit: the model fails for charged objects very close to the electrode whose field cannot be treated as uniform at the instrument scale, or when ambient humidity reduces air resistivity and allows charge leakage.

## 3. Industrial electrostatic precipitators

Electrostatic precipitators are used in thermal power plants and cement factories to remove dust and ash particles from combustion gases before their emission into the atmosphere. The principle is simple: a negatively charged central electrode generates a very intense field [[campo_electrico]] that ionizes the surrounding gas. Dust particles capture ions and acquire charge; the field [[campo_electrico]] between the central electrode and the collector plates then exerts a force [[fuerza_electrica]] on the charged particles, dragging them toward the plates where they are collected.

Collection efficiency depends on the time particles spend in the field and on the magnitude of [[fuerza_electrica]], which in turn depends on [[campo_electrico]] and the charge acquired by each particle. An insufficient field does not charge particles properly; an excessive field can cause electrical arcs that damage the equipment. Optimal design requires precisely computing the distribution of [[campo_electrico]] inside the precipitator.

Dominant variable: the electric field strength [[campo_electrico]] in the ionization region, which controls the particle charging rate and the drag force toward the collector plates.
Validity limit: the model fails for very small particles (below 1 μm) that do not acquire enough charge, and for gases with high conductivity that short-circuit the field before ionizing the particles.

## 4. Field-effect biosensors for immunoaffinity detection

Field-effect biosensors (biological FETs) detect the presence of biological molecules by measuring the change in the surface electric field of a semiconductor. When a protein or DNA fragment binds to the sensor surface, its electric charge alters the field [[campo_electrico]] at the semiconductor-fluid interface. That change modifies the conductance of the underlying semiconductor channel, producing a measurable electrical signal.

The operating principle depends entirely on the field generated by a single charged molecule—with charges of the order of the elementary charge, i.e. 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C—being detectable at the nanometric scale of the semiconductor channel. Sensor sensitivity is limited by the ionic screening of the biological fluid, which reduces the Debye length to distances of the order of 1 nm in physiological solutions. Beyond that distance, the molecular field is completely screened.

Dominant variable: the electric field [[campo_electrico]] generated by the charge of biological molecules at the sensor surface, which controls the conductance change of the semiconductor channel.
Validity limit: the model fails for molecules farther than the Debye length of the electrolyte from the sensor surface, and for high ionic concentrations that drastically reduce that screening length.

## 5. Electrostatic lenses in focused ion beam microscopes

Focused ion beam (FIB) microscopes use electric fields to focus and steer ion beams with sub-nanometer precision onto surfaces. An electrostatic lens consists of a series of cylindrical electrodes at different potentials that generate distributions of [[campo_electrico]] designed to converge ion trajectories toward a focal point, analogously to how an optical lens converges light.

The focal spot radius depends on the intensity and geometry of [[campo_electrico]] in the lens region. A poorly calibrated field produces aberrations—the equivalent of optical defocus—that broaden the impact spot and reduce microscope resolution. Designing electrostatic lenses requires numerically solving the [[campo_electrico]] distribution in complex three-dimensional geometries, where the point-charge formula serves only as a qualitative guide.

Dominant variable: the spatial distribution of [[campo_electrico]] along the lens axis, which determines the focal position and aberration of the ion beam.
Validity limit: the point-charge model is inadequate for describing the complete lens; Laplace's equations must be solved with real boundary conditions. The model is valid only for estimating the order of magnitude of the field in small sections of the axis where the geometry is locally simple.
