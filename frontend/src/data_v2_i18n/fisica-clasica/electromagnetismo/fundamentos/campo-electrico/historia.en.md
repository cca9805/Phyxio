## Historical problem

In the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, electricity was described exclusively in terms of forces between charges: Coulomb's law (1785) established the direct force between two charges, but there was no satisfactory answer to the question of **how** that force was transmitted across empty space. Did one charge act directly on another at any distance and instantaneously, or was there some intermediary mechanism in the space separating them?

This problem of *action at a distance* was not merely philosophical: it directly affected how the dynamics of systems with many charges should be formulated and, above all, how the propagation of electrical disturbances should be interpreted. Newton had implicitly accepted action at a distance for gravitation, but many nineteenth-century physicists found that idea conceptually unsatisfactory for electricity.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The central obstacle was the absence of a concrete physical object that "filled" the space between charges and could carry the interaction. Newtonian physics described the world in terms of particles and direct forces between them; vacuum was, by definition, the absence of anything.

Faraday lacked advanced mathematical training and could not express his intuitions in equations, which for decades prevented his idea of "lines of force" from being accepted by the dominant scientific community, which demanded a quantitative analytical treatment. Continental physicists, especially those of the French school (Ampère, Poisson), preferred action-at-a-distance models that produced correct results without invoking an intermediary entity in space.

## What changed

Michael Faraday, working at the Royal Institution laboratory in London between 1830 and 1850, systematically developed the concept of lines of force as real physical objects existing in space. His experiments with magnets, coils, and sulfur rubber surfaces dusted with iron filings showed him that lines of force had their own geometric properties: tension along their length and lateral repulsion between adjacent lines.

The decisive advance came when James Clerk Maxwell, between 1855 and 1873, translated Faraday's qualitative ideas into the mathematical language of vector analysis. Maxwell defined the electric field as a vector quantity at every point in space and showed that this field satisfied a set of differential equations (Maxwell's equations) that predicted the propagation of electrical disturbances at finite speed, equal to the speed of light. The field ceased to be a mathematical convenience and became the physical carrier of electromagnetic energy and information.

## Impact on physics

The field concept radically transformed the structure of theoretical physics. Before Maxwell, physics was essentially a physics of particles and direct forces. Afterward, the field became the fundamental object: charges create fields and fields act on charges. This conceptual inversion was the seed of all modern twentieth-century physics.

The formalization of the electric field paved the way for the unified electromagnetic theory, which demonstrated that light is an electromagnetic wave, and for Einstein's special relativity, which reformulated mechanics to be consistent with the finite speed of field propagation. Without the concept of the field as an independent physical entity, special relativity would have lacked conceptual motivation.

## Connection with modern physics

The classical electric field is the low-energy limit of quantum electrodynamics (QED), the theory describing the interaction between charges as the exchange of virtual photons. In QED, the field is not a continuous quantity but the expectation value of the quantum field operator, and the Coulomb interaction arises as the sum of infinitely many Feynman diagrams.

In contemporary technology, the electric field concept is inseparable from solid-state devices: transistors, integrated circuits, and LCD screens operate by controlling [[campo_electrico]] distributions at the nanometric scale. Semiconductor engineering is, in essence, the engineering of the electric field at length scales of a few nanometers, where the concept remains perfectly valid until quantization begins to matter.
