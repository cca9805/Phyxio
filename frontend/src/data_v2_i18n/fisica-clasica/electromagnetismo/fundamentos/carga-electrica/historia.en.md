## Historical problem

The nature of electricity was for centuries one of the darkest questions in natural philosophy. The Greeks knew that rubbed amber attracted light objects, and the Greek word for amber, *elektron*, would centuries later give its name to the entire discipline. But the question that no one could answer was: **what property of amber, and of electrified bodies in general, originates that attraction?**

During the 17th and 18th centuries, natural philosophers debated whether electricity was a single fluid (a kind of elastic ether) or two opposing fluids that could be separated and recombined. Benjamin Franklin proposed in 1750 that there was a single electric fluid and that positively charged objects had an excess of that fluid, while negatively charged ones had a deficit. The idea was conceptually rich but mechanistically wrong: the actual carriers are electrons, which move in the opposite direction to what Franklin assumed for his hypothetical fluid.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The central obstacle was the absence of an atomic framework for matter. Without knowing that matter is made of discrete particles — protons, electrons — there was no way to understand why charge should have an indivisible minimum. The idea that charge might be continuous (like Franklin's fluid) was perfectly coherent with 18th-century physics, and nothing in the macroscopic experiments of the era clearly contradicted it.

The second difficulty was instrumental: measuring very small charges required high-sensitivity electrometers that did not exist. Experiments with charged spheres, electrostatic machines and Leyden jars were only sensitive to charges of order microcoulombs or larger — scales at which the granularity of charge is completely invisible. The macroscopic physics of electricity worked perfectly with the continuous model, so no one had experimental reason to postulate the existence of a minimum.

## What changed

The decisive advance came in two stages. The first was the discovery of the electron by J. J. Thomson in 1897: by measuring the charge-to-mass ratio of cathode rays in vacuum tubes, Thomson demonstrated that all rays consisted of identical, light, negative particles. For the first time, an elementary charge carrier with well-defined properties was identified.

The second stage, which directly established quantization, was Robert A. Millikan's oil drop experiment between 1909 and 1913. Millikan suspended microscopic oil drops in a vertical electric field and measured the charge of each drop with precision. Analysing thousands of measurements, he found that all charges were integer multiples of a minimum value: 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C. This value was exactly that of the electron charge discovered by Thomson. Millikan received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1923, partly for this result.

## Impact on physics

The demonstration of charge quantization had transformative consequences. First, it definitively consolidated the atomic theory of matter: if charge is quantized, the matter that carries it must also be discrete. This decisively strengthened the atomist programme at a time when some physicists still resisted accepting the reality of atoms.

Second, the elementary charge [[carga_elemental]] became a fundamental constant of nature, alongside the speed of light and Planck's constant. Its precise value was necessary to connect macroscopic with atomic physics: without knowing [[carga_elemental]], it was impossible to calculate how many electrons flowed in a given current, or how much energy an electron released when dropping to an atomic level.

Third, the fact that [[carga_elemental]] was universal — the same for every electron in every experiment and every place in the universe — suggested a depth that classical physics could not explain. This universality was one of the driving forces behind the development of quantum mechanics and, later, quantum field theory.

## Connection with modern physics

The quantization of electric charge remains an empirical fact without full explanation within the standard model of particle physics: theoretical physics can describe what [[carga_elemental]] is and how it acts, but not why nature chose that particular value and not another. This open question connects electric charge with high-energy physics, where current research attempts to understand whether [[carga_elemental]] might be different in other hypothetical regions of the universe or under the extreme conditions of the early Big Bang.

In modern technology, [[carga_elemental]] directly defines the coulomb since the 2019 reform of the International System of Units: the coulomb is defined as exactly the charge of 1 / (1.602176634×10⁻¹⁹) electrons. This definition converts [[carga_elemental]] from a measured constant to an exact constant by definition, closing the circle that Millikan opened more than a century ago.
