# Applications

## 1. Magnetic-sector mass spectrometer

In isotopic analysis and analytical chemistry, the mass spectrometer ionises molecules or atoms and accelerates them with an electric field to a defined velocity. The ions then enter a sector where the magnetic field [[campo_magnetico]] is perpendicular to their velocity: [[fuerza_magnetica]] acts as the centripetal force and each ion traces a semicircular arc. The arc radius depends on the ion's mass, [[carga_electrica]] and [[campo_magnetico]], enabling identification and quantification of different isotopes or molecular fragments with parts-per-million precision.

The operating principle is that [[fuerza_magnetica]] does no work: the ion's speed entering the magnetic sector equals its speed exiting with the same magnitude, but directed 180° in the opposite direction. The impact position on the detector determines the radius and, from it, the ion's mass. For isotopes such as carbon-12 and carbon-13, the difference of one neutron produces radii differing by less than 1 %, and modern detectors resolve that difference with high reliability.

Dominant variable: the magnetic force [[fuerza_magnetica]], which acts as the centripetal force and determines the trajectory radius of each ion.
Validity limit: the uniform field model fails when non-uniformity of [[campo_magnetico]] along the trajectory exceeds 0.01 %; above that threshold, mass resolution degrades. For relativistic ions (high-energy accelerators), the relativistic correction to momentum is needed.

## 2. Wien velocity selector

In atomic spectroscopy and ion beam calibration, Wien's velocity selector combines crossed [[campo_electrico]] and [[campo_magnetico]] fields perpendicular to the beam direction. Only ions with a velocity [[velocidad_de_la_particula]] such that [[fuerza_electrica]] and [[fuerza_magnetica]] cancel exactly pass through in a straight line; all others are deflected and absorbed by the walls.

The selection condition depends only on [[campo_electrico]] and [[campo_magnetico]], not on [[carga_electrica]] or the ion's mass. This means the selector produces a velocity-monochromatic beam for any type of charged particle regardless of its mass or charge. This property is fundamental for preparing ion beams with well-defined kinetic energy before introducing them into a mass spectrometer.

Dominant variable: the particle velocity [[velocidad_de_la_particula]], which determines when [[fuerza_magnetica]] exactly compensates [[fuerza_electrica]] and the trajectory is rectilinear.
Validity limit: the model ceases to be exact when the fields are not perfectly uniform in the selection region or when beam width introduces angular dispersion. The velocity resolution of the device is limited by field precision and beam divergence.

## 3. Direct-current electric motor

In the DC electric motor, current-carrying conductors are immersed in a magnetic field [[campo_magnetico]] generated by permanent magnets or electromagnets. The Lorentz force on the electrons in the conductor is transmitted mechanically to the metal crystal lattice (through bonding forces) and produces a net force on the conductor as a whole. This force drives the motor rotor.

In this context, the relevant quantity is [[fuerza_magnetica]] on the charge carriers. The direction of the force (right-hand rule) determines the direction of rotation. Reversing the current or the field reverses the rotation direction. The mechanical power of the motor is proportional to [[fuerza_magnetica]] times the tangential velocity of the rotor.

Dominant variable: the magnetic force [[fuerza_magnetica]] on the current carriers in the rotor winding, which produces the driving torque.
Validity limit: the Lorentz force model on conductors is valid as long as the motor operates below the magnetic saturation of the iron core. Above that point, [[campo_magnetico]] does not increase linearly with the field current and the torque calculated with the linear model overestimates the actual torque.

## 4. Cathode ray tube (analogue oscilloscope)

In the classical cathode ray tube, an electron beam emitted by the electron gun is deflected by plates generating controlled [[campo_electrico]] and [[campo_magnetico]] fields. The horizontal and vertical deflection plates produce electric fields that apply [[fuerza_electrica]] to the electrons; some designs also use magnetic deflection coils that apply [[fuerza_magnetica]].

The beam deflection on the phosphorescent screen is proportional to the applied force, and therefore to the plate voltage. This converts the tube into a direct measurement instrument for varying voltages: the position of the bright spot on the screen is the visual analogue of the electrical signal. The precision of the instrument depends on the deflection being linear with force, which requires uniform fields in the deflection region.

Dominant variable: the electric force [[fuerza_electrica]] (in electrostatic deflection tubes) or the magnetic force [[fuerza_magnetica]] (in magnetic deflection tubes), which determines the spot position on the screen.
Validity limit: the linear deflection model fails for very large deflections where the beam is no longer perpendicular to the deflection field; the velocity component in the field direction reduces the effective force and the deflection is sublinear.

## 5. Aurora borealis (solar particle interaction with the geomagnetic field)

Charged particles of the solar wind (mainly electrons and protons) reaching Earth's magnetosphere experience the Lorentz force due to Earth's magnetic field [[campo_magnetico]]. Since [[campo_magnetico]] has a component that varies with latitude and distance from the geomagnetic equator, the particles are trapped in helical trajectories along the field lines and are channelled toward the poles.

As they approach the poles, where field lines converge and [[campo_magnetico]] increases in strength, the component of velocity parallel to [[campo_magnetico]] decreases (magnetic mirror effect) and the particles are reflected or precipitated into the atmosphere. Colliding with oxygen and nitrogen atoms at high altitude, they produce the light emissions that give rise to auroras. The energy of the precipitating particles comes from the initial acceleration of the solar wind, not from Earth's magnetic field (which, like all [[fuerza_magnetica]], does no work).

Dominant variable: the magnetic force [[fuerza_magnetica]] due to the geomagnetic field [[campo_magnetico]], which controls the helical trajectory and the precipitation of particles toward the poles.
Validity limit: the point-charge model in a non-uniform magnetic field fails at the scale of the entire radiation belt, where guiding-centre drifts and radiation pressure effects must be considered. For an individual particle at the scale of its local helical trajectory, the model is a good approximation.
