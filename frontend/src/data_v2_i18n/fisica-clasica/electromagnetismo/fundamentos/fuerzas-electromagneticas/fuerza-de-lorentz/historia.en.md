## Historical problem

At the end of the 19th century, physics was at a moment of productive crisis: Maxwell's theory described electromagnetic fields with precise and elegant equations, but the connection between those fields and the forces on the material particles that created them had not been formulated in a compact way. Physicists knew that a moving charge experiences electric and magnetic forces, but the unification of both in a single vector expression was missing.

The concrete problem was this: given an electric field and a magnetic field in a region of space, what exactly is the force on a charged particle moving in that region? The answer was not obvious because the magnetic force depends on the particle's velocity, something unprecedented in classical Newtonian mechanics where gravitational forces do not depend on velocity.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was conceptual: a force that depends on velocity was alien to classical mechanistic thinking. In Newtonian mechanics, forces derived from potentials that depended only on position. A force that changes according to how the particle moves could not be derived from an ordinary scalar potential, complicating the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulation of electrodynamics.

Moreover, the experiments of the time showed apparently unrelated phenomena: the deflection of cathode rays, electromagnetic induction and the force between parallel conductors. Without a unified expression for the force on a charge, these phenomena seemed to require separate explanations.

## What changed

Hendrik Antoon Lorentz systematised the force expression in the form we know today in his 1892 paper and 1895 treatise, building on earlier work by Maxwell and Heaviside. Lorentz explicitly stated that the force on a point charge is the vector sum of the force due to the electric field plus the cross product of the velocity with the magnetic field multiplied by the charge. This formulation unified all known cases of forces on charges in a single expression.

The key conceptual shift was recognising that the particle's velocity is as legitimate a state variable as its position when calculating the force. This opened the door to Lagrangian mechanics with velocity-dependent potentials, enabling classical electrodynamics to be formulated with variational tools.

## Impact on physics

Lorentz's formulation had transformative consequences in two directions. First, it enabled the development of the theory of the electron as a fundamental physical entity: if the force on a moving point charge is completely determined by the local fields, then the electron is the basic unit of electricity, and all matter is composed of these discrete units. This idea was revolutionary before Thomson's discovery of the electron in 1897.

Second, the velocity dependence of the magnetic force was a crucial ingredient for Einstein's special relativity (1905). Einstein showed that if the Lorentz force has the correct form in one reference frame, the Lorentz transformations are the only transformations between inertial frames that preserve the laws of electromagnetism. The Lorentz force is not merely extended electrostatics: it is a consequence of the geometry of spacetime.

## Connection with modern physics

In 20th-century physics, the Lorentz force gained a second life in the context of quantum electrodynamics (QED). The interaction between a photon and an electron — the fundamental vertex of QED — is the quantum version of the Lorentz force: the photon is the carrier of the electromagnetic interaction and the probability amplitude of the interaction corresponds, in the classical limit, to the macroscopic Lorentz force.

In plasma physics and in today's particle accelerators, the Lorentz force is the central engineering tool: linear accelerators, cyclotrons, synchrotrons and tokamaks for nuclear fusion are designed by manipulating precise [[campo_electrico]] and [[campo_magnetico]] fields to apply controlled Lorentz forces on particle beams or on confined plasmas. The force that Lorentz formulated in 1892 remains the design equation of 21st-century particle technology.
