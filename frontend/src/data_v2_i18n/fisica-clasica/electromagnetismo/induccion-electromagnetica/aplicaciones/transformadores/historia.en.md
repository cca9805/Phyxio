## Historical problem

By the mid-nineteenth century, the industrial revolution had domesticated electricity for lighting and motive power uses, but only in the immediate vicinity of generating stations. The impossibility of transporting electrical energy over long distances limited industrial development to the proximity of generation sources, whether hydroelectric or thermal power plants. The unanswered question was how to reduce Joule effect losses in transmission cables, which consumed most of the generated energy when attempting to transport it beyond a few kilometres.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the belief that electricity, like hydraulic fluids, had to be transported at constant pressure to be useful. Engineers of the time attempted to minimise losses by increasing conductor diameters to impractical dimensions, or accepting that factories had to be built alongside the rivers that fed the turbines. The idea that voltage could be arbitrarily modified without energy loss was counterintuitive because it violated the dominant hydraulic analogy. No known device allowed voltage to be raised for transmission and lowered for use while maintaining power.

## What changed

In 1831, Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction, establishing that a varying magnetic field could induce current in a nearby conductor. However, the transformer as a practical device did not develop until the 1870s and 1880s, when the war of the currents between Edison and Westinghouse/Tesla made the need for high-voltage systems evident. Pioneers Lucien Gaulard and John Dixon Gibbs in Europe, and subsequently George Westinghouse in America, developed practical transformers with laminated iron cores that minimised losses.

The decisive milestone was the 1891 exhibition in Frankfurt, where three-phase high-voltage transmission at 25 kV over 175 km was demonstrated, something impossible with direct current systems. The victory of alternating current, enabled by the transformer, determined the architecture of the world's electrical systems to the present day.

## Impact on physics

The transformer consolidated the understanding of mutual electromagnetic induction as a first-order operating principle. It demonstrated that energy could be transferred between circuits without material connection, through the magnetic field, establishing the physical reality of the field as an entity with measurable and manipulable properties. The exact mathematical relationship between the turns ratio and induced voltages provided one of the most direct verifications of Faraday's law under engineering conditions.

Furthermore, the development of magnetic materials for transformer cores drove the systematic study of the magnetisation of ferromagnetic materials, hysteresis losses and eddy currents. These phenomena, initially considered mere technical imperfections, became areas of physical research with ramifications in materials theory and solid-state physics.

## Connection with modern physics

The principles of the transformer are identical to those of mutual induction in quantum circuits and in superconducting devices. Radio frequency impedance transformers, used in communications, operate according to the same equations but with ferritic ceramic materials instead of laminated iron. At optical frequencies, nanophotonic structures perform similar coupling and transformation functions through coupled optical resonators.

In modern power engineering, solid-state transformers based on power electronics challenge the supremacy of traditional electromagnetic transformers. These devices, which use AC-DC-AC converters and high-speed semiconductors, can transform frequency in addition to voltage, enabling high-voltage direct current transmission systems that the conventional transformer cannot handle directly. The deep understanding of the classical transformer remains the indispensable foundation for designing these hybrid systems of the twenty-first century.
