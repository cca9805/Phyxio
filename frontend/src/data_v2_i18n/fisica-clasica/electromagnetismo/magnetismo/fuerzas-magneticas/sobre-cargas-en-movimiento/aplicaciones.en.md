# Applications

## 1. Mass spectrometry

The most direct application is ion separation in mass spectrometers. A charged particle enters a region with [[campo_magnetico]] and its path bends. The detector does not measure force directly; it measures an impact position associated with [[radio_de_trayectoria]].

Dominant variable: [[radio_de_trayectoria]].
Validity limit: the field must be uniform and ion [[rapidez]] must be controlled.

The physical reading is powerful because it converts a difference in [[masa_de_la_particula]] or [[carga_electrica]] into a measurable distance. More massive ions, at equal charge and [[rapidez]], deflect less. Ions with opposite signs bend toward opposite sides.

In practice, the instrument usually prepares a beam with known energy or [[rapidez]] first. Then the magnetic field acts as a curvature analyzer. Precision depends not only on measuring [[campo_magnetico]], but also on beam width, detector position, and the effective value of [[rapidez]].

This application shows the full reading of the leaf. [[fuerza_magnetica]] starts the deflection, [[masa_de_la_particula]] resists the change of direction, and [[radio_de_trayectoria]] translates that competition into an observable coordinate. The formula is therefore not an isolated calculation, but a bridge between a microscopic property and an experimental trace.

## 2. Accelerators and beam lines

In accelerators, magnets bend and focus charged particle beams. The magnitude [[fuerza_magnetica]] determines the ability to change beam direction, while [[radio_de_trayectoria]] sets the curvature of a transport section.

Dominant variable: [[campo_magnetico]].
Validity limit: at high energies, relativistic corrections must be included.

The design does not use the magnetic field to increase kinetic energy; electric fields do that. Magnets keep the beam on a planned path, select momentum, and correct transverse deviations.

In a beam line, small changes in [[campo_magnetico]] can move the impact point by many centimetres after several metres of travel. This requires precise magnet calibration and careful alignment of the perpendicular component of [[rapidez]]. Poor alignment changes [[s_theta]] and alters the real force.

The application also explains why an accelerator uses families of elements. Dipoles bend, quadrupoles focus, and other correctors tune the path. All start from the same local idea: a moving charge experiences magnetic deflection when it crosses the field with the proper orientation.

## 3. Cloud chambers and particle detectors

In visual detectors, an ionizing particle leaves a curved trace if it crosses a magnetic field. The curvature allows the sign of [[carga_electrica]] to be inferred and the relation between momentum and charge to be estimated.

Dominant variable: [[fuerza_magnetica]].
Validity limit: the trace must be sufficiently clean and the field known.

The experimental information appears not as an equation on a screen, but as a curve. A tight curve indicates strong deflection; an almost straight path may indicate high energy, weak field, or small charge.

The reading of direction is as important as the reading of magnitude. If two traces bend toward opposite sides under the same [[campo_magnetico]], the natural interpretation is opposite signs of [[carga_electrica]]. If they bend toward the same side but with different radii, the difference may lie in [[masa_de_la_particula]], [[rapidez]], or effective charge.

Modern detectors replace the visible droplet track with electronic sensors, but the physics logic remains. The trajectory is reconstructed, [[radio_de_trayectoria]] is estimated, and information about the particle is inferred. Magnetic force acts as a converter between invisible motion and measurable geometry.

## 4. Magnetic confinement of plasmas

In a plasma, charged electrons and ions tend to rotate around field lines. Although a real plasma requires collective models, the local idea of [[fuerza_magnetica]] on moving charges explains why the field can limit transverse transport.

Dominant variable: [[radio_de_trayectoria]].
Validity limit: collisions, gradients, and collective effects can dominate over the simple orbit.

The gyration radius, related to [[masa_de_la_particula]], [[carga_electrica]], [[rapidez]], and [[campo_magnetico]], helps estimate whether a species is well confined. Stronger fields reduce radius and make it harder for particles to cross the confinement region transversely.

This application forces species-by-species reasoning. Electrons, with much smaller mass, can have very different gyration radii from ions even in the same field. The result affects energy transport, stability, and particle loss toward walls.

The leaf does not replace plasma physics, but it supplies the local reasoning unit. Before discussing collective currents, waves, or turbulence, one must understand that each moving charge receives a deflection determined by [[fuerza_magnetica]] and its inertia.

## 5. Sensors and velocity selection

Devices that combine electric and magnetic fields can select particles by velocity. In the magnetic part, deflection depends on [[rapidez]] and [[campo_magnetico]], so adjusting the field controls which particles pass through a region without striking walls or apertures.

Dominant variable: [[rapidez]].
Validity limit: the beam must be collimated and unmodeled external forces must be small.

This application forces a separation between intensity and direction. A particle with the expected [[rapidez]] may follow the channel; another with different [[rapidez]] deflects more or less. The reading of [[s_theta]] also matters, because poor alignment changes the expected deflection.

In Hall-effect sensors and beam diagnostic devices, the deflection of moving charges produces a measurable transverse separation. Although the full device includes materials, contacts, and electronics, the first physical reading is still that charges moving inside [[campo_magnetico]] receive a lateral [[fuerza_magnetica]].

The practical limit appears when the material medium modifies mobility, when collisions are frequent, or when the device geometry no longer allows the motion to be treated as a free particle. Then the leaf remains the conceptual base, but it must be coupled to electric transport models.
