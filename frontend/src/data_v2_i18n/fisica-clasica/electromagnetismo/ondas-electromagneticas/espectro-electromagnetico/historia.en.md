## Historical problem

During the nineteenth century, physics knew phenomena that seemed to belong to separate worlds: visible light, radiant heat, electricity, magnetism, and invisible radiation detected beyond red and violet. Herschel had observed infrared radiation and Ritter had identified chemical effects associated with ultraviolet, but a unifying idea was still missing.

The physical problem was to order very different phenomena without reducing them to practical names. The deeper question was whether light was an isolated case or part of a broader electromagnetic structure.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty was that each region was detected with different instruments. The human eye sees only a narrow band; thermometers revealed infrared, photographic plates showed ultraviolet, and later discharge tubes and electrical detectors opened other regions. Without a common theory, it was natural to treat each radiation as an independent entity.

There was also no precise connection between spatial scale, temporal rate, and propagation. It was not enough to say that they were waves: [[lambda]], [[frecuencia]], and speed had to be linked quantitatively.

## What changed

The decisive change came with Maxwell, who showed that the equations of electromagnetism allow waves travelling at the speed of light. That coincidence was not accidental: it suggested that light was an electromagnetic wave. Hertz then confirmed experimentally the existence of radio waves, extending the idea beyond visible light.

Later, Planck and Einstein added the quantum reading: [[frecuencia]] does not only order waves, it also fixes the energy of each photon. The spectrum became a double scale, both wave-like and particle-like.

## Impact on physics

The electromagnetic spectrum unified optics, electricity, and magnetism. It made clear that radio, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X rays, and gamma rays are not disconnected phenomena, but regions of one family distinguished by [[lambda]], [[frecuencia]], and [[energia_de_un_foton]].

This unification changed experimental physics. Measuring one region helped anticipate properties of others. It also opened communication technology, medical imaging, spectroscopy, astronomy, and materials control.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the electromagnetic spectrum is central in physics, engineering, and medicine. Astronomy observes the universe in radio, infrared, visible, X-ray, and gamma bands; chemistry identifies molecules through absorption; wireless technology assigns bands by [[frecuencia]]; radiology uses high-energy photons with dose control.

The modern connection is direct: understanding the spectrum means knowing which quantity dominates in each context. For antenna design [[lambda]] dominates, for color [[frecuencia]] dominates, and for microscopic interaction [[energia_de_un_foton]] dominates.
