## Ideal model

The ideal model treats the spectrum as a continuous family of plane electromagnetic waves in vacuum. Each radiation is identified by [[frecuencia]] and [[lambda]], connected by [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]]. The individual energy of each quantum is read with [[energia_de_un_foton]] and [[constante_de_planck]].

This model separates spectral region from total intensity. It classifies radiation by [[frecuencia]], [[lambda]], and photon energy, not by brightness, source power, or number of emitted photons.

## Hypotheses

Vacuum propagation, or air as a first approximation, is assumed with no relevant material dispersion. The radiation is also assumed to be represented by one dominant [[frecuencia]], even though real sources may have broad bands.

The elementary quantum reading uses [[energia_de_un_foton]] for a single photon. It does not compute total power, dose, or photon flux. Those require additional magnitudes belonging to other leaves.

## Quantitative validity domain

The model is appropriate if the spectral band is narrow: for example, when relative [[frecuencia]] width is below 10 %. If the source has a broad spectrum, it is better to use a distribution rather than a single [[frecuencia]].

It is also reasonable if the medium does not appreciably change propagation. In ordinary air, many estimates use [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] with small error; in glass, water, or plasma, the error may exceed 1 % and a propagation-in-media model should be considered.

## Model failure signals

A failure signal appears when the problem mentions selective absorption, refractive index, chromatic dispersion, or broad filtering. Then a single [[lambda]] no longer summarizes the situation.

Another signal appears when [[energia_de_un_foton]] is used to decide total delivered energy. Photon energy does not state how many photons arrive per second. A source may have low-energy photons and high total power.

## Extended or alternative model

It is convenient to switch model when the question is no longer answered by one dominant [[frecuencia]] in vacuum. The explicit transition occurs if the medium is dispersive, if the source has broad bandwidth, if detailed material absorption is requested, or if intensity, dose, or photon flux matters.

The extended model may use power spectra, absorption curves, frequency-dependent index, or photon distributions. That approach is more realistic, but it hides the basic idea if introduced before [[lambda]], [[frecuencia]], and [[energia_de_un_foton]] are understood.

When a higher precision calculation is preferred, switching to the extended or alternative model introduces second-order corrections.

## Operational comparison

The ideal model is appropriate for classifying regions, converting between [[lambda]] and [[frecuencia]], and estimating photon energy. It is fast, clear, and shows why the spectrum forms a physical continuum.

The extended model is needed for real instruments, materials, filters, thermal sources, and biological risk. The practical rule is to start with the ideal model and switch only when the statement asks for bandwidth, material medium, or total power.
