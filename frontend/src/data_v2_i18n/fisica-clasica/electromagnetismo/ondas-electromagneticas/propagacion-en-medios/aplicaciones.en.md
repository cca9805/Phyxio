# Applications

## 1. Glass lenses in geometrical optics

A lens works because light changes speed when entering glass and changes again when leaving it. This change in [[v_m]] becomes a change in wavefront direction and allows rays to be focused or spread. The index [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]] determines how much deviation is obtained for a given geometry.

The dominant simplification is to treat glass as homogeneous and isotropic in the visible range. In that context, [[lambda_m]] matters for phase and interference, but the first geometrical design mainly relies on index. If the material is dispersive, different colors focus at slightly different positions.

In precision instruments, that small chromatic blur forces designers to combine different glasses or add corrections. Propagation in media is not an abstract detail: it determines sharpness, useful magnification, and color fidelity.

The same reasoning is used when choosing protective windows, microscope objectives, or laser lenses. The material must transmit the band and keep the intended index behaviour.

Dominant variable: [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]] controls speed reduction and optical deviation used to form images.
Validity limit: the simple model fails when chromatic dispersion, absorption, or material anisotropy dominates image formation.

## 2. Telecommunication optical fibers

In a fiber, core and cladding have different indices to keep light guided. The wave propagates with phase speed lower than [[velocidad_de_la_luz_en_el_vacio]] and with internal wavelength [[lambda_m]] shorter than in vacuum. That internal scale affects accumulated phase over long distances.

The real application requires separating simple estimate from full design. Effective index gives the first delay, but data pulses also depend on dispersion, modes, bends, and losses. Even so, control of [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]] is the material base of guiding.

Over long links, a small speed difference accumulates over kilometres. This is why telecommunication networks specify spectral windows, fiber type, and dispersion compensation carefully.

The first estimate from [[v_m]] tells whether a delay is plausible; the engineering model then adds modal and chromatic details.

Dominant variable: [[v_m]] governs phase delay, while [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]] controls guiding through material contrast.
Validity limit: for short pulses or long links, group speed, dispersion, and attenuation must be used.

## 3. Prisms and color separation

A prism separates colors because the effective index is not exactly the same for all frequencies. Each spectral component experiences a different phase speed inside the material and exits in a different direction. White light decomposes because the medium is dispersive.

The propagation reading avoids saying that the prism creates colors. The prism separates components that were already present, using differences in [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]]. The wavelength inside the material also changes for each component, affecting phase and optical path.

This application is didactically powerful because it shows that index is not only a speed correction. It also turns material response into a visible, measurable separation useful for spectroscopy.

A calibrated prism or grating system can therefore identify spectral content by translating propagation differences into positions on a detector.

Dominant variable: frequency-dependent [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]] determines angular separation of components.
Validity limit: if the beam is not narrow, if selective absorption is present, or if the material is not isotropic, the simple prism model is insufficient.

## 4. Antireflection coatings

Antireflection coatings use thin layers whose thickness is designed from the wavelength inside the material. The relevant quantity is not only vacuum wavelength [[lambda0]], but [[lambda_m]], because phase accumulates inside the layer.

The application combines propagation and interference. A suitable layer can make reflections from two surfaces partly cancel. To achieve this, index and thickness must be adjusted to the spectral region that should be transmitted.

The same idea is used in glasses, camera lenses, solar panels, and sensors. A coating optimized for visible light may not work the same way in infrared, because material wavelength and effective index change with region.

This is why real coatings are often multilayer designs rather than a single universal layer.

Dominant variable: [[lambda_m]] controls accumulated phase in the layer and the interference condition.
Validity limit: the ideal design works for a limited frequency band and for angles near the intended one.

## 5. Index measurement in liquids

A refractometer estimates the index of a liquid by observing how light bends or is guided when entering the sample. The value can identify concentration, purity, or approximate composition in food science, chemistry, and industrial control.

The physics of the instrument is the same as in the leaf: the medium modifies [[v_m]] and [[lambda_m]] through [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]]. But practical reading requires calibration, controlled temperature, and known wavelength. Without those conditions, the measured index may not represent the sample reliably.

In quality control, index is useful because small composition changes modify the optical response of the liquid. That sensitivity is valuable, but it also means that an index measurement is not a complete chemical identification.

The measurement is strongest when combined with temperature logs, wavelength calibration, and independent knowledge of the sample.

Dominant variable: [[indice_de_refraccion_efectivo]] is the measured quantity and is interpreted as an optical fingerprint of the medium.
Validity limit: the result depends on temperature, used frequency, sample homogeneity, and absence of strong turbidity.
