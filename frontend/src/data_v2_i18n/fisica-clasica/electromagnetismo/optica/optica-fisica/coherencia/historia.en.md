## Historical problem

Light interference was a decisive test for wave theory, but an experimental difficulty soon appeared: not every source produces clear fringes. Young, Fresnel, and other researchers needed illumination conditions that kept phase relations sufficiently stable.

The historical problem was not only to show that light interferes, but to understand why some sources and setups reveal interference while others hide it.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Before coherence was formalized, it was easy to attribute disappearing fringes to lack of intensity, screen errors, or imperfections in wave theory. The distinction between wave superposition and statistical phase stability was missing.

It was also difficult to separate temporal from spatial effects. An extended source, a broad-spectrum source, or a vibrating setup could produce similar symptoms: washed-out or unstable patterns.

## What changed

Wave optics and later coherence theory introduced a more precise description of correlation between fields. With the development of interferometers, spectroscopy, and lasers, coherence moved from a practical condition to a central quantity in modern optics.

The laser transformed the field because it provided sources with high temporal and spatial coherence, making highly precise interferometric measurements possible.

## Impact on physics

Coherence made clear that interference depends not only on the existence of waves, but on their correlation. This changed the reading of optical experiments, improved metrology, and opened applications such as holography, precision interferometry, and optical coherence tomography.

It also connected classical optics with statistical field theory and later with quantum optics.

## Connection with modern physics

Today coherence appears in stabilized lasers, optical clocks, interferometric sensors, communications, medical imaging, and quantum experiments. Designing a modern source means controlling not only power and frequency, but also phase stability.

The idea remains active because any technology based on interference must ask whether phase is preserved over the relevant scale of the experiment.
