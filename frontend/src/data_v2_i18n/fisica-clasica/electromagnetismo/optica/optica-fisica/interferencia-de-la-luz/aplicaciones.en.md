# Applications

## 1. [[lambda]] measurement with double slit

One of the most direct applications of interference is the experimental determination of the wavelength of an unknown light source. The procedure consists of illuminating two slits separated by a known distance [[separacion_entre_rendijas]] and measuring the fringe spacing [[separacion_entre_franjas_consecutivas]] on a screen placed at distance [[distancia_a_la_pantalla]]. From the relationship between these three geometric quantities, [[lambda]] is obtained with precision of the order of one per cent in standard laboratory setups.

This method is fundamental in low-resolution spectroscopy and in the calibration of educational light sources. The achievable precision depends on the quality of the [[separacion_entre_franjas_consecutivas]] measurement, the stability of the pattern and the monochromaticity of the source. In practice, a measuring microscope on the screen allows determining [[separacion_entre_franjas_consecutivas]] with error below one tenth of a millimetre.

Dominant variable: the fringe spacing [[separacion_entre_franjas_consecutivas]], directly proportional to the sought wavelength and to the geometric ratio between screen distance and slit separation.
Validity limit: the method requires coherent and monochromatic sources. For sources with significant spectral width, the pattern progressively loses contrast and precision decreases. The small-angle approximation restricts the measurement to the first orders of the pattern.

## 2. Spectroscopy with diffraction gratings

Diffraction gratings are devices containing thousands of parallel equally spaced slits that use constructive interference of multiple beams to decompose light into its spectral components. When polychromatic light strikes the grating, each wavelength satisfies the maximum condition at a different angle, spatially dispersing the spectrum. The spectral resolution improves with the number of illuminated slits.

Grating-based spectrometers are employed in analytical chemistry, stellar astrophysics, materials physics and molecular biology. A spectrometer with 1200 lines per millimetre can separate spectral lines less than one tenth of a nanometre apart in first order. Intensity decreases at high orders, but resolving power increases linearly with the order used.

Dominant variable: the diffraction order [[orden_de_la_franja]] and the total number of illuminated slits, which jointly determine the spectral resolving power of the device.
Validity limit: overlap of consecutive orders requires blocking filters when working in orders higher than the first. The available intensity decreases with order, limiting the signal-to-noise ratio at the detector.

## 3. Michelson interferometry for metrology

The Michelson interferometer splits a coherent light beam into two perpendicular arms using a semi-reflective beam splitter. Each arm contains a mirror that reflects light back. Upon recombination, the two beams interfere and the resulting intensity depends on the optical path difference [[delta]] between both arms. A displacement of the movable mirror by half a wavelength produces one complete cycle of the interference pattern.

This device is the basis of precision optical metrology, the international metre standard and gravitational wave detectors LIGO and Virgo. In industrial applications it is used to calibrate machine tools with nanometric resolution and to measure the flatness of optical surfaces with sub-fringe precision.

Dominant variable: the optical path difference [[delta]] between both arms, where the factor two arises from the round-trip travel of light in each arm.
Validity limit: the coherence length of the source limits the maximum useful path difference. Mechanical vibrations and thermal gradients limit precision in industrial environments, requiring isolation and active stabilisation.

## 4. Antireflection coatings by thin films

When light strikes a thin transparent film deposited on a substrate, beams reflected at the upper interface and the lower interface interfere. If the optical thickness of the film equals one quarter of the wavelength in the medium, the reflected beams cancel by destructive interference, eliminating reflection for that wavelength. This principle is applied to every surface of photographic lenses, electronic device screens and solar panels.

A typical antireflection coating reduces reflection from four per cent per surface to less than one per cent. Multilayer systems allow suppressing reflection over a broad spectral range by combining films of different thicknesses and materials so that destructive interference covers the entire visible spectrum.

Dominant variable: the optical thickness of the film, the product of geometric thickness and refractive index of the deposited material, which controls the path difference between beams reflected at both interfaces.
Validity limit: the film must have thickness comparable to [[lambda]] to maintain coherence between reflections. Thicknesses much greater than the temporal coherence length of the source produce incoherent superposition without selective antireflection effect.

## 5. Holography and three-dimensional recording

Holography records the interference pattern between coherent light scattered by a three-dimensional object and a plane reference wave. This pattern, recorded on a photosensitive plate or high-resolution sensor, contains complete amplitude and phase information of the optical field from the object. The phase information, inaccessible through conventional photography, is precisely what allows reconstructing depth.

When the hologram is illuminated with light similar to the original reference wave, a wavefront is reconstructed that faithfully reproduces the three-dimensional image with full parallax. Holography is applied in security banknotes, high-density data storage, digital holographic microscopy and non-contact deformation metrology.

Dominant variable: the path difference between the reference beam and the object beam at each point of the hologram, which determines the spatial frequency of the recorded fringes and encodes the angular information of the object.
Validity limit: requires sources with coherence length greater than the object depth and mechanical stability below a fraction of a wavelength during exposure. The resolution of the recording material limits the maximum angle between capturable beams.
