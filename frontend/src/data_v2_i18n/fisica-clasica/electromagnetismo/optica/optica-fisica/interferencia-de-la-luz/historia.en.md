## Historical problem

In the late 17th century, the nature of light was an open question. Newton championed a corpuscular model in his Opticks (1704), while Huygens proposed a wave model in his Traité de la Lumière (1690). Phenomena such as iridescent colours in thin films and Newton's rings lacked satisfactory explanation within the corpuscular framework. The unanswered question was: if light consists of particles, how can light added to light produce darkness?

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the prestige of Newton's corpuscular model, which satisfactorily explained reflection and refraction through forces on particles. Huygens' wave theory could not explain the rectilinear propagation of light or polarisation, making it appear inferior. Furthermore, no clean experiment existed that unequivocally demonstrated destructive superposition of light. The scientific community lacked the concept of temporal and spatial coherence, necessary to understand why ordinary sources do not produce stable interference patterns.

## What changed

In 1801, Thomas Young performed the double-slit experiment: he illuminated two narrow slits with sunlight and observed a stable pattern of bright and dark fringes on a screen. The dark fringes demonstrated that light added to light could produce darkness, something impossible for particles. Young introduced the concept of wavelength and estimated its value for visible light.

Augustin-Jean Fresnel completed the theory between 1815 and 1819, mathematically formulating the Huygens-Fresnel principle and winning the prize of the Paris Academy of Sciences. François Arago demonstrated in 1816 that perpendicularly polarised beams do not interfere, establishing the transverse nature of light.

## Impact on physics

Young's experiment and Fresnel's theory consolidated wave optics and definitively refuted Newton's corpuscular model. Maxwell identified light as electromagnetic waves in 1864, unifying optics and electromagnetism. Michelson developed the precision interferometer in 1881, whose null result in the Michelson-Morley experiment (1887) was the crisis that led to Einstein's special theory of relativity in 1905. Interferometry became the most precise measurement tool in physics.

## Connection with modern physics

The quantum version of the double-slit experiment, performed with individual particles, revealed that even isolated photons or electrons exhibit wave behaviour and interfere with themselves. This result is central to the interpretation of quantum mechanics. Modern interferometry is applied in gravitational wave detectors such as LIGO and Virgo, capable of measuring displacements on the order of fractions of a proton diameter, and in medical optical coherence tomography. The principles of interference are also the basis of the laser, holography and quantum cryptography.
