## Historical problem

By the late seventeenth century, light refraction and reflection were reasonably well described by geometrical optics, but one question remained unanswered: why did certain crystals, such as Icelandic calcite, split a single ray into two? Erasmus Bartholinus documented **double refraction** in 1669, and Christiaan Huygens attempted to explain it with his wave theory, yet he could not give a complete description because his model treated light as a longitudinal wave. The central question was whether light possessed some transverse property that existing theories failed to account for.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the Newtonian conception of light as a stream of particles (corpuscles) devoid of internal orientation. Newton observed that light reflected from glass surfaces showed distinct "sides" depending on orientation, but he could not interpret this fact in wave terms. Even within Huygens's wave theory, waves were thought to be purely longitudinal, like sound, preventing any explanation of the directional dependence of birefringence. The limiting paradigm was clear: without a transverse component, no model could distinguish between different orientations of the oscillating field.

## What changed

In 1808, Etienne-Louis Malus accidentally discovered polarization by reflection while observing sunset light reflected from the windows of the Luxembourg Palace through a calcite crystal. Upon rotating the crystal, one of the two images disappeared, demonstrating that reflected light had a preferred orientation. Malus formulated his empirical law relating transmitted intensity to the squared cosine of the angle between the polarisation axis and the analyser.

Shortly afterwards, in 1815, David Brewster established the quantitative relationship between the angle of maximum polarization by reflection and the refractive indices of the media. And in 1821, Augustin Fresnel completed the theoretical picture by proving that light is a **transverse wave**, resolving at once both birefringence and polarization.

## Impact on physics

The discovery of polarization forced the definitive abandonment of Newton's corpuscular model as a complete explanation of light and consolidated the wave theory. More importantly, it established that light waves are **transverse**, a property that radically distinguishes them from sound waves.

This conclusion directly influenced the later formulation of Maxwell's equations, in which the electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the propagation direction. Polarization thus became the most direct experimental evidence for the vectorial nature of electromagnetic radiation.

## Connection with modern physics

Today, polarization is fundamental in technologies such as LCD screens, fibre-optic communications and gravitational-wave detection, where field orientation is the measured signal. In quantum mechanics, single-photon polarization serves as a two-level system (qubit) in quantum cryptography and quantum computing. It is also central to observational cosmology: polarization of the cosmic microwave background provides information about conditions in the early universe. The concept that Malus discovered almost by chance in 1808 remains an active and fundamental tool of contemporary physics.
