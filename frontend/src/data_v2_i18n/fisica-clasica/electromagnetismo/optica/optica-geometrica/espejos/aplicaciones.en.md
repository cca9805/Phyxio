# Applications


## 1. Newtonian reflecting telescope

Reflecting telescopes use a large-diameter concave primary mirror to collect light from distant astronomical objects. Parallel rays from a star converge at the focus of the primary mirror, where a flat secondary mirror redirects the image to a lateral eyepiece. The light-gathering capacity depends on the primary mirror area, and the focal position is determined by [[distancia_focal_del_espejo]], which in turn depends on [[radio_de_curvatura_del_espejo]].

In large-aperture telescopes, the spherical aberration of the primary mirror is corrected by using parabolic rather than spherical surfaces. The spherical mirror equation serves as a first approximation for sizing the system, and ray tracing is used for the final design. The optical quality of the telescope depends directly on how accurately the mirror surface reproduces the parabolic shape.

Dominant variable: focal length of the primary mirror, which sets the image scale and the instrument's focal ratio.
Validity limit: the spherical model fails when the aperture exceeds 10 per cent of the radius of curvature, requiring parabolic correction or the use of field correctors.

## 2. Vehicle convex rear-view mirror

External rear-view mirrors on cars are convex mirrors with radii of curvature of 1 to 3 metres. Because [[distancia_focal_del_espejo]] is negative, they always produce virtual, upright and reduced images, covering a considerably wider field of view than a flat mirror of the same size. This property is critical for road safety, as the driver can observe adjacent lanes and vehicles in the blind spot.

The drawback is that reflected objects appear farther away than they actually are, because the image is reduced. This is why regulations require the warning "objects in mirror are closer than they appear". The magnification [[aumento_lateral]], always less than one in absolute value, determines exactly how much the image is reduced and therefore how much the distance is underestimated.

Dominant variable: radius of curvature of the mirror, which controls the field of view and the magnitude of image reduction.
Validity limit: the spherical model is adequate for commercial rear-view mirrors; spherical aberration is negligible given the low converging power of the convex mirror at these scales.

## 3. Solar concentrator furnace

Solar furnaces use large concave mirrors (or arrays of flat mirrors aimed at a central concave mirror) to concentrate solar radiation onto a small area near the focus. The power density at the focus can reach thousands of kilowatts per square metre, enough to melt metals or perform material tests at extreme temperatures.

The focal-point position depends on [[distancia_focal_del_espejo]], and the maximum concentration is limited by the angular size of the solar disc and the optical quality of the mirror. If the surface is not exactly parabolic, spherical aberration broadens the focal spot and reduces the energy density. In installations such as the Odeillo solar furnace in France, segmented mirrors reproduce a parabolic profile with millimetre precision.

Dominant variable: focal length of the concentrator, which determines the location of the peak energy-density point.
Validity limit: the simple spherical-mirror model fails for large-aperture concentrators where spherical aberration significantly reduces concentration; parabolic geometry or segmented correction is required.

## 4. Dental mirror for oral inspection

Dentists use concave mirrors with radii of curvature between 2 and 5 centimetres to inspect hard-to-reach areas in the oral cavity. By placing the tooth between the focus and the mirror vertex, the professional obtains a virtual, upright and enlarged image of the examined area, facilitating the detection of cavities, cracks and other defects.

The magnification [[aumento_lateral]] depends on the ratio between [[d_o]] and [[distancia_focal_del_espejo]]. With typical working distances of 1 to 2 centimetres and focal lengths of 1 to 2.5 centimetres, magnification reaches values of 2 to 5, sufficient to enlarge millimetre-scale details. If the dentist moves the mirror beyond the focus, the image inverts and loses practical utility, requiring a controlled working distance at all times.

Dominant variable: tooth-to-vertex distance, which determines whether the image is a magnified virtual one or an inverted real one.
Validity limit: the model holds as long as the examined dental surface is small enough to remain in the paraxial region; curved or large-area surfaces produce distortion at the image edges.

## 5. Vehicle headlight with parabolic reflector

Car and motorcycle headlights use a reflector with a parabolic profile whose focus coincides with the position of the bulb or LED. Light emitted from the focus is reflected as a beam parallel to the axis, providing directional long-range illumination. To generate the dipped-beam cut-off pattern, additional shields and lenses block part of the reflected light.

Although the reflector is parabolic rather than spherical, the operating principle is understood from the mirror equation: a source at the focus produces reflected rays parallel to the axis ([[d_o]] equal to [[distancia_focal_del_espejo]] gives [[d_i]] infinite). If the bulb shifts slightly from the focus, the rays cease to be parallel and the beam widens or deflects, allowing adjustment of the light-pattern geometry.

Dominant variable: position of the light source relative to the reflector focus, which controls beam collimation and spread.
Validity limit: the simple paraxial model does not describe the complete beam pattern, which includes secondary reflections and diffraction effects at the reflector edges; the final design requires full optical simulation.
