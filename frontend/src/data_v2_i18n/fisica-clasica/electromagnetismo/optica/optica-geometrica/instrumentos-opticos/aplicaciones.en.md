# Applications


## 1. Biological microscopy in the clinical laboratory

In clinical analysis laboratories, the compound microscope is the primary tool for examining blood samples, tissues and body fluids. The oil-immersion objective with a focal length of about 2 mm and numerical aperture of 1.25 forms a magnified intermediate image inside the standard 160 mm tube. A 10x eyepiece allows the pathologist to observe cellular detail with a total magnification of approximately 1000.

The quality of the diagnosis depends on the ability to distinguish structures such as cell nuclei, parasites or bacteria, whose size ranges from 0.2 to 10 micrometres. Insufficient magnification prevents identification; excessive magnification without an adequate numerical aperture produces empty magnification that confuses the observer. Contrast is improved by staining and by the illumination condenser, not by increasing magnification.

Dominant variable: the numerical aperture of the objective, which determines the maximum achievable resolution and, with it, the useful magnification of the instrument.
Validity limit: the paraxial model ceases to be accurate when the numerical aperture of the objective exceeds 0.65 without aberration correction; for immersion, achromatic or apochromatic objectives are required.

## 2. Electronic solder inspection with a stereo magnifier

In printed-circuit-board manufacturing, the stereo magnifier (stereomicroscope) with magnifications between 7 and 45 allows inspection of solder quality and detection of defects such as solder bridges, cold joints or misaligned components. The main objective focal length is typically 50 to 100 mm, providing sufficient working distance to manipulate the board under the lens.

The technician observes a three-dimensional image thanks to two separate optical channels. The relationship between the working distance and the objective focal length determines the trade-off between magnification and the space available for rework tools. A magnification of 10 suffices to detect 0.1 mm solder bridges; increasing to 45 allows locating micro-fractures in integrated-circuit pins.

Dominant variable: the objective focal length, which simultaneously controls the magnification and the available working distance.
Validity limit: for sub-micrometre defects (for example in advanced semiconductor interconnects), the stereo magnifier does not offer sufficient resolution and scanning electron microscopy is required.

## 3. Astronomical observation with a refracting telescope

The refracting telescope uses a large-focal-length objective lens (on the order of 500 to 2000 mm) to collect light from celestial objects and form a real image at its focal plane. The eyepiece, with a focal length of 10 to 40 mm, angularly magnifies that image for the observer. The resulting angular magnification is the quotient of the objective focal length divided by the eyepiece focal length.

To observe Saturn's rings a minimum magnification of about 50 is needed, obtained with a 1000 mm objective and a 20 mm eyepiece. To resolve double stars separated by less than 2 arcseconds, the objective diameter must be large enough for the diffraction limit to permit that resolution; magnification without adequate aperture only produces enlarged Airy discs.

Dominant variable: the ratio of the objective focal length to the eyepiece focal length, which sets the angular magnification of the telescope.
Validity limit: atmospheric turbulence (seeing) limits practical resolution to about 1 to 2 arcseconds regardless of optical magnification; to overcome this limit, adaptive optics or space-based telescopes are used.

## 4. Medical endoscopy for internal diagnosis

Modern endoscopes use lens systems or fibre optics to transmit the image of the interior of an organ to an eyepiece or digital camera. The internal optical system functions as an inverted microscope: a short-focal-length objective captures the image of the illuminated tissue and a relay lens system transports it along a flexible tube. Typical system magnification ranges from 5 to 30, although the final image is projected on a monitor and digitally magnified.

The endoscope objective focal length is optimised for a wide field of view (between 100 and 170 degrees) rather than high magnification, because the physician needs to orient within the cavity before approaching suspicious tissue. When more detail is required, a higher-magnification endoscope (endomicroscope) with focal lengths of a few millimetres is inserted, enabling observation of cellular architecture in vivo.

Dominant variable: the angular field of view of the objective, which determines how much tissue surface is observed simultaneously and the trade-off with magnification.
Validity limit: thin-lens formulae do not correctly describe multi-element relay lens systems; the ABCD matrix formalism or full ray tracing is required to design the endoscope.

## 5. Reading topographic maps with a field magnifier

Hikers and cartographers use folding pocket magnifiers with magnifications of 3 to 8 to read contour lines and symbols on topographic maps printed at reduced scale. The typical focal length of these magnifiers is 30 to 80 mm, allowing the user to hold the map at a comfortable distance and read 0.2 mm details printed on the paper.

The required magnification depends on the size of the smallest symbol that needs to be identified. For contour lines spaced 0.5 mm apart, a magnification of 3 (focal length of about 80 mm) is sufficient. To read elevation numbers with a print size of 0.3 mm, a magnification of at least 5 (focal length of about 50 mm) is needed. Unlike the microscope, depth of field matters in this application: a focal length that is too short reduces the depth of field and forces the user to maintain a very precise distance between the magnifier and the paper.

Dominant variable: the magnifier focal length, which sets both the angular magnification and the depth of field as well as the working distance.
Validity limit: for digital maps on screen, the optical magnifier is replaced by digital zoom, which has no focal-length constraints but is limited by the pixel density of the display.
