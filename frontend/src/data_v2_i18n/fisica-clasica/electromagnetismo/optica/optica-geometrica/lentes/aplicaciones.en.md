# Applications


## 1. Myopia correction with a diverging lens

The myopic eye forms images of distant objects in front of the retina because the eyeball is too long or the crystalline lens converges excessively. To correct this defect, a diverging lens ([[distancia_focal]] negative) is placed in front of the eye to reduce the total convergence of the eye-lens system. The diverging lens shifts the focal image backwards until it coincides with the retina.

The required focal length depends on the patient's far-point distance. If the far point is at 50 cm, the corrective lens must have an optical power of minus 2 dioptres (focal length of minus 50 cm) so that rays from an object at infinity leave the lens as though they came from 50 cm, the point the myopic eye can focus on.

Dominant variable: the focal length [[distancia_focal]] of the corrective lens, which determines how much the system convergence is reduced.
Validity limit: the thin lens model is valid for conventional spectacles; for contact lenses, the thickness and proximity to the cornea require considering the vertex distance and corneal curvature.

## 2. Simple magnifying glass for electronic component inspection

A technician uses a magnifying glass with a 50 mm focal length to inspect solder joints on a printed circuit board. By placing the board at roughly 40 mm from the lens (slightly less than [[distancia_focal]]), a virtual magnified upright image is obtained by looking through the magnifier.

The angular magnification is approximately the ratio of the conventional near-point distance (250 mm) to [[distancia_focal]] (50 mm), giving a factor of 5. Solder joints of 0.3 mm appear as if they measured 1.5 mm, sufficient to detect flux defects or micro-cracks. The image is virtual because [[d_o]] is less than [[distancia_focal]] in a converging lens.

Dominant variable: the focal length [[distancia_focal]] of the magnifier, which sets the maximum usable magnification before chromatic aberration degrades the image.
Validity limit: for magnifications above 10 the chromatic aberration of a single glass element produces unacceptable fringes; an achromatic doublet or a multi-lens system is required.

## 3. Photographic objective of a reflex camera

The camera objective forms a real inverted image of the scene onto the digital sensor. A 50 mm focal-length lens (considered normal for full-frame format) produces a scaled image of the scene in the focal plane when the subject is beyond 10 metres (effectively at infinity for the lens equation).

Focusing is achieved by shifting the lens slightly relative to the sensor. For a portrait at 2 metres, [[d_i]] works out to approximately 51.3 mm: the objective must advance 1.3 mm from the infinity position. The lateral magnification for a 1.7 m tall subject at 2 m is roughly minus 0.026, producing an image about 44 mm tall on the sensor.

Dominant variable: the object distance [[d_o]], which determines the required objective displacement for focusing and the resulting magnification.
Validity limit: the single thin lens model applies as a first approximation; real objectives are multi-element systems optimised to minimise aberrations across the entire field.

## 4. Condenser lens in a microscope illumination system

The microscope condenser concentrates illuminator light onto the specimen, ensuring uniform illumination across the entire field of view. It is placed between the light source and the stage, with a typical focal length between 10 and 20 mm.

The condenser forms a real image of the source in the specimen plane (Kohler illumination) or near it (critical illumination). By adjusting the condenser position (varying [[d_o]] between the source and the lens), the uniformity and the angle of the illumination cone are controlled. A higher numerical aperture of the condenser enables higher observation resolutions.

Dominant variable: the source position [[d_o]] relative to the condenser, which controls the spatial distribution of light on the specimen.
Validity limit: for high numerical-aperture condensers (above 0.9) the paraxial approximation breaks down; exact ray tracing is required and immersion oil is often used to achieve the necessary homogeneous medium.

## 5. Focusing lens in an industrial laser cutting system

In laser cutting, a short focal-length converging lens (typically 50 to 125 mm) focuses the collimated laser beam onto the metal workpiece. The high power density at the focal point (on the order of megawatts per square centimetre) melts or vaporises the material.

The focus position relative to the workpiece surface is critical: displacements of fractions of a millimetre drastically change the spot diameter and the power density. The relationship between [[d_o]] (distance of the collimated beam, effectively infinite) and [[d_i]] (focus position) simplifies to [[d_i]] equal to [[distancia_focal]]. The focal spot diameter depends on diffraction, but the axial position is dominated by the lens focal length.

Dominant variable: the focal length [[distancia_focal]], which determines the working distance and the focal spot size on the workpiece.
Validity limit: the geometric model predicts the focus position, but the actual spot size is limited by diffraction and thermal aberrations of the lens as it absorbs a fraction of the high-power beam.
