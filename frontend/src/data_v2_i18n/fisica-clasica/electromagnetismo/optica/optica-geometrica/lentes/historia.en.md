## Historical problem

Since antiquity, the ability of curved transparent bodies to concentrate sunlight and ignite fires was well known. However, no quantitative theory existed to predict where an image forms when light passes through a glass body with curved surfaces. Venetian artisans of the thirteenth century manufactured lenses to correct vision without understanding the relationship between the glass curvature, its material and the optical effect produced. The unanswered question was: what exactly determines the position and size of the image formed by a lens.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the confusion between reflection and refraction as image-forming mechanisms. Curved mirrors were relatively well understood since Euclid and Alhazen, but extending that knowledge to transparent bodies required mastering the law of refraction, which was not mathematically formulated until Snell and Descartes in the seventeenth century. Without a quantitative refraction law, it was impossible to predict how each ray bends upon entering and leaving a curved surface. Moreover, the notion that light travels in straight lines within homogeneous media, although intuitive, needed to be formulated as an operative principle for geometric ray tracing.

## What changed

In 1621 Snell empirically discovered the law of refraction, and Descartes published it in geometric form in 1637. With this tool, Huygens and subsequently Newton were able to systematically analyse refraction at curved surfaces. Newton studied chromatic dispersion and concluded (incorrectly) that lenses could not be corrected for colour. Euler and Dollond demonstrated in the eighteenth century that combining glasses of different refractive indices could produce achromatic doublets. Gauss, in the early nineteenth century, formulated the complete paraxial theory for lens systems, establishing the equations we use today in essentially unchanged form.

## Impact on physics

The quantitative understanding of lenses unlocked the development of fundamental optical instruments: the telescope (Galileo, 1609), the compound microscope (Jansen, circa 1590, perfected by Hooke and Leeuwenhoek) and later the photographic camera. These instruments transformed astronomy, biology and scientific documentation. The geometrical optics of lenses became the paradigm of how a simple physical theory can generate technology of enormous impact. Gauss's formulation established the linear-approximation paradigm in optics that persisted until the arrival of wave and quantum optics.

## Connection with modern physics

Lenses remain essential components in contemporary technology. Mobile phone camera objectives use stacks of aspherical plastic lenses designed by computational ray tracing. Extreme ultraviolet lithography, which manufactures the chips of current processors, employs high-precision optics where lens theory extends into the wave regime. Gravitational lenses, predicted by general relativity, bend the paths of light from distant galaxies following principles analogous to refraction in glass, extending the lens concept into the astrophysical domain.
