# Applications


## 1. Optical fibre for telecommunications

Long-distance data transmission via optical fibre relies on total internal reflection. Light is injected into the glass core at an angle exceeding the critical angle [[theta_c]], bouncing thousands of times per kilometre without transmission loss. Each bounce exactly follows the law of reflection: [[theta_r]] equals [[theta_i]] at every contact point with the cladding.

Modern fibres transmit terabits per second across entire oceans. The design of the contrast between [[n1]] of the core and [[n2]] of the cladding determines the numerical aperture of the fibre and the range of angles that propagate without leakage. A typical telecommunications fibre has a core index of 1.48 and cladding index of 1.46, producing a high critical angle that demands precise angular injection of the light source. Even a small deviation in the launch angle causes mode leakage and progressive attenuation of the transmitted signal.

Dominant variable: the critical angle [[theta_c]], which determines the acceptance cone of the fibre and the confinement of the optical signal.
Validity limit: the geometric model ceases to be valid in single-mode fibres with core diameter below 10 micrometres, where wave propagation modes dominate.

## 2. Corner-cube retroreflectors in road signage

Corner-cube retroreflectors return light exactly in the direction of origin through three successive reflections on perpendicular faces. Each reflection obeys the law: [[theta_r]] equals [[theta_i]] on each face. The geometry of three perpendicular mirrors ensures the output ray is antiparallel to the input, regardless of the arrival angle.

This property is used in vehicle reflectors, traffic signs and lunar reflectors left by the Apollo missions. The returned intensity depends on the specular quality of the faces. Lunar retroreflectors allow measuring the Earth-Moon distance with millimetre precision using laser pulses.

Dominant variable: the angle of incidence [[theta_i]] on each face, which determines the precision of the return.
Validity limit: if the faces are not exactly perpendicular to each other, the return ray deviates and retroreflection loses efficiency. Errors greater than 0.5 degrees significantly reduce the return.

## 3. Medical endoscopy

Endoscopes transmit images from inside the body through coherent bundles of optical fibres. Total internal reflection confines light within each individual fibre, allowing internal cavities to be illuminated and the image transported back to the observer without resolution loss.

The critical angle between the glass core and the polymeric cladding determines the instrument's flexibility: the smaller [[theta_c]], the wider the range of curvatures the fibre tolerates without rays escaping. In modern endoscopes, the cladding material is chosen to maximise the index difference and permit tight bends inside the human body.

Dominant variable: the critical angle [[theta_c]], which sets the maximum admissible curvature of the endoscope without image loss.
Validity limit: in extremely small-diameter fibres or very tight bends, rays exceed the acceptance angle and are lost into the cladding, degrading the image.

## 4. Reflecting telescopes

Newton and Cassegrain telescopes use concave mirrors as primary objectives. The law of reflection applied locally at each point of the parabolic mirror causes all rays parallel to the axis to converge at the focus. The angle of incidence [[theta_i]] with respect to the local normal varies depending on position on the mirror, but [[theta_r]] always replicates it exactly.

The advantage over refracting lenses is the absence of chromatic aberration: reflection does not depend on wavelength, so all colours converge at the same focal point. Large telescopes such as the James Webb use segmented mirrors where each segment applies the law of reflection with its own normal, and the alignment of all segments reconstructs the final image.

Dominant variable: the angle of reflection [[theta_r]] at each point of the mirror, which determines the convergence of rays at the focus.
Validity limit: the geometric reflection model fails if the mirror surface has imperfections larger than a fraction of the wavelength; scattering effects appear that degrade the image.

## 5. Total reflection prisms in binoculars

Porro prisms in binoculars use total internal reflection to invert the image without needing a metallic coating. Light enters the glass prism ([[n1]] of approximately 1.52) and reaches the internal face at an angle exceeding the critical angle (approximately 42 degrees for glass-air), reflecting totally.

The advantage over metallic mirrors is twofold: no loss from absorption in the reflective layer and no degradation over time (no coating to oxidise). High-end binoculars use BaK4 glass with index [[n1]] of 1.57, which offers an even lower critical angle and a more uniform field of view without edge darkening. This higher refractive index also reduces internal scattering, improving contrast in low-light conditions.

Dominant variable: the refractive index of the prism [[n1]], which determines the critical angle and the quality of total reflection.
Validity limit: if the prism face becomes contaminated with liquid or dirt that locally increases [[n2]], the critical angle rises and total reflection may fail in some zones, producing dark patches in the image.
