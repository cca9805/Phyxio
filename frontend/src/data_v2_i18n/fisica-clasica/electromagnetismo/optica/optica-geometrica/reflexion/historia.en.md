## Historical problem

The central question that drove the study of reflection was: when light bounces off a surface, what rule governs the direction of the reflected ray and why that rule and not another. The ancient Greeks, particularly **Euclid** (3rd century BC), already stated the equality of angles in his *Catoptrics*, but they treated it as a geometric postulate without physical justification. No deeper principle existed to explain why nature chooses that angular symmetry.

**Hero of Alexandria** (1st century AD) took the first step towards an answer by demonstrating that the reflected ray follows the shortest path between the object and the eye via the mirror. However, this demonstration did not resolve the complete problem: it did not explain why light should prefer the shortest path or how the reflected intensity compares to the transmitted intensity.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main intellectual obstacle was the absence of a theoretical framework connecting ray geometry to the physical nature of light. For centuries, optics developed as a branch of pure geometry: rays were abstract lines and laws were axioms without dynamical foundation.

Moreover, the dominant paradigm of vision (that the eyes emit rays that detect objects) confused the causal direction of the phenomenon. Without understanding that light travels from the object to the eye, it was impossible to correctly formulate the interaction between ray and surface. This error persisted until **Ibn al-Haytham** (Alhazen, 11th century) corrected it experimentally in his *Kitab al-Manazir*, demonstrating that light is emitted by sources and reflected by surfaces.

## What changed

The key breakthrough came with **Pierre de Fermat** in 1662, who demonstrated that the law of reflection follows from a single principle: light follows the path of minimum time. This variational principle unified reflection and refraction under one idea, eliminating the need to postulate the equality of angles as an independent axiom.

Subsequently, **Augustin-Jean Fresnel** (1821) and **Thomas Young** completed the picture by incorporating the wave nature of light. The Fresnel equations calculate what fraction of the amplitude is reflected and what fraction is transmitted as a function of [[theta_i]], the polarisation and the indices of the medium. The geometric angle law remains intact, but the intensity is now also known.

## Impact on physics

Fermat's principle transformed optics from a collection of empirical rules into a deductive discipline based on variational principles. This approach directly inspired the analytical mechanics of Lagrange and Hamilton, where particle trajectories are also obtained by minimising a quantity (the action).

The Fresnel equations, for their part, were one of the first quantitative victories of the wave theory over the corpuscular theory. They demonstrated that phenomena such as the Brewster angle (zero reflection for a specific polarisation) are natural consequences of electromagnetic boundary conditions, not inexplicable anomalies.

## Connection with modern physics

Quantum mechanics reformulates reflection as a sum of probability amplitudes over all possible paths (Feynman path integral). Most paths cancel through destructive interference, and the only one that survives is the one satisfying the classical law. The law of equal angles turns out to be a consequence of quantum interference, not an arbitrary postulate.

Today, reflection is studied in metamaterials with negative refractive index (where the direction of the reflected ray reverses compared to the classical case), in Bragg mirrors for semiconductors (reflection through constructive interference in periodic structures), and in nanostructured surfaces that control reflection at the subwavelength scale. The fundamental law remains valid at each individual interface, but complex systems generate collective behaviours that transcend it.
