## Historical problem

The refraction of light was known since antiquity: the Greeks observed that a partially submerged oar appears to bend at the air–water interface and that cut gems redirect light in surprising ways. However, for nearly two millennia nobody managed to establish a precise quantitative relationship between the angle of incidence and the angle of refraction. Ptolemy (2nd century AD) carried out careful measurements and proposed a direct proportional relationship between both angles, which works reasonably for small angles but fails progressively at larger incidences.

The central problem was not merely empirical but conceptual: a quantity characterising the "optical resistance" of each medium was missing —what we now call the refractive index— along with a suitable mathematical function to describe the nonlinear relationship between the angles. Without these two ingredients, any attempt at a quantitative law was limited to empirical fits of partial validity.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main intellectual barrier was the assumption that the relationship between angles had to be linear or at least a simple polynomial. Spherical trigonometry was known in the medieval Islamic world (Ibn Sahl in the 10th century correctly formulated the relationship, although his work remained unknown in Europe for centuries), but the sine function was not considered a natural candidate for describing optical phenomena.

Furthermore, the nature of light was completely unknown. Without a propagation theory —neither corpuscular nor wave-based— there was no conceptual framework from which to deduce the law; it could only be found by induction from experimental data. This made any proposal vulnerable to the criticism of being a numerical fit without physical foundation. The idea that each medium has an intrinsic property (the index) governing refraction was not obvious either: it required abstracting a universal number from measurements in multiple geometries.

## What changed

In 1621, Willebrord Snellius systematically measured angles of incidence and refraction at various interfaces and empirically discovered that the ratio of the distances from the ray to the point of incidence, measured in each medium along a perpendicular to the normal, remains constant. This geometric observation is exactly equivalent to the law of sines, although Snellius did not express it in that trigonometric form.

René Descartes published the law in its sinusoidal form in "La Dioptrique" (1637), deriving it from a mechanical analogy with particles that change speed upon crossing an interface. Although Descartes's derivation contained a conceptual error about the direction of the speed change, the resulting formula was correct. Subsequently, Pierre de Fermat derived the same law (1662) from his principle of least time, providing the first variational foundation and eliminating the need for ad hoc mechanical assumptions.

## Impact on physics

Snell's law was the first exact quantitative optical law, and its impact was transformative. It enabled the rational design of lenses and optical systems, inaugurating geometrical optics as a precise mathematical discipline. The telescope and microscope, invented empirically at the beginning of the 17th century, could be systematically optimised once refraction at each surface was described by an equation.

Philosophically, Fermat's derivation was revolutionary: it demonstrated that a physical phenomenon could be understood as the optimisation of a quantity (transit time), anticipating the principle of least action that would dominate 18th-century mechanics. Snell's law was thus one of the first examples of a natural law expressed as a variational principle, connecting optics with analytical mechanics a century before Hamilton.

## Connection with modern physics

Snell's law remains the fundamental equation of geometrical optics and is used daily in the design of optical fibres, camera lenses, microscopes, telescopes and augmented-reality devices. Its modern derivation from Maxwell's boundary conditions places it as a particular case of classical electrodynamics.

In 21st-century metamaterials physics, Snell's law has acquired renewed relevance: materials with negative refractive index produce "reversed" refraction (the refracted ray crosses to the same side of the normal as the incident one), a phenomenon theoretically predicted by Veselago in 1968 and experimentally demonstrated in 2001. These metamaterials allow the conception of "superlenses" capable of surpassing the diffraction limit, applying the same Snell's law but with inverted index sign.

In quantum optics, Snell's law applies to individual photons: each photon has a refraction probability and a reflection probability given by the Fresnel coefficients, but the direction of the refracted photon follows Snell's law exactly. This confirms that the law is not an artefact of classical optics but a consequence of transverse momentum conservation, valid even at the quantum level.
