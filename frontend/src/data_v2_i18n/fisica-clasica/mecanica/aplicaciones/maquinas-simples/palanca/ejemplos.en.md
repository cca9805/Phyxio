# Exam-type example

**The Playground See-saw**

## Problem statement

An 80 kg adult wishes to balance a 20 kg child on an ideal first-class see-saw that has a total length of 4 meters. The fulcrum is located exactly at the center of the bar. The child sits at the right end of the bar. Calculate exactly at what distance from the fulcrum the adult must be positioned so that the system remains in horizontal equilibrium. Furthermore, determine the ideal mechanical advantage of the system from the adult's perspective and analyze what would happen if the adult decided to sit at the opposite end from the child.

## Data

- Child's mass: 20 kg
- Resistance [[R]]: 196.2 N (calculated as 20 times 9.81)
- Resistance arm [[bR]]: 2 m (half of the 4 m bar)
- Adult's mass: 80 kg
- Power [[P]]: 784.8 N (calculated as 80 times 9.81)
- Gravity: 9.81 m/s²

## System definition

The system consists of a rigid bar supported at a central fulcrum (first-class lever). The weight forces of the adult and the child act vertically downward on opposite ends of the support. The system is considered in static rotational equilibrium when the sum of the moments of force with respect to the fulcrum is zero. In this case, the child generates a clockwise moment, and the adult must generate an equal counterclockwise moment to stabilize the see-saw horizontally. The bar is considered perfectly rigid for this analysis.

## Physical model

The **Rigid Body Model for Ideal Levers** is used. In this model, the bar is considered undeformable and massless, which simplifies the problem by not having to consider the moment generated by the weight of the wood or metal of the see-saw itself. The fulcrum is treated as a frictionless support point that only exerts an upward normal reaction force. Physical interaction is governed by the torque balance, where each force generates a turning tendency calculated as the product of the force and its perpendicular arm:

## Model justification

The approximation is **valid because** the see-saw beam's flexion is negligible compared to its total length and the masses are not large enough to compromise the material's structural integrity. **It is assumed** that there is no friction at the fulcrum axis, allowing energy losses to be ignored and the law of moments to be applied directly. The model is physically explicit for predicting static equilibrium conditions in low-speed simple machines and is the standard for the analysis of first-gender balance systems.

We isolate the main unknown, which is the adult's position or effort arm [[bP]] by transposing terms from the previous equations. The theoretical result is that the effort arm [[bP]] is proportional to the resistance arm [[bR]] and inversely proportional to the effort [[P]]. If the system were to move, we would consider the effort displacement [[sP]] and the resistance displacement [[sR]] under the work conservation rule that dictates energy conservation.

## Numerical substitution

1. **Value substitution:**
   We use the isolated relationship to introduce the known data of the problem.
   The arm [[bP]] is estimated as the quotient between the product (196.2 times 2) and the value 784.8.
   The resulting value for [[bP]] is approximately 0.5 meters.

2. **Calculation of Mechanical Advantage:**
   The magnitude [[VM]] is obtained by dividing 0.5 by 2, which results in a value of 0.25.

## Dimensional validation

We verify that the units in the equilibrium equation are consistent. On the left side, we have Newtons (N) multiplied by meters (m), resulting in Torque units (N meters). On the right side, the resistance [[R]] in N multiplied by the arm [[bR]] in m also results in N meters. Since both quantities have the same dimension L^2MT^-2, the equality is physically coherent, and the result for the arm [[bP]] in meters is correct.

## Physical interpretation

The adult must sit at only **0.5 meters** (50 cm) from the fulcrum to balance the child who is at 2 meters. This **means that** since the adult is four times heavier than the child, their effort arm [[bP]] must be four times shorter for the moments to be equal.

The mechanical advantage [[VM]] of **0.25** **indicates that** the adult "loses" force but gains stability and movement control. If the adult sat at the opposite end (2 m), the see-saw would fall sharply toward their side, as they would generate a torque [[tauP]] of 1569.6 N meters, four times higher than the child's torque [[tauR]].

# Real-world example

**The Lever Nutcracker**

## Context

The nutcracker is a household tool that uses the principle of the second-class lever to multiply the force of the human hand to break the shell of a dried fruit. In this design, the fulcrum (hinge) is at one end, the nut is placed in the middle (resistance [[R]]), and the hand applies the force at the other end (effort [[P]]).

## Physical estimation

We estimate an **order of magnitude** for a nutcracker where the hand applies a force of 50 N at a distance of 15 cm (0.15 m) from the hinge, thus defining the effort arm [[bP]]. The nut is placed only 3 cm (0.03 m) from the hinge, representing the resistance arm [[bR]].

The ideal mechanical advantage of the system is 5 units.
This implies that the force applied to the nut is five times greater than the hand's force, reaching a value of 250 N for the resistance [[R]].

In a real scenario, we must consider the efficiency [[eta]]. If we assume an efficiency of 90% ([[eta]] is approximately 0.9) due to slight hinge friction and the initial plastic deformation of the shell, the actual mechanical advantage [[VMreal]] on the nut would be 4.5.

## Interpretation

The second-class lever is extremely efficient for tasks requiring high pressures in small spaces. By doubling the length of the nutcracker handles, we would double the mechanical advantage [[VM]] to a value of 10, allowing much harder shells to be broken with the same manual effort. This example illustrates how everyday tool design uses the law of the lever to amplify human biological capabilities, transforming a small hand displacement [[sP]] into a large localized compressive force.
