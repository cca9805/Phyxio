# Historical Context of the Principle

## Historical problem

Since antiquity, philosophers tried to quantify the "motion" of bodies. The central problem was predicting what would happen after a collision: if two carts collided, what [[velocidad_individual]] would result? Descartes (1644) proposed that God conserved a constant amount of "motion" in the universe, defined as the product of [[masa_individual]] times scalar speed. Although intuitively attractive, this idea failed in oblique collisions: the same scalar quantity of motion could be generated from very different velocities oriented in different directions, producing contradictory predictions for non-frontal collisions.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The fundamental obstacle was the confusion between scalar and vector quantities. Leibniz, Newton's contemporary, argued that the conserved quantity should be *vis viva* (kinetic energy), not momentum. The debate lasted decades because both quantities are conserved in elastic collisions. The conceptual difficulty was that in inelastic collisions kinetic energy is partially lost, but the [[momento_lineal_total]] is always conserved whenever the [[fuerza_externa_neta]] is zero. Without a clear vector definition of [[momento_lineal_individual]], it was impossible to determine when to apply one law or the other, or how to reconcile seemingly contradictory experimental results.

## What changed

Newton (1687) formalized [[momento_lineal_individual]] as a vector quantity in his *Principia Mathematica* and stated his second law: the [[fuerza_externa_neta]] equals the rate of change of [[momento_lineal_total]]. This automatically implied that in the absence of external forces, the [[variacion_momento_lineal]] is zero. Newton also established the third law: internal forces act in equal and opposite pairs, so they do not alter the system's [[momento_lineal_total]]. The conceptual shift was decisive: moving from scalar "quantity of motion" to directed vector momentum resolved all the cases that Cartesian mechanics could not handle.

## Impact on physics

The conservation of [[momento_lineal_total]] solved problems that were impossible for earlier mechanics: firearm recoil, rocket propulsion, nuclear collisions, and the formation of stellar systems. In the 20th century, Emmy Noether proved that this law is the direct consequence of the translational symmetry of space. This discovery elevated [[momento_lineal_total]] conservation to a fundamental principle that goes beyond classical mechanics, unifying classical, relativistic, and quantum physics under the same symmetry principle.

## Connection with modern physics

In quantum mechanics, linear momentum remains conserved, though its measurement is subject to Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. In relativistic mechanics, [[momento_lineal_individual]] is redefined as p = γmv to maintain conservation at all velocities. In particle physics, the detection of "missing momentum" in detectors — a net [[variacion_momento_lineal]] inconsistent with known processes — confirmed the existence of the neutrino and remains the primary method for searching for physics beyond the standard model.
