# Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum

## General idea of the topic
This node is the logical core of conservation of linear momentum. You will find the formal statement, the minimum conditions for application, the role of external forces, breakdown by external impulse, and several contexts in which the principle solves the problem without knowing the details of contact.

The instructional purpose is for every application to come from a prior diagnosis: system, interval, external forces, and components. This prevents the common error of conserving momentum simply because "there is a collision", even when the system is poorly defined or an external force is relevant.

## What you will truly understand
You will understand that equality between initial and final momentum is the reduced form of a more general balance. If there is no net external impulse, the balance reduces to conservation; if there is, the total momentum change measures exactly that impulse.

You will also learn to separate internal and external forces according to the chosen system, and to recognize situations in which one component is conserved while another is not.

## Recommended learning route
1. **Statement and Conditions for Application**: establish exactly what the principle says and which physical hypotheses it requires.
2. **Isolated Systems and External Forces**: learn to classify forces from the system boundary, not from the type of force.
3. **External Impulse and Breakdown of Conservation**: study how an external force acting over a finite time changes total momentum.
4. **Applications of Linear Momentum Conservation**: apply the criterion to recoil, explosions, collisions, and separations where the law simplifies the analysis.

## Physical criterion you must acquire
The physical criterion is to conserve only what the system allows to be conserved: total momentum, one specific component, or nothing, depending on the net external impulse during the chosen interval.

## Connection with real problems
- Recoil of projectiles and platforms.
- Explosions and separation of fragments.
- One- and two-dimensional collisions with negligible external friction.
- Systems where a guide, wall, or support introduces external impulse in one direction.

## Final goal of the topic
The final goal is to write the conservation principle as a justified conclusion, not as a starting point: first diagnose the system and external impulses, then decide which momentum equations are legitimate.
