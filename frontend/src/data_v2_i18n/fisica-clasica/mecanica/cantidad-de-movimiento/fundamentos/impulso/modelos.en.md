# Physical Models: Impulse

## Ideal Model

The ideal model of **impulse** [[J]] is based on simplifying a complex interaction through the concept of **average force** [[Fmed]]. In this model, we do not attempt to track the exact evolution of the force at every millisecond of the collision; instead, we replace the actual variable force with a constant force that produces the same net dynamic effect.

{{f:impulso_fuerza_media}}

This approach is extraordinarily powerful because it allows connecting the initial and final states of a system without knowing the intimate details of the collision, treating the object as a particle or a rigid solid where the impulse is applied instantaneously over an interval [[dt]].

---

## Hypotheses

For the impulse model to be operational and reliable, we accept the following hypotheses:
1. **Impulse Approximation**: We assume that the interaction interval [[dt]] is so small compared to the total observation time that non-impulsive external forces (such as weight or environmental friction) are negligible during the impact.
2. **Rigid Solid**: It is assumed that the body does not undergo permanent deformations that dissipate energy in a way that alters the calculation of momentum, or that such deformation is the mechanism that defines [[dt]].
3. **Dominant Net Force**: The force generating the impulse is the dominant factor in the change of motion, ignoring mass variations [[m]] during the process (except in propulsion models).

---

## Quantitative validity domain

The impulse model is valid under the following criteria:
*   **Time Scale**: The contact time [[dt]] must be much smaller than the characteristic motion period of the system (typically [[dt]] < 0.1 s in macroscopic collisions).
*   **Force Regime**: The impact force must be orders of magnitude higher than the constant forces of the system (such as gravity), such that `F_{impact} / F_{grav} > 100`.
*   **Velocity**: The velocities involved must be far from the relativistic regime (`v << c`), where the linear definition of [[p]] would no longer be exact.

---

## Model Failure Signals

We can identify that the impulse model is failing or insufficient if:
*   **Divergent Results**: The calculated average force [[Fmed]] is less than the external forces we had neglected (e.g., if the impulse of a braking event is less than the object's weight).
*   **Excessive Deformation**: If the object deforms in such a way that the center of mass significantly changes its internal position during [[dt]], the single-particle model fails.
*   **Long Durations**: If the interaction lasts too long (like a slow tow), we are no longer talking about "impulse" but rather constant force dynamics under the standard Newton's Second Law.

---

## Extended or Alternative Model

When the ideal model is insufficient, we resort to:
1. **Integral Model**: Instead of [[Fmed]], we use the time function of the force `F(t)` and solve the integral to obtain the exact area. This is vital in advanced ballistics and suspension design.
{{f:impulso_integral}}
2. **Elastic/Inelastic Collision Dynamics**: We introduce the coefficient of restitution to model how much energy is conserved after the impulse.
3. **Variable Mass**: In systems like rockets, where impulse is generated by expelling mass, the model must be extended to include the `dm` / [[dt]] term.

---

## Operational Comparison

The choice of model depends on the objective:
*   **Use the Average Force Model** if you are only interested in the net effect of the collision and have time and velocity change data. It is the standard for road safety and sports.
*   **Switch to the Integral Model** when structural integrity depends on peak stress peaks [[Fmax]] and not just the cumulative net effect, as the average force model may underestimate impact damage in brittle materials.
*   **Use the Impulse-Momentum Theorem** whenever calculating force is difficult but velocity change is easy to measure (e.g., via high-speed cameras).
