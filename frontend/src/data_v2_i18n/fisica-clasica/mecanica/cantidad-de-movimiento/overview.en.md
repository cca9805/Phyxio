# Linear Momentum

## General idea of the topic
This block prepares the study of mechanical phenomena in which looking only at an instantaneous force is not enough. You will find the language of linear momentum, impulse, conservation, center of mass, collisions, and variable-mass systems: a complete chain for analysing short interactions, internal redistribution, and collective motion.

The idea running through the node is that the motion of a system can be described through balances. Sometimes each body must be followed separately; in other cases it is better to track the whole system, its center of mass, or the mass crossing a boundary, as in a rocket or a jet.

## What you will truly understand
You will understand that linear momentum is not merely "mass times velocity", but a vector quantity used to compare states before and after an interaction. You will also see that impulse explains how force accumulated over time changes momentum, and that conservation depends on a precise physical condition: the net external impulse must be zero or negligible.

By the end of the node, you should be able to decide whether a problem requires impulse, momentum conservation, center-of-mass reasoning, an additional energy condition, a coefficient of restitution, or a variable-mass balance. That decision is the central learning outcome, because it prevents treating every collision, explosion, rebound, and propulsion problem as the same exercise.

## Recommended learning route
1. **Fundamentals**: begin with **Linear Momentum** to establish the quantity being tracked, continue with **Impulse and Impulse-Momentum Theorem** to understand how it changes over an interval, and close with **Isolated Systems** to know when it may be conserved.
2. **Conservation of Linear Momentum**: then study the conservation principle. Within **Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum**, follow **Statement and Conditions for Application**, **Isolated Systems and External Forces**, **External Impulse and Breakdown of Conservation**, and **Applications of Linear Momentum Conservation**; this order prevents using the law without justifying its assumptions.
3. **Center of Mass**: move to **Definition of the Center of Mass**, **Center of Mass in Discrete Systems**, **Center of Mass in Extended Bodies**, **Motion of the Center of Mass**, and **Center-of-Mass Frame** to learn how to reduce complex systems without losing their global motion.
4. **Collisions**: apply the balances to short interactions through **Elastic Collisions**, **Inelastic Collisions**, **Perfectly Inelastic Collisions**, **Collision with a Wall**, **2D Collisions**, **Coefficient of Restitution**, **Center-of-Mass Frame in Collisions**, and **Angular Scattering in 2D Collisions**.
5. **Variable-Mass Systems**: finish with **Momentum Balance with Mass Flow** and **Rocket Equation**, where the difficulty is no longer the impact itself but the correct definition of a system that gains or loses mass.

## Physical criterion you must acquire
The physical criterion of the node is to define the system before writing equations: which bodies it includes, which interval is analysed, which forces are external, which components are conserved, and whether mechanical energy supplies a valid extra condition.

## Connection with real problems
- Vehicle crashes, sports impacts, rebounds, and safety tests.
- Explosions, separation of bodies, recoil, and propulsion.
- Collective motion of particle systems, people, loads, or extended parts.
- Rockets, jets, and devices in which mass enters or leaves carrying its own momentum.

## Final goal of the topic
The final goal is to face an interaction phenomenon and build the appropriate balance from scratch: choose the system, justify whether momentum is conserved, include external impulses when needed, interpret the center of mass, and add energy or restitution only when the physical model allows it.
