# Variable-Mass Systems

## General idea of the topic
This node extends linear momentum to systems whose mass is not fixed. You will find momentum balance with mass flow and the rocket equation, two tools for situations in which matter enters or leaves the system carrying momentum.

The main difficulty is not algebraic but conceptual: if the system boundary changes or is crossed by mass, one must decide precisely what is being tracked. Only then does it make sense to discuss external forces, relative exhaust speed, or velocity change.

## What you will truly understand
You will understand that a variable-mass system cannot be treated by simply applying the constant-mass form of Newton's second law. Momentum may change because the remaining material changes velocity and because mass enters or leaves carrying its own momentum.

In the rocket case, you will understand that acceleration comes from ejecting mass with relative velocity, not from pushing against air. You will also see why the relation between mass and velocity becomes logarithmic under idealized assumptions.

## Recommended learning route
1. **Momentum Balance with Mass Flow**: begin with the general balance for open systems, identifying incoming flow, outgoing flow, and relative velocity.
2. **Rocket Equation**: apply that balance to ideal propulsion and relate initial mass, final mass, exhaust speed, and velocity change.

## Physical criterion you must acquire
The physical criterion is to fix the system boundary before deriving equations: in open systems, momentum change includes what happens inside and the momentum crossing the boundary with the mass.

## Connection with real problems
- Rockets, space manoeuvres, and fuel estimates.
- Water, air, or gas jets exerting forces on nozzles and ducts.
- Conveyor belts, hoppers, and systems that load or unload material.
- Propulsion and braking in devices that eject mass.

## Final goal of the topic
The final goal is to formulate momentum balances for open systems, distinguish them from constant-mass problems, and recognize when the rocket equation is a valid consequence of the chosen assumptions.
