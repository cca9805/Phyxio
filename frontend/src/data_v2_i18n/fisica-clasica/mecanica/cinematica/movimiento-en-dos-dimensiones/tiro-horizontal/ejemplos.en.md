# Exam-type example

## Problem statement

A ball leaves the edge of a laboratory table horizontally from height [[y0]] = 1.25 m with initial speed [[v0]] = 3.20 m/s. Take [[g]] = 9.8 m/s² and [[x0]] = 0 m. Find the fall time, the horizontal range, the vertical velocity just before impact, the impact speed, and the trajectory equation.

## Data

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---:|
| [[y0]] | 1.25 m |
| [[v0]] | 3.20 m/s |
| [[g]] | 9.8 m/s² |
| [[x0]] | 0 m |
| Impact level | [[y]] = 0 m |

The horizontal axis is positive forward and the vertical axis is positive upward. The ball leaves with no initial vertical velocity, so the vertical motion begins as free fall from the table height.

## System definition

The system is the ball from the instant it leaves the table until it reaches the floor. The previous push and the friction on the table are not studied because they no longer act during the flight. In that ideal flight phase, gravity is the only relevant acceleration.

## Model justification

The model is valid because the height and speed are moderate, and a compact laboratory ball usually experiences air resistance that is small compared with its weight over such a short path. Gravity is also considered constant and the floor is close enough for fixed axes to be a good approximation.

The decisive assumption is that the initial velocity is purely horizontal. If the ball left with a noticeable upward or downward inclination, fall time would no longer be determined only by [[y0]] and [[g]]. The problem would then belong to oblique projectile motion, not horizontal projectile motion.

## Numerical substitution

**Fall time.** With [[y0]] = 1.25 m, [[t_caida]] = sqrt(2 · 1.25 / 9.8) = 0.505 s. This time does not use [[v0]], because vertical fall does not depend on horizontal speed.

**Range.** During those 0.505 s, the ball moves horizontally at 3.20 m/s. Therefore [[R]] = 3.20 · 0.505 = 1.62 m. The ball lands a little more than one and a half meters from the edge.

**Vertical velocity.** Just before impact, [[vy]] = -9.8 · 0.505 = -4.95 m/s. The negative sign indicates downward motion with the chosen convention.

**Impact speed.** The horizontal component is still [[vx]] = 3.20 m/s. The total speed is [[v]] = sqrt(3.20² + 4.95²) = 5.89 m/s. It is larger than the initial speed because the vertical component acquired during the fall is now added.

**Trajectory.** With [[x0]] = 0 m, the curve is a parabola that starts at [[y0]] = 1.25 m and descends to the floor. The quadratic term shows that vertical fall increases faster and faster as time passes.

## Dimensional validation

Fall time is expressed in seconds because it comes from the square root of a length divided by an acceleration. Range is in meters because it is velocity multiplied by time. Velocity components and final speed are in m/s. The sign of [[vy]] is also coherent: at the floor, the ball must be moving downward.

## Physical interpretation

The central result is not only that [[R]] is 1.62 m, but why. The height of the table fixes a flight interval [[t]] of 0.505 s. The ball cannot choose to fall more slowly because it moves forward faster; it simply has that amount of time available to cover horizontal distance.

If the ball left with twice [[v0]], the fall time would still be 0.505 s, but the range would double. If the table were higher, the time would increase and the range would increase too, although not linearly with height. This reading separates the roles of [[y0]], [[v0]], and [[g]].

The impact speed also has a clear physical meaning. The horizontal part tells how the ball was launched; the vertical part tells how long gravity has acted. A larger final speed is therefore not evidence of an extra push after leaving the table, but the result of vertical acceleration during the same shared time interval.

# Real-world example

## Context

In a packaging line, a box leaves the end of a conveyor belt horizontally and must fall into a container placed ahead. The technician knows the belt height and can adjust the exit speed.

## Physical estimation

If the belt is at [[y0]] = 0.80 m, fall time is about 0.40 s. With a horizontal speed of 2.0 m/s, the expected range is about 0.80 m. That estimate is enough to place the container near the correct position before fine tuning.

## Interpretation

Range summarizes the coordination between falling and advancing. If the technician increases belt speed, horizontal advance changes while fall time stays almost the same. If the belt height changes, the available time for horizontal advance changes. The model helps decide which adjustment should be changed first.
