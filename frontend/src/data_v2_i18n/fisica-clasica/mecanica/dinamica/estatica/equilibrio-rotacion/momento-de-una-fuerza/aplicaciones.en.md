# Practical Applications: Torque of a Force

## 1. Opening a door

When opening a door, the axis is at the hinges. The hand force [[F]] produces more torque if applied far from the axis and almost perpendicularly. That is why pushing near the handle is more effective than pushing near the hinges.

Dominant variable: [[tau]]

Validity limit: the door must be modeled as a rigid body and the rotation axis must remain fixed.

The application shows that torque does not depend only on force. If the same intensity is applied in a worse direction, [[theta]] reduces effectiveness and [[d_perp]] becomes smaller. Everyday experience agrees with the model: more effective arm means stronger turning tendency.

This reading diagnoses why a heavy door can be opened without greatly increasing force. Moving the push point toward the edge increases [[r]], and orienting the push more perpendicularly increases the effective arm. Both choices increase [[tau]] without changing the door's mass.

The same reasoning helps compare two users applying equal force. The user who pushes closer to the handle and more perpendicular to the door produces the larger torque. The difference is not strength alone; it is the geometry of the line of action.

## 2. Using a wrench

A wrench transmits force to a nut. The distance [[r]] between the nut axis and the hand increases the torque produced. If the hand pushes perpendicularly, the perpendicular arm equals the effective handle length. If it pushes obliquely, only part of that length counts.

Dominant variable: [[d_perp]]

Validity limit: the wrench should not deform appreciably and the force must act in a known plane.

This application explains why a longer handle helps. It does not increase muscular force, but it increases the arm. It also explains why a poor direction can waste effort: a large force with small [[theta]] produces little torque.

In real tools, design aims for a moderate user force with a large arm. If the handle is short, the required force rises. If the user pushes in a direction almost aligned with the handle, the effective arm falls even when the tool is long.

This is why instructions often say to pull at the end of the handle and at right angles. That advice is a direct translation of the formula: maximize the effective arm before asking for more force.

## 3. Bicycle pedal

On a bicycle, the foot applies force to the pedal and the rotation axis is the bottom bracket. Torque depends on crank position and force direction. When the crank is oriented so that the force is almost perpendicular, torque is larger.

Dominant variable: [[theta]]

Validity limit: one instant is analyzed and the foot force is approximated as a point force.

This reading prevents thinking that all of the cyclist's force always produces the same effect. During one revolution, [[theta]] changes, so [[d_perp]] changes and [[tau]] changes too. Motion geometry modulates muscular effectiveness.

The application shows that torque is instantaneous. In one crank position, the same push can be very effective; in another, it may add little rotation. Transmission systems try to take advantage of intervals where the effective arm is large.

It also explains why circular pedaling is not uniform in effectiveness. The leg can apply force throughout the motion, but the turning tendency changes as the angle between crank and force changes.

## 4. Crank design

A crank converts hand force into turning tendency. The designer can increase [[r]] or improve the application angle to obtain more torque with the same [[F]]. Torque analysis compares configurations before construction.

Dominant variable: [[r]]

Validity limit: the point of application must be well defined and the force must be modeled as concentrated.

If the crank is too short, more force is required. If it is too long, it may be uncomfortable or slow. Computing [[tau]] helps choose a useful length and an effective pushing direction.

The design decision is not simply maximizing [[r]]. A very long arm may hit the environment or require too much travel. Torque quantifies the compromise between comfort, available space, and rotational effectiveness.

For this reason, crank design is a balance between mechanical advantage and usability. The torque formula gives the mechanical side of that decision with a small number of measurable quantities.

## 5. Push on a hinged bar

In a laboratory, a hinged bar receives a force at a known point. The goal is to check whether torque increases when the application point moves or when the angle changes. Measuring [[d_perp]] directly helps verify the geometric relation with [[r]] and [[theta]].

Dominant variable: [[F]]

Validity limit: the pivot must be fixed and friction must be small compared with the force effect.

This application works as an experimental test of the leaf. If the line of action passes through the pivot, torque must vanish. If the force becomes perpendicular while distance is maintained, torque must reach its maximum for that force value.

The setup also helps detect measurement errors. If [[d_perp]] is larger than [[r]], the geometry is drawn incorrectly. If torque does not change when [[theta]] changes, the formula being used is ignoring the perpendicular arm.

Students can repeat the measurement for several angles and see the sinusoidal trend. The maximum should occur near a perpendicular force, and the values should fall toward zero as the line of action approaches the pivot.

Together, these applications show that torque is a measure of rotational effectiveness. A large force is not enough: the geometry must turn that force into rotation about the chosen axis.
