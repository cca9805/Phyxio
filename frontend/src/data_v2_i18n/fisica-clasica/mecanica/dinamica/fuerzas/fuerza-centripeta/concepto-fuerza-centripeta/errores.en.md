# Common mistakes: The Concept of Centripetal Force

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Reversing cause and effect

Students often think centripetal force appears because the path is curved. The causal order is the opposite: real interactions create a radial resultant [[Frad]], and that resultant produces [[ac]], which bends the trajectory.

### Error 2: Applying formulas without checking context

The equations look familiar, so substitution may feel automatic. But without fixed-radius assumptions, unit consistency, and a clear radial axis, the output may be numerically neat and physically wrong.

### Error 3: Mixing similar quantities

[[Frad]] and [[Fc]] are often treated as unrelated quantities, and [[omega]] is confused with frequency without proper conversion involving [[pi]] and [[T]].

## Model errors

### Error 4: Missing model-breakdown signals

A method that worked once is often reused blindly. Under sustained slip, variable curvature, or strong speed transients, the basic circular model may no longer represent the dominant dynamics.

### Error 5: Ignoring known validity limits

Learners may report precise values while skipping assumptions. Without radius, speed regime, and measurement quality, the result has weak physical meaning.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 6: Careless algebraic rearrangements

Quick symbolic manipulation can invert dependencies and produce trends that contradict basic circular-motion behavior.

### Error 7: Neglecting constants or unit conversion

Treating [[pi]] or time conversion as minor details can propagate large errors through [[omega]], [[ac]], and force estimates.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 8: Reporting false precision

Many decimals can hide model uncertainty. Significant figures should reflect data quality and model scope.

### Error 9: Stopping at the number

A value without causal interpretation is incomplete. A good solution states what change in [[v]], [[r]], or [[m]] would increase or reduce radial demand.

## Quick self-control rule

- Did I build [[Frad]] from real forces only?
- Did I distinguish [[ac]], [[Frad]], and [[Fc]] clearly?
- Did I verify conversions involving [[T]], [[omega]], and [[pi]]?
- Do my trends match physical intuition?
- Did I state a causal interpretation and a model-validity limit?

If any answer is no, the solution is not physically complete.
