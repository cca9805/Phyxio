# Common mistakes: Flat and Banked Curves

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Reversing cause and effect

Many learners say that path curvature creates centripetal force. The causal direction is the opposite: real interactions produce [[Frad]], and that resultant generates [[ac]] to bend the trajectory.

### Error 2: Applying formulas without context checks

Equation substitution feels straightforward, but without checking [[r]], [[mu]], and operating regime, the result may be numerically clean and physically invalid.

### Error 3: Mixing related quantities

[[Frad]], [[fs]], and [[Nn]] are often blended conceptually. [[Frad]] is the radial resultant; [[fs]] and [[Nn]] are specific contact forces that contribute depending on geometry and conditions.

## Model errors

### Error 4: Missing model-breakdown signals

Because the ideal model works in many classroom exercises, users may overextend it into transient, high-uncertainty scenarios where it no longer captures dominant behavior.

### Error 5: Ignoring explicit validity limits

Publishing a precise value without assumptions about grip, speed regime, and uncertainty creates false confidence and weak engineering decisions.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 6: Careless algebraic rearrangement

Small symbolic mistakes can invert trends and hide risk. Every rearrangement should be checked against physical monotonicity and dimensional consistency.

### Error 7: Unit-conversion neglect

Mixing km/h with SI equations or misusing geometric inputs can shift thresholds enough to invalidate safety conclusions.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 8: Reporting false precision

Many decimal places do not compensate for model uncertainty. Significant figures should match data quality and scope assumptions.

### Error 9: Stopping at the numeric output

A number without causal interpretation is operationally incomplete. A proper conclusion must state which intervention changes margin: lower [[v]], larger effective [[r]], improved [[mu]], or geometric redesign via [[th]].

## Quick self-control rule

- Did I build [[Frad]] from real forces only?
- Did I distinguish [[ac]], [[fs]], and [[Nn]] clearly?
- Did I state assumptions and validity boundaries?
- Did I verify units and trend direction?
- Did I close with an actionable physical conclusion?

If any answer is no, the solution is not physically complete.
