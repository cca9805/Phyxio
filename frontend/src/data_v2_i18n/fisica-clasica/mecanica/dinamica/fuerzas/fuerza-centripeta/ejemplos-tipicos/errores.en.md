# Common mistakes: Typical Examples

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Reversing cause and effect

Learners often think centripetal force appears because motion is curved. The causal direction is opposite: real interactions create [[Frad]], which produces [[ac]] and continuously bends velocity direction.

### Error 2: Applying equations without scenario validation

Because many relations include [[v]] and [[r]], students use them interchangeably. This is incorrect: each equation belongs to a specific physical setup (string, flat curve, banking, loop, or orbit).

### Error 3: Mixing related quantities

[[Frad]] and [[Fc]] are frequently blended without clear definition, and [[m]] is sometimes confused with weight. This creates unit and interpretation errors even when algebra looks clean.

## Model errors

### Error 4: Missing model-breakdown signals

The ideal model works well in clean cases, but can fail under sustained slip, strong coupling, non-circular trajectories, or highly perturbed operation.

### Error 5: Ignoring validity limits

A correct equation used outside its assumptions is still a bad model. Every result must include scenario and margin statements.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 6: Careless rearrangements

Small symbolic mistakes can invert trends and lead to unsafe conclusions. Always check monotonic behavior after rearrangement.

### Error 7: Unit and constant mistakes

In orbital and mixed-scale problems, poor unit handling (especially with [[G]] and large [[r]]) can produce absurd magnitudes.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 8: False precision

Extra decimals do not compensate for model and measurement uncertainty.

### Error 9: Stopping at the number

A numerical value without causal interpretation is incomplete. A good solution states which control variable should be changed and why.

## Quick self-control rule

- Did I identify the real radial-force provider?
- Did I distinguish [[Frad]] from [[Fc]] clearly?
- Did I validate units and scale?
- Did I state model limits?
- Did I close with an actionable physical conclusion?

If any answer is no, the solution is not physically complete.
