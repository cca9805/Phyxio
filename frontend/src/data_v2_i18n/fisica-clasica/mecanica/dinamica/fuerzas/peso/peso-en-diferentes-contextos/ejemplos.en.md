# Exam-type example

## Problem statement

A maintenance satellite is released near low orbit around an ideal spherical planet. At the initial stage, radial distance satisfies [[r]] = [[R]] + [[h]], where [[R]] is planetary radius and [[h]] is orbital altitude. The mission team wants to compare three quantities: real gravitational magnitude [[Pmod]], local field magnitude [[gmod]], and support reading [[Pap]] measured inside an accelerated calibration platform.

Tasks:

1. Compute [[gmod]] with a central-field model and compare with a near-surface estimate.
2. Estimate [[Pmod]] for a known [[m]].
3. Evaluate [[Pap]] when the internal frame has downward acceleration [[a]] close to [[gmod]].
4. Explain why near-weightlessness can occur while gravity is still present.

## Data

- [[G]] = 6.67e-11 SI.
- [[M]] = 5.97e24 kg.
- [[R]] = 6.37e6 m.
- [[h]] = 4.00e5 m.
- [[m]] = 80 kg.
- Frame acceleration in platform: [[a]] = -8.4 m/s^2.

Reference surface gravity is [[g0]] = 9.81 m/s^2.

## System definition

The main system is the test body of mass [[m]] inside the platform. The external field is generated by astronomical mass [[M]]. The local vertical axis is radial outward, so real gravitational force [[P]] points inward toward the center. The free-body description includes [[P]], support force [[N]], and inertial-frame contribution due to [[a]].

Internal friction terms are neglected in first approximation because the pedagogical objective is to separate real weight from apparent reading.

## Physical model

Gravity is modeled with the central relation using [[G]], [[M]], and [[r]] because altitude is not negligible for a high-quality estimate. Apparent weight is modeled with a one-axis accelerated-frame relation. Validity requires [[r]] > 0, [[m]] > 0, and consistent sign convention for [[a]].

## Model justification

Surface approximation with [[g0]] is useful for quick intuition but not sufficient for orbital-level comparison. Since the key educational objective is distinguishing gravitational force from support reading, frame acceleration [[a]] must be explicit in the calculation chain.

## Numerical substitution

1) Total radial distance: [[r]] = [[R]] + [[h]] = 6.77e6 m.

2) Central model gives [[gmod]] around 8.7 m/s^2. The altitude relation gives a compatible value, confirming internal coherence.

3) Real weight magnitude: [[Pmod]] = [[m]]*[[gmod]] close to 696 N.

4) Surface-reference value: [[m]]*[[g0]] around 785 N, larger because distance is smaller at the surface.

5) Apparent reading: with downward [[a]], [[Pap]] = [[m]]*([[gmod]] + [[a]]) becomes very small, around a few tens of newtons.

## Dimensional validation

- [[gmod]] in m/s^2.
- [[Pmod]] and [[Pap]] in N.
- [[r]], [[R]], [[h]] in m.

Signs and scale are physically coherent: if frame acceleration aligns with gravitational direction, support reading decreases. In the limit [[a]] = -[[gmod]], apparent reading tends to zero while gravity remains finite.

## Physical interpretation

The main causal conclusion is this: gravity remains active because [[G]], [[M]], and [[r]] still define a field, but perceived heaviness depends on contact force measured by [[N]] or [[Pap]]. The astronaut does not lose [[m]], and the planet does not stop attracting the body. What changes is frame dynamics and therefore support demand.

This interpretation matches the core purpose of the leaf: [[P]] is a body-field interaction, whereas [[Pap]] is a context-dependent support reading.

# Real-world example

## Context

An aerospace logistics company designs a calibration bench for load sensors that will fly in reusable launch systems. During tower tests, the instrumented module follows programmed vertical acceleration profiles. Engineers must map sensor readings between ground tests, suborbital trajectories, and low-support phases.

Ground calibration uses [[g0]] as a baseline. During dynamic maneuvers, readings change with [[a]], so engineers must separate [[Pmod]] from [[Pap]]. For altitude corrections they use [[gmod]] as a function of [[h]], and for broad trajectories they switch to central modeling with [[r]].

## Physical estimation

For a payload with [[m]] = 250 kg, near-surface reference gives about 2450 N. During upward acceleration, support reading can exceed 3000 N. During strong downward acceleration, it can drop below 1000 N despite nonzero gravity. Under near free-fall conditions, reading approaches zero.

When the team evaluates higher-altitude scenarios, they find that reusing [[g0]] everywhere is not acceptable for precision work. At several hundred kilometers, [[gmod]] changes enough to alter calibration margins. Their software therefore includes both quick local estimates and central-field mode.

## Interpretation

This case shows why the leaf is operationally relevant. Support sensors do not directly measure how much gravity exists; they measure how much contact force is required under current dynamics. Ignoring that distinction would propagate systematic errors into qualification and safety decisions.

The example also reinforces model governance: state dominant variable, state validity limit, and state frame assumptions before accepting any final number.
