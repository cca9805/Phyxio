# Pitot Tube — Applications

## 1. Primary Application: Aerodynamic Velocity Measurement in Aviation

**Dominant variable:** [[v]], aerodynamic speed of the aircraft relative to the air.

**Validity limit:** Incompressible flow (Ma < 0.3); at higher speeds the Rayleigh-Pitot equation with compressibility correction applies.

The most critical and widespread application of the Pitot tube is measuring aerodynamic velocity (airspeed) in aircraft. All commercial, military, and high-performance drones carry at least one Pitot-static probe as the primary velocity sensor of the Air Data System (ADS).

**Workflow for calculating indicated airspeed (IAS):**

1. **Measure pressure difference**: the probe's differential pressure transducer converts [[dp]] into a real-time electrical signal.
2. **Obtain density**: the barometric altimeter gives static pressure [[p]]; combined with outside air temperature, [[rho]] is computed.
3. **Calculate velocity**: apply the leaf speed relation with [[dp]] and [[rho]].
4. **Compressibility correction**: above Mach 0.3, the flight computer applies the compressible Bernoulli formula to obtain true airspeed (TAS).
5. **Cross-check with GPS/INS**: ground speed (GS) comes from GPS; the difference between GS and TAS gives the headwind/tailwind component, critical data for flight planning and fuel consumption.

**Reference parameter**: a Boeing 737 cruising at 10,700 m altitude with low temperature and [[rho]] around 0.38 kg/m^3 flies at TAS around 900 km/h. The corresponding dynamic pressure is on the order of 11.9 kPa.

about 11.7 % of atmospheric pressure at that altitude.

**Design risk**: probe masking (from ice, birds, or forgotten transport protection) produces erroneous or zero airspeed readings. It is one of the direct causes of the most serious aviation accidents. Modern systems incorporate electric probe heaters, triple redundancy, and discrepancy-detection algorithms between probes.

## 2. Industrial Technical Application: Velocity Profiles in Ducts via Pitot Traversal

**Dominant variable:** [[dp]], pressure difference at each grid point; integrated with the representative area to obtain flow rate.

**Validity limit:** Fully developed turbulent flow in a regular circular or rectangular section; for ducts with obstructions or nearby supports, the measurement grid must be at least 10 diameters downstream of any disturbance.

In ventilation systems, HVAC, industrial process ducts, and cooling towers, accurate measurement of air mass flow rate is essential for energy balancing and system commissioning. The standard method is the **Pitot traversal**: [[dp]] readings are taken at a regular grid of points across the duct cross-section and the velocity profile is integrated numerically.

**Flow rate by profile integration:**

Flow rate is obtained by integrating the velocity profile over the cross-section. In practical discretization, each grid point contributes local speed times representative area.

**Applicable standards**: ISO 7145 (flow in circular ducts), ASHRAE Standard 111 (measurement in HVAC systems).

**Typical engineering decision**: during commissioning of a smoke extraction system in a chemical plant, a Pitot traversal reveals that the centrifugal fan is operating in the instability zone of its characteristic curve because the actual flow rate is 15 % below design. Adjusting the damper position shifts the operating point to the stable zone, reducing noise level and electrical consumption by 8 %.

## 3. Everyday and Interdisciplinary Applications: Spirometers and Cycling

**Dominant variable:** volumetric flow of inhaled/exhaled air (spirometer) or frontal [[dp]] to estimate aerodynamic drag (cycling).

**Validity limit:** In spirometers, low-velocity laminar flow (Reynolds < 2,000 at the mouthpiece); in cycling, rapid turns and thermal instabilities can degrade accuracy.

### 3a. Medical Spirometers

Turbine spirometers and many differential-pressure types incorporate a Pitot tube variant to measure inhaled and exhaled airflow during pulmonary function tests. The device measures [[dp]] generated by air passing through a calibrated resistance; with known resistance, the flow is inferred.

The diagnosis of COPD, asthma, and other respiratory conditions depends on the accuracy of this measurement: a 5 % uncertainty in peak expiratory flow (PEF) can change the severity classification of the disease and the pharmacological treatment.

### 3b. Cycling and Personal Aerodynamics

High-performance cycling power meters (Garmin, Wahoo, CMetric) use Pitot-type differential pressure sensors integrated into the handlebar or fork to estimate the cyclist's aerodynamic drag coefficient in real time. By combining [[v]] (from GPS) with [[dp]] (from the frontal probe), aerodynamic power can be calculated without a velodrome or wind tunnel, allowing the rider to optimize their on-road position.

**Interdisciplinary bridge**: the transition of the Pitot tube from aviation to medicine and competitive sport illustrates how an elementary physical principle—converting velocity into pressure at a stagnation point—becomes a universal measurement platform when the application context involves different densities, velocity scales, and error tolerances.

## 4. Environmental Application: Wind Speed Measurement at Meteorological Stations

**Dominant variable:** [[v]], wind speed; associated dynamic pressure is recorded with high-resolution transducers.

**Validity limit:** Approximately parallel airflow along the sensor axis; for winds with a significant vertical component (orographic flow in mountains), three-axis probes are required.

High-performance meteorological stations (such as measurement masts at wind farms or high-altitude observatories) use Pitot-static probes to record wind speed with low uncertainty. Air density [[rho]] is corrected in real time from temperature and atmospheric pressure, and that correction is injected into the conversion from [[dp]] to [[v]]. These data are essential for wind resource models that determine the economic viability of a wind farm.

## 5. Process Engineering Application: Gas Flow Monitoring in Industrial Stacks

**Dominant variable:** gas mass flow rate in the stack, calculated from a Pitot traversal with temperature correction.

**Validity limit:** Clean, homogeneous gas; if the gas contains suspended particles (>10 mg/m³) or condensates, the Pitot orifice may clog. In that case, purge-air Pitot probes or ultrasonic pressure analyzers are used.

European standard EN ISO 10780 and US EPA Method 2 require industrial facilities to measure gas flow rates in stacks to calculate pollutant emissions reported to environmental registries. The standard procedure is a Pitot traversal in the stack cross-section, operating with gases at temperatures up to 400 °C. At those temperatures, gas density must be recalculated from state variables and then used to convert [[dp]] into [[v]]. Without this [[rho]] correction, emissions can be underestimated.
