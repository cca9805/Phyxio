# Bernoulli's Equation — History

Bernoulli's equation represents the transition from the qualitative observation of fluids to the rigorous quantification of their dynamic behaviour through an energy-conservation principle.

## Historical problem

Before the 18th century the central question was: how are the pressure, velocity, and height of a moving fluid quantitatively related? Hydraulic engineers designed channels and aqueducts in a purely empirical manner, unable to predict accurately the forces exerted by moving water or the exit velocity through orifices. Flow metering was impossible without a law connecting these three variables.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Pascal's hydrostatic pressure (1647) and Leonardo da Vinci's continuity relationship were known, but no unified energy-conservation framework applied to moving fluids existed. Phenomena such as water accelerating through constrictions, suction in pipes, or the height reached by jets were explained with disconnected rules lacking internal physical coherence. The notion of "energy" as a conserved quantity had not been formalised for continuous media.

## What changed

The conceptual leap was the publication of *Hydrodynamica* by Daniel Bernoulli in 1738, in which he formulated the principle of mechanical energy conservation for an ideal fluid: the sum of pressure, kinetic energy, and potential energy per unit volume is constant along a streamline. Leonhard Euler mathematically formalised this result in 1757 with his equations of motion for inviscid fluids, establishing the rigorous analytical basis from which Bernoulli's equation is derived by integration.

## Impact on physics

In the 19th century, Claude-Louis Navier (1822) and George Gabriel Stokes (1845) generalised the Euler equations to include viscosity, giving rise to the Navier-Stokes equations. This made it possible to understand when and why Bernoulli fails: in highly viscous flows, in boundary-layer separation regions, or in rough ducts with significant head losses. In parallel, Henry Darcy and Julius Weisbach developed the empirical friction correlation that extends Bernoulli to real pipes. The impact was twofold: on the one hand, Bernoulli was consolidated as a fundamental law for ideal fluids; on the other, its domain of validity was precisely delimited.

## Connection with modern physics

Bernoulli's equation is operationally used in aeronautics (Pitot tube for measuring flight speed), hydraulic engineering (sizing dams and distribution networks), medicine (Venturi effect in oxygen masks and nebulisers), and fluid metrology (orifice-plate flow meters). It is not mere historical content: it is a design criterion still in use across multiple engineering and applied-science disciplines. Its evolution from ideal model to extensions with losses and compressibility still follows the same logic that Bernoulli established in 1738.
