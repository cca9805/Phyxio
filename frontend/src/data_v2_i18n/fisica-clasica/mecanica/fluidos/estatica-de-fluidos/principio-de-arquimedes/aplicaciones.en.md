# Applications of Archimedes' Principle

## 1. Sovereign Application: Naval Architecture and Large Vessel Design
In the monumental field of naval engineering, Archimedes' principle is not a simple classroom equation but the constitutive and absolute law that allows steel structures weighing hundreds of thousands of tons to defy gravity and stay on the ocean surface. The design of a supertanker or an aircraft carrier is based on the precise calculation of the volume of the submerged hull to generate a buoyancy [[E]] that matches or exceeds the total weight of the vessel and its payload. Engineers must sculpt the geometry of the hull so that, even with the maximum tonnage loaded, the displaced volume is sufficient to maintain the necessary freeboard for safe navigation. This application requires an absolute mastery of seawater density [[rho_f]], which varies slightly according to salinity and temperature, forcing ships to carry load line marks (such as the Plimsoll disk) that indicate how far they can submerge according to the medium in which they operate.

- **Dominant variable**: [[V_des]] (Volume displaced by the geometry of the hull).
- **Validity limit**: Only operational as long as the hull remains watertight and there are no compartmentalized floods that drastically reduce the internal air volume, altering the average density of the system.

## 2. Strategic Application: Submarine Engineering and Active Buoyancy Control
Unlike surface ships, submarines must operate in a state of dynamic equilibrium alternating between positive, neutral, and negative flotation. This is achieved through the use of ballast tanks that can be filled with seawater or emptied with compressed air. By altering the amount of water inside, the submarine changes its own weight [[W]] without altering its external volume. Thus, Archimedes' principle allows that, by equating [[W]] with buoyancy [[E]], the submarine reaches a neutral flotation ([[W_ap]] = 0) that allows it to "float" at a constant depth. To emerge, the submarine expels the water from the tanks using pressurized air, reducing its total weight until Archimedes' buoyancy pushes it back to the surface.

- **Dominant variable**: [[W_ap]] (Apparent weight of the submersible controlled by ballast).
- **Validity limit**: Limited by the structural resistance of the hull to extreme hydrostatic pressure, which could compress the total volume of the submarine and reduce its buoyancy at great depths.

## 3. Aerostatic Application: Balloon and Zeppelin Navigation in the Atmosphere
Although commonly associated with liquids, Archimedes' principle is the driving force behind all aerostatics. A hot air or helium balloon rises because it displaces a massive volume of atmospheric air. Since air has a density [[rho_f]] of approximately 1.2text{ kg/m}^3 at sea level, a balloon with a volume [[V_des]] of 1000text{ m}^3 receives an upward thrust of approximately 12000text{ Newtons}. If the total weight of the balloon (including the gondola, burner, and internal gas) is less than this thrust, the balloon will rise. This application is critical for the design of meteorological probes that carry scientific instruments into the stratosphere, where air density decays and the balloon's volume must expand to compensate for the loss of buoyancy.

- **Dominant variable**: [[rho_f]] (Density of the surrounding air, highly variable with altitude).
- **Validity limit**: Buoyancy gradually disappears as the balloon reaches layers of air so thin that its density matches the average density of the system (flight ceiling).

## 4. Metrological Application: Determination of Purity and Density via Hydrostatic Balance
Inherited directly from Archimedes' original experiment with King Hiero's crown, this application allows identifying materials and detecting fraud in alloys without destroying the piece. A hydrostatic balance is used to measure the real weight [[W]] of an object in the air and its apparent weight [[W_ap]] when submerged in a liquid of known density (usually pure water). From the difference between the two values, which is equal to the buoyancy [[E]], the exact volume [[V_des]] of the object can be calculated, no matter how irregular its shape. Dividing the real weight by this volume multiplied by gravity, we obtain the density of the material, allowing confirmation of whether an ingot is pure gold or if it has been adulterated with less dense metals.

- **Dominant variable**: [[E]] (Magnitude of buoyancy detected by weight loss).
- **Validity limit**: Requires the object not to be porous and not to trap air bubbles on its surface, which would falsify the measurement of the displaced volume.

## 5. Instrumental Application: Hydrometers and Industrial Immersion Densimeters
In the chemical, food, and oil industries, hydrometers are used to instantly measure the density of liquids such as milk, wine, or fuel. A hydrometer is a weighted glass cylinder with a graduated stem that floats vertically in the sample. Archimedes' principle states that the hydrometer will sink until the weight of the displaced liquid volume equals its own weight. If the liquid is very dense, less volume is required to generate the necessary buoyancy and the hydrometer floats high. If the liquid is not very dense, the hydrometer sinks more. This application allowed for fast and precise quality control in fermentation and distillation processes through direct reading of the flotation level.

- **Dominant variable**: [[rho_f]] (Intrinsic property of the liquid under test).
- **Validity limit**: The reading must be corrected according to temperature, as the fluid density varies and the thermal expansion of the instrument itself can introduce systematic errors.
