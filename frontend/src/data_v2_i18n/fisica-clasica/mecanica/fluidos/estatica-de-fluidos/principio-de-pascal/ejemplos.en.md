# Exam-type example

## Problem statement
In a precision mechanical workshop, a hydraulic lift is used to lift an industrial engine with a mass of M  igual a  2500text{ kg}. The system consists of two circular pistons connected by a pipe filled with incompressible hydraulic oil. The input piston (where the force is applied) has a diameter of D_1  igual a  5text{ cm}, while the output piston (where the engine rests) has a diameter of D_2  igual a  45text{ cm}. Calculate the input force [[F1]] that the operator must apply using a lever to keep the engine in equilibrium. Furthermore, determine the pressure [[p]] that the fluid supports inside the system. Take gravity as g  igual a  9.81text{ m/s}^2.

## Data
- Mass of the engine to be lifted: M  igual a  2500text{ kg}
- Input piston diameter: D_1  igual a  0.05text{ m}
- Output piston diameter: D_2  igual a  0.45text{ m}
- Gravitational acceleration: g  igual a  9.81text{ m/s}^2
- Necessary output force (engine weight): [[F2]]  igual a  M cdot g

## System definition
The system is defined as an ideal hydraulic press containing an incompressible fluid confined in a closed circuit. The system is in static equilibrium. The environment is the mechanical workshop, which exerts atmospheric pressure, which is cancelled out by working with gauge pressures or by being applied equally to both pistons. We consider the pistons as rigid surfaces and without friction with the walls.

## Physical model
We will apply **Pascal's Principle**, which states that the pressure applied at piston 1 is transmitted undiminished to piston 2. The model assumes that the fluid does not compress and that there are no head losses or leaks. The resulting force [[F2]] on the larger area piston will be proportional to the applied force [[F1]] multiplied by the ratio of areas [[A1]] and [[A2]].

## Model justification
The use of the Pascal model is justified because hydraulic oil has a very high bulk modulus, behaving like a rigid solid under pressure. We ignore the weight of the oil itself and the height difference between pistons since the pressure generated by the external load (2500text{ kg}) is several orders of magnitude higher than the hydrostatic pressure of the liquid contained in the lift.

## Symbolic solution
We first calculate the areas of the pistons from their diameters:
A_1  igual a  pi (D_1/2)^2
A_2  igual a  pi (D_2/2)^2

We use Pascal's law to equalize the pressures:

{{f:ley_pascal}}


frac{F_1}{A_1}  igual a  frac{F_2}{A_2} implies F_1  igual a  F_2 cdot frac{A_1}{A_2}


Since F_2 is the engine's weight (M cdot g), the final expression for the input force is:

{{f:multiplicacion_fuerza}}

F_1  igual a  M cdot g cdot frac{D_1^2}{D_2^2}


For pressure [[p]]:

p  igual a  frac{F_1}{A_1}


## Numerical substitution
1. Output force: F_2  igual a  2500 cdot 9.81  igual a  24,525text{ N}.
2. Areas:
   A_1  igual a  pi cdot (0.025)^2  igual a  0.001963text{ m}^2
   A_2  igual a  pi cdot (0.225)^2  igual a  0.159043text{ m}^2
3. Input force [[F1]]:
   F_1  igual a  24,525 cdot frac{0.001963}{0.159043}  igual a  24,525 cdot (5/45)^2  igual a  24,525 cdot (1/81) approx 302.78text{ N}.
4. Pressure [[p]]:
   p  igual a  frac{302.78}{0.001963} approx 154,243text{ Pa} approx 1.54text{ bar}.

## Dimensional validation
[F]  igual a  text{M} cdot text{L} cdot text{T}^{-2} (Newton).
[F cdot (A/A)]  igual a  [F cdot (text{L}^2 / text{L}^2)]  igual a  [F].
The force formula is dimensionally correct.
[F/A]  igual a  (text{M} cdot text{L} cdot text{T}^{-2}) / text{L}^2  igual a  text{M} cdot text{L}^{-1} cdot text{T}^{-2} (Pascal). Correct.

## Physical interpretation
The result [[F1]] approx 303text{ N} **means** that a single operator can lift a 2.5text{-ton} engine by applying a force equivalent to lifting only 30text{ kg}. This **indicates** the enormous mechanical advantage (factor of 81) provided by the hydraulic system thanks to the undiminished transmission of pressure [[p]].

# Real-world example

## Context
The hydraulic braking system of a car is the most common application of Pascal's principle. When you step on the brake pedal, you apply a force to a master cylinder (small piston), which is transmitted through brake fluid to the calipers (large pistons) at each wheel, multiplying the force to stop the vehicle safely.

## Physical estimation
If you apply a force of 100text{ N} to a pedal with an area ratio of 1:10 between the master cylinder and the wheel cylinders, we **estimate** that the total braking force will be:

{{f:multiplicacion_fuerza}}


F_2  igual a  F_1 cdot 10  igual a  100 cdot 10  igual a  1000text{ N}


This value of 1000text{ N} per wheel is sufficient to generate the necessary friction against the disc and stop the car in a few meters. This **order of magnitude** is typical in power-assisted braking systems.

## Interpretation
This calculation **means** that the fluid allows moderate human effort to be converted into powerful braking force. Pascal's principle **indicates** that if the liquid has air bubbles (which is compressible), pressure would not be transmitted undiminished and the pedal would feel "spongy", compromising safety.
