# Exam-type example

## Problem statement
A small reconnaissance aircraft has a total wing area [[A]] of  15text{ m}^2 . In steady, level flight conditions at an altitude where air density [[rho]] is  1.0text{ kg/m}^3 , the aircraft maintains a constant cruise speed [[v]] of  60text{ m/s} . Knowing that the lift coefficient [[C_L]] for this regime is  0.45  and the drag coefficient [[C_D]] is  0.035 , the following is requested: 1) Calculate the lift force [[L_f]] generated by the aircraft to stay airborne, 2) Determine the drag force [[D_f]] that the engines must compensate for with their thrust, and 3) Estimate the total weight of the aircraft based on the balance of vertical forces.

## Data
-   Wing area: [[A]]  igual a   15text{ m}^2 
-   Air density: [[rho]]  igual a   1.0text{ kg/m}^3 
-   Cruise speed: [[v]]  igual a   60text{ m/s} 
-   Lift coefficient: [[C_L]]  igual a   0.45 
-   Drag coefficient: [[C_D]]  igual a   0.035 
-   Gravity:  g  igual a  9.8text{ m/s}^2 

## System definition
The study system is the complete aircraft considered as a rigid body in a ground-linked reference system. Air is modeled as a continuous and incompressible fluid (since the speed of  60text{ m/s}  is much lower than the speed of sound). It is assumed that the flow is steady and that there are no external wind gusts, allowing direct use of the constant aerodynamic coefficients of the leaf.

## Model justification
The physical justification of the model lies in the fact that the Mach number ( M approx 0.17 ) is significantly lower than 0.3, which ensures that compressibility effects are negligible and air acts as a constant density fluid. Furthermore, for the aircraft's dimensions and flight speed, the Reynolds number is high enough to ensure a flow regime where the [[C_L]] and [[C_D]] coefficients are stable and do not depend on small-scale viscous fluctuations, allowing for a precise static force analysis.

## Symbolic solution
First, we calculate the dynamic pressure  q  acting on the aircraft surfaces:

q  igual a  frac{1}{2} [[rho]] [[v]]^2


From this value, we obtain the lift by multiplying by the area and the corresponding coefficient:

[[L_f]]  igual a  q cdot [[A]] cdot [[C_L]]


Similarly, we calculate the total drag:

[[D_f]]  igual a  q cdot [[A]] cdot [[C_D]]


Finally, to find the weight  W , we equate lift to the gravitational force according to vertical equilibrium:

W  igual a  [[L_f]]


## Numerical substitution
We calculate the dynamic pressure:

q  igual a  0.5 cdot 1.0 cdot (60)^2  igual a  1800text{ Pa}


We calculate the lift:

[[L_f]]  igual a  1800 cdot 15 cdot 0.45  igual a  12150text{ N}


We calculate the drag:

[[D_f]]  igual a  1800 cdot 15 cdot 0.035  igual a  945text{ N}


The weight of the plane is, therefore:

W  igual a  12150text{ N} Rightarrow m  igual a  frac{12150}{9.8} approx 1240text{ kg}


## Dimensional validation
Dynamic pressure has units of Pascals ( text{N/m}^2 ). When multiplied by area ( text{m}^2 ) and by a dimensionless coefficient, the final result is in Newtons ( text{N} ), which is the correct unit for a force in the SI. The dimensional structure is consistent for a force in SI units.

## Physical interpretation
The plane generates a lift of  12150text{ N} , allowing it to sustain a mass of approximately 1.24 tons in the air. It is interesting to observe that the drag ( 945text{ N} ) is much lower than the lift, indicating good aerodynamic efficiency ( L/D approx 12.8 ). This means that for every Newton of thrust provided by the engines, the wing generates almost 13 Newtons of upward force "for free" thanks to its interaction with the air.

# Real-world example

## Context
In the design of high-efficiency ground vehicles, such as a solar car prototype, the main goal is to minimize drag to maximize battery range. A team of engineers is testing a body with a projected frontal area [[A]] of  1.2text{ m}^2 . They need to estimate the additional power required to overcome air resistance when the vehicle increases its speed from  50text{ km/h}  to  100text{ km/h} , assuming a constant [[C_D]] of  0.18  and an air density of  1.225text{ kg/m}^3 .

## Physical estimation
To perform this quantitative estimation, we first convert the speeds to meters per second:
-    v_1  igual a  50text{ km/h} approx 13.89text{ m/s} 
-    v_2  igual a  100text{ km/h} approx 27.78text{ m/s} 

We calculate the initial drag [[D_f]] at  50text{ km/h} :

[[D_f]]_1  igual a  0.5 cdot 1.225 cdot 0.18 cdot 1.2 cdot (13.89)^2 approx 25.5text{ N}


We calculate the final drag at  100text{ km/h} :

[[D_f]]_2  igual a  0.5 cdot 1.225 cdot 0.18 cdot 1.2 cdot (27.78)^2 approx 102.1text{ N}


We observe that by doubling the speed, the drag has multiplied by four (from 25.5 to 102.1). The required power ( P  igual a  F cdot v ) is even more affected:
-    P_1  igual a  25.5 cdot 13.89 approx 354text{ W} 
-    P_2  igual a  102.1 cdot 27.78 approx 2836text{ W} 

## Interpretation
The power required to overcome the wind has gone from 354 watts to almost 2836 watts. This represents an **eightfold increase** in power demand from the solar motor. This physical estimation is crucial for designers, as it shows that above  80text{ km/h} , aerodynamics becomes the dominant factor in energy consumption, even surpassing tire friction. This is why electric and hyper-efficient cars prioritize extremely smooth shapes and minimal frontal areas; any small reduction in [[C_D]] or area [[A]] translates into a massive range gain at high cruise speeds.
