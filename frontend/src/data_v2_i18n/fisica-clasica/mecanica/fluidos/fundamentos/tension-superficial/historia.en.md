## Historical problem
The historical problem of surface tension was to understand why some small-scale phenomena could not be explained well by weight, pressure or volume alone. Nearly spherical drops, rise in thin tubes, curved menisci and soap films showed visible forces in situations where the amount of liquid was very small.

The difficulty was that macroscopic classical physics tended to look at volume, while these phenomena depended on the boundary. The new question was whether a surface could have its own energy and produce measurable mechanical effects.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
Before the modern formulation, many capillary effects were described qualitatively: adhesion, cohesion, attraction to walls or a natural tendency of liquids to rise. These words were useful, but they did not provide a quantitative law relating [[sigma]], [[L]], [[R]] or [[h]].

The conceptual difficulty was separating three mixed phenomena: pressure, wetting and curvature. Without that separation, a drop, a capillary and a film seemed like different problems.

## What changed
The key advance was interpreting the surface as a region with energetic cost. With that idea, surface tension could be expressed as energy per area or force per length. Later, interfacial curvature was connected with the pressure jump [[dp]], and wetting with capillary rise [[h]].

Physics stopped depending on vague images and began to use geometric balances. The surface became a mechanical object with its own variables.

## Impact on physics
Surface tension connected fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, materials science and surface chemistry. It made it possible to understand emulsions, detergents, aerosols, foams, drops, coatings and biological processes where interfaces dominate over volume.

It also helped clarify that scale matters. In large systems, gravity often dominates; in small systems, the interface may decide the whole behavior.

## Connection with modern physics
In modern physics, surface tension appears in microfluidics, nanotechnology, materials science and biophysics. Designing drops, membranes, hydrophobic surfaces and porous systems requires controlling [[sigma]], [[theta]] and geometry.

The present idea is not only to measure a tension, but to design interfaces. That transition makes surface tension a tool of microscopic engineering.
