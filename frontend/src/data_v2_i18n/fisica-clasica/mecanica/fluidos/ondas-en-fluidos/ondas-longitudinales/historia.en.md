# History of Longitudinal Acoustics

## Historical problem
Since antiquity, philosophers like Aristotle sensed that sound required the movement of air, but the exact nature of that movement remained a mystery for centuries. The central problem was understanding how something intangible could travel at such high speeds without perceiving a constant wind or airflow. In the 17th century, with the birth of experimental physics, the question became quantitative: What does the speed of sound depend on?

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The first major attempt to calculate the speed of sound was made by Isaac Newton in his *Principia* (1687). Newton correctly derived that the speed must depend on elasticity and density (the speed relation between [[c]], [[B]] and [[rho]]), but he made a fundamental error by assuming that the air compression process was **isothermal** (at constant temperature). His calculations yielded a value of about 298  m/s for air, which was 15% lower than the value experimentally measured by his contemporaries. This discrepancy became known as "Newton's speed of sound problem."

## What changed
It was **Pierre-Simon Laplace** in 1816 who resolved the paradox. Laplace realized that the compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave occur so rapidly that the generated heat has no time to dissipate into the surrounding air. Therefore, the process is not isothermal, but **adiabatic**. By introducing the adiabatic coefficient gamma into the formula (the speed relation between [[c]], [[B]] and [[rho]]), the theoretical value matched the observed 340  m/s perfectly. This was one of the great triumphs of early thermodynamics over purely Newtonian mechanics.

## Impact on physics
Laplace's success demonstrated that sound was not just a mechanical phenomenon, but a manifestation of gas thermodynamics. This allowed **physical acoustics** to develop as a rigorous discipline. Furthermore, the understanding of longitudinal waves in solids and fluids enabled the development of seismology and sonar in the late 19th century—technologies that depend on the precise interpretation of compression wave arrival times.

## Connection with modern physics
Today, the study of longitudinal waves extends to **quantum acoustics**, where "phonons" (quanta of mechanical vibration in crystal lattices) are analyzed. In cosmology, "Baryon Acoustic Oscillations" (BAO) describe longitudinal density waves that traveled through the early universe, leaving a mark on the distribution of galaxies that we can observe today, proving that the laws of longitudinal acoustics rule from the atomic scale to the scale of the universe.
