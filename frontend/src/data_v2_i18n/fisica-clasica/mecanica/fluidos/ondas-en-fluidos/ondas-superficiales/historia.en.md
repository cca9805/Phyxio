# History of Surface Waves

## Historical problem
For centuries, sailors and scientists observed the majesty of waves without truly understanding their physical engine. The fundamental problem was determining whether water moved with the wave or if the wave was a purely energetic phenomenon. Leonardo da Vinci was among the first to intuit that water did not displace, comparing waves to the movement of wind over a wheat field. However, he lacked the mathematical framework to demonstrate why waves change speed as they approach the shore.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The main difficulty was that fluid mechanics at the time focused on steady flows (like rivers) or statics (Archimedes). There was no theory explaining the free surface as a dynamic entity subject to gravity. Newton attempted to model waves by comparing them to the motion of a pendulum, which allowed him to obtain an approximation for speed, but he failed by not considering water depth as a critical variable.

## What changed
The great breakthrough occurred in 1845 with **Sir George Biddell Airy**, a British mathematician and Astronomer Royal. Airy applied the laws of hydrodynamics to a disturbed liquid surface, formulating what we know today as linear wave theory (Airy Theory). He was the one who mathematically derived the deep and shallow water regimes, demonstrating that wave speed at the beach depends solely on depth [[h]].

## Impact on physics
Airy's work transformed oceanography from a descriptive discipline into a predictive science. It allowed for the first time the calculation of storm arrival times and the understanding of the phenomenon of dispersion (long waves traveling faster than short ones). Furthermore, it laid the groundwork for the study of electromagnetic waves, as many concepts of phase and group velocity were first explored on the water's surface.

## Connection with modern physics
Today, Airy's equations remain the basis for global climate models and tsunami warning systems. The connection with modern physics is profound: the study of surface waves in quantum fluids (Bose-Einstein condensates) uses direct analogies with gravity waves to study event horizons and artificial Hawking radiation, demonstrating that the laws discovered in the ocean have echoes in the very fabric of space-time.
