# Common mistakes in gravitational field and potential

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Treating [[g]] and [[V]] as the same quantity
Why it seems correct: both depend on radial distance [[r]] and are taught together.
Why it is incorrect: [[g]] is a local acceleration reading, while [[V]] is energy per unit mass.
Detection signal: the student solves an energy-state question using only local field language.
Conceptual correction: decide first whether the target is local dynamics or state-to-state energy.
Mini contrast example: during outward radial motion, [[g]] decreases while [[V]] becomes less negative.

### Error 2: Swapping source mass and test mass roles
Why it seems correct: [[M]] and [[m]] share the same unit and appear near each other.
Why it is incorrect: [[M]] builds field structure; [[m]] scales energetic response only.
Detection signal: changing [[m]] is claimed to modify [[g]] at a fixed position.
Conceptual correction: separate generator variables from response variables before substitution.
Mini contrast example: doubling [[m]] does not change [[V]], but it does change [[U]] and [[Wg]].

## Model errors

### Error 3: Using local approximation over a wide radial span
Why it seems correct: it shortens the calculation.
Why it is incorrect: large separation between [[ri]] and [[rf]] makes field variation non-negligible.
Detection signal: mismatch appears between expected shift from [[gi]] to [[gf]] and chosen model.
Conceptual correction: switch to full radial dependence when relative span crosses practical threshold.
Mini contrast example: broad orbital transfer should use explicit state radii in energy-change analysis.

### Error 4: Ignoring potential reference
Why it seems correct: students assume potential sign is absolute.
Why it is incorrect: absolute [[V]] depends on reference choice, so cross-problem comparison may fail.
Detection signal: opposite signs are reported in similar contexts without reference explanation.
Conceptual correction: declare reference first and keep it fixed.
Mini contrast example: with infinity-zero reference, bound states should return negative [[V]].

## Mathematical errors

### Error 5: Reversing [[ri]] and [[rf]]
Why it seems correct: it looks like a small transcription issue.
Why it is incorrect: state ordering controls the sign of [[DeltaU]] and therefore [[Wg]].
Detection signal: the final physical claim contradicts motion direction.
Conceptual correction: write initial and final states on a timeline before substitution.
Mini contrast example: outward motion paired with potential-energy decrease usually indicates swapped state labels.

### Error 6: Mixing units before SI normalization
Why it seems correct: numbers may still look plausible.
Why it is incorrect: km-versus-m and ton-versus-kg inconsistencies produce order-of-magnitude failures.
Detection signal: extreme outputs appear without physically consistent explanation.
Conceptual correction: normalize everything to SI prior to first substitution.
Mini contrast example: using [[r]] in km with SI [[G]] strongly distorts the result.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 7: Ending with a number and no causal interpretation
Why it seems correct: quick assessments often reward final values.
Why it is incorrect: sign, scale, and validity mistakes remain hidden without causal reading.
Detection signal: no sentence explains what the number means for system behavior.
Conceptual correction: include directional and energetic interpretation every time.
Mini contrast example: stating only that [[DeltaU]] is positive without explaining why is incomplete.

### Error 8: Not cross-checking dynamics and energy views
Why it seems correct: each method seems self-contained.
Why it is incorrect: field and potential must provide compatible narratives of the same process.
Detection signal: dynamic output and energetic output imply opposite tendencies.
Conceptual correction: enforce coherence check among [[g]], [[V]], [[DeltaU]], and [[Wg]].
Mini contrast example: if [[gi]] drops with radius, energy reading must align with that trend.

## Quick self-control rule
Before submitting, run five checks.
1. The functional difference between [[g]] and [[V]] is explicit.
2. A common reference is used for [[ri]] and [[rf]].
3. All data are converted to SI.
4. The sign of [[DeltaU]] matches radial motion direction.
5. Final interpretation states causal physics, not only arithmetic output.

If any check fails, the model setup should be revised before finalizing.
