# Models and Scope in Gravitational Field and Potential

## Ideal model
The ideal model assumes that the source mass [[M]] can be treated as a point mass or as an equivalent spherical distribution observed from outside. Under this assumption, the system is described with a radial coordinate [[r]] and an explicit potential reference at infinity. This framework allows a coherent dual reading: local dynamics through [[g]] and energetic structure through [[V]].

Within this ideal layer, the test mass [[m]] does not reshape the field generated by the source. It only responds to that field. This generation-response separation is what keeps field equations, potential equations, and energy balance mutually consistent.

## Hypotheses
1. Classical non-relativistic regime.
2. Effective spherical symmetry or valid point-mass approximation.
3. Shared geometric reference for [[ri]] and [[rf]].
4. Explicit and stable potential reference throughout the analysis.
5. Strict SI conversion before numerical substitution.

These hypotheses are not formal decoration. Each one removes a specific physical ambiguity and prevents model errors from being mistaken for algebra mistakes.

## Quantitative validity domain
Operationally, the ideal model is reliable when radial scale changes do not break symmetry assumptions or classical-regime assumptions. A practical decision rule can be based on simple ratios.

- If abs([[rf]] - [[ri]]) / [[ri]] <= 0.1, local approximation is typically acceptable for quick estimates.
- If abs([[rf]] - [[ri]]) / [[ri]] > 0.1, full radial dependence should be used to avoid bias in [[DeltaU]].
- If abs([[gf]] - [[gi]]) / [[gi]] <= 0.1, near-constant [[g]] approximation can be reasonable.
- If abs([[gf]] - [[gi]]) / [[gi]] > 0.1, local approximation should be replaced by full radial treatment.

These thresholds are practical triggers, not universal laws. Their role is to support model-switch decisions with explicit criteria.

## Model failure signals
The model is failing when numerical outputs look plausible but physical interpretation becomes contradictory. A common signal is a sign mismatch between radial movement and potential-energy change. Another signal is forcing a single [[g]] value over a range where the shift from [[gi]] to [[gf]] is clearly significant.

Failure also appears when real source geometry breaks the assumed symmetry and introduces effects that one radial coordinate cannot capture. In that situation, keeping the ideal model for convenience weakens physical credibility.

## Extended or alternative model
When ideal assumptions become fragile, the natural extension is multi-source superposition with proper vector treatment for field and scalar treatment for potential. For stronger-regime cases, the alternative framework is relativistic gravitation.

The transition criterion must be explicit. Change model when abs([[gf]] - [[gi]]) / [[gi]] > 0.1 or when source geometry clearly violates effective spherical approximation. This criterion avoids elegant but physically weak conclusions.

## Operational comparison
The ideal model is fast and conceptually transparent. The extended model is more demanding but more faithful under complex geometry or large radial spans. In practice, ideal modeling is useful for first-pass estimation, while extended modeling is useful for final validation.

The key competence is not memorizing both models, but switching between them at the right moment with explicit physical justification.
