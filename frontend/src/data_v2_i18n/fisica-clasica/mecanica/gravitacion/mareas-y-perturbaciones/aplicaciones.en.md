# Applications

## 1. Terrestrial ocean tides

Ocean tides are the most visible application of differential acceleration [[am]]. The Moon and the Sun do not only attract Earth as a whole; they generate field differences between the near side, the center, and the far side. That difference organizes bulges, currents, sea-level variation, and regions of strong dissipation.

Dominant variable: [[d]], because closeness of the perturber weighs more than isolated mass. The Moon dominates many tidal aspects over the Sun through the cubic dependence on distance, even though the Sun has a much larger mass.

Validity limit: the first-order estimate does not predict local tide heights. Bathymetry, continents, basin resonances, and meteorology are required for that. The leaf gives the basic gravitational cause, not the complete coastal map.

In teaching, this application separates astronomical cause from oceanic response. The basic cause is understood through gravitational gradient; the final height at a specific coast belongs to a much richer hydrodynamic model.

## 2. Internal heating of moons

Satellites such as Io show intense geological activity because tides deform not only water, but also rock. The mass of the giant planet [[M]], the satellite radius [[R]], and its distance [[d]] generate a tidal scale repeated on every orbit. If orbital resonances are also present, the deformation does not relax symmetrically and part of the mechanical energy becomes heat.

Dominant variable: [[am]] as the scale of repeated deformation, together with [[tau]] when dissipation and phase lag are considered. The instantaneous tide alone does not explain volcanism; accumulated work matters.

Validity limit: the simple calculation does not determine thermal power. Converting tide into heat requires elasticity, internal structure, viscosity, and phase of deformation. This leaf identifies the mechanical source and its scale.

The didactic value is seeing that a small force can become internal energy if it acts periodically. This is not an instantaneous explosion, but accumulated work over astronomical times.

## 3. Artificial satellite stability

A satellite in Earth orbit does not move in a pure two-body problem. The Moon, the Sun, Earth's oblateness, and other effects produce [[ap]] relative to the central acceleration [[ac]]. The parameter [[eps]] helps decide which perturbations can be ignored in a preliminary estimate and which must enter the orbital propagator.

Dominant variable: [[eps]], because it classifies the perturbation by comparison with the central motion. A small acceleration may be acceptable for a classroom estimate and mandatory for precise navigation if it accumulates for months.

Validity limit: the scalar ratio does not by itself predict node, inclination, or eccentricity. A real mission uses vector addition, high-degree gravitational models, and non-gravitational corrections.

This application shows why a classroom calculation and a mission calculation do not require the same level of detail. The classroom identifies hierarchies; the mission integrates small perturbations because accumulated positional error matters.

## 4. Sun-Moon comparison in tides

The comparison between two perturbers explains why the Sun does not always dominate terrestrial tides. The relation between [[M1]], [[M2]], [[d1]], [[d2]], [[am1]], and [[am2]] shows that the distance factor can compensate for an enormous mass difference. This application is didactically powerful because it forces students to abandon the argument "more mass means more tide."

Dominant variable: the cubic ratio of distances, not one isolated magnitude. The nearby perturber gains tidal weight very rapidly.

Validity limit: the comparison assumes that both bodies act on the same Earth and that the same scale [[R]] is analyzed. It does not replace a full ocean-tide model and does not include relative phases.

The comparison also prepares the explanation of spring and neap tides when orbital geometry is added. The leaf does not compute phases, but it builds the criterion for understanding why alignment of perturbers changes the observed response.

## 5. Synchronization and rotational braking

Many moons always show the same face to their planet because tides have transferred angular momentum over long intervals. A tidal deformation does not point exactly toward the perturber when dissipation is present; a phase lag appears and with it a torque [[tau]]. That torque can brake rotation, couple orbital periods, and slowly modify distances.

Dominant variable: [[tau]] as a conceptual scale of rotational evolution, connected with [[am]] but dependent on internal properties. Differential acceleration generates deformation; dissipation turns that deformation into secular evolution.

Validity limit: this leaf does not calculate synchronization times. Internal parameters, rigidity, dissipation factor, and mass distribution are needed for that. Here the goal is to recognize when a tidal effect can produce accumulated torque.

The educational value of this application is that it connects translation, rotation, and dissipation. A single gravitational cause can deform, heat, and change periods, but each prediction requires knowing what additional information the model provides.

Across these five applications, the same operational idea is repeated with different physical consequences: first identify the dominant gravitational scale, then read the differential or perturbative correction, and finally decide whether an ideal model is enough. That sequence is useful for students because it prevents isolated formula use and forces every numerical result to be tied to a real modeling decision.
