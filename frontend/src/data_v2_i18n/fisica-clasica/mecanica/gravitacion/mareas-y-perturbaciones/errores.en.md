# Common errors

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: confusing tide with total gravitational force

The most common mistake is to think that a tide simply measures how much gravity the perturbing mass [[M]] exerts. In reality, a tide measures acceleration difference inside the affected body. If the field were uniform, there would be global attraction but no tidal deformation. The key magnitude is [[am]], not the total acceleration toward the perturber.

### Error 2: choosing the dominant perturber from mass alone

Comparing only [[M1]] and [[M2]] leads to wrong conclusions. The distance [[d1]] or [[d2]] enters with cubic effect, so a much less massive body can dominate if it is close enough. The correct physical reading compares mass and distance together.

### Error 3: assuming a small perturbation has no effect

A small value of [[eps]] permits approximation, but it does not always permit ignoring the effect. If the action repeats over many orbits, if resonance is present, or if dissipation exists, a weak perturbation can produce accumulated changes. The criterion is not only instantaneous size, but also time of action and phase.

## Model errors

### Error 4: using the tidal approximation near the geometric limit

The first-order estimate requires [[R]] much smaller than [[d]]. If the affected body occupies a significant fraction of the distance to the perturber, the field cannot be represented by a single gradient. In that case the spatial variation must be computed in more detail or an extended model must be used.

### Error 5: treating tidal torque as a closed formula

The magnitude [[tau]] depends on deformation, phase lag, dissipation, and mass distribution. Using it as if it came from one universal scalar expression gives false precision. In this leaf, tidal torque is introduced as a conceptual scale for rotational evolution, not as a universal numerical calculation.

### Error 6: applying relative comparison to different affected bodies

The comparison between [[am1]] and [[am2]] assumes that both perturbers act on the same body and the same scale [[R]]. If different situations are mixed, for example a tide on Earth and a tide on a moon of Jupiter, the ratio loses its direct interpretation.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 7: forgetting the cube of distance

Tidal dependence does not attenuate like an ordinary central force. If [[d]] is doubled, tidal acceleration decreases much more than by one half. This error usually shows that the student is recalling universal gravitation instead of thinking about field gradient.

### Error 8: reversing the distance ratio

In comparisons involving [[d1]] and [[d2]], reversing the ratio can change the answer by orders of magnitude. A useful self-check is physical direction: if perturber 1 moves closer, [[am1]] must increase, not decrease.

### Error 9: adding accelerations as scalars without geometry

The central acceleration [[ac]] and the perturbing acceleration [[ap]] have direction. The parameter [[eps]] estimates importance, but it does not replace vector addition when the problem asks for trajectory, phase, eccentricity, or energy transfer.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 10: reading [[am]] as direct sensation of weight

Terrestrial tides are very small compared with everyday weight. Their importance is not that humans directly feel them, but that they act on extended masses of water, rock, or plasma and repeat periodically. A correct interpretation connects small value with collective effect.

### Error 11: not distinguishing instantaneous deformation and secular evolution

A visible deformation may be small and still indicate transfer of energy or angular momentum over astronomical times. In tidal problems one must ask whether dissipation and phase lag are present, because that is where internal heating, rotational braking, or synchronization enter.

### Error 12: presenting a number without validity conditions

A result for [[am]] or [[eps]] is physically useful only if conditions are stated. One must say whether distance is large compared with radius, whether the perturbation is small, and whether vector geometry has been ignored. Without those statements, the calculation is incomplete.

## Quick self-control rule

Before accepting an answer, check three questions: do I calculate field difference and not total gravity, did I include the cubic distance effect, and did I compare the perturbation with the central scale through [[eps]] or an equivalent argument? If any of these fails, the solution needs revision.
