# Physical models

## Ideal model

The ideal model treats the affected body as an extended object with characteristic radius [[R]] exposed to the field of a perturbing mass [[M]] located at a distance [[d]] much larger than its size. The aim is not to reconstruct the full deformation of the body, but to estimate the differential acceleration [[am]] that appears because the external gravitational field changes between the near side, the center, and the far side.

In orbital dynamics, the ideal model separates a dominant central acceleration [[ac]] from a perturbing acceleration [[ap]]. The main motion is interpreted as a two-body orbit, while the perturbation is treated as a small correction. This separation makes the parameter [[eps]] an operational validity criterion: if the perturbation is small compared with the central acceleration, the simplified model remains physically meaningful.

## Hypotheses

The model assumes classical Newtonian mechanics, positive masses, and distances expressed in SI units. The essential geometric condition is that [[d]] is clearly larger than [[R]], because the first-order tidal acceleration approximates the gravitational gradient through one internal length scale of the affected body. If the distance is comparable to the radius, the field changes too much inside the body and the approximation is no longer reliable.

It is also assumed that the compared perturbers act on the same affected body. Therefore [[M1]], [[M2]], [[d1]], [[d2]], [[am1]], and [[am2]] can be compared cleanly only when the internal scale [[R]] and physical context are common. For tidal torques, [[tau]] is not closed by a single relation: deformation, dissipation, and phase lag are required.

## Quantitative validity domain

A safe use of the model requires [[R]] much smaller than [[d]] and [[eps]] much smaller than one when discussing orbital perturbations. As a practical criterion, a ratio [[eps]] below one hundredth usually supports a first perturbative reading, whereas values approaching one tenth already require checking accumulation, resonances, and the direction of [[ap]]. For tides, the differential acceleration [[am]] should remain small compared with the dominant gravitational acceleration of the system.

In comparisons between perturbers, the quantitative criterion is not just the mass ratio. Distance enters with cubic effect, so a farther perturber needs a much larger mass to compete. When the result changes strongly after a small variation in [[d1]] or [[d2]], the answer must mention geometric sensitivity instead of presenting the prediction as exact.

## Model failure signals

The model begins to fail if the affected body is very close to the perturber, if deformation is no longer small, if strong resonances exist, or if the perturbation changes the orbit within only a few periods. It also fails if [[tau]] is used as a numerical result without a dissipation model. Observable signals include precession, internal heating, rotational synchronization, or secular variation not explained by instantaneous tide alone.

Another failure signal appears when perturbation direction matters as much as its magnitude. The parameter [[eps]] measures scale, not geometry. If the problem asks for orbital phase, eccentricity, inclination, or energy transfer, the scalar reading must become a vector model.

## Extended or alternative model

The extended model uses vector addition of accelerations, numerical multi-body integration, or orbital perturbation theory. For deformable tides, Love numbers, internal dissipation, viscosity, elasticity, and angular phase lag are added. In systems with close satellites or rings, resonances and secular evolution are included.

When to switch to the extended model: if [[eps]] is no longer small, if [[R]] is not negligible compared with [[d]], if the effect accumulates over many orbits, or if the desired result depends on the direction of [[ap]] and not only on its size. In those cases this leaf remains useful as an initial estimate, but not as a complete predictive closure.

## Operational comparison

The ideal model answers scale questions quickly: which perturber dominates, how the tide changes when a body moves closer, or whether a secondary acceleration can be treated as a correction. The extended model answers evolution questions: how much a satellite heats, how an orbit precesses, when synchronization appears, or what path a spacecraft follows under several bodies.

The operational decision must follow the objective. For a school or order-of-magnitude estimate, [[am]] and [[eps]] are sufficient if their limits are declared. For precise orbital prediction, long-term stability, or real body deformation, the scalar approximation becomes a starting point rather than the final result.
