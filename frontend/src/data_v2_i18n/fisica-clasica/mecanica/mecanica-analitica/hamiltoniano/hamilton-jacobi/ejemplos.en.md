Choose a unit-scale estimate for the original system. The displayed formulas should keep [[S]] on the expected scale. Every term must preserve the unit declared for [[S]] or for its associated quantity. The check is physical: a wrong unit means the formal expression no longer represents the same model.

# Real-world example

## Context

A teacher compares two solutions of the same advanced mechanics problem. One solution is shorter; the other explicitly checks the structure of Hamilton-Jacobi Theory. The second solution is preferred because it proves that the simplification did not change the physical system.

## Physical estimation

The estimate uses the original energy, action, frequency, or phase-space scale. The order of magnitude must remain reasonable after applying the advanced relation. A scale error reveals either a wrong variable, a broken approximation, or an inadmissible transformation.

## Interpretation

Physically, the example shows that advanced mechanics is a diagnostic language. It helps decide whether a result is valid, preserved, equivalent, separable, or coherent. That is why [[S]] must be interpreted, not merely calculated.

In exam grading, the teacher should look for three signs: the student recognizes action as a function, interprets momentum as a gradient, and understands autonomous separation as a reduction of time dependence. The result should not be presented as a single trajectory, but as a compact way of organizing families of solutions.

## Teacher check

A complete answer must contain a sentence about validity, a sentence about the dominant quantity, and a sentence about the limiting case. This prevents the example from becoming a formal substitution exercise. The expected student product is a physically argued decision: the advanced method is allowed, conditionally allowed, or rejected because the model assumptions have failed.

To complete the example, require a final verbal check: what the action function represents, how its gradient is read, and what role energy plays in separation. This prevents students from treating the equation as a recipe without physical trajectory. It also distinguishes a partial solution from one that organizes complete families.


## Teaching extension of the example

The example can also be extended by asking students to interpret two levels of solution: a partial action function and a function complete enough to reconstruct trajectories. This distinction prevents confusing solving an equation with understanding dynamics. If the action contains adequate parameters, its derivatives recover momenta and motion conditions. If it does not, the expression may be formally correct but insufficient. The extension forces students to state what dynamical information is stored in the action and what information is still missing.
