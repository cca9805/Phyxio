## Ideal Model

The ideal model separates energy associated with motion from energy associated with configuration. Kinetic energy [[T]] is computed from a speed compatible with the system; potential energy [[V]] is computed from a declared reference. Then [[L]] and [[E]] are built according to the physical question.

The central simplification is to assume that the relevant interactions are conservative and that kinetic energy can be expressed quadratically. This idealization allows familiar energy balances to become a clear Lagrangian formulation.

## Hypotheses

- [[m]] is positive and represents the effective mass of the system.
- [[v]] or [[qdi]] belongs to the coordinate used in the model.
- [[V]] uses an explicit reference, either [[h]] for gravity or [[qi]] for elasticity.
- Friction and motors are not hidden inside [[V]].
- [[T]] and [[V]] describe the same system and the same instant.

## Quantitative Validity Domain

As an order of magnitude, a mass of 1 kg at 1 m/s has [[T]] close to 0.5 J, whereas at 10 m/s it has [[T]] close to 50 J. This difference shows the weight of quadratic dependence. In Earth's gravity, 1 kg raised by 1 m has [[V]] close to 10 J.

The model is reasonable when the computed energy scales are compatible with the system. If a 100 g laboratory object produces megajoule energies without extreme speeds or huge heights, the model or units are wrong.

## Model failure signals

One failure signal is obtaining negative [[T]]. Another is interpreting an arbitrary shift in the zero of [[V]] as if it were a physical force. The model also fails when [[L]] is used as if it were [[E]], because conservation and dynamical generation are then mixed.

Another observable signal appears in the graph: if [[V]] should be constant because height is fixed but changes with [[v]], variables have been mixed. If [[T]] does not grow parabolically, the elementary kinetic dependence has been broken.

## Extended or Alternative Model

The transition appears when it is convenient to leave the ideal model: relativistic speeds, non-uniform fields, non-quadratic potentials, dissipation, actuators, or coupled coordinates. In those cases, [[V]] is replaced by a more appropriate expression, generalized forces are added, or a coordinate-dependent mass matrix is built.

An extended model may keep the Lagrangian structure, but it requires more explicit hypotheses. In a real pendulum, for example, kinetic energy depends on angular speed and gravitational potential depends on angle. In a dissipative system, [[E]] is no longer conserved even if [[L]] remains a partial tool when combined with non-conservative forces.

## Operational Comparison

- Uniform gravitational model: clear, fast, and useful for small heights.
- Linear elastic model: adequate near equilibrium and before large deformation.
- Generalized model: allows [[qi]] and [[qdi]] when the natural coordinate is not Cartesian.
- Extended model: required with couplings, losses, actuation, or nonlinear domains.
- Selection criterion: use the simplest model that preserves units, reference, and physical reading.
