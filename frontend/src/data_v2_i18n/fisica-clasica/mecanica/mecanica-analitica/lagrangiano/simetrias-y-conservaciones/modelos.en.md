# Models of Symmetries and Conservation Laws

## Ideal Model

The ideal model describes a conservative Lagrangian system whose [[L]] has a continuous symmetry. The central object is not a particular trajectory but the structure that remains unchanged under a displacement, a rotation, a shift of the time origin, or an infinitesimal transformation generated by [[X_i]]. In that setting, the charge [[Q]] is conserved because the change of [[L]] under the transformation vanishes or, in more advanced formulations, becomes a total derivative.

The simplest case is a cyclic coordinate: if [[L]] does not depend on a coordinate, the associated conjugate momentum [[p_i]] satisfies [[dpdt]] equal to zero. This rule turns a symmetry of the model into a measurable or computable quantity. In ordinary mechanical systems, translational symmetries produce [[P]], rotational symmetries produce [[J]], and time-translation invariance produces [[E]].

## Hypotheses

- The system is described by a sufficiently regular Lagrangian [[L]]. If not, [[DLv]] and [[p_i]] lose operational meaning.
- The continuous transformation generated by [[X_i]] leaves the action invariant, not merely one conveniently written equation.
- Hidden nonconservative external forces are absent. If friction, active control, or external torques are present, conservation of [[Q]] can be broken.
- The generalized variables are independent within the chosen domain. If the parametrization is redundant, [[p_i]] may not be directly interpretable as a physical quantity.
- For the elementary forms [[P]] = [[m]][[v]] and [[J]] = [[r]][[P]], constant mass, a defined geometry, and a direction compatible with the scalar simplification are assumed.

## Quantitative Validity Domain

Quantitatively, the model is reliable when relative changes of the conserved charge remain small compared with the scale of the problem. In an ideal simulation, a ratio such as `|Delta Q|/|Q| < 10^-3` is often acceptable in introductory numerical work, while a bound below `10^-6` for [[dpdt]] can be used as a local cyclic-coordinate check. If those bounds grow systematically, roundoff is not a sufficient explanation. One must check whether the symmetry is genuinely present in [[L]] or whether an external interaction breaks the assumption.

For conservation of [[E]], the Lagrangian must not depend explicitly on time. For conservation of [[P]], the environment must not select a privileged position. For conservation of [[J]], there must be no external torque about the chosen origin. These are physical conditions, not decorative labels: they determine whether an equality can be used as a diagnostic, a shortcut, or a prediction.

## Model failure signals

A first failure signal is a monotonic change of [[Q]] in a system declared to have an exact symmetry. Another signal is [[dpdt]] not being zero even though a coordinate has been called cyclic; this usually indicates an explicit dependence in [[L]] that was not recognized. The model also fails if [[P]] or [[J]] are treated as conserved in the presence of external forces or torques.

The most common didactic failure is to memorize the pair "symmetry-conservation" without checking the model condition. Noether's theorem does not replace physical analysis; it organizes it. Before using a conservation law, one must identify the symmetry, its generator [[X_i]], the charge [[Q]], and the limit that could break it.

## Extended or Alternative Model

An extended model admits broken symmetries, nonconservative generalized forces, or approximate invariances. In that case, the charge is no longer exactly conserved, but its rate of change can be interpreted as a measure of the symmetry breaking. For instance, weak drag can make [[E]] decrease slowly; an external torque can change [[J]]; a horizontal external force can change [[P]].

It is appropriate to move to the extended model when data show systematic drift, when the system exchanges energy with the environment, or when the effective Lagrangian contains explicitly time-dependent terms. The question then changes from "what is exactly conserved" to "which approximate symmetry explains the observed scale of variation".

## Operational Comparison

- Ideal model: reduces calculation, checks solutions, and explains why [[Q]], [[E]], [[P]], or [[J]] remain constant.
- Extended model: handles friction, external driving, nonhomogeneous media, and only approximate symmetries.
- Precision: the ideal model is exact within its assumptions; the extended model estimates deviations and loss rates.
- Complexity: the ideal model requires identifying a symmetry and a generator; the extended model requires quantifying the breaking mechanism.
- Didactic use: the ideal model teaches the deep connection between the form of [[L]] and conservation; the extended model prevents that connection from becoming an automatic recipe.

The practical decision is direct: use the ideal model to discover invariants and simplify the dynamics, and move to the extended model when the system itself shows that the invariant is no longer maintained within the expected physical tolerance.
