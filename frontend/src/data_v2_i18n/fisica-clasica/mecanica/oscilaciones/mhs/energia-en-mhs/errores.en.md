# Frequent mistakes

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: assuming energy is lost just because [[K]] decreases

Why it seems correct:
Part of the cycle shows lower [[K]], so people conclude that total energy is no longer conserved.

Detection sign:
The explanation ignores the simultaneous rise of [[U]] in the same interval.

Mini contrast example:
Near a turning point, [[K]] may be small while [[U]] is large and total [[E]] remains stable.

Conceptual correction:
Do not read one component in isolation; the relevant criterion is balance among [[K]], [[U]], and [[E_total]].

### Error 2: thinking constant total energy means constant components

Why it seems correct:
If [[E]] does not change, students may expect [[K]] and [[U]] to stay fixed too.

Detection sign:
They treat temporal variation of components as a contradiction.

Mini contrast example:
At equilibrium, [[K]] may dominate while [[U]] is low; at turning points the opposite holds without breaking conservation.

Conceptual correction:
Constancy belongs to the ideal sum, not to each component separately.

## Model errors

### Error 3: using ideal conservation when visible dissipation exists

Why it seems correct:
The ideal energy equation is simple and gets reused even when the data clearly degrade.

Detection sign:
[[E]] shows systematic drift and pure exchange interpretation is still forced.

Mini contrast example:
If the total level drops cycle by cycle, the ideal lossless model is no longer sufficient.

Conceptual correction:
When drift is persistent, move to a damped model or justify why the drift is not physical.

### Error 4: confusing a parameter with an energetic state

Why it seems correct:
[[A]] or [[k]] get mixed with truly time-varying quantities such as [[K]] and [[U]].

Detection sign:
The solution treats [[A]] as if it were an instantaneous energy variable.

Mini contrast example:
[[A]] sets the scale of [[E]], but it does not replace dynamic reading of component exchange.

Conceptual correction:
Separate structural parameters from instantaneous energetic states.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 5: mixing units in energy reconstruction

Why it seems correct:
Numerical factors may look small, so the final value appears “close enough.”

Detection sign:
[[k]] is used in one unit system while [[x]] or [[A]] is used in another.

Mini contrast example:
One missed conversion can multiply or divide reconstructed energy by a large factor.

Conceptual correction:
Normalize units before computing any energetic quantity.

### Error 6: ending with a numeric sum and no sign or range check

Why it seems correct:
The final sum is positive, so it is accepted without deeper review.

Detection sign:
No check is made that [[K]] and [[U]] remain non-negative or that [[E]] stays in a plausible order of magnitude.

Mini contrast example:
A negative potential energy in this ideal model usually indicates flawed algebra or data handling.

Conceptual correction:
Pair each calculation with non-negativity and scale checks.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 7: confusing energy exchange with a change of physical mechanism

Why it seems correct:
Because [[K]] and [[U]] cross, students may think the system entered a different regime.

Detection sign:
Terms like “model transition” are used when only normal component exchange occurred.

Mini contrast example:
Component crossover is expected in ideal SHM and does not by itself imply new physics.

Conceptual correction:
Distinguish internal redistribution of energy from a true mechanism change.

### Error 8: reporting conservation as a slogan instead of evidence

Why it seems correct:
The phrase “energy is conserved” is written without showing how it was verified.

Detection sign:
No quantitative comparison appears between [[K]]+[[U]] and [[E]] or [[E_total]].

Mini contrast example:
A conservation claim without a verifiable balance is narrative, not validation.

Conceptual correction:
Claim conservation only after quantitative and physical coherence has been demonstrated.

## Quick self-control rule

1. Identify whether the question asks for one energetic component or total energy.
2. Check units before computing.
3. Verify non-negativity of [[K]] and [[U]].
4. Contrast [[K]]+[[U]] with [[E]] or [[E_total]].
5. Close with physical reading of exchange, not only with a sum.
