# History of work and torque

## Historical problem

The historical problem was extending work from linear forces to rotating machines. Wheels, shafts, mills, and engines were not described comfortably with straight displacements, because the relevant action was a tendency to rotate about an axis.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty was separating torque from energy. Force times lever arm produced a quantity with energy units, but it did not represent transferred energy if no rotation occurred. Angular displacement was needed to recover the idea of work.

## What changed

The formulation with [[tau]] and [[theta]] made it possible to write [[W]] as transfer by rotation. Later, angular power [[P]] connected torque with angular speed [[omega]], making motors with load-speed curves easier to analyze.

## Impact on physics

This reading made linear and rotational systems comparable. It supported the design of transmissions, brakes, turbines, and generators with clear energy balances. It also explained why a shaft can carry high torque without delivering power if it does not rotate.

## Connection with modern physics

The idea of work as an integral of a generalized action over a generalized coordinate reappears in analytical mechanics. Torque and angle are a rotational pair, just as force and position are a translational pair. This continuity connects classical machines with more abstract formulations.

## Present functional reading

Today the historical value of this distinction appears in any motor data sheet. Maximum torque alone does not determine delivered energy or useful power: one must know through what angular displacement it acts and at what angular speed the shaft operates. Modern engineering therefore separates torque curves, power curves, efficiency, and thermal limits. Elementary physics keeps the same conceptual structure, even when it writes it with idealized models.
