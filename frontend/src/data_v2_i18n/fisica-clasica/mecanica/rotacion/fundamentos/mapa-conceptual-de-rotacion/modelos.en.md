# Models in Rotational Mechanics

## Ideal model
The fundamental model in this block is the **Rigid Body**. It is defined as a system of particles whose relative distances remain constant under any external force or applied [[torque]]. This model allows simplifying the movement of billions of atoms into a single rotation vector and a constant [[momento-de-inercia]], avoiding the need to analyze each particle separately. It is the cornerstone that allows us to apply Newton's laws to extended objects with superior mathematical elegance.

In the rigid body model, it is assumed that no elastic or plastic deformations exist, implying that the work done by internal forces is zero during rotation. This abstraction is extraordinarily useful because it reduces the infinite degrees of freedom of a real body to just six in space (three translational and three rotational), greatly facilitating the calculation of trajectories, energies, and equilibria in complex mechanical systems.

## Hypotheses
1. **Absolute Geometric Invariability**: The object maintains its shape and volume under any mechanical load condition.
2. **Well-Defined Rotation Axis**: Rotation occurs relative to a straight line (axis), which can be fixed in space or move while maintaining its orientation.
3. **Stable Mass Distribution**: The object's density does not change during motion, ensuring that the [[momento-de-inercia]] is a constant geometric property for a given axis.
4. **Absence of Relativistic or Quantum Effects**: Velocities are much lower than c (

{{f:relacion-lineal-angular-v}}

) and scales are macroscopic.

## Quantitative validity domain
This model is extremely accurate for everyday objects like steel pulleys, wooden wheels, and metal gears. It is valid as long as internal stresses generated by rotation do not exceed the material's elastic limit (typically

{{f:posicion-angular-definicion}}

 for structural steel). In terms of angular velocity, the model is robust until the centripetal acceleration at the object's edge,

{{f:posicion-angular-definicion}}

, begins to compromise the material's structural integrity. For most industrial applications with

{{f:posicion-angular-definicion}}

, the error introduced by ignoring small deformations is less than 0.1\%.

## Model failure signals
- **Visible or Structural Deformation**: If a wheel becomes elliptical or widens due to centrifugal force, the rigid body model is no longer applicable.
- **Internal Vibrations and Resonances**: The appearance of vibrational modes indicates that internal distances are no longer constant.
- **Dynamic Change in Mass Distribution**: If the object changes shape voluntarily (like a gymnast) or involuntarily (like fluid in a tank), the [[momento-de-inercia]] stops being constant.
- **Thermal Dissipation through Hysteresis**: If the object heats up significantly due to repeated internal deformations, energy is not conserved mechanically alone.

## Extended or alternative model
For systems that do not meet these hypotheses, we turn to **Deformable Solid Mechanics** (Elasticity and Plasticity) or **Fluid Mechanics**. These models include parameters like Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, or viscosity to describe how the object changes shape while rotating. **The rigid body model should be abandoned and it is advisable to change to a deformable model when relative deformations exceed 0.5\% of the object's characteristic dimension**, or when the body enters a plastic regime.

## Operational comparison
While the rigid body allows solving problems using relatively simple ordinary differential equations (

{{f:segunda-ley-rotacion}}

), deformable models require using stress and strain tensors, typically solved using the finite element method (FEM). The rigid body model is the essential "zero-order" approximation: it provides the base solution onto which deformation corrections are added if the problem's precision requires it.

### Transition to Extended Models
The transition from the rigid body model to more complex models occurs under the following conditions:
1. **Deformable Solid**: One must switch to this model when centrifugal forces or applied torques produce relative deformations exceeding 0.1% of the body's dimensions.
2. **Non-linear Dynamics**: If the rotation axis does not coincide with a principal axis of inertia and is not constrained, the system will enter a regime of complex precession and nutation requiring full vector treatment (Euler equations).
3. **Quantum Mechanics**: When the system's angular momentum is on the order of Planck's constant hbar, the classical model fails and must transition to a quantum description of spin and rotation.
