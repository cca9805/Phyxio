# Applications

## 1. Laboratory ramp

Dominant variable: [[a_cm]]

In a practical activity with disks, hoops, and spheres, the strategy prevents using the same acceleration for all bodies. The method starts by identifying [[I]] and comparing I/(mR^2). Then the student decides whether the incline-acceleration relation is enough or whether friction must be measured. The comparison shows that the body with larger relative inertia spends more response in spin and descends with smaller acceleration.

Validity limit: the body must roll without slipping, keep its shape, and maintain a defined axis.

## 2. Vehicles and tires

Dominant variable: [[tau]]

In a real wheel, [[R]] can change slightly through deformation and contact can dissipate energy. To estimate speed, [[E_total]] may be a good first balance; to evaluate grip, an FBD and the contact torque [[tau]] are required. The strategy separates a speed question from a safety or traction question.

Validity limit: if slipping, locked braking, or large deformation appears, the ideal model must be replaced by a model with losses.

## 3. Industrial rollers

Dominant variable: [[omega]]

Rollers in a conveyor system require separating material advance from roller spin. [[omega]] may remain almost steady while the material speed changes with load. The strategy helps decide whether the problem is kinematic, because it only relates speeds, or dynamic, because contact forces are required.

Validity limit: the contact condition depends on sufficient friction and no relative slipping.

## 4. Sports

Dominant variable: [[K_total]]

A ball rolling on a track has [[K_total]] split between translation and rotation. A launch with initial sliding does not immediately satisfy ideal rolling; first there is dissipation, then nearly ideal rolling may be reached. The strategy prevents conserving [[E_total]] during the lossy phase.

Validity limit: the surface and ball must not deform enough to dominate the energy balance if ideal energy is used.

## 5. Experiment design

Dominant variable: [[E_total]]

To compare bodies with the same [[m]] and [[R]], choose geometries with different [[I]]. If the goal is to measure [[a_cm]], use a ramp and timing. If the goal is to validate energy, measure final speed and compute [[K_total]]. The chosen method determines which instruments are needed and which uncertainty dominates.

Validity limit: uncertainties in timing, radius, and angle [[phi]] must be smaller than the difference being observed.

In all applications, the strategy prevents solving by habit. First classify the unknown; then check contact; finally choose among geometric constraint, FBD, energy, or graphical comparison. If the result contradicts the order of magnitude or the graph, review the model before tuning numbers.

The laboratory ramp also allows uncertainty estimates: a small error in [[phi]] changes the component of [[g]] and therefore [[a_cm]]. In vehicles, a change in effective radius changes the relation between [[v_cm]] and [[omega]]. In rollers, a variable load changes contact even if the motor keeps angular speed nearly fixed. In sports, the transition from sliding to rolling marks the boundary between dissipation and conservation. In experiment design, sensor choice depends on whether energy, force, or acceleration is the target. These decisions are real applications of the strategy, not decorative examples.

The cross-cutting value is documenting the decision. In a teaching lab, writing "use energy" is not enough; the solution must say that [[E_total]] is conserved because static friction performs no net dissipative work. In a tire evaluation, writing "use dynamics" is not enough; the solution must justify that the unknown is a contact force or torque. In a conveyor belt, writing "use kinematics" is not enough; the solution must check that contact imposes the same tangential speed. In a sports ball, writing "use rolling" is not enough if initial sliding is still present. In experiment design, writing "measure speed" is not enough if the real goal is to separate translational and rotational [[K_total]].

Each application can therefore close with three operational sentences: which magnitude dominates, which method answers best, and which observation would force a model change. That closing style turns the leaf into a solution tool. The student does not memorize a list of cases; they learn to recognize physical signals, choose a defensible model, and review the result when the graph or order of magnitude does not agree.

In classroom terms, this makes it possible to compare different solutions without debating preferences. An energy solution is good if it conserves [[E_total]] under clear assumptions. A dynamic solution is good if it identifies forces and [[tau]]. A kinematic solution is good if the contact constraint is justified. The shared criterion is always physical.

The same criterion works for short technical reports: record the dominant datum, chosen method, validity limit, and graphical check. With that template, a rolling application stays connected to a verifiable decision.

It also prevents mixing goals: measuring speed, estimating force, validating contact, and conserving energy are different tasks with different evidence.

That separation is the practical value of the method in real work.
