## Historical problem

Rolling was historically important because it showed that the motion of an extended body cannot be reduced to the motion of a particle. A wheel advances as a whole and, at the same time, every point rotates around the center. Mechanics needed a language that joined both readings.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty was distinguishing spin, advance, and contact. Without that separation, a slipping wheel, a locked wheel, and an ideally rolling wheel look like variants of the same thing. The key was recognizing that no-slip contact imposes a geometric constraint.

## What changed

By introducing center of mass, angular variables, and radius as a geometric translator, rolling could be formulated as a bridge between linear and angular kinematics. The equality between center advance and accumulated spin stopped being intuition and became a model condition.

## Impact on physics

The impact was large in machines, transport, and teaching. Wheels, pulleys, and rollers showed that one physical situation may require two coordinated descriptions. They also prepared the path toward rolling energy and rigid-body dynamics.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the same bridge appears in vehicle sensors, mobile robotics, contact simulations, and video analysis. Modern models may be more complex, but they still begin by asking whether contact imposes ideal rolling or whether slipping, deformation, or loss is present.

The historical continuity lies in the model question. It once applied to wheels and pulleys; today it applies to tires, robots, and simulators. The mathematical tool changed, but the physical reading remains the same: decide whether body spin and center advance describe one coupled motion.
