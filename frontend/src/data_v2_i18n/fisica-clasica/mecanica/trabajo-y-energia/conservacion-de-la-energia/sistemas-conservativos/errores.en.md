# Errors

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: believing that a conservative system means constant speed

Why it seems correct  
Students often hear conservation and immediately think nothing important is allowed to change.

Detection signal  
They claim that if [[K]] changes, then [[Em]] cannot remain conserved.

Mini-contrast example  
A body falls without friction. [[K]] rises while [[U]] drops. The process is still conservative because the total mechanical budget can remain unchanged.

Conceptual correction  
Conservation refers to total [[Em]], not to each contribution staying fixed.

### Error 2: treating [[U]] as absolute rather than baseline-dependent

Why it seems correct  
Potential-energy numbers can look objective if the reference choice is not discussed.

Detection signal  
The baseline changes between initial and final states without explicit justification.

Mini-contrast example  
If the initial state uses the floor as zero and the final state uses another point, [[Ui]] and [[Uf]] no longer support a meaningful comparison.

Conceptual correction  
[[Ui]], [[Uf]], and [[U]] are only comparable under the same baseline.

## Model errors

### Error 3: imposing ideal conservation when [[Wnc]] is already relevant

Why it seems correct  
The ideal balance is elegant, familiar, and often overused.

Detection signal  
The statement includes friction, braking, or external input but the solution still forces the ideal conservative equation alone.

Mini-contrast example  
A smooth descent may be modeled conservatively. A rough segment added afterward requires [[Wnc]].

Conceptual correction  
When non-conservative exchange controls the outcome, it must appear explicitly in the balance.

### Error 4: confusing the definition of [[Em]] with a conservation law

Why it seems correct  
The expression for [[Em]] is so common that students sometimes read it as an invariant by default.

Detection signal  
They infer conservation directly from the state definition.

Mini-contrast example  
A driven system still has [[Em]] defined, but that does not make it conservative.

Conceptual correction  
Definition and conservation must be kept separate.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 5: losing the sign relation between [[Wc]] and [[dU]]

Why it seems correct  
Both quantities refer to the same physical change, so students may expect them to share the same sign.

Detection signal  
They obtain positive [[Wc]] and positive [[dU]] in a situation where potential energy clearly drops.

Mini-contrast example  
In a downward motion with decreasing potential, [[dU]] and [[Wc]] must carry opposite signs under the usual convention.

Conceptual correction  
The conservative-work reading must remain consistent with the potential-energy change.

### Error 6: mixing states and combining [[Ki]] or [[Kf]] from different moments

Why it seems correct  
Long problems provide many numbers and students may combine them without checking whether they belong to the same state pair.

Detection signal  
[[Ki]], [[Uf]], and [[Wnc]] are taken from different phases of motion.

Mini-contrast example  
In a launch plus rebound problem, values before and after impact cannot be mixed into one unqualified balance.

Conceptual correction  
Each balance equation must refer to one system and one state interval.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 7: assuming negative [[Wnc]] always means bad data

Why it seems correct  
Once ideal conservation is learned first, any deviation may look like noise.

Detection signal  
The student discards the result instead of asking what dissipative mechanism produced it.

Mini-contrast example  
In a real braking process, negative [[Wnc]] is not noise. It is the energetic signature of dissipation.

Conceptual correction  
[[Wnc]] should be interpreted causally, not treated as an algebraic inconvenience.

### Error 8: using the conservative-system language as a universal template

Why it seems correct  
Once the ideal model is comfortable, students may try to force it everywhere.

Detection signal  
The regime changes but the model choice does not.

Mini-contrast example  
An almost lossless laboratory descent and a long dissipative descent do not deserve the same closure equation.

Conceptual correction  
The model must change when the dominant mechanism changes.

## Quick self-control rule

Before closing a problem, check four points. The same system appears in both states. [[Ui]] and [[Uf]] use the same baseline. The conclusion distinguishes ideal conservation from the case with [[Wnc]]. The sign of [[Wc]] is consistent with the sign of [[dU]]. If one fails, the issue is usually in model selection before it is in arithmetic.
