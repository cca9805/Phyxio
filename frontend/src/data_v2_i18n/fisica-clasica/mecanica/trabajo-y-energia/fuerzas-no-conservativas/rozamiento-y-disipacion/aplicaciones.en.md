# Applications

## 1. Vehicle brakes

Dominant variable: [[Pdis]].

During braking, friction converts the vehicle's mechanical energy into heat in discs, pads, tires, and road surface. Braking distance depends on total work [[Wr]], but failure risk depends on how much energy [[Edis]] is concentrated per unit time. Two braking events with the same initial energy are therefore not equivalent if one happens in a few seconds and the other over a longer segment.

The useful teaching reading links three levels: [[fr]] explains contact force, [[Wr]] explains mechanical-energy loss, and [[Pdis]] explains heating. This lets students see why the same energy balance can speak about safety, distance, and material damage.

Validity limit: the coefficient [[mu]] changes with temperature, humidity, wear, and wheel lock-up. With ABS braking, degraded tires, or wet asphalt, constant [[mu]] is only a first approximation.

## 2. Machine tools and linear guides

Dominant variable: [[Edis]].

In guides, slides, and machine beds, friction reduces efficiency and may distort parts through local heating. Energy dissipated per cycle helps compare materials, surface treatments, and lubricants. If a machine repeats thousands of strokes, a small loss per cycle becomes an important energetic and thermal load.

The balance between [[Emi]] and [[Emf]] distinguishes useful work from energy degraded in contact. This is didactically valuable because it shows that friction is not only a force that slows motion, but a mechanism that converts organized mechanical energy into less available forms.

Validity limit: when hydrodynamic lubrication is present, dry contact is no longer the main model, and the force depends on viscosity, speed, and film thickness.

## 3. Conveyor belts and handling systems

Dominant variable: [[fr]].

Friction force determines minimum motor power and accumulated energy losses. A small increase in [[mu]] or [[N]] can multiply costs when the distance [[d]] repeats over thousands of daily cycles. In industrial maintenance, this reading helps detect poor alignment, excessive load, or contaminated surfaces.

The model also supports decisions: reducing [[N]], changing materials, or controlling [[mu]] can reduce [[Edis]]. If operating time is known, [[Pdis]] translates energy loss into power demand and component heating.

Validity limit: if rollers, belt deformation, or intermittent slip are present, dry friction, rolling resistance, and internal material losses must be separated.

## 4. Sports and safety

Dominant variable: [[mu]].

The grip of shoes, tires, skis, or sports surfaces controls acceleration, braking, and fall risk. The same [[Wr]] that slows motion also dissipates energy and heats or wears the contact. A surface with low [[mu]] reduces dissipative work per meter and increases stopping distance.

This application shows why friction can be desirable or undesirable. In braking it is necessary; in a mechanical joint it may be a loss; in climbing or athletics it is a safety condition. The criterion is not that more friction is always better, but that [[mu]] must match the system's function.

Validity limit: the coefficient is not universal; it depends on material, humidity, roughness, contamination, and temperature.

## 5. Bearing and joint design

Dominant variable: [[Pdis]].

In bearings, hinges, and joints, sustained dissipated power can raise temperature even if energy per turn seems small. Design must remove the heat generated by [[Edis]] and reduce [[fr]] through lubrication or geometry. The key question is not only how much energy is lost, but whether the system can dissipate it without changing regime.

The friction balance also helps maintenance: if [[Pdis]] increases, there may be lack of lubricant, increased [[N]], or contact degradation. In class, this example shows that an energy formula can also work as a technical diagnostic tool.

Validity limit: in lubricated regimes, dry friction is replaced by a viscous or elastohydrodynamic model.

## Integrated application reading

All applications share the same causal chain: contact sets [[N]] and [[mu]], those quantities determine [[fr]], distance [[d]] accumulates [[Wr]], and the result is interpreted as [[Edis]] or as [[Pdis]] when time matters. This continuity makes the leaf a cross-context tool rather than an isolated formula.

The difference between contexts lies in the design question. In brakes the goal is stopping safely; in guides it is precision; in conveyors it is efficiency; in sport it is controlled grip; in bearings it is avoiding overheating. The same model adapts because it separates force, energy, and power.

This is also useful pedagogically: students can compare a small classroom block with a real industrial component and see that the equations keep the same structure while the physical consequences change scale. That comparison prevents rote substitution and encourages model judgment.

As an applied closing criterion, the numerical value should never stand alone. One must ask whether [[Edis]] is large for the system, whether [[Pdis]] can be removed, and whether [[mu]] will remain valid after heating. This triple check turns the calculation into physical judgment.

That final check is what makes friction and dissipation a practical engineering idea rather than a classroom-only computation.
