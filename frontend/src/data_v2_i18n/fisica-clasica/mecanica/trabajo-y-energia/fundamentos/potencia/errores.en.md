# Common Errors in Power

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Confusing total energy with power

**Why it seems correct**
Both quantities appear together in machines and energy use.

**Why it is incorrect**
[[W]] measures transferred amount; [[P]] measures rate. The same work can be done with different powers if [[t]] changes.

**Detection signal**
A system is called more powerful only because it delivers more total energy.

**Conceptual correction**
Always compare energy per unit time.

**Contrast mini-example**
Lifting the same load in 2 s requires more average power than lifting it in 20 s.

### Error 2: Believing that large force implies large power

**Why it seems correct**
Force seems to be the direct cause of work.

**Why it is incorrect**
Without motion of the point of application, [[v]] is zero and instantaneous mechanical power vanishes.

**Detection signal**
Power is computed for a wall being pushed while it does not move.

**Conceptual correction**
Check force and speed at the same point.

**Contrast mini-example**
Holding a suitcase still may be physiologically tiring, but ideal external mechanical power can be zero.

## Model errors

### Error 3: Using an average when peaks matter

**Why it seems correct**
Average power is easy to compute.

**Why it is incorrect**
An engine or brake may fail because of peaks even when the average is low.

**Detection signal**
The average result does not explain heating, saturation, or maximum demand.

**Conceptual correction**
Use instantaneous power or a time function when the process is not uniform.

**Contrast mini-example**
A sudden start can require much more power than the average over the whole trip.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 4: Omitting the angle between force and velocity

**Why it seems correct**
The simplified form is remembered as force times speed.

**Why it is incorrect**
Only the component of [[F]] parallel to [[v]] transfers energy; [[theta]] is not decorative.

**Detection signal**
The total force magnitude is used even when the force is perpendicular.

**Conceptual correction**
Include the projection or use components.

**Contrast mini-example**
An ideal centripetal force can change direction without instantaneous mechanical power.

### Error 5: Mixing intervals

**Why it seems correct**
The data seem to belong to the same exercise.

**Why it is incorrect**
If [[W]] belongs to one phase and [[t]] to another, average power describes no real process.

**Detection signal**
The time interval does not match the stage where work was computed.

**Conceptual correction**
Define system, start, and end before dividing.

**Contrast mini-example**
The work of acceleration should not be divided by the total time of a long trip.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 6: Ignoring the sign of power

**Why it seems correct**
Catalogs often report only positive power ratings.

**Why it is incorrect**
In mechanics, the sign indicates delivery or removal of energy.

**Detection signal**
Braking and engine action are described with the same sign without explanation.

**Conceptual correction**
State whether the force gives energy to the system or removes it.

**Contrast mini-example**
The engine's power on the car may be positive; the brake's power on the car is negative.

## Quick self-control rule

Before accepting a result, check: same phase for [[W]] and [[t]], same point for [[F]] and [[v]], correct [[theta]], units in watts, and reasonable scale. If one condition fails, the number may be algebraically correct and physically useless.
