# History of Power

## Historical problem

The notion of power came from a practical need: comparing machines that could perform work, but not at the same rate. In mines, mills, pumps, and steam engines, it was not enough to know how much load could be moved; it was crucial to know how much work could be delivered per unit time.

This question became central during industrialization, when engines and draft animals had to be compared operationally.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty was separating total capacity from speed of action. A machine could perform much work if given enough time, but still be useless for an urgent task. Without a magnitude like [[P]], comparison between machines mixed energy, force, and duration.

It was also difficult to distinguish static effort from mechanical work. A large force without displacement delivered no external mechanical power.

## What changed

The change was the introduction of a time ratio: work per time. This idea made machines comparable with a common unit, the watt, and with historical units such as horsepower. Power turned transfer rate into a measurable quantity.

In theoretical mechanics, the instantaneous form connected force, velocity, and the scalar product.

## Impact on physics

Power made it possible to analyze engines, brakes, dissipation, efficiency, and machines through one question: at what rate is energy transferred? In teaching, it connects work and energy with real technology.

It also supports graph interpretation and variable processes, because the slope of work versus time has direct physical meaning.

## Connection with modern physics

Today power appears in mechanics, electricity, thermodynamics, biomechanics, and energy systems. In all these fields it preserves the same central idea: transfer per unit time.

Although this leaf focuses on classical mechanics, it prepares the language for efficiency, dissipation, energy balances, and real system design.
