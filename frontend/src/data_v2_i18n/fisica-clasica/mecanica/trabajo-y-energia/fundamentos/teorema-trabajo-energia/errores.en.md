## Conceptual errors

### Error
Treating [[Wnet]] as if it were the work of one convenient force only.

Why it seems correct
Students often identify the most visible interaction, such as traction, gravity, or friction, and then let that one contribution stand for the whole process.

Detection signal
The solution discusses one force but never asks for the global external balance acting through the interval.

Mini-contrast example
A system may speed up because a motor pulls it while friction removes part of the gain. Reading only the motor work overstates the kinetic change.

Conceptual correction
Remember that [[Wnet]] is the total relevant work balance. The theorem closes only when [[Wnet]] and [[dK]] describe the same complete process.

### Error
Assuming that a modest speed change must imply a modest kinetic-energy change.

Why it seems correct
Daily intuition compares speeds almost linearly, so a moderate-looking shift from [[vi]] to [[vf]] may feel energetically unimportant.

Detection signal
The student accepts the number without checking whether the change in motion budget is actually much larger than the visual change in speed suggests.

Mini-contrast example
Doubling speed does not simply double the kinetic-energy budget. The required net work can be far larger than naive intuition predicts.

Conceptual correction
Read the process through [[Ki]], [[Kf]], and [[dK]], not only through an informal comparison of [[vi]] and [[vf]].

## Model errors

### Error
Using the theorem when the task actually asks for detailed time evolution.

Why it seems correct
The theorem is so efficient that students try to use it even when the problem asks for instant-by-instant behavior.

Detection signal
The calculation produces [[Wnet]] and [[dK]] but still does not answer the actual question, such as instantaneous force behavior or detailed timing.

Mini-contrast example
The theorem may tell how much kinetic budget is lost during braking, but not the exact time profile of the braking force.

Conceptual correction
Check first whether the question is global between states or local in time. If it is local, this leaf must be combined with another model.

### Error
Changing the system definition in the middle of the solution.

Why it seems correct
Multi-body problems invite students to shift attention from one object to another without noticing that the theorem requires a fixed boundary.

Detection signal
[[Ki]] and [[Kf]] are computed for one object while [[Wnet]] is interpreted for a different one.

Mini-contrast example
For a cart pulling a load, the balance is different if the system is the cart alone or the cart plus load.

Conceptual correction
Fix the system at the start and keep [[Ki]], [[Kf]], [[dK]], and [[Wnet]] attached to that same system all the way through.

## Mathematical errors

### Error
Losing the sign of [[dK]] or [[Wnet]].

Why it seems correct
Students often work with absolute values out of habit and postpone sign interpretation until the end.

Detection signal
The answer reports only positive magnitudes and never clarifies whether the process gained or lost kinetic budget.

Mini-contrast example
In braking, [[vf]] smaller than [[vi]] forces [[dK]] to be negative and therefore [[Wnet]] to be negative as well.

Conceptual correction
Treat sign as core physical information, not as decorative algebra.

### Error
Combining inconsistent units and still trusting the final number.

Why it seems correct
The arithmetic can proceed formally even when the data no longer correspond to a coherent mechanical scale.

Detection signal
The result has an absurd order of magnitude or cannot be interpreted as work or kinetic-energy change.

Mini-contrast example
Using [[m]] in grams together with speed in meters per second produces a number, but not a trustworthy work balance for the leaf.

Conceptual correction
Check that mass, speed, and energetic outputs are expressed in mutually consistent units before interpreting the result.

## Interpretation errors

### Error
Ending the exercise with a number and no causal reading.

Why it seems correct
Many students are trained to stop once a numerical value appears, as if interpretation were optional commentary.

Detection signal
The answer gives [[Wnet]] or [[dK]] but never explains what interaction balance added or removed kinetic budget from the system.

Mini-contrast example
Saying that [[Wnet]] has a certain value is not enough. One must say whether the surroundings delivered energy to the motion or extracted it.

Conceptual correction
Always end with a cause-and-effect sentence linking the change from [[Ki]] to [[Kf]] with the global work balance.

## Quick self-control rule

1. Fix the system boundary.
2. Decide whether the task is global between states or local in time.
3. Predict the expected sign of [[dK]] and [[Wnet]].
4. Check the order of magnitude of the result.
5. Finish with one sentence that links net work and kinetic-state change.
