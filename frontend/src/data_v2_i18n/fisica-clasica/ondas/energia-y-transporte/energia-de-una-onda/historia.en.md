## Historical problem

During the 19th century, physicists understood that waves transported momentum (experimentally demonstrated with pressure waves on obstacles), but the exact nature of the energy they carried remained elusive. The central question was: where does energy reside in a mechanical wave, and how does it flow through the medium without dragging matter with it?

Attempts to directly apply particle mechanics formulas to infinitesimal elements of the medium led to contradictions. If each element simply oscillated around its equilibrium position, how could energy travel from the source to a distant receiver be explained? The confusion between local medium motion and global energy transport was profound.

The problem intensified with Maxwell's discovery of electromagnetic waves in 1865. In vacuum, without any material medium, how could energy propagate? This question challenged the dominant mechanistic paradigms that assumed all waves required a material medium (the luminiferous ether) to exist.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The Newtonian view of the world privileged material particles as exclusive carriers of energy and momentum. Waves were seen as secondary phenomena, collective properties of material media, not autonomous physical entities with their own conserved magnitudes.

The technical difficulty resided in correctly defining energy density in a continuous medium. Early attempts by Euler and Lagrange treating fluids as sets of interacting particles did not provide a clear vision of how energy was stored and flowed in the perturbation field itself, independent of mass transport.

Additionally, the emerging statistical mechanics of the 19th century, developed by Boltzmann, considered energy as a property of discrete particle systems. Extending this concept to continuous fields where there were no individually identifiable particles required a significant conceptual leap.

Energy conservation, formulated by Helmholtz in 1847, needed to be reconciled with wave propagation. If energy was globally conserved, how were local variations of energy density balanced? No local conservation principle yet existed that would allow defining energy densities and fluxes.

## What changed

Lord Rayleigh (John William Strutt) revolutionized the field in 1877 with his treatise "The Theory of Sound", where he rigorously defined the kinetic and potential energy of waves in strings, membranes, and fluids. He introduced the concept of wave energy density and established that the total energy in a wave is the sum of kinetic and potential contributions appropriately averaged.

The key conceptual advance was the distinction between local velocity of medium elements and wave propagation velocity. Rayleigh demonstrated that energy travels at the group velocity (for wave packets), not at phase velocity nor at the maximum oscillation velocity of particles.

Poynting's 1884 treatise on electromagnetic energy flow (the Poynting vector) extended these concepts to fields without material medium. Maxwell had predicted that E and B fields carried energy; Poynting provided the quantitative expression of that flow, demonstrating that energy traveled from sources toward sinks through empty space at the speed of light.

## Impact on physics

Understanding wave energy transport enabled the development of quantum field theory. Planck, when analyzing the energy of stationary modes in cavities (quantized harmonic oscillators), was able to derive the black body radiation law in 1900. Einstein extended this analysis in 1905 to explain the photoelectric effect, postulating that electromagnetic energy is quantized in packets (photons) whose energy is proportional to frequency through Planck's constant.

In fluid mechanics, Reynolds' work in the 1890s on turbulent energy transport established the foundations for understanding how waves in fluids transport not only mechanical energy but also momentum and scalar properties (temperature, concentration). This was fundamental for meteorology and physical oceanography.

The development of architectural acoustics by Sabine at the beginning of the 20th century directly applied these principles to design spaces with controlled reverberation, optimizing sound energy transport from sources to audiences.

In particle physics, the modern description of quantum fields (quantum electrodynamics, quantum chromodynamics) treats all particles as excitations of underlying fields. The energy and momentum of these excitations are transported exactly like classical waves, but with additional quantization.

## Connection with modern physics

Today, the concepts of energy density and wave flow are fundamental in technologies that define the digital era. Optical fibers, which transport information encoded in infrared light pulses, confine electromagnetic energy densities of the order of microwatts per square micrometer, guiding this energy through thousands of kilometers with minimal losses.

In medicine, shock wave therapy (lithotripsy) and focused ultrasound ablation directly apply the principles of concentration and transport of wave energy for non-invasive treatments. Ultrasonic energy is selectively deposited in target tissues, demonstrating the precise control of energy flow that modern physics enables.

Wave renewable energy systems, still in commercial development, represent the return to historical challenges of efficiently capturing energy transported by ocean waves. The specific power calculations and Budal limits described in this leaf are direct descendants of the energy analyses developed by Rayleigh over a century ago.

In fundamental physics, the detection of gravitational waves by LIGO (2015) confirmed that even spacetime perturbations transport energy. Although the exact interpretation of "gravitational energy density" remains nuanced in general relativity, the energy flow transported by gravitational waves is measurable and has opened a new observational window on the universe.
