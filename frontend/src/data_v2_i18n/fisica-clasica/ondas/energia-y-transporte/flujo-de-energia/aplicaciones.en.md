# Applications

## 1. Power transmission through vibrating strings

In certain industrial mechanical systems, especially in textile and materials processing machinery, vibrating strings or belts are used to transmit mechanical power between separated components. The energy flux through the string determines how much power actually reaches the receiver.

Optimal design requires maximizing flux [[densidad_de_flujo_de_energia]] while maintaining mechanical integrity. This is achieved by selecting materials with high linear density [[mu]] and tensions that provide high velocities [[velocidad_de_propagacion]] without risk of rupture. [[amplitud]] is controlled through initial excitation, remembering that flux depends quadratically on it.

Dominant variable: the string tension, which simultaneously determines the propagation velocity and the amplitude limit before rupture.

Validity limit: when the amplitude exceeds 10% of the wavelength, nonlinear effects appear that redistribute flux toward higher harmonics, reducing transmission efficiency at the fundamental frequency.

## 2. Sound intensity evaluation in auditoriums

In architectural acoustics, energy flux (called "sound intensity" I in this context) determines the quality of sound perception in different positions in the auditorium. Acoustic designers calculate and modify the energy flux through reflectors, diffusers, and absorbers to achieve uniform distributions.

Sound intensity is related to acoustic pressure through a quadratic law analogous to the string flux model. This expression allows quantifying how much acoustic power reaches each listener.

Design seeks to avoid zones where flux is excessive (unpleasant echo) or insufficient (acoustic shadows). The geometry of the room modifies the direction of flux through reflections, creating complex flux fields that the designer must predict.

Dominant variable: the acoustic pressure at the source (voice, instrument, loudspeaker), which quadratically determines the sound intensity.

Validity limit: at frequencies above 10 kHz, air behaves as a significantly absorbing medium, attenuating flux with distance more rapidly than predicted by the ideal non-dissipative model.

## 3. Solar irradiance and photovoltaic capture

Although electromagnetic waves require vector treatment through the Poynting vector, the concept of energy flux is identical: power per unit area perpendicular to propagation. Solar irradiance (solar constant ≈ 1361 W/m² in space, ≈ 1000 W/m² at Earth's surface) is the energy flux of solar radiation.

Photovoltaic panels are designed to intercept maximum area perpendicular to the solar flux. Panel orientation determines the perpendicular component of flux through a cosine projection law, where the angle is measured between the panel normal and the solar direction.

Solar system engineers calculate expected flux throughout the day (variation of solar angle) and throughout the year (variation of declination) to size installations. The total power generated equals the incident solar flux multiplied by panel area and by conversion efficiency (typically 15-22% for crystalline silicon).

Dominant variable: the solar incidence angle, which modulates effective flux from maximum (perpendicular incidence) to zero (grazing incidence).

Validity limit: on cloudy days, radiation becomes diffuse (coming from all directions) and the concept of directional flux loses precision, requiring hemispherical radiance models.

## 4. Medical focused ultrasound therapy

HIFU (High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound) systems concentrate ultrasonic energy fluxes of 10⁴ to 10⁸ W/m² in foci of millimeters, heating biological tissue to lethal temperatures (above 56°C) for non-invasive tumor ablation.

The transducer design creates convergent flux geometry through emitter arrays that interfere constructively at the focal point. The flux at the focus results from the coherent superposition of multiple beams, each transporting energy according to flux relations in fluid media.

Treatment planning calculates exactly how much flux is required to reach therapeutic temperature in the tumor without exceeding the cavitation flux (approximately 10⁹ W/m²), which would generate harmful vapor bubbles and scatter the energy.

Dominant variable: the acoustic pressure amplitude at the focus, which quadratically determines the deposited energy flux in a way analogous to the string flux model.

Validity limit: fluxes above 10⁹ W/m² induce cavitation that drastically modifies the acoustic properties of the medium, invalidating predictions of the linear acoustic model.

## 5. Design of vibration isolation systems

In mechanical engineering, especially in precision machinery and civil construction, systems are designed that minimize the flux of vibratory energy transmitted from vibration sources (motors, traffic, machinery) to sensitive structures.

The operating principle is opposite to maximizing flux: elements are sought that disperse, dissipate, or reflect the flux of mechanical energy. Vibration isolations use materials with high internal dissipation that convert the flux of mechanical energy into heat, exponentially reducing the energy that crosses the isolation.

The design calculates the incident flux from the source and sizes the isolation so that transmitted flux remains below acceptable thresholds for the application (typically 1% of incident flux for precision instrumentation).

Dominant variable: the mechanical impedance of the isolating material, which determines how much flux is transmitted (matched impedance) versus how much is reflected (mismatched impedance).

Validity limit: for very low frequencies (below 10 Hz for typical mechanical systems), the wavelength becomes comparable to the system dimensions, invalidating one-dimensional transmission models and requiring complete modal analysis of the structure.
