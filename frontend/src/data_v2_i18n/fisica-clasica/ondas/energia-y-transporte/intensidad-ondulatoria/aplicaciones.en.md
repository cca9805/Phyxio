# Applications

## 1. Design of sound reinforcement and PA systems

In professional audio installations (concerts, conferences, sporting events), sound engineers must calculate the distribution of acoustic intensity to ensure that all attendees receive adequate sound level without exceeding safety limits. The inverse square law determines how many loudspeakers are required and where to position them.

A sound reinforcement system for an outdoor concert must provide intensities of at least 0.1 W/m² (100 dB) in the front audience area, while in nearby residential areas the intensity must be kept below 0.001 W/m² (90 dB) to comply with urban noise regulations. By calculating the power of each loudspeaker and its distance to these critical zones, engineers design directional arrays that concentrate energy toward the audience while minimizing dispersion toward neighborhoods.

Dominant variable: the directivity of loudspeakers and their radiation pattern, which allows concentrating intensity in desired directions.

Validity limit: at distances greater than 500 meters, atmospheric absorption reduces intensity additionally to the geometric prediction, which must be considered in the planning of massive events.

## 2. Environmental noise measurement and regulations

Environmental and public health authorities use measurements of acoustic intensity (expressed in decibels) to establish noise zones, issue activity licenses, and protect the health of workers and residents. Occupational exposure to intensities above 85 dB (approximately 3×10⁻⁴ W/m²) for 8 hours requires hearing protection according to international regulations (OSHA, EU-OSHA).

Calibrated sound level meters measure instantaneous acoustic pressure and calculate the time-averaged equivalent intensity through the pressure-intensity relation. Noise maps represent acoustic intensity in different zones of a city, identifying "noise islands" where intensity exceeds healthy limits (generally 70 dB during the day, 55 dB during the night).

Acoustic engineers use propagation models based on the inverse square law modified by atmospheric absorption to predict the acoustic impact of new infrastructures (highways, airports, industrial plants).

Dominant variable: the equivalent level of acoustic intensity (Leq) measured in dB, which integrates temporal exposure.

Validity limit: in urban environments with multiple reflections on buildings, intensity does not follow the pure inverse square law; complex propagation models considering reverberation must be used.

## 3. Solar irradiance and photovoltaic panels

Solar irradiance (intensity of electromagnetic radiation from the Sun) at Earth's surface is approximately 1000 W/m² under clear sky conditions (known as "standard sun" AM1.5). This intensity determines the maximum power that photovoltaic panels can generate.

Solar installation designers calculate the available intensity throughout the year considering the geometry of solar movement and panel inclination. The generated power results from the product of incident irradiance by panel area and by conversion efficiency (typically 15-20% for crystalline silicon).

In large photovoltaic solar plants, solar intensity is considered constant over extensions of several kilometers (the distance to the Sun is so large that variation due to the inverse square is negligible on Earth's surface). However, mutual shading between adjacent panels locally reduces the effective intensity received, requiring careful spacing.

Dominant variable: the angle of incidence of solar radiation on the panel, which determines the perpendicular component of intensity according to the cosine law.

Validity limit: on cloudy days, radiation becomes diffuse (coming from the entire celestial hemisphere) and perpendicular direct intensity loses meaning; total hemispherical irradiance must be used.

## 4. Radiometric safety and radiation protection

In medical (radiology, radiation oncology) and industrial (non-destructive radiography) facilities, the intensity of ionizing radiation (measured in W/m² or equivalent in sieverts for biological dose) determines exclusion zones and shielding requirements. The inverse square law is fundamental for establishing safety perimeters around radioactive sources.

For a gamma radiation source of 1 curie emitting approximately 3.7×10¹⁰ disintegrations per second, the radiation intensity at 1 meter can reach dangerous levels. Lead or concrete shielding additionally reduces transmitted intensity according to exponential attenuation laws, complementing geometric decrease.

Radiological protection professionals calculate the "safety distance" for different exposure times, ensuring that the accumulated dose (proportional to intensity × time) remains below annual limits (typically 20 mSv for workers, 1 mSv for the general public).

Dominant variable: the exposure time constant, which together with intensity determines the total received dose.

Validity limit: for charged particle radiation (alpha, beta), the range in air is limited and the inverse square law does not apply; attenuation is almost total over short distances by ionization of the medium.

## 5. Telecommunications and antenna design

In wireless communication systems (radio, television, mobile telephony, WiFi), the intensity of the received electromagnetic signal determines communication quality and data transfer rate. Telecommunications engineers calculate the "field intensity" (related to power intensity) to design antenna coverage and avoid interference.

The effective radiated power (ERP) of an antenna is distributed over the spherical area of the electromagnetic wave, decreasing with distance according to the inverse square law. Mobile phone operators position repeater antennas to maintain the intensity received by phones within the operational range (typically between 10⁻⁶ and 10⁻¹⁰ W/m², equivalent to -30 dBm to -70 dBm).

Directional antennas (parabolic, Yagi, sectorial panels) concentrate intensity in reduced solid angles, multiplying the intensity on the main axis by directivity factors that can reach 1000× or more compared to an isotropic source.

Dominant variable: the frequency of the signal, which determines both atmospheric attenuation and diffraction around obstacles, complementing geometric attenuation.

Validity limit: in urban environments with multiple paths (multipath), the received intensity fluctuates by constructive and destructive interference between direct and reflected waves, deviating significantly from the prediction of the free space model.
