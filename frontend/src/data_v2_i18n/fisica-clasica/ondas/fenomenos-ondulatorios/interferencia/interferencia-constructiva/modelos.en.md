# Models of constructive interference

## Ideal model

The ideal model describes two coherent harmonic waves reaching the same point after traveling comparable paths. The physical reading has two steps: first the phased disturbances are added, and only then the average intensity [[I_resultante]] is interpreted. Maximum reinforcement appears when the path difference [[Delta_r]] corresponds to whole cycles of [[lambda]], so the phase difference [[delta_phi]] becomes compatible with in-phase arrival again.

In this leaf, the ideal model is not any casual crossing of waves. It is a controlled situation where the sources preserve a stable phase relation, the frequencies match, and the detector observes long enough to measure an average intensity without averaging away the pattern through random fluctuations.

## Hypotheses

- The waves remain coherent during the measurement.
- The waves have the same frequency and compatible polarization when light is involved.
- The medium is linear, so superposition applies without significant distortion.
- The individual intensities [[I1]] and [[I2]] are stable in the observed region.
- The path difference [[Delta_r]] can be represented by a defined geometry or by an equivalent optical path.
- The order [[m_orden]] is interpreted as an integer when well-defined constructive maxima are sought.

## Quantitative validity domain

The model is reasonable if coherence is maintained for more than 10 wave periods and if the variation of [[Delta_r]] across the detector is smaller than a fraction of [[lambda]]. In bench optics, the relevant order of magnitude is often micrometric or nanometric; in classroom acoustics, it can be centimetric or metric.

If the source has a broad spectral width, the pattern remains sharp only when the path difference is below the coherence length. If the detector averages many positions or many phases, the measured maximum is reduced even when the local geometric condition is constructive.

## Model failure signals

A clear failure signal is predicting stable fringes from independent incoherent sources. Another signal appears when [[I_resultante]] is negative or when the result is interpreted as net energy creation. The model also fails if a purely geometric path difference is applied in media with different refractive properties without considering accumulated phase.

In real sound, the model starts failing if wall reflections add many uncontrolled paths. In light, it fails if polarizations are incompatible or if the source is too extended to maintain contrast.

## Extended or alternative model

It is useful to switch to the extended model when fringes lose contrast, when more than two waves contribute, or when the medium changes phase non-uniformly. The extended model may use temporal coherence, spatial coherence, optical path, fringe visibility, and the sum of multiple contributions.

In optics, the natural change is from two ideal waves to interference with optical path and finite contrast. In acoustics, the natural change is from two point sources to a sound field with reflections, absorption, and position-dependent phase.

## Operational comparison

Use the ideal model if the problem asks for maxima from [[Delta_r]], [[lambda]], and [[m_orden]], or if it gives [[I1]], [[I2]], and [[delta_phi]] to estimate [[I_resultante]]. Use the extended model if the statement mentions spectral width, polarization, source size, temporal stability, medium index, reflections, or fringe visibility.

The operational decision is direct: if the relative phase is controlled, the ideal model predicts the maximum; if the relative phase degrades or mixes with other paths, the extended model explains why the real maximum is weaker or less sharp.
