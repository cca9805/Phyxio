## Ideal model

The ideal model treats the medium as **linear, homogeneous, and stable**. Each wave produces its own disturbance and the observable state is obtained by adding those disturbances at the same point and instant. Superposition does not alter wave identity: after crossing, each wave continues propagating as if the other had not permanently modified the medium.

The central simplification is separating cause from local reading. [[y_1]] and [[y_2]] are individual contributions, while [[y_resultante]] is what a sensor placed at the crossing point measures. Energy or intensity is interpreted later, using [[A_resultante]] and [[I_relativa]] when appropriate.

## Hypotheses

- The medium is linear: if a small disturbance is doubled, the medium response also doubles; if violated, distortions appear.
- Waves are evaluated at the same point and instant; otherwise the sum mixes incompatible physical states.
- Amplitudes are small compared with the system scale; otherwise tension, pressure, or effective index may change.
- Waves are not significantly absorbed during crossing; with strong absorption, the local sum does not describe the whole evolution.

## Quantitative validity domain

The model is reasonable when amplitude is less than 5 % of the dominant geometric scale of the system. On a 1 m string, millimetre displacements usually behave linearly. In ordinary acoustics, sound pressures small compared with ambient pressure keep the linear regime.

As an order of magnitude rule, if the disturbance visibly changes medium properties, simple superposition must be abandoned. An amplitude 10 % larger than the linearity scale already requires checking nonlinear effects.

## Model failure signals

A clear failure signal is that two pulses do not recover their shape after crossing. If they remain deformed, delayed, or contain new harmonics, the medium has introduced nonlinear interaction or significant dissipation.

Another signal is that the measured result does not change proportionally when one source is doubled. If doubling [[A_1]] does not double the expected amplitude contribution, the response is no longer linear and the direct sum formula loses predictive value.

## Extended or alternative model

When to switch to the extended model: if amplitudes are large, if the medium changes during propagation, or if new harmonics appear. In those cases, nonlinear equations, dispersive models, or numerical simulation are used.

The transition to the extended model does not remove the idea of addition, but makes it local and approximate. Decomposing a signal into modes can remain useful, although those modes no longer evolve fully independently.

## Operational comparison

| Criterion | Linear superposition model | Extended model |
|---|---|---|
| Precision | High for small amplitudes | Better for large amplitudes |
| Complexity | Low, direct algebraic sum | High, requires nonlinear equations |
| Graph reading | Individual curves and sum | Coupled or distorted evolution |
| Limit | Does not predict shocks or saturation | Can include shock, absorption, or dispersion |
