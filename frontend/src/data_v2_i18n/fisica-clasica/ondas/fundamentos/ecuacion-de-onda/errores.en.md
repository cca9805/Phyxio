## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: believing that the equation describes only sinusoidal shapes

**Why it seems correct:** many courses introduce a harmonic wave first, so the mind associates the wave equation with a smooth periodic curve.

**Why it is incorrect:** the equation connects spatial curvature with local temporal change. A triangular shape, a short pulse, or a superposition of pulses can also obey it if the medium satisfies the model assumptions.

**Detection signal:** the student decides whether a wave is present only by checking for a sine curve.

**Correction:** first ask whether the disturbance propagates with little change of shape and with a well-defined speed [[v]].

**Contrast mini-example:** a pulse on a taut string is not periodic, but its profile travels and allows the same local law to be read.

### Error 2: interpreting [[y]] as net transport displacement

**Why it seems correct:** the graph shows a disturbance moving forward, and it may look as if every material point travels with it.

**Why it is incorrect:** [[y]] measures the local disturbance of the medium. Energy and information propagate; the material point oscillates around its equilibrium position.

**Detection signal:** someone claims that a particle of the string travels along the whole pulse length.

**Correction:** separate the spatial coordinate [[x]] from the disturbance [[y]], and read each point's motion through time [[t]].

**Contrast mini-example:** an ink mark on the string moves up and down as the pulse passes, but it does not follow the crest to the end.

## Model errors

### Error 3: using a single speed in a dispersive medium

**Why it seems correct:** the ideal equation introduces a constant [[v]] and makes calculations clean.

**Why it is incorrect:** in real dispersive media, different frequency components may travel at different speeds. Then the pulse changes shape.

**Detection signal:** a narrow packet spreads in the experiment, but the calculation treats it as rigid.

**Correction:** check whether the speed depends on [[omega]] or [[k]] before using the ideal model.

**Contrast mini-example:** a low-amplitude sound wave in ordinary air fits the model well; a signal in a complex guide may require an extended model.

### Error 4: ignoring damping and boundaries

**Why it seems correct:** the ideal equation is written for a uniform and lossless medium.

**Why it is incorrect:** friction, absorption, fixed ends, and impedance changes modify the real evolution. The basic equation describes the region where those influences are small.

**Detection signal:** the amplitude [[A]] clearly decreases, yet the reasoning still assumes a conserved wave.

**Correction:** restrict the time and space interval where relative loss is small, or add damping terms and boundary conditions.

**Contrast mini-example:** a short string with fixed ends forms standing modes; it is not interpreted as an indefinitely free pulse.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 5: confusing slope with curvature

**Why it seems correct:** the slope seems to show where the shape changes.

**Why it is incorrect:** the wave equation does not compare the disturbance with its slope, but with its curvature. A tilted straight line has slope, but no curvature.

**Detection signal:** temporal acceleration is expected in a straight part of the profile.

**Correction:** locate regions where the graph bends. There the spatial curvature controls the temporal change.

**Contrast mini-example:** near a rounded crest there is curvature; in an almost linear segment the local contribution is much smaller.

### Error 6: treating [[omega]] and [[k]] as independent quantities

**Why it seems correct:** they appear as separate symbols in the harmonic solution.

**Why it is incorrect:** for the ideal medium, the relation between angular frequency and wave number fixes the phase speed [[v]]. Changing one without reviewing the other changes the physical wave.

**Detection signal:** a frequency and a wavelength are chosen arbitrarily without checking the resulting speed.

**Correction:** after choosing the medium, verify that the ratio between [[omega]] and [[k]] matches the compatible speed.

**Contrast mini-example:** on a given string, doubling [[k]] requires doubling [[omega]] if the same [[v]] is to be preserved.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 7: reading the equation as a global recipe, not a local law

**Why it seems correct:** the formula is presented in one compact line.

**Why it is incorrect:** the law acts point by point. Each location of the medium responds to the curvature of the disturbance in its immediate neighborhood.

**Detection signal:** the whole profile is explained using only the maximum value of [[A]].

**Correction:** interpret the graph as a collection of local responses connected by the medium.

**Contrast mini-example:** two profiles with the same amplitude can evolve differently if their curvatures are different.

## Quick self-control rule

Before solving, check four questions. Does [[y]] represent a local disturbance rather than permanent transport? Does the medium allow an almost constant speed [[v]]? Does the shape being studied have relevant spatial curvature? Are [[omega]] and [[k]] compatible with the speed of the medium?

If any answer fails, the problem should not be attacked as a direct application of the ideal equation. The model must be reviewed, the validity domain must be restricted, or the description must include losses, dispersion, or boundaries.
