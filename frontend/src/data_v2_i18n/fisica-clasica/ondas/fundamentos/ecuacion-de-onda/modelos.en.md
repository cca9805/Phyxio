## Ideal model

The ideal model is the one-dimensional linear wave equation. It describes a disturbance [[y]] that depends on [[x]] and [[t]], with temporal acceleration tied to spatial curvature through [[v]].

The central simplification is that the medium is uniform and does not change its properties during propagation. The mathematical form of the law is preserved, strong losses are ignored, and nonlinear energy exchange with the medium is not included.

## Hypotheses

- Linear medium: if violated, the shape may change depending on amplitude [[A]].
- Uniform medium: if violated, [[v]] may vary with position.
- Small disturbance: if violated, the simple equation no longer represents the real response.
- One-dimensional propagation: if violated, additional spatial coordinates are required.

These hypotheses are not decorative. They are the conditions that allow [[k]], [[omega]], and [[v]] to be interpreted as stable parameters of a harmonic solution.

## Quantitative validity domain

As a practical criterion, the model is reasonable when amplitude is below 10 % of the characteristic spatial scale and when variations in [[v]] remain below 5 % in the observed segment. On a classroom string, the order of magnitude of [[v]] usually ranges from 1 m/s to 100 m/s.

The model also requires the shape to be measured with enough resolution. If barely one cycle is recognizable, [[k]] and [[omega]] may be poor estimates even though propagation remains useful.

## Model failure signals

An observable failure signal is that the wave broadens while advancing. Another one is that amplitude falls systematically although attenuation is not included in the model. Failure also appears if the calculated speed changes strongly between equivalent segments.

In graphs, the problem appears when the curve stops preserving shape, when crests do not keep regular spacing, or when phase does not advance uniformly.

## Extended or alternative model

An extended model may include dispersion, attenuation, reflections, external sources, or several spatial dimensions. The concrete difference is that the simple relation between curvature and temporal change no longer closes by itself.

When to switch to the extended model: if [[v]] depends on frequency, if amplitude decreases with distance, if echoes appear, or if geometry requires more than one spatial coordinate.

## Operational comparison

| Criterion | Ideal model | Extended model |
|---|---|---|
| Medium | Uniform and linear | May vary or dissipate |
| Main reading | Curvature-change balance | Balance with corrections |
| Key parameters | [[v]], [[k]], [[omega]] | Variable speed, losses, or modes |
| Risk | Oversimplifying | Adding complexity without data |

Use the ideal model to introduce the law and verify compatibility. Use the extended model when real data show deformation, loss, or clear frequency dependence.
