## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: confusing wavelength with amplitude

**Why it seems correct:** a wave drawing shows height and spacing at the same time.

**Why it is incorrect:** [[lambda]] is measured horizontally between equivalent states; amplitude measures vertical disturbance size.

**Detection signal:** measurement is taken from equilibrium line to a crest.

**Correction:** look for two consecutive crests or two equivalent cycle points.

**Contrast mini-example:** a low wave may have widely separated crests.

This error often appears when a wave is drawn on paper. The vertical and horizontal dimensions are in the same figure, but they answer different physical questions.

### Error 2: measuring between non-equivalent states

**Why it seems correct:** any distance on the graph looks like spatial separation.

**Why it is incorrect:** only equivalent states represent one complete repetition.

**Detection signal:** measurement is taken from a crest to an equilibrium crossing.

**Correction:** fix the phase state before measuring.

**Contrast mini-example:** crest to trough is half a repetition, not a full length.

The way to avoid it is to name the chosen state before measuring: crest to crest, trough to trough, or upward crossing to upward crossing.

## Model errors

### Error 3: using one wavelength for an isolated pulse

**Why it seems correct:** the pulse occupies a spatial width.

**Why it is incorrect:** pulse width is not necessarily [[lambda]].

**Detection signal:** there are no repeated crests or periodic pattern.

**Correction:** speak about width, dominant component, or spectrum.

**Contrast mini-example:** a sinusoidal wave repeats; a single hit does not.

In real pulses, a dominant spatial scale may exist, but it must be justified through component analysis and not by direct reading of non-existing crests.

### Error 4: ignoring that medium speed matters

**Why it seems correct:** frequency and wavelength are often presented together.

**Why it is incorrect:** [[f]] alone does not determine [[lambda]] unless [[v]] is known.

**Detection signal:** wavelength is calculated from frequency without mentioning the medium.

**Correction:** identify propagation speed before using the relation.

**Contrast mini-example:** the same frequency has different wavelength in air and in water.

Speed is the bridge between time and space. Without that bridge, frequency only informs the temporal rhythm of the source.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 5: counting crests instead of intervals

**Why it seems correct:** crests are visible and easy to count.

**Why it is incorrect:** wavelength lies between crests, not in the crests themselves.

**Detection signal:** three crests are treated as three intervals.

**Correction:** the number of intervals between consecutive crests is one less than the number of crests.

**Contrast mini-example:** five consecutive crests contain four separations.

This error changes the result systematically. The fewer crests are used, the more serious the difference between counting visible points and counting intervals becomes.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 6: believing that larger wavelength always means larger speed

**Why it seems correct:** a larger distance seems to imply faster advance.

**Why it is incorrect:** speed also depends on frequency.

**Detection signal:** [[lambda]] is compared without looking at [[f]].

**Correction:** interpret [[lambda]] together with temporal rhythm and medium.

**Contrast mini-example:** a long and slow wave may have moderate speed.

## Quick self-control rule

Before solving, verify that you are measuring horizontally between equivalent states, that you count intervals and not only crests, and that you do not use [[f]] to obtain [[lambda]] without knowing [[v]].
