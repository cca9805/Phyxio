## Historical problem

The historical problem was to measure the spatial scale of repeated phenomena. In water, sound, and light, patterns changed in space, but a common quantity was needed to compare separations between equivalent states.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Before amplitude and wavelength were clearly distinguished, wave drawings could mislead. Visible height and horizontal spacing appeared together, although they represented different physical properties.

It was also difficult to connect a spatial measurement with a temporal one. Frequency was counted in time, while [[lambda]] was measured in space. The relation depended on medium speed.

## What changed

Wave formulation treated [[lambda]] as spatial period and [[T]] as temporal period. Wavenumber [[k]] added a reading of phase per distance, useful for comparing waves of very different scales.

Measuring several crests and dividing by complete intervals turned a graphical observation into a reproducible quantity. This was essential for laboratories, acoustics, and optics.

## Impact on physics

Wavelength explained interference, diffraction, resonance, and standing modes. It also connected experimental geometry with observable results: maxima, minima, nodes, and fringes.

In technology, the same idea later guided antennas, optical instruments, and signal analysis. The spatial scale of the wave stopped being only a drawing and became a design parameter.

## Connection with modern physics

In modern physics, wavelength appears in light, matter, spectroscopy, and quantum mechanics. Although models change, the classical idea remains: spatial separation between equivalent states reveals deep physical structure.

The relation between wavelength and experimental scale remains central. When wavelength is comparable to obstacle size, diffraction appears; when it is much smaller, a geometric reading dominates.
