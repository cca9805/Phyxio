## Ideal model

The ideal model describes a periodic wave with constant wavelength. The profile repeats in space, the medium is uniform, and the separation between equivalent states is preserved as the wave advances.

In that context, [[lambda]], [[k]], [[f]], and [[v]] form a coherent description of the pattern.

## Hypotheses

The first hypothesis is that recognizable equivalent states exist, such as consecutive crests or equivalent crossings. The second is that the wave keeps its shape during measurement. The third is that the speed [[v]] belongs to the same medium where [[lambda]] is measured.

Correct counting of spatial intervals [[n]] is also required when direct measurement is used.

## Quantitative validity domain

The model works well if several consecutive separations differ by less than 5 %. Measuring three or more complete intervals improves the estimate because location error is distributed.

On classroom strings, wavelengths between centimetres and metres are reasonable. In audible sound in air, the scale may range from centimetres to several metres, depending on frequency.

## Model failure signals

The model fails if crests do not keep their spacing, if the medium changes abruptly, or if the observed pattern is a complex superposition. It also fails if measurement is made between points that do not share phase state.

A clear failure signal is obtaining very different wavelengths when choosing different pairs of consecutive crests.

## Extended or alternative model

When to switch to the extended model: if there are short pulses, dispersion, or several frequencies, describe the signal through components, envelope, or spectrum. Each component may have its own wavelength.

In standing waves, the reading adapts to nodes and antinodes, because some visible separations represent half a wavelength rather than a full one.

## Operational comparison

The ideal model works for clean harmonic waves and crest-to-crest measurements. The extended model is needed when the pattern does not repeat uniquely or when a boundary modifies the spatial form.

Operationally, start by measuring [[Delta_x]] between equivalent states and counting [[n]]. If the result changes strongly when repeated, review the model before calculating [[k]] or [[v]].

The practical comparison is clear: the ideal model gives one representative [[lambda]]; the extended model accepts that spatial scale may depend on component, position, or boundary conditions. Stating which one is used avoids mixing a graph reading with a general property of the medium.

In a report, the measurement method should also be stated. A value obtained from crest spacing does not carry the same assumptions as a value inferred from [[v]] and [[f]], even when both should agree in the ideal model.
