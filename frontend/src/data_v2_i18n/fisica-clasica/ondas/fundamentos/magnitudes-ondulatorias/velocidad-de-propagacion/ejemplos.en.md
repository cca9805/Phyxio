# Exam-type example

## Problem statement

In a laboratory channel, an almost periodic surface wave is produced. In a frozen image, the distance between two consecutive crests is 0.80 m. A fixed sensor records 3.0 crests passing each second. In another video record, a recognizable crest advances 2.4 m during 1.0 s.

Determine the propagation speed by the two methods and decide whether both readings are compatible. Also explain what error would be made if the vertical oscillation of the water were measured as pattern advance.

## Data

- Observed wavelength: 0.80 m.
- Crest-passing frequency: 3.0 Hz.
- Horizontal displacement of the tracked crest: 2.4 m.
- Tracking interval: 1.0 s.
- Observed medium: water channel with an approximately uniform pattern.

## System definition

The system is the surface pattern travelling along the channel. The target magnitude is [[v]], understood as horizontal speed of the pattern. The crest spacing represents [[lambda]], the number of crests per second represents [[f]], the observed advance represents [[Delta_x]], and the tracking time represents [[Delta_t]].

The vertical speed of a water particle is not being analysed. Each point of the surface may move up and down while the shape of the wave travels. The question is about the travelling shape.

## Physical model

We use a travelling periodic-wave model in an almost uniform medium. The first reading combines [[lambda]] and [[f]]. The second follows a crest during [[Delta_t]] and measures its advance [[Delta_x]]. Both estimate [[v]], but they use different evidence.

The model assumes that the tracked crest keeps its identity during the record and that the pattern speed does not change appreciably. If there were strong reflections, breaking waves, or severe deformation, the average reading would be less reliable.

## Model justification

The periodic relation is valid because the sensor counts cycles of the same wave whose spatial separation was measured in the image. Physically, each cycle passing the sensor corresponds to the advance of one complete spatial separation of the pattern.

The direct-tracking relation is valid because the same crest is observed at two instants. It would fail if one crest were confused with another, if video perspective were not corrected, or if the water changed regime during the interval.

Here [[Delta_x]] must be pattern displacement and [[Delta_t]] the observation duration. If a vertical height is measured, the calculation no longer answers the physical question.

## Numerical substitution

For the periodic reading, multiplying 0.80 m by 3.0 cycles per second gives 2.4 m/s. Therefore [[v]] is approximately 2.4 m/s.

For the direct reading, dividing 2.4 m by 1.0 s also gives 2.4 m/s. Therefore the second estimate of [[v]] agrees with the first within the given data.

The agreement does not prove that the experiment is perfect, but it does show internal coherence: the pattern counted by cycles seems to be the same pattern tracked in the video.

## Dimensional validation

- Periodic reading: a length `[L]` multiplied by a frequency `[T⁻¹]` produces a speed `[L T⁻¹]`.
- Direct reading: a distance `[L]` divided by a time `[T]` produces a speed `[L T⁻¹]`.
- Expected result: m/s, corresponding to `[L T⁻¹]`.

The dimension confirms that both relations calculate speed. It does not by itself confirm that the correct crest was tracked; that part depends on the physical system definition.

## Physical interpretation

The result indicates that the wave shape advances about 2.4 m each second along the channel. That speed belongs to the pattern, not to the vertical motion of water molecules. A small float could move up and down with little net translation while crests pass at that horizontal speed.

If [[f]] increased and the medium kept approximately the same speed, the spacing [[lambda]] would have to decrease. If the same [[lambda]] were observed with a larger frequency, the calculated speed would increase and one should ask whether the medium or generation regime changed.

The compatibility of the two methods is strong evidence that [[Delta_x]], [[Delta_t]], [[lambda]], and [[f]] describe the same pattern. If the direct reading had produced a very different value, the first suspicion would be a tracking error, a badly calibrated video scale, or a wave that deforms during measurement.

# Real-world example

## Context

In a swimming pool, one wants to estimate the speed of small surface waves generated by a periodic tap. There are no sensors, but a video can be recorded from above and crests can be counted as they cross a marked line at the edge.

The video shows crests separated by about 0.50 m. At the edge mark, approximately 4 crests pass each second. In addition, one particular crest takes about 0.75 s to travel 1.5 m between two floor marks.

## Physical estimation

The spatial-temporal repetition reading gives a speed close to 2.0 m/s, because 0.50 m per cycle combined with four cycles per second produces two metres per second. The direct-tracking reading also gives about 2.0 m/s, because 1.5 m travelled in 0.75 s corresponds to the same order.

This quantitative estimate is reasonable for small pool surface waves. If the result were 200 m/s, it would be absurd for that setup; if it were 0.002 m/s, crests would take many minutes to cross the pool, contradicting the observation.

## Interpretation

Propagation speed turns a visual observation into a physical magnitude. The pool is not only showing a shape; it is showing how the medium transmits a disturbance. Agreement between the two methods suggests that the same pattern was tracked and that the video scale is adequate.

It also helps predict changes. If the tapping is faster and the wave speed in that regime remains similar, the crests will become closer together. If depth or amplitude changes substantially, speed may change and the relation between frequency and wavelength must be checked again.
