## Historical problem

The historical problem was distinguishing local oscillation from a travelling disturbance. In water, strings, and sound, repetitions were observed, but it was not immediate to separate medium motion from the speed at which the shape was transmitted.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Before wave speed was formalized, waves could be described as vibrations without a clear measure of advance. Frequency was counted in time and wavelength was read in space, but a magnitude linking both readings was missing.

There was also an experimental difficulty. In many phenomena, one cannot mark an individual particle and track it like a projectile. One must track a phase, a crest, a front, or a recognizable compression.

## What changed

Wave theory made it possible to interpret [[v]] as the speed of the pattern. By relating [[lambda]] with [[f]], a periodic wave became a spatial repetition advancing with a temporal rhythm. By using [[Delta_x]] and [[Delta_t]], the same idea could be measured by direct tracking.

This double reading made it possible to compare waves of very different nature. Strings, sound, water, and light began to share a language: what mattered was not only that something oscillated, but how the disturbance propagated.

## Impact on physics

Propagation speed was central for understanding refraction, reflection, resonance, and dispersion. If the medium changes, speed changes; if speed changes while temporal rhythm is conserved, wavelength changes. That idea organizes much of wave physics.

It also turned simple measurements into diagnostics. Counting crests, measuring distances, and timing intervals made it possible to estimate medium properties and detect experimental errors.

## Connection with modern physics

In modern physics, the idea is refined through phase speed, group speed, and information transport. Even so, the classical starting point remains: a wave must be described by how it repeats and how it advances.

The question about [[v]] remains essential because it forces us to specify what is propagating. That precision connects school models with acoustics, optics, telecommunications, and materials physics.
