## Ideal model

The ideal model considers a wave with a well-defined propagation direction and a disturbance separable into components. The central simplification is geometric: [[u_perp]] measures what is perpendicular to advance and [[u_parallel]] measures what is parallel. The amplitude [[A]] acts as common scale.

In this model, mode identity and advance direction are preserved during observation. Mode mixing, strong medium anisotropy, and polarization changes that would vary disturbance orientation are ignored.

## Hypotheses

- The propagation direction is fixed; otherwise perpendicular and parallel are ambiguous.
- [[A]] is positive and common to the components; otherwise indices are badly normalized.
- [[u_perp]] and [[u_parallel]] are measured on the same wave state; otherwise they cannot be compared.
- The observed mode is stable; if it changes, pure classification loses meaning.
- The medium allows displacement or compression to be identified; otherwise another experimental magnitude is needed.

## Quantitative validity domain

The model is useful when one component clearly dominates, for example with an index greater than 0.7 and the other much lower. If the difference between [[C_T]] and [[C_L]] is below 10 %, it is better to speak of a mixed mode or inconclusive measurement.

The order of magnitude of the indices must lie between zero and one. Values outside that interval indicate that [[A]] was not used as a common scale or that a component was taken from another record.

## Model failure signals

A failure signal appears when the same setup changes from transverse to longitudinal without changing excitation or medium. Another signal appears when the graph only shows visible crests but does not allow the real disturbance direction to be identified.

It also fails if the medium has coupled modes, as in surface water waves, where motion may have relevant vertical and horizontal components. In that case, a single label hides part of the phenomenon.

## Extended or alternative model

When to switch to the extended model: if [[C_T]] and [[C_L]] are comparable, if the medium is anisotropic, or if several wave families appear. The transition to a modal model describes dominance, mixture, and variable polarization.

Switch to this model does not remove classification; it makes it local and quantitative. Instead of saying "transverse" or "longitudinal" without nuance, it states which component dominates and under which medium conditions.

## Operational comparison

| Model | Strength | Limit | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pure ideal | Quick classification | Does not describe mixture | Simple string or sound |
| Component indices | Compares [[C_T]] and [[C_L]] | Requires measuring [[A]] | Laboratory and graphs |
| Extended modal | Describes coupled modes | Greater complexity | Solids, water, and seismic waves |

The ideal model is the first reading. If failure signals appear, switching to the extended model avoids forcing a label that does not represent the real medium.
