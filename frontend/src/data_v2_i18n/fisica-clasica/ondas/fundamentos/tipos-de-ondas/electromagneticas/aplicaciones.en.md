# Applications of Electromagnetic Waves

## 1. Radio communications

Dominant variable: [[lambda]].

Validity limit: plane-wave approximation far from the antenna and without dominant obstacles.

Radio communications use electromagnetic waves whose wavelength can be comparable to antennas, buildings, or urban distances. [[lambda]] helps decide whether an antenna couples well to a band, whether a wave bends around obstacles, or whether one reflection can interfere with another signal. Frequency [[f]] identifies the channel, but the spatial reading becomes practical when it is translated into wavelength through [[c]].

In a real application, a transmitter must do more than emit: it must deliver enough field amplitude with suitable polarization. [[E0]] guides the electric field strength that can induce voltage in the receiving antenna. [[B0]] confirms that the wave is a complete electromagnetic wave, even if the receiver often uses the electric field more directly.

## 2. Visible light and vision

Dominant variable: [[f]].

Validity limit: vacuum or ideal-air reading; dispersive media require another model for color and speed.

Visible light is a narrow band of the electromagnetic spectrum. In that context, [[f]] and [[lambda]] are related to color, but not to a different speed in vacuum. Higher frequency moves perception toward violet and reduces wavelength. This application is useful for correcting the idea that each color travels at a different speed when the medium is ignored.

The amplitude [[E0]] is related to the field that interacts with charges in the retina or in a sensor. However, full brightness requires energy and intensity, not only amplitude. This leaf gives the base: light is a wave of coupled transverse fields, and its color depends on temporal and spatial scale.

## 3. Microwave ovens

Dominant variable: [[E0]].

Validity limit: heating is not calculated without absorption, cavity behavior, and intensity.

In a microwave oven, the electromagnetic wave has a frequency chosen to interact efficiently with electric dipoles, especially polar molecules such as water. The electric amplitude [[E0]] matters because the electric field exerts torques and changes molecular rotational energy. The application is no longer only propagation in vacuum, because absorbing matter and a reflecting cavity appear.

The model of this leaf is a starting point for identifying oscillating electric and magnetic fields. Then an extended model of absorption, cavity behavior, and intensity is needed. The boundary between models matters: calculating [[lambda]] from [[f]] gives wave scale, but it does not directly calculate heating.

## 4. Optical fiber

Dominant variable: [[c]].

Validity limit: [[c]] is only a reference; fiber requires an effective speed in the medium.

In optical fiber, information travels through electromagnetic waves guided in a transparent material. The speed is no longer exactly [[c]] because the medium has a refractive index. Even so, the conceptual structure remains: there are transverse fields, frequency, wavelength, and transported energy. [[c]] works as a reference for understanding why the real speed is lower.

This application shows a model transition. In vacuum, [[c]] is used; in the fiber, an effective speed depending on material and sometimes [[f]] is used. If a student keeps the same speed in every case, the difference between the ideal model and the material model is lost.

## 5. Astronomy and spectrum

Dominant variable: [[f]].

Validity limit: it classifies the band but does not identify the emission mechanism by itself.

Astronomy observes electromagnetic waves across many bands: radio, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X rays, and gamma rays. Frequency [[f]] classifies physical processes such as cold gas, dust, hot stars, ionized regions, or high-energy events. [[lambda]] gives instrumental scale, because different telescopes are designed for different wavelengths.

In this application, the wave often travels through vacuum over huge distances, so [[c]] is a robust reference. The received amplitude, related to [[E0]] and [[B0]], can be extremely small, yet it carries information about the source. Physical reading is not just looking at one number; it links band, intensity, and emission mechanism.

## 6. Safety and electromagnetic exposure

Dominant variable: [[E0]].

Validity limit: it does not replace regulatory thresholds or biological absorption models.

In exposure assessments, the electric field [[E0]] is used as an operational indicator because it can be measured and compared with thresholds. However, this leaf should not be confused with a safety standard. Estimating absorbed power requires intensity, frequency, tissue properties, and exposure time. The electromagnetic wave is the starting point, not the complete risk model.

The didactic use is clear: if someone claims every electromagnetic wave is dangerous because it travels at [[c]], speed has been confused with interaction. A weak radio wave, intense visible light, and ionizing radiation belong to the same family, but they interact very differently because of their [[f]], [[lambda]], and amplitude values.

## 7. Remote control and sensors

Dominant variable: [[lambda]].

Validity limit: the local plane approximation does not replace antennas, reflections, or noise.

Remote controls, simple radars, and sensors use electromagnetic waves to send signals or detect objects. [[lambda]] conditions resolution, diffraction, and antenna size. [[f]] conditions the regulated band and available technology. [[E0]] and [[B0]] condition the received signal, although the circuit usually translates those fields into voltages.

Here the plane model can be a local approximation. Real design must include antennas, reflections, and noise. Even so, understanding the wave as transverse fields prevents the belief that the signal is a material fluid leaving the emitter. What propagates is information encoded in an electromagnetic disturbance.

The same reading helps compare technologies without changing the core concept. A garage remote, a Wi-Fi router, and a radar pulse use different bands and engineering constraints, but each begins with fields, propagation scale, and received amplitude. The practical question is which variable dominates the design limit.
