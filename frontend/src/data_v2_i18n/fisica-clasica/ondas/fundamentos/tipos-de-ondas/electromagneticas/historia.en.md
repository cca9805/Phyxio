# History of Electromagnetic Waves

## Historical problem

For a long time, electricity, magnetism, and light were studied as separate phenomena. Sparks, compasses, and colors seemed to belong to different domains. The historical problem was to understand whether these observations could share one structure or merely similar mathematics. The decisive question was whether electric and magnetic fields could propagate by themselves.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty came from mechanical intuition. Familiar waves required a medium: water, air, string, or elastic solid. It was therefore hard to accept a wave without material support. It was also hard to imagine that a changing field could generate another changing field and sustain a traveling disturbance. Reading [[E0]] and [[B0]] as parts of one wave was not immediate.

## What changed

The change came with Maxwell's synthesis. By joining laws of electricity and magnetism, the theory predicted that electromagnetic disturbances should propagate with a speed equal to that of light. This was not a numerical coincidence; it was a physical identification. Light could be understood as an electromagnetic wave, with transverse fields and speed [[c]].

## Impact on physics

The impact was enormous. Optics stopped being an isolated topic and became part of electromagnetism. Radio waves, later produced and detected experimentally, showed that the theory did not describe only visible light. The relation between [[lambda]], [[f]], and [[c]] allowed a vast spectrum to be organized under one structure.

## Connection with modern physics

Modern physics keeps this base and extends it. Special relativity gives [[c]] a universal role. Quantum physics introduces photons and energy tied to frequency. Current technology, from optical fiber to wireless communication, rests on the same central idea: coupled electric and magnetic fields can carry energy and information through space.
