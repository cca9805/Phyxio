# Models of Electromagnetic Waves

## Ideal model

The ideal model treats the electromagnetic wave as a plane wave in vacuum, far from sources and obstacles. The wavefront is taken as large enough for a small region to be described with one propagation direction. In that region, [[E0]] and [[B0]] are coupled transverse amplitudes, [[lambda]] fixes the spatial scale, [[f]] fixes the temporal scale, and [[c]] acts as the reference speed.

This model is appropriate for introducing light as a particular electromagnetic wave and for comparing spectral bands without yet entering polarization, absorption, or dispersion. Its pedagogical advantage is that it separates three clean readings: propagation, field amplitude, and spectral scale.

## Hypotheses

The assumptions are: propagation in vacuum or ideal air, plane wave, fields perpendicular to each other, fields perpendicular to the propagation direction, no appreciable absorption, and observation far from accelerated charges or antennas. It is also assumed that [[E0]] and [[B0]] represent maximum magnitudes, not signed instantaneous values.

When [[lambda]] and [[f]] are used, frequency is assumed to be well defined over many oscillations. When the field relation is used, the observation point is assumed not to lie in a near-source region, where the electric-to-magnetic proportion may not look like that of a free plane wave.

## Quantitative validity domain

The model works well when distance from the source is much larger than [[lambda]], for instance more than 10 times [[lambda]] as a first criterion. It is also reasonable when the medium is close to vacuum or air, with speed errors below 1 % for many school-level exercises. In order-of-magnitude calculations, [[c]] is expected around 3 x 10⁸ m/s.

For amplitudes, the reading is coherent if [[E0]] and [[B0]] are nonnegative and their ratio recovers a speed of the order of [[c]]. If the result demands a macroscopic [[B0]] for a low-intensity wave, or if [[lambda]] and [[f]] give a speed ten times below [[c]] without mentioning a material medium, the model is being misapplied.

## Model failure signals

The model begins to fail with dispersive materials, waveguides, strong reflections, absorption, nearby sources, or non-plane geometry. It also fails if one tries to describe microscopic interaction with matter, ionization, or quantum effects using only [[lambda]], [[f]], [[E0]], and [[B0]].

A conceptual failure signal is changing [[c]] whenever [[f]] changes without changing medium. Another signal is drawing [[E0]] and [[B0]] parallel, or adjusting one amplitude without modifying the other in a plane vacuum wave. In those cases the issue is not just algebra; the model itself has been broken.

## Extended or alternative model

When to switch models is decided by the physical question. The first extension is to introduce refractive index or phase speed in the medium. If there is absorption, attenuation and intensity are needed. If transported energy matters, the Poynting vector is the better object. If the wave is generated close to an antenna, a radiation model with near and far fields is required.

In advanced courses, the model is extended with polarization, dispersion, boundary conditions, and Maxwell equations. This leaf does not replace those models. It gives the minimum nucleus for recognizing the electromagnetic nature of the wave and deciding when the plane representation is sufficient.

## Operational comparison

For identification exercises, the ideal model is the most efficient: read perpendicularity, propagation, and scale relation. For material media, the refractive-index model is more appropriate. For power or radiation safety, amplitude alone is insufficient and energy per area per time is needed. The operational decision is simple: if the question asks what the wave is, use this leaf; if it asks about interaction, loss, or a concrete medium, switch model.
