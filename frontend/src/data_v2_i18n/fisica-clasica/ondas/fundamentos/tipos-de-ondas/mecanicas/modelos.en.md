# Models of Mechanical Waves

## Ideal model

The ideal model treats a mechanical wave as a small disturbance traveling through a uniform material medium. The medium has inertia and a restoring response. The wave shape is considered periodic, so [[lambda]], [[f]], and [[v]] are enough to describe pattern repetition. The amplitude [[A]] measures local displacement, but by itself it does not set speed in the linear regime.

An ideal string is used to illustrate a concrete medium. In that case [[T]] represents restoration and [[mu]] represents linear inertia. The model is not meant to describe every mechanical wave; it gives a clear template for recognizing that there is always material support and local coupling between neighboring regions.

## Hypotheses

The assumptions are: continuous medium or a medium that can be approximated as continuous, small disturbances, uniform properties, negligible dissipation during observation, and recognizable periodicity. It is assumed that [[A]] is much smaller than the geometric scale of the system, so that the medium properties are not changed by the deformation itself.

For the ideal string, specific assumptions are added: constant tension [[T]], uniform linear density [[mu]], flexible string, small slopes, and no important friction. If any condition fails, the string formula may still guide intuition, but it is no longer a reliable calculation.

## Quantitative validity domain

As a first criterion, [[A]] should be below 10 % of [[lambda]] to preserve a linear shape reading. For a string, [[T]] and [[mu]] must be positive, with relative variations below 5 % over the observed segment. The wavelength must be larger than the microscopic scale of the medium; if [[lambda]] is comparable to spacing between elements, the continuous model is no longer adequate.

The order of magnitude of [[v]] must fit the medium: meters per second on loose strings, hundreds of meters per second for sound in air, and thousands of meters per second in solids. A result outside these ranges is not automatically impossible, but it requires justification of medium, tension, density, and regime.

## Model failure signals

A failure signal is that the medium does not exist or cannot deform. Another is that the wave attenuates so strongly within one wavelength that periodicity is no longer clear. The model also fails if a shock wave, a rupture, or bulk mass transport is confused with a small periodic mechanical wave.

On a string, failure signals include variable tension, large amplitudes, appreciable stiffness, ends that impose dominant standing modes, or strong friction. If energy, power, or dissipation must be described, the model of this leaf must be extended.

## Extended or alternative model

When to switch model depends on the medium. For sound, a compression model and bulk modulus are used. For water surface waves, gravity, depth, and surface tension are introduced. For solids, elastic moduli are used. For nonperiodic pulses, group behavior, dispersion, and spectrum must be analyzed.

The correct transition is not to abandon the idea of mechanical wave, but to replace the law that fixes [[v]]. The relation among [[lambda]], [[f]], and [[v]] remains a pattern reading, while the formula connecting speed with medium properties changes.

## Operational comparison

To classify the phenomenon, check whether there is a material medium, local displacement, and propagation. To calculate speed from a periodic drawing, use the relation with [[lambda]] and [[f]]. To explain why a string transmits faster, use [[T]] and [[mu]]. For sound, water, or solids, switch to the specific model. This operational decision prevents applying a correct formula to the wrong medium.
