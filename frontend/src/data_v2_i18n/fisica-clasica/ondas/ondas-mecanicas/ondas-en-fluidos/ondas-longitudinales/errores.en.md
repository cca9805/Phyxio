## Conceptual errors


### Error 1: Believing that molecules travel with the wave

**Why it seems correct**
The student observes that sound "arrives" from one place to another and generalises that air molecules travel that same distance, by analogy with a projectile moving from A to B.

**Why it is incorrect**
Fluid molecules oscillate locally around their equilibrium position. What propagates is the pattern of compressions and rarefactions, not matter. The molecular oscillation amplitude is on the order of micrometres, while the wave can travel kilometres.

**Detection signal**
The student describes the longitudinal wave as a "jet of molecules" or states that the fluid moves in bulk towards the receiver.

**Conceptual correction**
The wave transports energy and linear momentum, but no net matter. Molecules push their neighbours and retreat, transmitting the disturbance without leaving their immediate surroundings.

**Contrast mini-example**
If molecules travelled with sound, speaking in front of a candle one metre away would produce a sustained air jet that would blow it out instantly. In reality, the flame barely flickers because it only receives tiny pressure oscillations.

### Error 2: Attributing the wave [[frecuencia]] to the medium

**Why it seems correct**
Seeing that [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] depends on [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] and [[rho]], the student infers that all wave properties (including [[frecuencia]]) are set by the medium, as if the fluid "chose" at what [[frecuencia]] to vibrate.

**Why it is incorrect**
The [[frecuencia]] is imposed by the oscillating source. The medium determines only [[rapidez_de_propagacion]]; the [[lambda]] adjusts as a quotient. When changing medium, [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] and [[lambda]] vary simultaneously, but [[frecuencia]] remains invariant.

**Detection signal**
The student writes that when a wave passes from air to water "the [[frecuencia]] changes because water has a different density".

**Conceptual correction**
The source sets the number of compressions per second. At the interface, each arriving compression generates a compression in the second medium at the same rate. What adjusts is the spatial separation [[lambda]], not the temporal rate [[frecuencia]].

**Contrast mini-example**
A 440 Hz tuning fork emits sound in air and water. If the [[frecuencia]] changed with the medium, partially submerging the fork would produce two different notes simultaneously. In reality, the same note is heard in both media; only [[lambda]] changes.

### Error 1: Applying the ideal model to large-amplitude waves

**Why it seems correct**
The formula for [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] does not explicitly show an amplitude limit, so the student applies it without restriction to any disturbance, including explosions and sonic booms.

**Why it is incorrect**
The linear model presupposes that the pressure variation is much smaller than the static pressure. When this condition is violated, the compression zone travels faster than the rarefaction, the wave progressively deforms, and a shock wave forms where the speed no longer matches the linear formula prediction.

**Detection signal**
The student calculates [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] for a nearby explosion and obtains the same value as for a normal conversation, without questioning the validity of the result.

**Conceptual correction**
Always verify that the acoustic pressure amplitude is below 1 % of the static pressure before applying the linear formula. For larger disturbances, nonlinear models such as the Burgers equation are required.

**Contrast mini-example**
A shock wave generated by an explosion initially travels at over 1000 m/s in air, well above the predicted 343 m/s. If the linear formula is used, the actual speed is underestimated by more than a factor of three, invalidating any arrival time calculation.

### Error 1: Forgetting the square root in the speed relationship

**Why it seems correct**
The student remembers that [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] depends on [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] and [[rho]], and writes a direct proportionality (simple quotient) omitting the square root. Mentally, "more stiffness, more speed" seems like a linear relationship.

**Why it is incorrect**
The actual dependence is with the square root of the quotient. Doubling [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] at constant [[rho]] does not double [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] but multiplies it by only a factor of approximately 1.41.

**Detection signal**
The student's numerical result is abnormally large (if the root is omitted and the direct quotient is calculated) or inconsistent with reference tables for the medium.

**Conceptual correction**
The square root appears because the wave equation is second-order: it involves second-degree temporal and spatial derivatives. The speed is the square root of the ratio of the differential equation coefficients, not the direct quotient.

**Contrast mini-example**
In water, with [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] of 2.2 GPa and [[rho]] of 1000 kg/m³, the direct quotient gives 2 200 000 m/s (faster than light). Applying the square root yields approximately 1483 m/s, consistent with the experimental reference value.

### Error 1: Interpreting [[impedancia_acustica]] as energy dissipation

**Why it seems correct**
The term "impedance" evokes electrical resistance, where higher impedance implies greater energy loss through Joule heating. The student extrapolates this analogy and believes that high [[impedancia_acustica]] means the medium absorbs more acoustic energy.

**Why it is incorrect**
The acoustic impedance [[impedancia_acustica]] measures the medium's opposition to deformation, not dissipation. In the ideal model there are no losses; [[impedancia_acustica]] determines how much energy is reflected and how much is transmitted at an interface, but does not consume energy within the medium.

**Detection signal**
The student claims that "water absorbs more sound than air because its impedance is higher" or that "a medium with high [[impedancia_acustica]] attenuates the wave rapidly".

**Conceptual correction**
Acoustic impedance [[impedancia_acustica]] governs reflection and transmission at boundaries. Attenuation within the medium depends on viscosity and thermal conductivity, parameters absent from the ideal model. A medium with high [[impedancia_acustica]] can transmit waves over great distances if its viscosity is low.

**Contrast mini-example**
Water has a [[impedancia_acustica]] about 3500 times greater than air, yet whales communicate over hundreds of kilometres underwater because the actual attenuation of water is very low. If [[impedancia_acustica]] measured absorption, submarine communication would be impossible.

## Quick self-control rule

Before accepting a result as valid, apply this three-step verification:

1. **Order of magnitude**: check that [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] is close to 343 m/s in air or 1480 m/s in water. Any result that differs by more than a factor of two from the reference value for the medium requires immediate review.

2. **Physical coherence**: if [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] increases and [[rho]] stays the same, [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] must increase. If [[rho]] increases and [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] stays the same, [[rapidez_de_propagacion]] must decrease. Verify that the direction of variation is consistent with the square-root dependence.

3. **Square root applied**: confirm that the result is not the direct quotient of [[modulo_de_compresibilidad_volumetrica]] divided by [[rho]]. A result of millions of metres per second in a liquid reveals that the square root was omitted.
