## Conceptual context

**Surface waves** are disturbances confined to the interface between two fluid media, characteristically the air-water interface. Unlike bulk waves, these disturbances concentrate their energy in the contact zone and propagate horizontally along that boundary. Their physics arises from the dynamic equilibrium between restoring forces of different natures: **gravity** acts at large scales and **surface tension** dominates at microscopic scales.

This phenomenon is ubiquitous: ocean waves, the rings produced by a falling water drop, and capillary ripples in a glass are all examples of surface waves. What makes this concept especially rich is that the same propagation equation produces qualitatively different behaviours depending on the spatial scale of the disturbance, defined by [[lambda]]. Understanding when each regime applies is the core skill of this leaf.

Surface wave theory also connects with hydrodynamics, meteorology and coastal engineering, making this concept a bridge between laboratory physics and environmental sciences.

## 🟢 Essential level

A surface wave is an undulation of the liquid interface that propagates horizontally. When a disturbance raises the surface, an imbalance is created: gravity tends to pull the displaced fluid back down, while the curvature of the surface generates an additional pressure due to [[sigma]] that also contributes to the restoration. The result is a **travelling oscillation** along the interface.

The intuitive key is the competition between the two restoring forces. **Long ocean waves** (of the order of metres) are dominated by gravity: the longer the wave, the faster it travels, because a long crest displaces more volume and gravitational pressure acts on a greater mass. **Short capillary waves** (of millimetres), on the other hand, are dominated by [[sigma]]: curvature is intense for small waves and [[sigma]] produces a restoring force proportional to that curvature. In this regime, shorter waves travel faster than long ones.

> [!NOTE]
> Wavelength [[lambda]] is the variable that decides the regime. One cannot compute [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] without first knowing whether one is in the gravity domain, the capillary domain, or the transition zone.

## 🔴 Structural level

The deep structure of surface waves lies in the **dispersion relation**: the function linking [[numero_de_onda]] to [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]]. This function is nonlinear, meaning that different wavelengths travel at different speeds, producing the phenomenon known as **dispersion**. When a group of waves with different wavelengths propagates together—as with waves generated by a storm—the wave packet broadens over time because each component advances at its own [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]].

The critical wavelength marking the transition between gravity and capillary regimes is obtained by equating the two terms under the square root in the combined formula. In pure water at ambient temperature, with [[sigma]] approximately 0.072 N/m, [[rho]] approximately 1000 kg/m³ and [[aceleracion_gravitatoria]] approximately 9.81 m/s², this value is around 1.7 cm. Below this wavelength, [[sigma]] dominates; above it, [[aceleracion_gravitatoria]] dominates. At this critical length, [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] reaches its **global minimum**—a remarkable result: there exists a minimum propagation speed, and waves at that wavelength are the hardest to excite.

> [!WARNING]
> The deep-water formula ceases to be valid when the fluid depth is comparable to [[lambda]]. In shallow water, the bottom exerts additional friction and the dispersion relation is modified by a hyperbolic tangent.

The dependence of [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] on [[numero_de_onda]] also has consequences for the **group velocity**, which is the speed at which the energy of the wave packet travels, distinct from [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] in dispersive media. In the gravity regime, the group velocity is half of [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]]; in the capillary regime, it is one and a half times [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]]. This difference between phase velocity and group velocity is fundamental for understanding energy propagation in ocean waves.

The [[rho]] of the fluid determines the inertia of the system: a denser fluid contains more mass per unit volume and therefore requires more energy to oscillate at the same amplitude. This causes [[rho]] to appear in the denominator of the capillary contribution: doubling [[rho]] reduces capillary [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] by a factor of square root of two. In the deep-water gravity regime, [[rho]] cancels algebraically and does not appear in the formula, reflecting that gravity exerts its restoring force directly on the displaced volume independently of its density.

Finally, [[sigma]] is not a universal constant: it depends on the liquid, the temperature, and the presence of contaminants. Fresh water has higher [[sigma]] than salt water or soapy water. A thin oil film on the surface can significantly reduce [[sigma]], altering the capillary regime and producing abnormally calm surfaces—the phenomenon historically observed by sailors near oil spills.

## Deep physical interpretation

The dispersion of surface waves has direct implications for maritime weather prediction. Ocean storms generate a broad spectrum of [[lambda]]. Long components travel faster and arrive first as a "train" of regular waves; short components arrive later. This **temporal separation by [[frecuencia_de_la_onda_superficial]]** makes it possible to estimate storm distance by measuring the period of arriving waves. The relevant quantity for predicting wave arrival is the group velocity, not the phase [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]].

## Personalized resolution method

The standard protocol for computing [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] in surface waves follows four ordered steps.

**Step 1**: Identify [[lambda]] from the problem context and compute [[numero_de_onda]] as two pi divided by [[lambda]]. **Step 2**: Evaluate the two terms under the square root in the combined formula: the gravity term [[aceleracion_gravitatoria]] divided by [[numero_de_onda]] and the capillary term [[sigma]] multiplied by [[numero_de_onda]] divided by [[rho]]. **Step 3**: Compare both terms; if the gravity term exceeds the capillary term by two or more orders of magnitude, use the simplified gravity formula directly; if the capillary term exceeds the gravity term, use the capillary formula. In the transition zone, use the full combined formula. **Step 4**: Verify dimensionally that the result is in m/s and that it is physically reasonable by comparing with the order-of-magnitude scales.

> [!TIP]
> If the problem mentions ocean waves or river waves, one is almost certainly in the gravity regime. If it mentions drops, capillaries or millimetre-scale phenomena, one is in the surface tension regime.

## Special cases and extended example

**Pure gravity limit**: when [[lambda]] is much larger than the critical capillary length, the [[sigma]] term in the combined formula is negligible. [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] grows with the square root of [[lambda]], and longer waves are always faster. This is the familiar behaviour of ocean waves.

**Pure capillary limit**: when [[lambda]] is much smaller than the critical length, the [[aceleracion_gravitatoria]] term is negligible. [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] grows with the square root of [[numero_de_onda]], that is, decreases with [[lambda]]. Capillary waves thus show the inverse dependence: shorter waves, faster propagation. This phenomenon is observable with the ring of ripples formed when a water drop falls.

**Transition zone and minimum velocity**: around [[lambda]] of 1.7 cm, both terms are comparable. The group [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] exhibits a minimum and the system is sensitive to disturbances at that scale. This critical length depends solely on [[sigma]], [[rho]] and [[aceleracion_gravitatoria]] of the liquid, not on the excitation frequency.

**Surfactant effect**: adding soap to water reduces [[sigma]] from 0.072 N/m to values around 0.025–0.040 N/m. This shifts the critical length towards smaller values and reduces the minimum [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]], visibly changing the wave patterns on the surface.

## Real student questions

**Why do long waves travel faster than short waves in the ocean?**
Because in the gravity regime, [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] grows with the square root of [[lambda]]: a wave with [[lambda]] of 100 m has [[numero_de_onda]] much smaller than one with [[lambda]] of 1 m, and the gravity formula gives [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] proportional to the inverse square root of [[numero_de_onda]]. Gravity acts on a greater displaced volume and generates a larger restoring force per unit of horizontal length.

**Why do capillary waves travel faster as they become shorter?**
In the capillary regime, the restoring force is [[sigma]] multiplied by the surface curvature. Curvature is inversely proportional to the radius of curvature, which in turn grows with [[lambda]]. Therefore shorter waves have greater curvature, greater restoring force and greater [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]]. The effect is opposite to the gravitational one: the capillary regime has anomalous dispersion.

**What happens exactly at the critical wavelength?**
At that point, the two terms under the square root are equal and [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] reaches its minimum. Physically, neither restoring force clearly dominates. No wave packet can propagate more slowly than this minimum in deep water; it is the minimum velocity of the system.

**How does [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] change if salt is added to water?**
Salt increases [[rho]] from approximately 1000 to 1025 kg/m³ and slightly reduces [[sigma]]. In the capillary regime, larger [[rho]] reduces [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] (larger denominator) and smaller [[sigma]] also reduces [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] (smaller numerator), so seawater has capillary waves slightly slower than fresh water.

## Cross-cutting connections and study paths

Surface waves connect directly with **dispersion**, present in optics, quantum mechanics and electromagnetic waveguides. The idea that [[rapidez_de_propagacion_de_ondas_superficiales]] depends on [[numero_de_onda]] is universal in dispersive media. An advanced step is the study of **Stokes waves** (nonlinear regime) and **solitary waves**, where nonlinearity counteracts dispersion. These phenomena connect with tsunami physics and shallow-water effects.

## Final synthesis

Surface waves illustrate how the competition between two restoring mechanisms—gravity at large scales and [[sigma]] at small scales—produces a nonlinear dispersion relation. The key variable is [[lambda]]: it determines which formula to apply, which regime dominates, and which magnitudes—[[aceleracion_gravitatoria]], [[sigma]], [[rho]]—control the result. Mastering this preliminary diagnosis is the central skill of this leaf.
