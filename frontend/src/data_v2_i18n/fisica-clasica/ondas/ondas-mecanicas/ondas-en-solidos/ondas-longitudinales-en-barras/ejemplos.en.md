# Exam-type example


## Problem statement

A steel bar with circular cross-section of 12 mm diameter is used as a waveguide to transmit a compression pulse generated by a piezoelectric transducer at 50 kHz. Steel data: Young's modulus 210 GPa, volumetric density 7850 kg/m³.

Determine: longitudinal wave speed, wavelength, mechanical impedance of the bar, and whether the slender bar model is valid at this frequency.

## Data

- Section diameter: d of 0.012 m
- Frequency: f of 50 000 Hz
- [[modulo_de_young]]: 210 × 10⁹ Pa
- [[rho_vol]]: 7850 kg/m³

## System definition

Uniform circular steel bar (12 mm diameter) acting as a waveguide for longitudinal pulses generated by a transducer at 50 kHz.

## Physical model

One-dimensional slender bar: homogeneous, isotropic, linearly elastic material. The longitudinal wave propagates without dispersion at a speed determined exclusively by material properties.

The physical model considers fundamental system quantities such as [[area_de_la_seccion_transversal]], [[impedancia_mecanica_longitudinal_de_la_barra]], [[k_long]].

## Model justification

The slender bar model is appropriate if the resulting wavelength is much larger than the diameter (at least 6 times). Since expected speed is of order 5100 m/s and frequency is 50 kHz, wavelength will be of order 0.1 m, about 8.5 times the 12 mm diameter. The condition is satisfied with moderate margin.

## Symbolic solution

{{f:velocidad_longitudinal_barra}}

[[v_barra]] is computed as the square root of ([[modulo_de_young]] / [[rho_vol]])

{{f:relacion_dispersion_barra}}

[[omega]] is computed as 2pi × f, and [[k_long]] is computed as [[omega]] / [[v_barra]]

{{f:longitud_onda_longitudinal}}

[[lambda_long]] is computed as [[v_barra]] / f

{{f:impedancia_barra}}

[[area_de_la_seccion_transversal]] is computed as pi times d² divided by 4

[[impedancia_mecanica_longitudinal_de_la_barra]] is computed as [[rho_vol]] × [[area_de_la_seccion_transversal]] × [[v_barra]]

Validity condition: [[lambda_long]] greater than 6 × d

## Numerical substitution

[[v_barra]] gives sqrt(210 × 10⁹ / 7850) yielding sqrt(2.675 × 10⁷)

[[v_barra]] gives **5172 m/s**

[[omega]] gives 2pi × 50 000 yielding 314 159 rad/s

[[k_long]] gives [[omega]] / [[v_barra]] yielding 314 159 / 5172 resulting in 60.7 rad/m

[[lambda_long]] gives 5172 / 50 000 yielding **0.1034 m** (10.34 cm)

[[area_de_la_seccion_transversal]] gives pi × (0.012)² / 4 yielding 1.131 × 10⁻⁴ m²

[[impedancia_mecanica_longitudinal_de_la_barra]] gives 7850 × 1.131 × 10⁻⁴ × 5172 yielding **4593 kg/s**

Validity check: [[lambda_long]] / d gives 0.1034 / 0.012 yielding 8.6, greater than 6 ✓

## Dimensional validation

`[M L⁻¹ T⁻²] / [M L⁻³]` gives `[L² T⁻²]`; square root: `[L T⁻¹]` ✓

For impedance: `[M L⁻³] · [L²] · [L T⁻¹]` gives `[M T⁻¹]` ✓

## Physical interpretation

The speed of 5172 m/s is consistent with tabulated values for steel (5100–5200 m/s), confirming correct data. A compression pulse traverses 1 metre of bar in 0.19 ms, enabling precise transit-time measurements in non-destructive testing applications.

The wavelength of 10.3 cm is about 8.6 times the diameter, placing the bar within the validity regime but relatively close to the limit. If frequency doubled to 100 kHz, wavelength would drop to 5.2 cm (4.3 diameters) and the model would begin losing accuracy: Rayleigh-Love corrections would become relevant.

The impedance of 4593 kg/s determines what fraction of energy reflects if the bar connects to a different one. If this bar were joined to an aluminium bar of the same diameter (impedance about 1940 kg/s), the reflection coefficient would be (1940 - 4593)/(1940 + 4593) giving -0.41, indicating reflection with sign inversion and 59% amplitude transmission.

# Real-world example


## Context

A non-destructive testing engineer needs to calibrate a crack-detection system in reinforced concrete piles using impact echo. The method consists of striking the pile head and measuring the return time of the echo from the tip. The pile is 12 m long and the concrete has Young's modulus of 30 GPa and density of 2400 kg/m³.

## Physical estimation

Concrete data: [[modulo_de_young]] of 30 × 10⁹ Pa, [[rho_vol]] of 2400 kg/m³, pile length L of 12 m, diameter 0.6 m.

Applying the [[v_barra]] formula: square root of (30e9 / 2400) gives **3536 m/s**.

Round-trip time: 2L / [[v_barra]] gives 2 × 12 / 3536 yielding **6.79 ms**.

Dominant impact frequency: approximately 1000 Hz. [[lambda_long]] gives 3536/1000 yielding 3.54 m, which is 5.9 times the diameter (0.6 m). The model is marginally valid.

[[impedancia_mecanica_longitudinal_de_la_barra]] of the pile: 2400 × (pi × 0.3²) × 3536 giving 2400 × 0.283 × 3536 yielding **2.40 × 10⁶ kg/s**.

## Interpretation

The tip echo should appear at 6.79 ms after the blow. If an echo appears earlier (e.g. at 4 ms), it indicates a defect at a depth of 4e-3 × 3536 / 2 giving 7.1 m. The engineer can thus map internal defects without excavation.

The pile's high impedance (2.4 × 10⁶ kg/s) implies that junctions with surrounding soil (much lower impedance) produce strong reflections at the tip, facilitating echo detection. If the pile were surrounded by rock (similar impedance), the echo would be weak and the technique would lose sensitivity.

The model limit is in frequency: at 1 kHz with 0.6 m diameter the ratio lambda/d is 5.9, barely above threshold. For higher frequencies (harmonic content of the blow) the lateral inertia correction will be needed and arrival times will deviate slightly from the simple calculation.
