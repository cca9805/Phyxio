## Historical problem

In the mid-19th century, scientists knew that earthquakes generated "vibrations" but no theoretical framework existed to describe how seismic energy propagated through Earth's interior. The central question was: what types of waves travel inside an elastic solid and how do their properties relate to the material? Without this answer, it was impossible to use earthquakes as a diagnostic tool for Earth's interior.

Early seismologists observed that records showed distinct phases separated by regular intervals, but could not explain why or what their physical nature was. Quantitative seismology did not yet exist.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main barrier was the lack of a complete three-dimensional elasticity theory. **Navier** and **Cauchy** had formulated the equations of elasticity in the 1820s, but had not analyzed wave solutions in detail. It was not known that an isotropic medium admits exactly two types of body waves with different velocities.

Furthermore, the idea that the Earth could behave as an elastic solid at planetary scale was counterintuitive: volcanoes suggested a molten interior, which contradicted shear wave propagation. The scientific community needed direct observational proof to accept that most of the mantle was solid.

## What changed

In 1829, **Poisson** mathematically demonstrated that in an isotropic solid there exist two independent propagation modes: one of compression (dilatational, later called P) with velocity depending on K and G, and one of shear (equivolumetric, later called S) with velocity depending only on G. This theoretical result predicted that the compression wave always travels faster.

In 1889, **Ernst von Rebeur-Paschwitz** recorded for the first time a distant earthquake (Japan) on a gravimeter in Potsdam, confirming that waves traveled through the globe. In 1906, **Richard Dixon Oldham** identified the three phases P, S, and surface waves in seismograms and associated the first two with Poisson's predictions. In 1936, **Inge Lehmann** used the absence of S waves to demonstrate the existence of the liquid core and propose a solid inner core.

## Impact on physics

The identification of P and S waves transformed seismology from a descriptive discipline into a quantitative science. It allowed:

- **X-raying the Earth**: determining internal structure (crust, mantle, cores) from travel times.
- **Unifying elasticity and geophysics**: connecting elastic moduli (K, G, E, nu) with seismic observables.
- **Establishing magnitude scales**: Richter and Gutenberg calibrated their scales using body wave amplitudes.
- **Developing plate tectonics**: high seismic velocity zones reveal subducted plates.

## Connection with modern physics

Today, P and S wave seismology is the basis of **seismic tomography**, which generates 3D images of Earth's interior with resolution of tens of kilometres. The same principles are applied in **exploration geophysics** (oil and gas prospecting) using artificial sources and multichannel recordings.

In **earthquake engineering**, knowledge of [[v_s]] in the first 30 metres of soil (VS30) is the key parameter for site classification and seismic-resistant design. International codes (Eurocode 8, NEHRP) are directly based on this magnitude.

Gravitational wave detection by LIGO also has conceptual parallels: it detects shear deformations of spacetime propagating at the speed of light, analogous to how S waves deform a solid.
