## Historical problem

In the late 19th century, seismologists observed that records of distant earthquakes contained a large-amplitude, long-duration phase arriving after P and S waves. This phase dominated seismograms of shallow earthquakes and caused the greatest damage, but its physical nature was unknown. It could not be explained by the known body waves (longitudinal and transverse): it travelled slower than both and its amplitude was excessive for a simple reflection or diffraction.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The theoretical framework of elasticity in infinite media, developed by Navier, Cauchy, and Poisson in the 1820s-1830s, predicted only two types of waves: longitudinal and transverse. The existence of a free surface as a generator of a new propagation mode was not contemplated in the original theory. The difficulty lay in the fact that the free surface imposes boundary conditions (zero stresses) that couple P and S components in a way that does not occur in the interior. Recognising that this coupling could produce a proper mode, with distinct velocity and confined to the surface, required a significant conceptual leap.

## What changed

In **1885**, Lord Rayleigh published his celebrated paper "On Waves Propagated along the Plane Surface of an Elastic Solid" in the Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society. He demonstrated mathematically that free-surface boundary conditions admit a non-trivial wave solution: a wave propagating without radiation into the interior, whose amplitude decays exponentially with depth, and whose velocity is less than the transverse wave velocity.

Rayleigh derived the secular equation (sixth-degree polynomial in the velocity ratio) and proved it has a unique physically admissible real root. This established that the free surface is not a mere passive reflector but a generator of a fundamentally new propagation mode.

Subsequently, in **1911**, Augustus Love complemented this work by demonstrating the existence of another surface mode (Love waves) in stratified media, expanding the catalogue of surface waves. In the 1950s-1960s, inversion of Rayleigh wave dispersion curves became a standard tool in seismology for determining crustal structure.

## Impact on physics

Rayleigh's discovery had impact across multiple fields:

- **Seismology**: Rayleigh waves explained the highest-amplitude phase in seismograms and enabled the development of location and magnitude estimation methods based on surface waves (surface wave magnitude Ms).
- **Exploration geophysics**: Rayleigh wave dispersion in stratified media became the basis of the MASW method for soil profiling, one of the most widely used geotechnical techniques today.
- **Acoustics and microelectronics**: in 1965, White and Voltmer demonstrated the generation of surface acoustic waves (SAW) on piezoelectric crystals, creating the foundation for SAW filters in telecommunications (used in billions of mobile devices).
- **Non-destructive testing**: detection of surface defects via Rayleigh waves (cracks, corrosion) became an industrial standard in aerospace and nuclear energy.

## Connection with modern physics

Rayleigh waves find analogues in modern physics:

- **Surface phonons in nanostructures**: in thin films and nanodevices, Rayleigh modes determine thermal dissipation and the performance of MEMS/NEMS resonators.
- **Seismic Rayleigh waves and gravitational wave detection**: LIGO's detection of gravitational waves requires modelling the Newtonian noise caused by seismic Rayleigh waves perturbing mirror positions.
- **Acoustic metamaterials**: periodic surface structures are designed to manipulate Rayleigh wave propagation, creating "seismic invisibility cloaks" around buildings.
- **Biology and medicine**: Rayleigh waves in soft tissues (transient elastography) are investigated for diagnosing liver fibrosis and tumours by measuring [[velocidad_de_onda_de_rayleigh]] in tissue.
