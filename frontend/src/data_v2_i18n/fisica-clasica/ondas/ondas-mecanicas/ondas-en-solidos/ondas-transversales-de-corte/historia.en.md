## Historical problem

The problem was not merely to identify another arrival on a seismogram. Physicists and engineers had to understand why some ground motions were transversely polarized and why they failed to pass through certain media. The S wave forced a distinction between resistance to compression and resistance to shear, a distinction absent from simple fluid models.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Before modern elasticity, many mechanical disturbances were explained through pressure, visible displacement or tension analogies. A clear concept of shape change without appreciable volume change was missing. Without that concept, [[modulo_de_cizalla]] could not play its present role in interpreting [[v_s]].

## What changed

The major change was the recognition that not every bulk mechanical wave is a compression wave. Elastic theory showed that solids admit different modes: longitudinal and transverse. Seismic observation confirmed that S waves have arrival times, polarization and interface behaviour different from P waves.

## Impact on physics

The impact was large because S waves made the inside of opaque bodies readable. They helped infer Earth's internal structure, separate shear and compression properties in materials, and turn [[v_s]] into a diagnostic variable. The absence of S waves through the outer core became decisive evidence for its liquid state.

## Connection with modern physics

The same idea now appears in geotechnics, ultrasonics, medical elastography, metamaterials and complex media. Measurements of [[lambda_s]], [[impedancia_transversal]] or [[nu_poisson]] inherit the conceptual shift: a solid transmits shape, not only pressure. This is why shear-wave methods remain useful from planetary seismology to laboratory material control.
