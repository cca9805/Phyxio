## Historical problem

Since antiquity, builders knew empirically that different materials responded differently to loads: stone resisted compression but fractured easily, wood bent before breaking and metals could be bent and return to their original shape. However, no quantitative framework existed to describe these differences. The unanswered question was: how to objectively measure and compare the ability of a material to resist deformation?

In the seventeenth century, Robert Hooke formulated his famous law (ut tensio sic vis, 1676), establishing that the extension of a spring is proportional to the applied force. But Hooke worked with springs and wires, not with the three-dimensional response of a continuous solid. The step from a spring constant to the intrinsic properties of the material was still missing.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The fundamental difficulty was separating the geometry of the specimen from the properties of the material. Two bars of the same material but with different length or cross-section deform by different amounts under the same load. Without the notions of stress (force per unit area) and strain (relative change in length), it was impossible to define a constant depending only on the material.

Furthermore, the three-dimensional elastic response could not be reduced to a single number. A material behaves differently under tension, compression, shear and hydrostatic pressure. Without a tensorial theory, there was no way to organise these responses or to demonstrate how many constants are truly independent.

## What changed

Thomas Young, in his lectures of 1807, introduced the concept of elastic modulus as an intrinsic property of the material, independent of geometry. He defined the ratio of stress to strain, creating what we now call [[modulo_de_young]]. This was the key conceptual leap: moving from a spring constant to a universal material property.

Shortly afterwards, in the 1820s, Augustin-Louis Cauchy developed the theory of stresses and strains in three dimensions, formalising the stress tensor. Gabriel Lame, in the 1830s, introduced the constants bearing his name and demonstrated that for an isotropic solid only two independent parameters suffice to describe the entire elastic response. Simeon Denis Poisson quantified the transverse-longitudinal coupling with the coefficient that now bears his name.

## Impact on physics

These advances transformed solid mechanics from an empirical art into a quantitative science. Elasticity theory allowed, for the first time, predicting the deformation of complex structures (beams, plates, shells) from material properties and applied loads, without needing to test each particular configuration.

The demonstration that only two constants suffice for an isotropic solid was a profound symmetry result, a precursor to the systematic use of tensors in physics. This same mathematical structure would later extend to electromagnetism (Maxwell tensor) and general relativity (metric tensor).

The connection between elastic moduli and wave velocities, established by Navier and Stokes in the nineteenth century, opened the door to quantitative seismology and modern non-destructive testing.

## Connection with modern physics

Elastic moduli remain living quantities in current research. Modern computational mechanics (finite elements, molecular dynamics) uses elastic constants as fundamental input data. Materials science designs new materials (auxetic metamaterials, foams with negative Poisson ratio) by deliberately manipulating the relationships between moduli.

In geophysics, seismic tomography reconstructs the internal structure of the Earth by measuring P and S wave velocities, which depend directly on [[modulo_de_compresibilidad]], [[modulo_de_cizalla]] and density. In nanotechnology, elastic moduli of nanotubes and graphene are measured with atomic force microscopy techniques, verifying that the laws of continuum elasticity remain applicable at nanometric scales.
