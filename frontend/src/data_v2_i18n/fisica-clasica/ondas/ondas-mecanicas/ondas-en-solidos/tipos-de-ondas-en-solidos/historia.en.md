## Historical problem

In the nineteenth century, mathematical physicists faced a puzzling question: if light is a transverse wave (as Fresnel had demonstrated experimentally around 1820), the luminiferous ether supposedly transmitting it should behave as a rigid solid capable of supporting shear stresses. Yet planets move through the ether without apparent resistance, as if it were a fluid.

At the same time, the theory of solid elasticity was undergoing rapid development. Augustin-Louis Cauchy, Siméon Denis Poisson and George Gabriel Stokes were formulating the general equations of linear elasticity. Poisson demonstrated in 1831 that in an isotropic solid two types of wave must exist: one of compression and one of shear, with velocities determined by the material's elastic moduli. This prediction, made from pure theory, was the first formal recognition of the classification of elastic wave types in solids.

The central question of that period was whether matter — or the ether — could transmit only longitudinal vibrations, or whether both wave families necessarily coexist.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was conceptual: before the elasticity theory of Cauchy and Poisson, no mathematical framework existed that formally distinguished between volume deformation (compression) and shape deformation (shear). Newtonian mechanics treated bodies as point masses or rigid objects, and the hydrodynamics of Euler and Bernoulli handled only fluids without shear stresses.

The concept of the stress tensor, formalised by Cauchy around 1822, was the advance that allowed the two deformation mechanisms to be distinguished and, with that, two independent wave velocities to be predicted. Without the stress tensor, it was impossible to see that the compression wave and the shear wave are independent normal modes of the elastic medium, each with its own stiffness and its own velocity.

Poisson's ratio, introduced by Poisson himself, was essential for quantifying the relation between the two velocities: its value determines by how much the longitudinal wave exceeds the transverse one in the same material.

## What changed

The definitive advance was the derivation by John William Strutt (Lord Rayleigh) in 1885 of the third wave family: the surface waves that bear his name. Rayleigh demonstrated mathematically that in an elastic half-space with a flat free surface, a wave must exist that propagates confined to that surface, with elliptical particle motion and a velocity slightly lower than the shear wave.

This result was surprising because no one had anticipated that the wave character of the elastic medium would manifest in a special way at the surface. Rayleigh showed that the surface wave is an exact analytical solution of the elasticity equations with free-surface boundary conditions, not an approximation or a mathematical artefact.

Experimental verification of Poisson's and Rayleigh's predictions had to wait for the development of instrumental seismology at the end of the nineteenth century. With the first precision seismographs of Milne and von Rebeur-Paschwitz, it became possible to record the differentiated arrival of P, S and surface waves in distant earthquakes, unequivocally confirming the theoretical classification.

## Impact on physics

The classification of wave types in solids had consequences far beyond material elasticity. The discovery that S-waves do not propagate in fluids was the key that allowed Richard Oldham, in 1906, to demonstrate that the Earth has a liquid core: analysing earthquake seismograms, Oldham identified a shadow zone where S-waves did not arrive, explainable only if the core has no shear modulus — that is, if it is liquid.

This finding transformed geophysics into a science capable of knowing the internal structure of the Earth without drilling it. Wave type classification became the primary instrument of modern seismic tomography, which reconstructs the three-dimensional distribution of wave velocities to infer temperature, composition and phase state of the Earth's interior.

In the technological realm, Rayleigh's theory established the foundations of Surface Acoustic Wave (SAW) devices, developed from the 1960s onwards, which are today essential components in radio-frequency filters in all mobile phones.

## Connection with modern physics

The distinction between longitudinal and transverse waves remains the foundation of two very active branches of current research. In acoustic and elastic metamaterials, periodic structures are designed that selectively manipulate P or S waves, creating bandgaps for one wave type without affecting the other. This has opened the possibility of acoustic cloaking and frequency-selective vibration isolation.

In gravitational wave detection (LIGO, Virgo), seismic noise is one of the fundamental sensitivity limits. The classification of waves in the ground — P, S and Rayleigh — allows the seismic isolation systems of the interferometer mirrors to be designed differently for each type of disturbance, reducing noise below the gravitational signal level.

Rayleigh's 1885 theory connects directly with current materials physics: at the nanoscale, high-frequency Rayleigh waves (hypersonic, in the GHz range) are used in Brillouin spectroscopy to measure the elastic moduli of thin films and nanostructures that cannot be characterised by conventional mechanical methods.
