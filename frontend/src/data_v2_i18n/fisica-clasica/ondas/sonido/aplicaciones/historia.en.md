## Historical problem

During the nineteenth century, exploitation of the seas was limited to what could be seen from the surface or reached with a lead-weighted sounding line. Ocean depth was largely unknown, and ships were lost running aground on uncharted shallows. The question pressing on navigators and scientists was direct: how to measure water depth without a cable reaching the bottom?

At the same time, the First World War revealed an urgent military problem: German submarines were sinking Allied ships without being detected. The British Navy needed a method to locate objects underwater without physical contact. This dual problem — submarine cartography and submarine detection — was the driving force behind the development of acoustic sound applications throughout the twentieth century.

On the medical front, the challenge was different but equally ancient: how to examine the interior of the human body without surgery? Before the twentieth century, the physician had only percussion and direct auscultation to infer the state of internal organs, with very limited precision.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle before the development of sonar was not conceptual but technological: no practical way existed to generate high-frequency sound pulses with sufficient power and to detect weak echoes in the presence of marine noise. Audible-frequency sound attenuates too rapidly in water and its long wavelength (tens of metres at 100 Hz) prevents the spatial resolution needed to locate objects precisely.

The second obstacle was temporal: travel times in water are on the order of milliseconds to seconds, and the electronics of the era lacked means to measure those intervals with sufficient precision. The first experiments with underwater bell sounds by Colladon and Sturm in 1826 on Lake Geneva demonstrated that sound propagates in water, but offered no clear path to object detection.

The third obstacle in medical application was biological: human body tissues have very similar acoustic impedances to each other (except bone and air), making echoes at the interfaces weak and difficult to distinguish from the system's own noise.

## What changed

The discovery of piezoelectricity by Pierre and Jacques Curie in 1880 was the advance that changed everything. Piezoelectric materials transform electrical signals into mechanical vibrations and vice versa, allowing the construction of transducers capable of emitting high-frequency ultrasonic pulses and detecting echoes with equal efficiency.

Paul Langevin, a French physicist, applied the piezoelectric effect during the First World War to develop the first submerged ultrasonic hydrophones and emitters. In 1917, his team detected the echo from a test submarine in the Mediterranean Sea using a quartz transducer, establishing the principle of active sonar. The echo formula we use today — [[d_eco]] is half the product of [[v_sonido]] and [[t_vuelo]] — was the direct result of that work.

In medicine, the Austrian Karl Theodore Dussik produced the first images of brain structures using ultrasound in 1942. However, modern medical ultrasound was born in the 1950s with the work of Ian Donald in Glasgow, who developed the first B-mode equipment for obstetric diagnosis, obtaining real-time fetal images in 1958.

## Impact on physics

The development of sonar and medical ultrasound had consequences that extended far beyond their direct applications. The need to generate and detect ultrasound with precision drove the development of **physical acoustics** as an independent discipline, with its own mathematical tools (wave equations in viscous media, transducer theory, signal processing).

The measurement of sound velocity in seawater under different conditions of temperature, salinity and pressure — necessary to calibrate sonars — produced the first systematic database of the ocean's acoustic properties. Those data revealed the existence of the SOFAR channel (Sound Fixing and Ranging), a layer of water at about 1000 m depth where [[v_sonido]] is minimum and sound is trapped by refraction, propagating thousands of kilometres with very little attenuation. The SOFAR channel was exploited militarily during the Cold War to detect underwater explosions anywhere on the planet.

In the medical field, ultrasound eliminated the need for X-ray fluoroscopy for pregnancy monitoring, removing the exposure to ionising radiation of millions of fetuses and expectant mothers. Its impact on perinatal mortality in the countries where it became widespread was measurable and significant.

## Connection with modern physics

Sound applications remain a very active research field. **High-intensity focused ultrasound** (HIFU) allows destroying deep tumours by heating them locally without surgery or radiation, using exactly the same echo principle in reverse: instead of detecting echoes, ultrasonic beams are focused on a precise point in the tissue to deposit thermal energy. The echo formula allows calculating the focal depth from the travel time.

**Quantum acoustics** uses phonons (quanta of mechanical vibration) in crystals to study quantum correlation phenomena at ultra-low temperatures, connecting classical sound with modern solid-state physics.

At the largest scale, **seismology** — which is essentially terrestrial sonar using P and S waves as probes — allows reconstructing the internal structure of the Earth with a resolution of tens of kilometres. Signal processing techniques developed for military sonar are today the basis of global seismic tomography.

Gravitational wave detection by LIGO requires reducing the acoustic noise of the mirrors to unprecedented levels, applying vibratory isolation principles that are direct heirs of the structural acoustics developed to isolate sonar equipment from submarine hull noise.
