## 1. Medical ultrasound and Doppler imaging

Medical ultrasound uses frequencies between 1 and 20 MHz to obtain images of internal tissues. The choice of [[f_son]] directly determines [[lambda_son]] in tissues (where [[v_son]] is approximately 1540 m/s) and therefore the spatial resolution of the image: at 5 MHz, [[lambda_son]] is about 0.31 mm; at 1 MHz, about 1.54 mm. A higher frequency produces more detailed images, but [[p0_ac]] is attenuated more rapidly with depth, limiting penetration.

The [[I_ac]] of diagnostic ultrasound is carefully controlled to remain below safety limits (typically below 720 mW/cm²). Above these values, thermal and cavitation effects can damage tissues. The relationship between [[I_ac]] and [[p0_ac]] explains why doubling penetration without changing frequency requires a significant increase in [[p0_ac]]: attenuation in tissues grows exponentially with distance.

Dominant variable: frequency [[f_son]], which determines the trade-off between resolution (high frequency, small [[lambda_son]]) and penetration (low frequency, lower attenuation).
Validity limit: the plane wave model is an approximation; in ultrasound, beams are focused with curved transducers and the geometry is not strictly planar. At depths greater than about 20 cm, attenuation is so high that image quality degrades regardless of the transducer's [[p0_ac]].

## 2. Room acoustic treatment

The acoustic design of music halls, theatres, and recording studios is based on controlling how the [[I_ac]] of each frequency is distributed in space. Absorbing materials reduce the [[p0_ac]] of reflections; diffusing panels scatter energy without dissipating it. The effectiveness of each treatment depends on the relationship between the [[lambda_son]] of the target frequency and the physical dimensions of the treatment.

For low frequencies (125 Hz in air, [[lambda_son]] of about 2.75 m), absorbing panels need a thickness comparable to [[lambda_son]]/4, approximately 0.7 m, to be effective. For high frequencies (4000 Hz, [[lambda_son]] of about 8.6 cm), centimetre-thick panels are sufficient. This fact explains why low-frequency control is the most costly and complex problem in acoustic treatment.

Dominant variable: the wavelength [[lambda_son]] of the problematic frequency, which determines the minimum physical size of the required acoustic treatments.
Validity limit: the plane wave model describes far-field behaviour well, but in the near-field zone (distances smaller than [[lambda_son]]) pressure patterns are complex and require finite element analysis.

## 3. Seismic subsurface exploration

Oil and mining exploration companies use low-frequency sound waves (between 10 and 200 Hz) generated by compressed air sources or explosives to probe subsurface structure. [[v_son]] in rocks (between 2000 and 6000 m/s depending on lithology) determines the [[lambda_son]] of the signals, which can range from tens to hundreds of metres.

Reflections of the waves at interfaces between layers of different acoustic impedance (product of density and velocity) arrive at the surface with different transit times. By analysing those times and the reflected [[p0_ac]] values, geophysicists reconstruct subsurface geometry. The [[I_ac]] of the sources must be high enough for the signal to arrive with measurable [[p0_ac]] after penetrating kilometres of absorbing rock.

Dominant variable: the propagation speed [[v_son]] in each layer, which determines [[lambda_son]] and the arrival time of reflections.
Validity limit: the plane wave model fails when layers have lateral property variations at scales smaller than [[lambda_son]]. At very large depths, the signal is attenuated below the detection threshold of the geophones.

## 4. Industrial noise control and hearing protection

In industrial environments such as foundries, printing plants, and machining shops, [[I_ac]] can exceed hearing risk thresholds (above 85 dB, equivalent to [[I_ac]] of 3.2×10⁻⁴ W/m²). The design of hearing protection and acoustic insulation requires knowing the [[p0_ac]] and [[f_son]] of noise sources to select the appropriate treatment.

Earplugs and earmuffs are designed to attenuate [[p0_ac]] in different frequency bands. Their effectiveness is measured as the reduction of [[I_ac]] in decibels in each band. For low-frequency noise (motors, compressors), attenuation is lower because [[lambda_son]] is comparable to the size of the protector; for high-frequency noise (grinders, saws), attenuation is greater because [[lambda_son]] is small relative to the protector.

Dominant variable: the frequency [[f_son]] of the dominant noise, which determines the effectiveness of different types of hearing protection and the ease or difficulty of acoustically isolating the source.
Validity limit: the plane harmonic wave model describes pure tones well. Industrial noise is in reality a superposition of many frequencies (broadband noise), and the protective [[I_ac]] must be calculated for the full spectrum, not just for a single frequency.

## 5. Marine sonar and underwater detection

Sonar (Sound Navigation And Ranging) systems emit acoustic pulses at [[f_son]] between 1 kHz and 100 kHz in sea water, where [[v_son]] is approximately 1500 m/s. The resulting [[lambda_son]] (between 15 mm and 1.5 m) determines both the resolution of the system and its ability to detect objects of different sizes.

The emitted [[I_ac]] must be sufficiently high for the signal reflected by the target to carry enough [[p0_ac]] when it reaches the receiver, after having travelled twice the source-target distance and been attenuated by the absorption coefficient of water (which increases with [[f_son]]). The trade-off between range (low [[f_son]], lower absorption, larger [[lambda_son]], lower resolution) and resolution (high [[f_son]], higher absorption, smaller [[lambda_son]], higher resolution) defines the operating [[f_son]] of the sonar for each application.

Dominant variable: the emission frequency [[f_son]], which simultaneously controls spatial resolution (through [[lambda_son]]) and maximum range (through acoustic absorption, which increases with [[f_son]]).
Validity limit: the plane wave model is an approximation; real sonars emit focused beams. At large distances, Earth's curvature and water temperature gradients modify [[v_son]] with depth, creating underwater acoustic channels that require more complex propagation models.
