# Applications

## 1. [[entropia]] calibration in a solid
Dominant variable: [[entropia]]
Validity limit: [[numero_microestados]] > 1
In a materials laboratory, the connection with classical thermodynamics lets [[entropia]] be read as the result of compatible microscopic configurations. A macroscopic thermal response is measured, but the model asks how many vibrational microstates support that response. The result helps decide whether a classical [[temperatura]] description is sufficient or whether the microscopic structure must be analyzed. [[constante_boltzmann]] keeps the scale correct between counting and macroscopic units. This application predicts how increased microscopic accessibility becomes a measurable classical quantity. It also avoids a purely visual reading of entropy: a material can look ordered and still have large vibrational multiplicity. The comparison matters when different heat treatments produce similar macroscopic responses but not the same internal statistical structure. Plotting [[entropia]] against [[numero_microestados]] reveals the logarithmic curve: if the curve flattens, the classical signal is nearly saturated and further gain in multiplicity adds little measurable entropy. This is particularly relevant when comparing two samples with similar heat capacity but different internal vibrational structures, because the logarithmic shape distinguishes which sample has more statistical room to absorb energy.
## 2. Comparison of energy states
Dominant variable: [[probabilidad_relativa]]
Validity limit: [[temperatura]] > 0 K
In physical chemistry, [[probabilidad_relativa]] compares two configurations with different [[energia_estado]]. The practical question is whether an excited state contributes significantly to the observable behavior. At low [[temperatura]], small energy differences can greatly reduce statistical weight. At higher temperature, the system spreads weight over more states. This connects microscopic occupation with a classical equilibrium variable without saying any state is impossible. The calculation guides spectra, molecular populations, and reactivity. When the relative value is very low, the state can be omitted in a first approximation; when it is close to other weights, it must be included because it changes observable averages. The decay curve of [[probabilidad_relativa]] versus [[energia_estado]] shows how quickly statistical weight falls off at a given [[temperatura]], revealing which excited states remain relevant for the equilibrium description.
## 3. Free energy under thermal equilibrium
Dominant variable: [[energia_libre]]
Validity limit: [[temperatura]] approximately constant
In material design, [[energia_libre]] helps compare phases or configurations under a fixed thermal environment. Looking only at [[energia_interna]] is not enough, because a configuration with higher energy can be favored if it has much larger [[entropia]]. The application restricts the analysis to approximately constant temperature. It predicts which state is thermodynamically more stable through an energy-multiplicity balance. This idea is used in alloys, molecular folding, and phase changes. If the environment does not maintain temperature, the conclusion can change and another potential may be needed. The practical value is turning many microscopic possibilities into a classical stability criterion. When [[energia_libre]] is plotted against [[temperatura]], a crossover between two phases signals the transition point predicted by the entropic balance: the phase with higher multiplicity wins at sufficient temperature. Below that crossover, the energetically favorable phase dominates; above it, the more accessible statistical structure takes over, and the classical criterion changes sign accordingly.
## 4. [[temperatura]] from entropy slope
Dominant variable: [[temperatura]]
Validity limit: [[derivada_entropia_energia]] > 0
In simulations, [[temperatura]] can be obtained by reading the sensitivity of [[entropia]] to [[energia_interna]]. The measured object is not a thermal sensation, but a statistical slope. This application compares a computational result with classical equilibrium temperature. If the slope is not stable, the system may not be at equilibrium and the macroscopic translation becomes doubtful. [[derivada_entropia_energia]] therefore becomes a coherence test. It helps detect whether microscopic data really admit a thermodynamic reading. In small systems, fluctuations can distort the slope and require averages or ensemble methods. A graph of [[entropia]] against [[energia_interna]] lets you check whether [[derivada_entropia_energia]] is stable across the range or shows local curvature that signals departure from equilibrium.
## 5. Microstate analysis in gases
Dominant variable: [[numero_microestados]]
Validity limit: [[numero_microestados]] >> 1
In an ideal gas, the huge [[numero_microestados]] explains why classical variables are so stable. Although every molecule has a different configuration, the macroscopic average becomes reproducible. The application predicts when microscopic fluctuation is hidden behind [[entropia]] and [[temperatura]]. If the system is too small, fluctuations may remain visible and the classical connection loses precision. This analysis justifies why pressure gauges and thermometers work for ordinary samples. It also explains why the same model weakens in nanocavities or few-particle systems. The key is recognizing when multiplicity is large enough to produce smooth variables. When [[numero_microestados]] is small, [[entropia]] shows discrete steps rather than the continuous logarithmic curve that supports classical behavior. Those visible steps in the graph are the signature that macroscopic thermodynamics cannot yet be applied. Only when the system size is large enough for fluctuations to become negligible relative to mean values does the smooth logarithmic shape appear and justify treating [[entropia]] and [[temperatura]] as continuous classical variables. In practice, this analysis is combined with the Coord graph to check whether the trend answers the central question. If the curve does not respect the physical domain, the result should not be accepted as a reliable classical reading. This gives the application an operational check rather than a decorative interpretation: the microscopic description must produce a stable classical variable.
