# Applications

## 1. Image sensors
Dominant variable: [[energia_cuantizada]]
Validity limit: 10^14 Hz <
A digital sensor does not directly count a smooth classical wave; it records microscopic events in pixels. Each event depends on whether the [[energia_cuantizada]] of a photon can excite charge in the material. The measured output is electrical, but the prediction uses [[frecuencia]], [[longitud_onda]], and [[separacion_energia]]. Classical physics describes propagation to the lens; modern physics explains why some colors activate a material better than others. The [[energia_nivel]] of the charge carriers sets the minimum excitation threshold. Below that threshold, no amount of intensity produces a registered event because no photon carries enough energy to bridge the gap. [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] helps estimate whether a given material can respond to a chosen spectral region. A large ratio signals that classical wave theory cannot describe pixel activation: individual photon energy is the decisive quantity, not wave amplitude. Sensor engineers therefore choose materials based on [[separacion_energia]] matching the target spectral band, and they use [[energia_nivel]] diagrams to predict which photon frequencies will reliably produce signal and which will be lost as heat.

## 2. Solar cells
Dominant variable: [[separacion_energia]]
Validity limit: [[energia_cuantizada]] >
In a photovoltaic cell, the question is not only how much light arrives, but whether each photon exceeds the energy gap of the material. [[frecuencia]] controls energy per photon, and [[longitud_onda]] indicates the useful spectral region. The model predicts which radiation creates useful carriers and which part is lost as transmission or heat. The limit appears when a simple photon model ignores full band structure. [[separacion_energia]] of the semiconductor sets the activation threshold. [[constante_planck]] relates this gap to the minimum usable [[frecuencia]]. Photons with lower [[frecuencia]] convert to heat even when arriving in large numbers, which is why solar panel efficiency depends on the spectral match, not just total intensity. Tuning [[energia_nivel]] through material engineering shifts the threshold to better overlap the solar spectrum. This is why multi-junction solar cells stack materials with different [[separacion_energia]] values: each layer captures a different spectral region, and together they exploit a larger portion of the solar [[frecuencia]] range. The combined [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] for each layer guides whether a classical or quantum description is needed for that band.

## 3. Atomic spectra
Dominant variable: [[energia_nivel]]
Validity limit: [[numero_cuantico]] positive integer
Spectra contain lines because atoms do not accept arbitrary energy. [[energia_nivel]] depends on the allowed state, and transitions are read through [[separacion_energia]]. One measures emitted or absorbed [[longitud_onda]] and infers the discrete structure of the system. This application constrains classical models: a continuous orbit cannot explain narrow lines without quantization. Each spectral line corresponds to a specific [[separacion_energia]] between two levels labeled by [[numero_cuantico]]. From the measured [[longitud_onda]], [[frecuencia]] and [[energia_cuantizada]] can be calculated, identifying the element and transition type. [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] confirms why lines are so narrow: the quantum scale is the only relevant one in that regime, and classical continuity does not exist in that energy neighborhood. Each identified line directly gives the [[energia_cuantizada]] of the emitted photon, which in turn fixes [[frecuencia]] and [[longitud_onda]] with a precision that allows element identification at parts-per-million level, impossible with any continuous wave model.

## 4. Lasers
Dominant variable: [[frecuencia]]
Validity limit: spectral width much smaller than [[frecuencia]]
A laser combines a highly ordered wave reading with a quantum reading of stimulated emission. [[frecuencia]] fixes the [[energia_cuantizada]] of emitted photons, and coherence enables intense directional radiation. The model predicts color, energy per photon, and possible interaction with materials. The basic model fails if level populations, pumping, and losses are ignored. In a three- or four-level scheme, [[energia_nivel]] of each active state determines which transition produces optical gain. [[separacion_energia]] between the upper and emission levels directly fixes the output [[frecuencia]] via [[constante_planck]]. Changing laser wavelength requires changing material or level structure, not simply adjusting pump intensity. [[longitud_onda]] of output is therefore a direct read of the quantum structure of the gain medium. Tuning a laser means engineering [[energia_nivel]] and [[separacion_energia]] through material choice, doping, or heterostructure design, not simply adjusting a continuous parameter. The [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] for a laser transition is always close to unity by definition: if it were small, the transition would not produce a coherent quantum effect.

## 5. Classical limit
Dominant variable: [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]]
Validity limit: [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] <
In macroscopic systems, individual quanta are often invisible because [[escala_energia_clasica]] is huge compared with [[separacion_energia]]. This application measures when a continuous model is acceptable. [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] predicts whether discrete jumps matter. The validity limit does not mean quanta disappear; it means they fall below the relevant resolution. A thrown ball has [[escala_energia_clasica]] of several joules and [[separacion_energia]] near 10^-33 J, so [[cociente_cuantico_clasico]] is negligibly small and Newtonian mechanics works perfectly. An electron in an atom has both scales at comparable values — around electronvolts — so the ratio approaches unity and discrete quantum treatment is essential. This scale comparison is the most practical tool for deciding whether [[energia_cuantizada]] needs a quantum description or whether classical mechanics is sufficient.
