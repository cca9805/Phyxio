## Ideal model

The ideal model treats system and surroundings as clearly defined. The physical direction is decided by [[variacion_entropia_universo]], not by visual appearance. If real irreversibility is present, [[entropia_producida]] appears and the process departs from the reversible limit.

## Hypotheses

- The system-surroundings boundary is defined; if it changes, [[variacion_entropia_sistema]] and [[variacion_entropia_entorno]] cannot be compared directly.
- Macroscopic states have defined [[entropia]]; without local equilibrium, the balance is approximate.
- Macrostate statistics are relevant; if [[numero_microestados]] does not represent accessible configurations, the probability reading fails.
- [[entropia]] units are coherent; mixing J/K with multiples of [[constante_boltzmann]] without conversion distorts the result.

## Quantitative validity domain

For isolated spontaneous processes, [[variacion_entropia_universo]] >= 0. The reversible ideal is approached when [[entropia_producida]] <= 10^-6 J/K for a reference macroscopic process or when it is negligible relative to experimental resolution. For microscopic systems, comparison with [[constante_boltzmann]] is more appropriate.

## Model failure signals

The simple model fails if a sustained negative trend in [[variacion_entropia_universo]] is claimed, if the surroundings are omitted, if a local fluctuation is read as a global reversal, or if the process is so far from equilibrium that state entropy is not meaningful.

## Extended or alternative model

An extended model is needed for non-equilibrium systems, mesoscopic fluctuations, information dynamics, or spatially complex transport. Local entropy production, transport equations, or stochastic thermodynamics may then be required. The extended model does not invalidate the simple one; it contains it as a limit when corrections are negligible.

Knowing when to switch to a more detailed model depends on whether the global balance reproduces the measured outcome within experimental uncertainty. If the simple second-law balance fails to match observations, or if the spatial distribution of entropy production matters to the problem, transition to a transport or stochastic model is warranted. The [[grado_irreversibilidad]] indicator serves as a sentinel: if it is large compared with the experimental resolution, the simple model remains adequate.

## Operational comparison

| Situation | Useful model | Criterion |
| Ideal reversible process | Limit balance | nearly zero [[entropia_producida]] |
| Real process | Total second law | positive [[variacion_entropia_universo]] |
| Local system | Partial balance | check surroundings |
| Microscopic fluctuation | Statistical model | finite but tiny probability |

## Transition criterion between models

The criterion for switching models is observational: if the simple model reproduces measurements within experimental uncertainty, added complexity is unnecessary. When the global entropy balance diverges from observed outcomes beyond an acceptable margin — or when the spatial profile of entropy generation is relevant to the question — switch to a transport or stochastic description. This is the standard engineering judgment behind model selection: not correctness in principle, but adequacy in practice. The variable [[constante_boltzmann]] provides the microscopic scale reference that separates macroscopic from fluctuation-dominated regimes.
