# Applications

## 1. Conduction electrons in metals

Dominant variable: [[energia_fermi]]
Validity limit: [[temperatura_absoluta]] << [[temperatura_fermi]] and approximately free electrons

In a simple metal, conduction electrons are fermions filling states up to a scale marked by [[energia_fermi]]. What is measured or predicted is not just an average speed, but which states near [[potencial_quimico]] can change [[ocupacion_estado]] when an electric field is applied or [[temperatura_absoluta]] rises. [[densidad_numerica]], together with [[masa_particula]] and [[constante_planck_reducida]], fixes the filling scale. If [[temperatura_absoluta]] is far below [[temperatura_fermi]], most deep states remain blocked by occupation, and only a narrow region near [[potencial_quimico]] participates in transport. This constrains electronic heat capacity and explains why not all electrons contribute equally to conduction. In a resistivity measurement, the signal mainly comes from states near the occupation boundary, not from every occupied state. The graph of [[ocupacion_estado]] versus [[energia]] shows which region can respond. That is why a small thermal change can affect transport while leaving the deeply filled sea almost unchanged.

## 2. Semiconductors and band occupation

Dominant variable: [[ocupacion_estado]]
Validity limit: thermal equilibrium with well-defined [[potencial_quimico]] and bands not strongly disturbed

In semiconductors, the occupation of electronic states determines whether carriers are available in the conduction band or holes exist in the valence band. The [[energia]] of each state is compared with [[potencial_quimico]], and [[temperatura_absoluta]] smooths the occupation probability. What is predicted is the fraction of available states that will be occupied, not an individual trajectory. Fermionic statistics prevents many identical electrons from collapsing into the same state and forces the count to respect [[degeneracion_estado]]. In real devices, shifting [[potencial_quimico]] through doping strongly changes [[ocupacion_estado]] for relevant states. The limit appears under strong injection, rapid illumination, or nonequilibrium processes requiring separate quasi-chemical potentials. In a p-n junction, for example, changing the electric environment shifts which states lie near the occupation boundary. The useful prediction is carrier availability, not the exact position of every electron. This is the bridge between microscopic occupation rules and measurable current, threshold behavior, and temperature-dependent conductivity.

## 3. White dwarfs and degeneracy pressure

Dominant variable: [[densidad_numerica]]
Validity limit: degenerate fermions, with relativistic corrections if [[energia_fermi]] approaches relativistic scales

In a white dwarf, electrons form an extremely dense fermionic system. As [[densidad_numerica]] increases, Pauli exclusion forces states of higher [[energia]] to be occupied even at low [[temperatura_absoluta]]. What is constrained is the compressibility of matter: the volume cannot be reduced without pushing fermions into more energetic states. [[energia_fermi]] and [[temperatura_fermi]] indicate whether the gas is degenerate. If [[temperatura_absoluta]] is much lower than [[temperatura_fermi]], behavior is dominated by quantum occupation rather than classical thermal agitation. This application shows that [[ocupacion_estado]] and [[degeneracion_estado]] are not decorative microscopic details; they support astronomical objects. The model predicts when adding mass forces occupation of more energetic states. The observable signature is a pressure that does not mainly come from heating the gas. If the density grows, exclusion forces the occupation of higher momentum states, so compression is resisted even when thermal motion is small.

## 4. Ultracold atomic gases

Dominant variable: [[temperatura_fermi]]
Validity limit: dilute gas, [[temperatura_absoluta]] comparable to or lower than [[temperatura_fermi]], controlled interactions

In ultracold-atom laboratories, fermions are cooled until [[temperatura_absoluta]] approaches or falls below [[temperatura_fermi]]. What is measured is how the distribution of [[ocupacion_estado]] stops looking classical and begins to display a Fermi surface. The [[energia]] of trapped states, [[densidad_numerica]], and atomic [[masa_particula]] determine the scale. When the cloud expands or is imaged, the shape of the distribution reveals whether low states are almost filled. The validity of the simple reading depends on the gas being dilute and the interactions being controlled. Near strong resonances, the ideal interpretation must be replaced by a many-body model. These experiments are powerful because [[densidad_numerica]], confinement, and [[temperatura_absoluta]] can be tuned. One can directly observe how statistics limits occupation. The measured cloud profile becomes a map of available states, with low [[energia]] regions nearly filled and higher [[energia]] regions progressively emptied.

## 5. Occupation capacity in degenerate levels

Dominant variable: [[numero_ocupacion]]
Validity limit: 0 <

In atoms, nuclei, or grouped-level models, the practical question is how many fermions fit into a set of substates with the same [[energia]]. [[degeneracion_estado]] fixes the maximum and [[ocupacion_estado]] fixes the mean fraction per substate. The model predicts the expected [[numero_ocupacion]] of the group and prevents the error of treating occupation as unlimited. This reading is useful for building electronic configurations, interpreting nuclear shells, or counting states in discrete systems. The validity limit is that the grouped substates must truly be equivalent; if external fields split the degeneracy, each new [[energia]] value must be treated separately relative to [[potencial_quimico]]. In spectroscopy, this bookkeeping prevents assigning impossible transitions. It also distinguishes a level that is weakly occupied because of high [[energia]] from a level limited by the lack of available substates. This matters whenever one predicts shell filling, selection rules, or the response of a grouped level to changing [[temperatura_absoluta]].
