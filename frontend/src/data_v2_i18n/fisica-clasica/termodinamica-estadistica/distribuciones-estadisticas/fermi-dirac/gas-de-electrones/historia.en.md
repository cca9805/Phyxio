## Historical problem

At the beginning of the twentieth century, classical physics struggled to explain why metals conducted electricity and heat as they did. Drude's model treated electrons almost like a classical gas, but it did not properly explain electronic heat capacity or the stability of occupied states. What was missing was the idea that [[densidad_electronica]] could impose its own energy scale, different from the ordinary thermal scale associated with [[temperatura_absoluta]].

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The obstacle was imagining many electrons distributed over states without following Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics. In a classical view, increasing [[numero_electrones]] inside a [[volumen]] would only increase density and collisions, not create a quantum filling frontier. Pauli exclusion and Fermi-Dirac statistics changed the picture: electrons cannot all accumulate in one state, and [[ocupacion_estado]] must depend on [[energia_estado]], [[potencial_quimico]], and [[temperatura_absoluta]].

## What changed

With Fermi and Dirac came a description in which [[energia_fermi]] organizes state population. Sommerfeld applied these ideas to metals and showed that, even at room [[temperatura_absoluta]], [[temperatura_fermi]] could be enormous. This explained why only electrons near [[energia_fermi]] contribute efficiently to many thermal and electrical properties. [[constante_planck_reducida]] became more than a microscopic constant: it set the scale for counting states.

## Impact on physics

The electron gas opened a path toward modern solid-state physics. It helped interpret metals, semiconductors, electron emission, heat capacities, and later condensed-matter phenomena. It also connected to dense matter: if [[densidad_electronica]] becomes extremely large, [[energia_fermi]] and [[velocidad_fermi]] can support degeneracy pressure.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the ideal electron gas is a first approximation, not the whole story. Band models, effective masses, and superconductivity refine it, but preserve its central lesson: electronic occupation is limited by fermionic character. The modern question remains the same with better tools: how [[densidad_electronica]] determines [[energia_fermi]], and how that scale controls [[ocupacion_estado]] around [[potencial_quimico]].
