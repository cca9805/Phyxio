## Historical problem

The historical problem was to understand how systems made of enormous numbers of particles could display stable macroscopic properties. Microscopic mechanics described individual motions, but by itself it did not explain why pressure, temperature, or state occupation could remain steady.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The conceptual obstacle was the confusion between equilibrium and rest. In gases, solids, and molecular systems, particles keep moving and changing state. Physics needed a statistical reading in which [[probabilidad_estado]] and [[flujo_probabilidad]] replaced the individual trajectory as central objects.

## What changed

Statistical physics introduced the ensemble, probability normalization, and balance among transitions. Equilibrium could then be interpreted as a stationary distribution. Boltzmann showed that the number of microstates compatible with a macrostate determines [[entropia]]. Gibbs extended that to the ensemble: instead of tracking one real trajectory, one averages over a mental collection of system copies. That collection lets [[probabilidad_estado]] and [[flujo_probabilidad]] be defined precisely, turning equilibrium from a statement about individual trajectories into a statement about a distribution. Later, detailed balance made it possible to distinguish true equilibrium from steady states with internal currents.

## Impact on physics

This change connected [[entropia]], [[temperatura]], [[energia_estado]], and [[peso_boltzmann]] with macroscopic observations in a quantitative and testable way. Equilibrium stopped being a picture of stillness and became a precise condition on compensated statistical flows.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the same idea appears in Monte Carlo simulations, condensed matter, physical chemistry, biological systems, and nonequilibrium processes. This leaf keeps that legacy: recognizing equilibrium requires asking whether probabilities stop changing and whether net [[flujo_probabilidad]] vanishes.
