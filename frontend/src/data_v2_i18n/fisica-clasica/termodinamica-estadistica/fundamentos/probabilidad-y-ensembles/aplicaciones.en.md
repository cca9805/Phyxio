# Applications

## 1. Occupation estimates in molecular simulations
Dominant variable: [[probabilidad_estado]]
Validity limit: 0 ≤ [[probabilidad_estado]] ≤ 1 and total sum equal to 1
In a molecular dynamics simulation, rather than tracking every [[estado_microscopico]] at every instant, many configurations compatible with the same [[restriccion_macroscopica]], such as fixed temperature and volume, are collected. Each recorded configuration acts as one copy of the [[ensemble]]. The [[probabilidad_estado]] of each class of configuration is estimated as the [[conteo_sistemas]] divided by the total number of sampled configurations. If the sampling is broad, this frequency converges to the ideal [[ensemble]] distribution. The practical result is identifying which conformations dominate, which are rare, and how each class contributes to the [[valor_medio_ensemble]] of an observable such as mean potential energy or molecular radius of gyration. Normalization is mandatory before computing any average; partial sampling produces a biased distribution that overestimates the [[probabilidad_estado]] of the most frequently visited states. The [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] signals when sampling covers only part of the configuration space and the estimated distribution is not representative of the full system. The implicit [[peso_estadistico]] of each configuration class must be consistent with the rules of the chosen [[ensemble]], and any change in [[restriccion_macroscopica]] requires recalculating the full procedure from the beginning.

## 2. Energy averages in thermal populations
Dominant variable: [[valor_medio_ensemble]]
Validity limit: all [[valor_estado]] must have the same unit and probabilities must be normalized
In a system in contact with a thermal bath, accessible [[estado_microscopico]] are weighted according to canonical-type distributions. For each energy level, the [[peso_estadistico]] depends on temperature and the value of that level. The [[valor_medio_ensemble]] of energy is computed by summing each [[valor_estado]] multiplied by its [[probabilidad_estado]]. This average is what thermodynamics calls internal energy. When temperature changes, the probability distribution shifts and the [[valor_medio_ensemble]] shifts with it. The [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] of energy is proportional to the heat capacity of the system: ensembles with high [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] can absorb more heat without a large temperature change. In practice, the calculation requires normalizing Boltzmann-type weights, which depend exponentially on energy and temperature, and then applying the weighted average formula. The calculation fails if [[valor_estado]] from different observables are mixed or if probabilities are not normalized before averaging, producing a [[valor_medio_ensemble]] at the wrong scale with no connection to any measurable thermodynamic quantity.

## 3. Fluctuation control in small systems
Dominant variable: [[fluctuacion_ensemble]]
Validity limit: [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] cannot be neglected if it exceeds ten percent of [[valor_medio_ensemble]]
In systems with few components, such as individual molecules studied by single-molecule spectroscopy or nanoscale quantum devices, the [[valor_medio_ensemble]] alone can be misleading. A system with only three or four accessible [[estado_microscopico]] may have a [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] reaching fifty percent of the mean. A single measurement of one copy at one moment will produce a result that differs noticeably from the [[valor_medio_ensemble]]. Characterizing the system properly requires [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] as additional and indispensable information. The experimental [[conteo_sistemas]] must be large enough that observed frequencies represent the ideal distribution; if [[numero_copias_ensemble]] is small, frequencies fluctuate across experiments and the obtained mean does not converge to the model [[valor_medio_ensemble]]. Experimental design in nanoscience is guided by this: a small number of repetitions gives a noisy estimate of [[probabilidad_estado]], while a large number reveals the real [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] without confusing it with sampling noise. The [[peso_estadistico]] of extreme states, even when low, can contribute significantly to [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] even when the [[valor_medio_ensemble]] appears stable.

## 4. Weight normalization in discrete models
Dominant variable: [[peso_estadistico]]
Validity limit: weights must be nonnegative and belong to the same set of [[estado_microscopico]]
In many statistical models, states do not come with direct probabilities but with relative [[peso_estadistico]] derived from symmetry arguments, quantum selection rules, or unnormalized Boltzmann distributions. Normalization converts these weights into the physical [[probabilidad_estado]] of the [[ensemble]]. The procedure is always the same: sum all [[peso_estadistico]] in the set compatible with the [[restriccion_macroscopica]], then divide each individual weight by that sum. Only then can [[valor_medio_ensemble]] or [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] be computed in a valid way. The most common error in this context is applying the average formula using raw [[peso_estadistico]] without normalization, producing results whose magnitude does not represent any physical prediction. A second error is including incompatible [[estado_microscopico]] in the weight sum, inflating the denominator and artificially reducing all probabilities. The [[conteo_sistemas]] can verify the procedure: if the ideal [[numero_copias_ensemble]] is known, the [[conteo_sistemas]] of each state must be consistent with its normalized [[probabilidad_estado]], and any discrepancy signals an error in the state definition or in the weight sum.

## 5. Frequencies in repeated experiments
Dominant variable: [[conteo_sistemas]]
Validity limit: [[numero_copias_ensemble]] must be large enough for statistically stable frequencies
When an experiment is repeated many times under the same preparation, the number of times each macroscopic outcome appears constitutes an experimental [[conteo_sistemas]]. If the total number of repetitions equals the real [[numero_copias_ensemble]] of the experiment, the frequency of each outcome estimates the model [[probabilidad_estado]]. This frequentist interpretation of the [[ensemble]] allows direct connection between the model and the data: the theoretical [[probabilidad_estado]] must agree with the observed frequency within statistical uncertainties. The [[valor_medio_ensemble]] computed from theoretical probabilities must reproduce the experimental mean of outcomes, and the theoretical [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] must be consistent with the observed variance. If persistent discrepancy exists, either the distribution model is wrong or the declared [[restriccion_macroscopica]] does not accurately describe the experimental preparation. An experimental [[numero_copias_ensemble]] that is too small gives noisy [[conteo_sistemas]] estimates; a practical criterion is that the count for each state should exceed thirty for the frequency to be a reasonably stable estimator of [[probabilidad_estado]]. The [[peso_estadistico]] of the model must be adjusted to match the data before using derived probabilities to compute [[valor_medio_ensemble]] or predict [[fluctuacion_ensemble]] under new conditions.
