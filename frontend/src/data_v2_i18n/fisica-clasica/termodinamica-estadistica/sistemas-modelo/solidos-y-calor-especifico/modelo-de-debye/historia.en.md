## Historical problem

At the beginning of the twentieth century, classical physics could explain the Dulong-Petit limit fairly well: many solids had [[calor_especifico_molar]] close to 3R at high [[temperatura]]. The problem appeared when solids were cooled. Experiments showed that [[calor_especifico]] decreased much more strongly than classical equipartition predicted and tended toward very small values. Classical theory had no natural way to switch off vibrational modes.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Einstein’s model introduced identical quantized oscillators and explained part of the decrease, but it did not reproduce the correct low-[[temperatura]] law. The missing ingredient was the collective nature of the lattice: atoms do not vibrate as isolated copies, but as coupled waves. The challenge was to count modes in a way that respected both the solid’s structure and quantum energy spacing.

## What changed

Debye treated the crystal as an elastic medium with acoustic modes up to a maximum [[frecuencia_debye]]. This cutoff is chosen to preserve the correct number of vibrational modes associated with [[numero_atomos]]. By converting that frequency into [[temperatura_debye]], the model provided a clear material scale. The cubic low-[[temperatura]] law emerged naturally from counting accessible low-frequency modes.

## Impact on physics

The model connected macroscopic thermodynamics with microscopic lattice vibrations. It made it possible to interpret [[calor_especifico_molar]] curves, estimate [[temperatura_debye]], and distinguish quantum and classical thermal regimes. It also showed that [[constante_gases]] fixes the high-[[temperatura]] ceiling, while the material’s vibrational structure controls how that ceiling is approached.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the Debye model remains a first compass for phonons, solids, low-[[temperatura]] physics, and materials. Modern calculations often use measured or computed densities of states, but the central idea survives: [[temperatura]] activates modes, [[temperatura_debye]] sets the scale, and [[calor_especifico]] reveals how many vibrational channels are participating.
