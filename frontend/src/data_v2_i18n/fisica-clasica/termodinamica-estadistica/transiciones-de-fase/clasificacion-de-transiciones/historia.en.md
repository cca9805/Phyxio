## Historical problem

The historical study of phase transitions began with visible phenomena such as boiling, melting, freezing, and volume changes. The problem was that not all changes followed the same pattern. Some showed phase coexistence and [[calor_latente]], while others seemed to transform without a discontinuous energy exchange. The modern question emerged when physicists tried to organize these behaviors using quantities such as [[temperatura]], [[temperatura_critica]], [[energia_libre_gibbs]], and [[parametro_de_orden]].

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Before precise classification, it was tempting to call any abrupt observation a transition of the same kind. However, appearance alone does not separate equilibrium, kinetics, hysteresis, or finite-size effects. There was also no unified language for comparing liquid-gas behavior with magnetic transitions. The conceptual obstacle was understanding that the decisive feature is not only the final phase, but the continuity or discontinuity of thermodynamic quantities and [[parametro_de_orden]].

## What changed

Thermodynamic potentials made it possible to use [[energia_libre_gibbs]] as a stability criterion. Classification by derivatives organized transitions according to which quantity became discontinuous or singular. Later, the idea of [[parametro_de_orden]] made changes of symmetry and internal organization measurable. With [[exponente_critico_beta]] and [[amplitud_critica]], continuous transitions were no longer treated as merely smooth changes, but as critical phenomena with quantitative laws.

## Impact on physics

This classification connected macroscopic thermodynamics with microscopic physics. [[calor_latente]] and [[salto_entropia]] identify first-order behavior, while continuous disappearance of [[parametro_de_orden]] reveals criticality. The language became useful in magnetism, superconductivity, fluids, mixtures, and functional materials. It also showed that a single measurement can mislead: classification requires coherence among energy, entropy, internal order, and experimental conditions.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the classification of transitions remains a gateway to critical phenomena, scaling, universality, and symmetry breaking. In Phyxio, this leaf turns that history into a reading tool: near [[temperatura_critica]], one must ask whether [[calor_latente]], [[salto_entropia]], or [[salto_parametro_de_orden]] exists, and how [[energia_libre_gibbs]] behaves. Thus a label such as [[orden_de_transicion]] becomes not verbal memory, but a physical diagnosis.
