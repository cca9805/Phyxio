## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Assuming temperature keeps rising during the phase change

**What it is**
The student treats the phase change as ordinary sensible heating. Energy is added and temperature is expected to rise even though the system is at [[T_trans]] with two phases present.

**Why it happens**
Students remember that heat often raises temperature, but forget that during a transition the energy changes molecular structure. In melting it breaks crystal order; in vaporization it separates liquid molecules.

**Warning sign**
A temperature above [[T_trans]] appears while some initial phase remains, or the graph is drawn without a horizontal plateau.

**Correction**
Before computing final temperature, apply the latent segment using [[Q_lat]], [[m]], and [[L]]. Until the transition is complete, temperature remains fixed at [[T_trans]].

**Mini contrast example**
If 250 kJ are supplied to 500 g of ice at 0 C, 167 kJ are first spent on melting. Only the remaining energy warms the liquid water.

## Model errors

### Error 2: Confusing latent heat of melting with latent heat of vaporization

**What it is**
The melting tabulated value is used for vaporization, or the reverse. The general formula is the same, but [[L]] must match the type of transition.

**Why it happens**
Both data are called latent heat and often appear close together in tables. The student memorizes one water number without attaching it to the physical process.

**Warning sign**
The heat to vaporize water is of the same order as the heat to melt the same mass of ice. For water, vaporization is several times larger than melting.

**Correction**
Read the physical verb first: melt, solidify, vaporize, or condense. Then choose the correct [[L]] and keep the sign of [[Q_lat]] according to whether the system absorbs or releases energy.

**Mini contrast example**
To vaporize 200 g of water at 100 C, use the vaporization value. If the melting value is used, the result is almost seven times too small.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 3: Not checking whether available energy completes the phase change

**What it is**
The whole mass [[m]] is assumed to change state without comparing available energy with the required latent segment.

**Why it happens**
In calorimetry problems students quickly search for final temperature. But with two phases, the unknown may be transformed mass rather than temperature.

**Warning sign**
The computed temperature lies below or above [[T_trans]] even though the setup assumed a complete transition. Another sign is available heat lower than the amount required by [[m]] and [[L]].

**Correction**
First compute the latent cost of the mass to be transformed. If energy is not enough, the system remains at [[T_trans]] and only a fraction of mass changes state.

**Mini contrast example**
If hot water can release 10 kJ and melting all the ice requires 16 kJ, there is no final temperature above 0 C. The correct state is an ice-water mixture at [[T_trans]].

## Interpretation errors

### Error 4: Confusing the transition temperature of a pure substance with that of a solution

**What it is**
The [[T_trans]] of pure water is used for a mixture, saline solution, or fluid at pressure different from atmospheric pressure.

**Why it happens**
The values 0 C and 100 C for water are familiar, but they are not universal. They change with pressure and composition.

**Warning sign**
The statement mentions salt, solute, high pressure, autoclave, real refrigerant, or non-pure fluid, and a standard transition temperature is still used.

**Correction**
Identify material, pressure, and purity before choosing [[T_trans]]. If the problem gives an effective value, use that value even if it differs from pure water.

**Mini contrast example**
Sea water does not freeze exactly at 0 C. Using pure water for sea ice shifts the transition point and changes the latent heat balance.

## Unit errors

### Error 5: Using L in J/g instead of J/kg with mass in kg

**What it is**
A mass in kg is mixed with a table value of [[L]] in J/g, or an energy in kJ is mixed with a balance written in J.

**Why it happens**
Tables use several scales to keep numbers manageable. Copying only the number without the unit shifts the result by factors of one thousand.

**Warning sign**
[[Q_lat]] is a thousand times larger or smaller than expected, or it dominates the balance absurdly for a small mass.

**Correction**
Unify units before applying the formula. To obtain [[Q_lat]] in J, use [[m]] in kg and [[L]] in J/kg.

**Mini contrast example**
With 200 g of water, write the mass as 0.200 kg if [[L]] is used in J/kg. If the table is in J/g, convert the data before multiplying.

## Quick self-control rule

1. If two phases coexist, temperature must be [[T_trans]].
2. Before searching for final temperature, check whether available energy completes the latent segment.
3. Verify that [[L]] matches the process and the units used.
4. In the temperature-heat graph, every phase change must appear as a horizontal plateau.
