## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: Confusing total heat capacity C with specific heat c

**Why it seems correct**
Both quantities express resistance to heating and often appear together in tables or exercises.

**Why it is incorrect**
[[C]] belongs to the complete body and is measured in J/K. Specific heat belongs to the material and must be combined with mass to obtain total heat capacity.

**Detection signal**
The result does not change when the mass is doubled, or the unit contains per kilogram when J/K was expected.

**Conceptual correction**
Always ask whether the quantity refers to the material or to the complete object. For the complete object, use [[C]].

**Mini contrast example**
One litre of water has much larger [[C]] than a spoonful of water, even though the material and its specific heat are the same.

## Model errors

### Error 2: Computing [[DeltaT]] as initial temperature minus final temperature

**Why it seems correct**
The student wants the size of the change and uses a positive subtraction without thinking about physical direction.

**Why it is incorrect**
[[DeltaT]] keeps sign: final minus initial. If the body cools, [[DeltaT]] and [[Q]] are negative.

**Detection signal**
A cooling body appears to have absorbed heat, or a warming body appears with negative [[Q]].

**Conceptual correction**
Fix the convention before substituting: final temperature minus initial temperature. Then check that [[Q]] and [[DeltaT]] have the same sign.

**Mini contrast example**
Water going from 80 C to 30 C cools. The temperature change is negative and the heat exchanged by the water must also be negative.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 3: Obtaining negative [[C]] and not identifying it as an error

**Why it seems correct**
Dividing two quantities with opposite signs can produce a negative number, and the student accepts it as algebra.

**Why it is incorrect**
[[C]] is positive for stable bodies. A negative value means signs of [[Q]] or [[DeltaT]] were mixed.

**Detection signal**
Heat capacity is below zero or changes sign between two measurements of the same body.

**Conceptual correction**
Check heat direction and the order of temperatures. Do not interpret negative [[C]] as a real property.

**Mini contrast example**
If a resistor heats a sample, [[Q]] is positive and [[DeltaT]] must be positive. If one of them is negative, the sign was assigned wrongly.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 4: Believing the material with larger specific heat always has larger [[C]]

**Why it seems correct**
High specific heat makes a given mass change temperature slowly, so it seems to determine everything.

**Why it is incorrect**
Mass also matters. A large iron piece may have greater [[C]] than a small amount of water.

**Detection signal**
Materials are compared without mentioning mass or body size.

**Conceptual correction**
Compare complete bodies, not only materials. The question in this leaf is always about [[C]] of the real object.

**Mini contrast example**
A glass of water may have lower [[C]] than a large metal beam, even though water has higher specific heat than the metal.

## Validity errors

### Error 5: Applying sensible heat when a phase change occurs

**Why it seems correct**
The heat-capacity formula is simple and seems to work for any heating process.

**Why it is incorrect**
During a phase change, part of the heat is latent and does not produce [[DeltaT]]. The linear relation with [[C]] no longer describes the whole process.

**Detection signal**
The temperature interval crosses melting, boiling, or an experimental temperature plateau.

**Conceptual correction**
Split the process into sensible segments and latent segments. Use [[C]] only where temperature changes.

**Mini contrast example**
Heating ice from below zero to liquid water is not one segment with constant [[C]]: first ice warms, then it melts at constant temperature, and then water warms.

## Quick self-control rule

1. [[C]] describes a complete body, not only the material.
2. [[Q]] and [[DeltaT]] must have the same sign.
3. Negative [[C]] means sign or data error.
4. If a phase change occurs, split sensible and latent heat.
