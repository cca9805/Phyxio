## Ideal model

The base model for evaluating irreversibility assumes that the **surroundings** behave as an ideal thermal reservoir at constant temperature [[T_ent]] (infinite heat capacity), and that the **system** can be described by well-defined equilibrium states at the beginning and end of the process. The system's entropy is calculated via a reversible path between those states (state function), while the surroundings' entropy is calculated directly as −[[Q_sis]]/[[T_ent]].

The central simplification is treating the surroundings as a trivial thermodynamic object (reservoir) whose only relevant quantity is [[T_ent]]. This allows calculating [[DeltaS_ent]] without needing to know internal details of the surroundings. Conserved quantities are total universe energy and mass; the quantity that can only increase is [[DeltaS_univ]].

## Hypotheses

- **Ideal thermal reservoir**: the surroundings have such large heat capacity that their temperature does not change when absorbing or releasing heat to the system.
  - Violation: if the surroundings are small (finite capacity), their temperature changes and one must integrate to calculate [[DeltaS_ent]].

- **Defined equilibrium states**: at the beginning and end of the process, both system and surroundings are in internal thermodynamic equilibrium (uniform temperature, pressure, and composition).
  - Violation: during the irreversible process, the system may be out of equilibrium (internal gradients), but the initial and final entropy must be well-defined.

- **Single identified irreversibility source**: the generation formula for heat transfer assumes the only irreversibility is the thermal gradient. It does not include friction or mixing.
  - Violation: if there is mechanical friction, the heat generated by friction must be included as an additional source of [[S_gen]].

- **Measurable real heat**: the exchanged heat [[Q_sis]] can be measured or calculated independently (first law: Q = ΔU + W).
  - Violation: if the process is so fast or turbulent that Q cannot be measured, the first law with ΔU and W measurements is used.

- **Closed system**: no mass transfer with the surroundings during the process.
  - Violation: in open systems, there are additional entropic contributions from mass flow (mixing entropy, transport entropy).

## Quantitative validity domain

The constant-temperature reservoir model is valid when:
- The surroundings' heat capacity is at least 100 times greater than the system's ([[T_ent]] variation below 1%).
- The process occurs on a time scale slow relative to the thermal relaxation of the surroundings.
- Temperatures are in the range 50–5000 K (below 50 K, quantum effects; above 5000 K, molecular dissociation).

> [!NOTE]
> For a 1 kg block (c_p ≈ 500 J/(kg·K)) in contact with a lake at 293 K, the reservoir hypothesis is excellent: the lake (>10⁶ kg of water) does not appreciably change temperature.

The generation formula for thermal gradient is quantitatively exact when:
- Both temperatures are constant throughout the entire transfer (ideal reservoirs on both sides).
- Transfer is performed without additional resistances (no friction in the conducting medium).
- Error is < 1% if each body's temperature variation is < 1% of its absolute T during the process.

## Model failure signals

- **[[DeltaS_univ]] < 0**: unambiguous signal of error (not model failure, but incorrect calculation or erroneous data).
- **[[DeltaS_univ]] = 0 exactly for a real process**: if there is any finite gradient, friction, or mixing, the result should be > 0. An exact zero indicates it was calculated as if reversible.
- **Calculated [[S_gen]] very different from experimentally measured**: possible signal that there are unincluded irreversibility sources (friction, mixing, radiative losses).
- **Surroundings [[T_ent]] changes appreciably during the process**: the reservoir hypothesis fails and one must use an integral surroundings balance.
- **Persistent internal gradients in the system**: if the system does not reach internal equilibrium between initial and final state measurements, [[DeltaS_sis]] is not well-defined.

## Extended or alternative model

To overcome the limitations of the reservoir model:

- **Integral entropy balance**: when the surroundings change temperature, calculate [[DeltaS_ent]] by numerical integration, treating the surroundings as a body with finite heat capacity.

- **Multiple source model**: total generation is decomposed into thermal, friction, mixing, and reaction contributions. Each source has its own generation formula based on flow and thermodynamic force.

- **Non-equilibrium thermodynamics** (Onsager, Prigogine): for processes far from equilibrium, the entropy production rate is expressed as a sum of products of thermodynamic flows (heat, mass, reaction) by their conjugate forces.

The transition to the extended model is justified when:
- The surroundings cannot be modelled as a reservoir ([[T_ent]] changes by more than 5%).
- There are multiple irreversibility sources that must be broken down.
- The temporal generation rate (J/(K·s)) is required for steady-state process design.

## Operational comparison

| Criterion | Reservoir model | Integral / multiple source model |
|---|---|---|
| **Precision** | Excellent if [[T_ent]] is truly constant | High in all cases |
| **Complexity** | Simple algebraic formula | Numerical integration or sum of contributions |
| **Application range** | Surroundings >> system thermally | Universal |
| **Main advantage** | Direct calculation, immediate understanding | Detailed breakdown of losses |
| **Main limitation** | Fails if [[T_ent]] is not constant | Requires detailed process data |
| **Typical use** | Teaching, exercises, quick estimates | Industrial design, optimisation, energy auditing |

When to switch model: if [[T_ent]] is no longer nearly constant, if several sources of [[S_gen]] are comparable, or if the system remains far from internal equilibrium, abandon the reservoir model and use the integral balance or the multiple-source model.
