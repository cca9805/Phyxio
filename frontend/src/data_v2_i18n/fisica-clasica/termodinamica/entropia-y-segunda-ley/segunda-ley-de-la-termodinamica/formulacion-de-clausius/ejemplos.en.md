# Exam-type example

## Problem statement

A domestic refrigerator must maintain its interior compartment at a temperature of 277 K (4 °C) when the ambient temperature is 298 K (25 °C). It is desired to extract an amount of heat [[Q]] = 5000 J from the cold interior to the warm environment.

Determine:
a) Calculate the minimum theoretical work required to perform this transfer.
b) Determine the coefficient of performance (COP) of Carnot for these temperatures.
c) If the real refrigerator has a COP that is 30% of the Carnot value, calculate the actual work consumed and the energy efficiency of the appliance.
d) Calculate the entropy change of the universe for the real process.

## Data

- Interior temperature (cold reservoir): [[T_B]] = 277 K (4 °C)
- Ambient temperature (hot reservoir): [[T_A]] = 298 K (25 °C)
- Heat to extract: [[Q]] = 5000 J
- Temperature difference: [[DeltaT]] = [[T_A]] - [[T_B]] = 21 K
- Real refrigerator performance: COP_real = 0.30 × COP_Carnot

## System definition

The analyzed system is the refrigerator, operating as a cyclic device that transfers heat from the cold reservoir (interior of the fridge) to the hot reservoir (external environment). The system receives electrical work from outside and transfers heat against the natural thermal gradient.

- **Cold reservoir**: Refrigerator interior ([[T_B]] = 277 K)
- **Hot reservoir**: External environment ([[T_A]] = 298 K)
- **Process**: Forced transfer of [[Q]] = 5000 J from cold to hot
- **External agent**: Compressor consuming electrical work [[W]]

## Physical model

The infinite thermal reservoirs model with constant temperatures is applied. The refrigerator operates through a compression-expansion cycle of a refrigerant that:

1. Evaporates in the cold interior at low pressure, absorbing heat [[Q]] from the compartment
2. Is mechanically compressed (consuming work [[W]]) raising its pressure and temperature
3. Condenses in the hot exterior, delivering heat [[Q]] + [[W]] to the environment
4. Expands, returning to initial conditions

The model assumes:
- Stationary cyclic operation
- Constant temperatures of both reservoirs
- Reversible process for the Carnot limit
- Real irreversibilities for practical calculation

## Model justification

The thermal reservoirs model is applicable because:
- The refrigerator is a cyclic device operating continuously
- The heat capacity of the interior and environment is effectively infinite compared to [[Q]] = 5000 J
- Temperatures remain constant due to thermostat action and environment thermal inertia

Real irreversibilities (mechanical friction, finite temperature transfers, thermal losses) reduce performance compared to the Carnot ideal, justifying the 0.30 factor applied.

## Numerical substitution

**Part a) Minimum theoretical work:**

Applying the minimum work formula:

{{f:trabajo_reversion_clausius}}

For the minimum work, multiplying 5000 J by the temperature difference (21 K) and dividing by the cold reservoir temperature (277 K) gives approximately 379 J. Therefore [[W_min]] ≈ 379 J.

**Part b) Carnot COP:**

{{f:eficiencia_carnot_refrigeracion}}

For the Carnot COP, dividing 277 K by the temperature difference (21 K) gives approximately 13.2. Therefore COP_Carnot ≈ 13.2.

**Part c) Real work and efficiency:**

COP_real ≈ 0.30 × 13.2 → 3.96 ≈ 4.0

The actual work consumed is:

W_real is calculated as Q/COP_real → 5000 J / 4.0 → 1250 J

**Part d) Universe entropy change:**

Cold reservoir entropy: [[DeltaS_B]] = -5000 J / 277 K ≈ -18.05 J/K

Heat delivered to environment: Q_ambiente results from 5000 J + 1250 J → 6250 J

Hot reservoir entropy: [[DeltaS_A]] = 6250 J / 298 K ≈ 20.97 J/K

Entropy generated by irreversibilities:
[[DeltaS_generada]] = [[DeltaS_A]] + [[DeltaS_B]] = 20.97 - 18.05 = 2.92 J/K

## Dimensional validation

- Work units: [W] = J (consistent with energy [M L² T⁻²])
- COP units: dimensionless [1] = [K]/[K] (consistent)
- Entropy units: [ΔS] = J/K (consistent with [M L² T⁻² Θ⁻¹])

Order of magnitude verification:
- Minimum work (379 J) << Transferred heat (5000 J): correct, W/Q ≈ 0.08
- Carnot COP (13.2) >> 1: correct, high theoretical limit for small [[DeltaT]]
- Real work (1250 J) > Minimum work (379 J): correct, real process is less efficient
- Generated entropy (2.92 J/K) > 0: correct, second law satisfied

## Physical interpretation

The result shows that extracting 5000 J of heat from the cold interior of a refrigerator theoretically requires a minimum of 379 J of electrical work. This absolute limit, derived directly from the Clausius statement, cannot be surpassed by any real device.

However, the real refrigerator consumes 1250 J, that is, 3.3 times more work than the theoretical limit. This difference is due to the multiple irreversibilities of the real process: mechanical friction in the compressor, heat transfers through finite temperature differences, and thermal losses through the compartment walls.

The real COP of 4.0 means that for every joule of electricity consumed, approximately 4 joules of heat are extracted from the cold interior. This represents a significant energy gain compared to the hypothetical situation of direct heating, where 1 J of electricity would only produce 1 J of cooling.

Physically, the increase in universe entropy (2.92 J/K) quantifies the irreversibility of the process. This entropic increment is inevitable in any real process and represents the "degradation" of energy: high-quality energy (electricity) is partially converted into low-quality thermal energy (dispersion to the environment).

If the refrigerator operated with the Carnot COP (13.2), it would only consume 379 J, reducing the electricity bill by 70%. However, reaching this limit would require infinitely slow and frictionless processes, impractical in a domestic application.

# Real-world example

## Context

**Heat pumps** are devices that directly apply the principle of the Clausius statement to heat buildings efficiently. Unlike refrigerators (which extract heat from a cold space), heat pumps extract heat from the external environment (air, water, or soil, even at low temperature) and transfer it to the building interior at higher temperature.

In a dwelling in temperate climate, the underlying soil maintains an approximate temperature of 285 K (12 °C) throughout the year, while the interior needs to be heated to 293 K (20 °C).

## Physical estimation

To maintain a room at 293 K when the exterior (soil) is at 285 K, a heat pump must transfer [[Q]] = 10 000 J of heat from the cold soil to the warm interior for each operation cycle.

Temperature difference: [[DeltaT]] results from 293 K minus 285 K → approximately 8 K

Minimum theoretical work according to Clausius:

{{f:trabajo_reversion_clausius}}

For the minimum work, multiplying 10000 J by the temperature difference (8 K) and dividing by the cold reservoir temperature (285 K) gives approximately 281 J. Therefore [[W_min]] ≈ 281 J.

Carnot COP for heating (heat pump):
The Carnot COP for heating yields approximately 36.6 (for [[T_A]] = 293 K and [[DeltaT]] = 8 K)

A real heat pump, with 40% efficiency relative to Carnot, would have:
COP_real results from 0.40 × 36.6 → approximately 14.6

Actual work consumed: dividing 10000 J by 14.6 yields approximately 685 J

## Interpretation

The result demonstrates the extraordinary energy efficiency of heat pumps: to provide 10000 J of heat to the dwelling, only 685 J of electrical energy are consumed. The rest of the heat (9315 J) is extracted freely from the external soil.

Comparatively, a conventional electric resistance would only produce 685 J of heat for the same 685 J of electricity. The heat pump is approximately 14.6 times more efficient than direct electric heating.

This energy gain is possible precisely because the heat pump **does not generate** heat, but **pumps** existing heat from a cold source (the soil) to a warm destination (the dwelling). The electrical work only serves to force this transfer against the natural thermal gradient, following exactly the Clausius principle.

The Carnot limit indicates that theoretically even greater efficiency could be obtained if compression and finite-temperature-transfer irreversibilities were reduced. However, even with a real [[COP_R]] near 14.6, the technology remains very energetically attractive.

The small temperature difference (8 K) between the cold source and warm destination is key to this high efficiency. In colder climates where the soil is at 275 K and the dwelling at 293 K ([[DeltaT]] = 18 K), the COP would drop to approximately 16 (Carnot) and 6.4 (real), significantly reducing the energy advantage.
