# Applications of the Kelvin-Planck Statement

## 1. Steam Thermal Power Plants

Steam power plants are the classical application of the Kelvin-Planck statement. Fuel heats water, steam drives a turbine, and the condenser rejects [[Q_C]] to a cold reservoir. The design goal is to increase [[W]] and [[eta]], but never to eliminate thermal rejection completely.

Dominant variable: [[eta]] compared with [[eta_carnot]], because that comparison separates technical improvement from physical impossibility.

Validity limit: If start-up, shutdown or fast load changes are analyzed, the stationary cyclic model must be replaced by a transient plant model.

In practice, this application forces the condenser to be treated as a central part of performance. A plant that rejects much heat to the environment is not necessarily poorly conceived: part of that rejection is imposed by the second law. Reasonable improvement means raising steam temperatures, reducing irreversibilities and using residual heat, not promising that [[Q_C]] will disappear.

## 2. Internal Combustion Engines

In gasoline or diesel engines, part of the heat released by fuel becomes mechanical work and another part leaves through exhaust, cooling and radiation. Although the real cycle is not perfectly reversible, Kelvin-Planck explains why no engine can convert all fuel thermal energy into shaft work.

Dominant variable: Available [[Q_H]] per cycle and the fraction converted into useful [[W]].

Validity limit: The simple model does not include finite combustion, pumping losses, mixture preparation, friction or material constraints; real performance needs an Otto or Diesel cycle model.

The Kelvin-Planck reading prevents comparison with an impossible ideal. In city driving, much heat leaves through the radiator and exhaust; on highways, the operating point improves, but thermal rejection remains. This is why a 5 percent efficiency improvement can be important even though the engine remains far from total conversion.

## 3. Gas Turbines and Aircraft Engines

Gas turbines use high temperatures to raise the performance limit. Increasing [[T_H]] improves [[eta_carnot]], but materials, blade cooling and emissions constrain the increase. Kelvin-Planck still states that part of the energy must leave as residual heat in the jet or exhaust gases.

Dominant variable: [[T_H]], because it determines the thermodynamic ceiling together with [[T_C]].

Validity limit: In propulsion, not everything is evaluated as shaft work; jet kinetic energy appears, so the balance must be adapted to the chosen system.

The statement also helps distinguish thermal efficiency from propulsive efficiency. A turbojet can produce effective thrust without converting all heat into shaft work. The correct analysis defines the system, identifies [[Q_H]] and separates residual heat, kinetic energy and useful work according to the technical objective.

## 4. Industrial Waste Heat Recovery

In furnaces, refineries and chemical processes, waste heat has value, but not all of it can become electricity. The temperature of the residual heat determines the maximum fraction recoverable as work. Kelvin-Planck prevents unrealistic estimates that treat all residual heat as available electrical energy.

Dominant variable: Effective [[T_C]] of the sink and the temperature of the residual source.

Validity limit: If sources have variable temperature, exergy analysis or heat integration is required, not only an average efficiency.

This application matters in energy audits. Two streams with the same thermal energy can have different value if their temperatures differ. Kelvin-Planck anticipates that idea: low-temperature heat has less capacity to become work, even when its energy amount is large.

## 5. Evaluation of Free-Energy Claims

Many pseudotechnical proposals promise to extract heat from the environment and convert it entirely into continuous work. Kelvin-Planck provides a quick test: if the device is cyclic, uses one reservoir and presents no compensating effect, it violates the second law.

Dominant variable: [[ciclo]], because the condition of indefinite repetition prevents hiding state changes or consumption of internal reserves.

Validity limit: If the device consumes chemical or electrical energy or changes internally, it is no longer a single-reservoir engine; it must be analyzed with the corresponding complete balance.

The test does not require knowing every internal detail. It is enough to ask whether after each repetition the device is unchanged, whether it only takes environmental heat, and whether it delivers net work. If all three answers are yes, the proposal contradicts Kelvin-Planck. If not, the real energy source or accumulated internal change must be declared.

In teaching and public communication, this application is useful because it turns a spectacular claim into a verifiable checklist of conditions. Free-energy claims often hide a reservoir, a battery, a chemical reaction or an undeclared cooling process. Kelvin-Planck requires that cost to be stated before the device is accepted.

The same logic is used in engineering reviews. A proposed waste-heat generator must specify source temperature, sink temperature, net work and rejected heat. Without those quantities, the claim cannot be compared with the second-law limit.

This makes the statement practical rather than merely philosophical in technical decisions.
