# Common Errors in Thermodynamic Equilibrium

## Conceptual Errors

### Error 1: "Equilibrium means nothing moves"

**Description.** The student imagines a microscopically frozen system.

**Why it seems correct.** In everyday language, equilibrium is associated with rest.

**Detection signal.** Molecular motion is denied or particles are said to be still.

**Conceptual correction.** Equilibrium means absence of net macroscopic change. Particles keep moving, but state variables remain constant.

**Contrast mini-example.** A gas at equilibrium has moving molecules; nevertheless macroscopic [[T]] and [[P]] are stable.

### Error 2: "Uniform temperature is enough for complete equilibrium"

**Description.** Equilibrium is declared only because [[DeltaT]] is zero.

**Why it seems correct.** Thermal equilibrium is the most familiar case.

**Detection signal.** [[DeltaP]], composition or possible reactions are ignored.

**Conceptual correction.** Complete equilibrium requires checking every allowed process: heat, boundary motion, diffusion and reaction.

**Contrast mini-example.** Two gases may have the same [[T]] and different [[P]]; if a piston moves, mechanical equilibrium is absent.

## Model Errors

### Error 3: "Steady state is the same as equilibrium"

**Description.** Any time-constant system is treated as equilibrium.

**Why it seems correct.** In both cases variables may not change in time.

**Detection signal.** Permanent flows pass through the system.

**Conceptual correction.** A steady state may sustain internal currents. Thermodynamic equilibrium requires absence of internal net flows.

**Contrast mini-example.** A bar with heat entering one end and leaving the other is steady, but not in equilibrium.

## Mathematical Errors

### Error 4: "Using exact equality without experimental tolerance"

**Description.** Two sensors are required to show exactly the same value.

**Why it seems correct.** Equilibrium formulas are written as equalities.

**Detection signal.** Equilibrium is rejected because of differences smaller than measurement uncertainty.

**Conceptual correction.** In the laboratory, equilibrium is evaluated within tolerances. What matters is whether [[DeltaT]] or [[DeltaP]] are physically appreciable.

**Contrast mini-example.** Readings of 300.00 K and 300.02 K may be indistinguishable if sensor uncertainty is 0.05 K.

## Interpretation Errors

### Error 5: "Equilibrium is always reached quickly"

**Description.** The system is assumed to reach equilibrium as soon as it is connected or isolated.

**Why it seems correct.** Many school examples idealize relaxation.

**Detection signal.** The relaxation time [[tau]] is not considered.

**Conceptual correction.** Equilibrium requires sufficient time scale. If [[tau]] is large, the system may look stable without being equilibrated.

**Contrast mini-example.** A thick block may take hours to equalize internal temperature even if its surface looks stable.

## Quick self-control rule

Before declaring equilibrium, ask: which exchanges does the boundary allow, which gradients remain, and on what time scale are they observed. If relevant [[DeltaT]], [[DeltaP]] or [[A]] remains, complete equilibrium is absent.
