# History of Thermodynamic Equilibrium

## Historical Problem

Thermodynamics emerged from the study of engines, gases and heat without detailed microscopic knowledge of molecules. To compare states, physicists needed to identify when a system could be described by a few stable variables. The historical problem was to distinguish a reproducible final state from a process still in progress.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

Before equilibrium was formalized, mechanical rest, equal temperature and absence of reaction were often mixed as if they were the same. A gas could look still and still exchange heat; a mixture could have uniform temperature and still react slowly. An integrating idea based on intensive variables and constraints was missing.

## What Changed

The concept of thermodynamic equilibrium made it possible to define states operationally. It was no longer enough to see whether something moved: one had to ask whether gradients of [[T]], [[P]] or [[mu]] remained capable of producing change. This idea made equations of state and comparisons between reversible and irreversible processes possible.

It also changed the interpretation of temperature. Two bodies in thermal equilibrium share a property that allows thermometers to be built. Mechanical equilibrium justified common pressure. Chemical equilibrium extended the criterion to composition and reactions.

## Impact on Physics

Equilibrium became the basis of classical thermodynamics. Without it, state tables, phase diagrams, heat capacities, gas equations and thermodynamic potentials would not have operational meaning. Most engineering calculations begin by assuming initial and final states in equilibrium.

## Connection with Modern Physics

Modern physics keeps the concept but refines it. Statistical thermodynamics interprets it as the most probable macrostate; non-equilibrium physics studies how it is approached; complex systems show metastability and slow relaxations. The question remains the same: which internal drives remain and on what time scale do they vanish?
