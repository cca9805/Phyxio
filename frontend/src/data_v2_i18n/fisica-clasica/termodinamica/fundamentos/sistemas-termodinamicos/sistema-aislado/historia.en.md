## Historical problem

In the mid-nineteenth century, physics faced a question that seemed simple but proved deeply difficult: why do natural processes have a preferred direction? Heat flowed from hot bodies to cold ones, gases expanded into vacuums, and mixtures formed spontaneously, but the reverse was never observed without external intervention. Newtonian mechanics, established since the seventeenth century, was completely time-reversible: its equations worked equally well whether time moved forward or backward. Yet nature clearly preferred one temporal direction. The unanswered question was why.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The central intellectual obstacle was the incompatibility between the reversibility of microscopic mechanics and the observable macroscopic irreversibility. If every individual molecular collision was reversible, how could the collective behavior of millions of molecules be irreversible? This paradox, later known as the reversibility paradox or Loschmidt's paradox, stalled conceptual development for decades. The physicists of the era lacked the concept of statistical probability applied to thermodynamic systems, and had no precise definition of what it meant for a system to be "in equilibrium" or "out of equilibrium." The notion of an isolated system as an analytical framework was not formally established either: systems were analyzed in terms of heat and work exchanges without a systematic classification based on boundary type.

## What changed

Rudolf Clausius introduced in 1850 the distinction between heat and work as two distinct forms of energy transfer, and in 1865 formulated the concept of entropy as a state function. In doing so, he provided a well-defined quantity to quantify irreversibility: the entropy of the universe never decreases. Ludwig Boltzmann extended this idea to the microscopic level between 1872 and 1877, showing that the entropy of a system is proportional to the logarithm of the number of microstates compatible with its macroscopic state. This statistical interpretation of entropy transformed the isolated system into the natural analytical framework: in the absence of exchanges with the surroundings, the system evolves statistically toward the most probable macrostate, which is the one with maximum entropy and maximum number of microstates.

## Impact on physics

The formulation of the isolated system as the reference framework for the second law had consequences that extended far beyond classical thermodynamics. It established the thermodynamic arrow of time: a macroscopic temporal asymmetry with a statistical basis that did not exist in Newtonian mechanics. It allowed a precise definition of thermodynamic equilibrium as the state of maximum entropy compatible with the constraints of the isolated system. It opened the way to Gibbs's statistical thermodynamics, which unified macroscopic thermodynamics with microscopic statistical mechanics. Gibbs's formulation of the microcanonical ensemble — the statistical ensemble describing an isolated system with fixed energy, volume, and particle number — became the starting point of all modern statistical mechanics.

## Connection with modern physics

The concept of the isolated system remains central in contemporary physics. In quantum mechanics, the wave function formalism and the Schrödinger equation describe the evolution of an isolated system: the system's Hamiltonian completely determines its temporal evolution without external intervention. In quantum information physics, decoherence — the process by which a quantum system loses its quantum properties through interaction with its environment — is studied precisely as the breaking of isolation: a perfectly isolated system would maintain its quantum coherence indefinitely. In cosmology, the universe is modeled as the ultimate isolated system and predictions about its future evolution — including the hypothesis of heat death or maximum entropy of the universe — are direct consequences of the second law applied to the largest conceivable isolated system.
