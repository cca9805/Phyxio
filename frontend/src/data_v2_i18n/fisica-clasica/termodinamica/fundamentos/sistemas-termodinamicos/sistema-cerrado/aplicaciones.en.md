## 1. Piston engine in the Otto combustion cycle

In a four-stroke gasoline engine, the air-fuel mixture inside the cylinder constitutes a closed system during the compression and expansion strokes: the valves are closed and there is no mass flow through the boundary. The piston does work on the mixture during the compression stroke (W positive in IUPAC convention, the surroundings compress the system) and the mixture pushes the piston during the expansion stroke (W negative, the system does work on the surroundings). The compression stroke is approximately adiabatic because it occurs in milliseconds, too fast for significant heat exchange. The expansion stroke is also approximately adiabatic, while combustion involves an intense brief positive [[Q]] exchange.

The energy analysis of each stroke uses the first law directly: [[DeltaU]] equals [[Q]] plus [[W]]. The cycle efficiency is determined by comparing the net work produced (the difference between expansion work and compression work) with the total heat supplied by combustion.

Dominant variable: change in internal energy [[DeltaU]] of the mixture in each stroke, which determines the gas temperature and pressure and therefore the force on the piston.
Validity limit: the adiabatic closed system model holds while the valves are closed and the process time is less than the thermal relaxation time of the cylinder, typically below 50 ms for engines at normal speed.

## 2. Bomb calorimeter for combustion enthalpy measurement

The bomb calorimeter is a high-strength stainless steel vessel, completely closed and submerged in a temperature-controlled water bath. A fuel sample is ignited inside the vessel by an electric filament and burns at constant volume (isochoric process). The system boundary (the bomb) is impermeable to matter and rigid: pressure-volume work is zero, so [[DeltaU]] equals [[Q]].

The heat released by combustion is measured indirectly through the temperature rise of the surrounding bath water. With the known heat capacity of the calorimeter, the temperature change allows [[Q]] to be calculated with precision of the order of 0.1 %. This is the most direct application of the first law to the isochoric closed system: when there is no work, all the change in internal energy is heat.

Dominant variable: heat [[Q]] released by the reaction at constant volume, directly equal to [[DeltaU]] of the isochoric closed system.
Validity limit: the model holds when heat losses from the calorimeter to the surroundings are less than 0.5 % of the total released heat, requiring measurement times below 30 minutes in well-insulated calorimeters.

## 3. Lithium-ion battery during charge and discharge

A lithium-ion electrochemical cell is a closed system: the electrodes, electrolyte, and casing form a sealed unit that does not exchange matter with the surroundings during its normal useful life. The boundary transmits heat (batteries warm and cool with the surroundings) and electrical work (electric current is a work mechanism, not heat). The first law establishes that [[DeltaU]] of the cell equals the heat [[Q]] exchanged with the surroundings plus the electrical work [[W]] received or delivered.

During charging, the electrical work supplied by the charger increases the stored internal energy as chemical energy ([[DeltaU]] positive, [[W]] positive, [[Q]] generally negative as the cell releases heat to the surroundings). During discharge, the stored chemical energy converts to electrical work ([[DeltaU]] negative, [[W]] negative). The difference between the charged and discharged energy, expressed as [[Q]] released to the surroundings, is the irreversibility loss (Joule effect in internal resistance).

Dominant variable: change in internal energy [[DeltaU]] stored as chemical energy, which determines the state of charge and available capacity of the battery.
Validity limit: the closed system model holds throughout the battery's useful life under normal conditions; it ceases to be valid when electrode degradation generates gases that escape or when electrolyte loss occurs by evaporation through the casing, making the boundary permeable to matter.

## 4. Sterilization process in an autoclave

An autoclave is a thick-walled steel vessel containing water and the material to be sterilized. Once closed and sealed, it constitutes a closed system: the boundary is impermeable to matter (steam does not escape) and transmits heat (the electric resistance or external steam heats the system). The process consists of increasing the internal temperature above 121 °C by pressurization with internally generated steam.

The first law establishes that the heat [[Q]] supplied by the resistance increases the internal energy [[DeltaU]] of the system (water plus steam plus the material to be sterilized). The expansion work of the steam phase against the container walls is zero because the vessel is rigid, so [[DeltaU]] equals [[Q]]. The equilibrium temperature and pressure of the steam inside the closed system are what determine the sterilization effectiveness.

Dominant variable: heat [[Q]] supplied at constant volume, which determines the temperature and pressure reached inside the sealed autoclave.
Validity limit: the isochoric closed system model holds while the vessel remains sealed and the total volume does not change significantly; it ceases to hold if the safety valve opens (converting the system to open with steam mass flow to the exterior).

## 5. Photovoltaic solar cell under variable illumination

A silicon solar cell can be modeled as a closed system during operation: the boundary is impermeable to matter (electrons circulate internally, they are not emitted) and transmits two forms of energy: the incoming electromagnetic radiation (which acts as the equivalent work form of illumination) and the heat released to the environment by convection and infrared radiation. The useful electrical work produced flows through the terminals to the external circuit.

The first law establishes that [[DeltaU]] of the cell equals the absorbed radiative energy (which acts as incoming [[Q]]) minus the heat released to the environment (outgoing [[Q]]) plus the electrical work produced ([[W]] negative in IUPAC convention, the system does work on the circuit). Under steady-state conditions, [[DeltaU]] is zero and all absorbed radiative energy converts to released heat plus useful electrical work. Conversion efficiency is the fraction of absorbed radiative energy that converts to electrical work.

Dominant variable: heat [[Q]] absorbed from solar radiation, which determines the generated photocurrent and, together with the extracted electrical work, the energy balance of the cell.
Validity limit: the steady-state closed system model holds when the cell temperature is stable (typically occurring within the first minutes after illumination starts); during the initial heating transient, [[DeltaU]] is positive and the energy balance changes until thermal equilibrium with the surroundings is reached.
