# Common Errors in Extensive Variables

## Conceptual errors

### Error 1: "Extensive means large"

**Description.** The student thinks a magnitude is extensive if its numerical value is large.

**Why it seems correct.** The word extensive suggests extension or size.

**Detection signal.** A high pressure is classified as extensive or a small mass as non-extensive.

**Conceptual correction.** Extensive means proportional to system size, not large in absolute value.

**Contrast mini-example.** A milligram sample has extensive mass [[m]] even though it is small; a huge pressure remains intensive.

### Error 2: "Every magnitude calculated with mass is extensive"

**Description.** [[rho]] is classified as extensive because [[m]] appears in its formula.

**Why it seems correct.** Mass is the most familiar example of extensivity.

**Detection signal.** Density is said to double when the sample is duplicated.

**Conceptual correction.** A ratio of extensives can remove scale. If [[m]] and [[V]] double together, [[rho]] remains constant.

**Contrast mini-example.** Two liters of water have twice the mass of one liter, but approximately the same density.

## Model errors

### Error 3: "Adding specific properties as if they were totals"

**Description.** Values of [[x_esp]] from two samples are added to obtain a total.

**Why it seems correct.** The word specific may look like just another way to write the same property.

**Detection signal.** Total specific energy is computed by adding kJ/kg from two subsystems.

**Conceptual correction.** First recover the total extensive by multiplying by mass; then add totals.

**Contrast mini-example.** Two samples at 10 kJ/kg do not give 20 kJ/kg when joined; they remain at 10 kJ/kg if equivalent.

## Mathematical errors

### Error 4: "Applying additivity with incompatible references"

**Description.** Internal energies or entropies calculated with different references are added.

**Why it seems correct.** The additivity formula seems to authorize any sum of values.

**Detection signal.** Two tables or zero references are mixed without conversion.

**Conceptual correction.** Extensives add if they represent the same magnitude with the same reference.

**Contrast mini-example.** Energy measured from one arbitrary zero is not added to energy defined from another zero without adjustment.

## Interpretation errors

### Error 5: "Comparing samples by extensives without normalization"

**Description.** A sample is judged hotter or more energetic because it has larger total [[U]].

**Why it seems correct.** Greater total energy appears to indicate greater energetic intensity.

**Detection signal.** A large sample is declared hotter than a small one only because it has greater internal energy.

**Conceptual correction.** To compare states of different-size samples, use normalized magnitudes such as [[x_esp]] or intensive variables.

**Contrast mini-example.** A warm swimming pool may have more total [[U]] than a hot cup of coffee, but it is not at higher temperature.

## Quick self-control rule

Before classifying a magnitude, imagine two identical copies of the system. If the total value doubles, it is extensive. If it stays the same, it is probably intensive or normalized. If it changes nonlinearly, check mixing, surface, or interactions.
