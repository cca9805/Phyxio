## Historical problem

Since antiquity it was known that air has weight and that fire transforms it. But the question of **how the pressure, volume and temperature of a gas are quantitatively related** had no answer until the seventeenth century. The problem was twofold: there were no adequate measuring instruments (the thermometer and the manometer were developed precisely during that period) and there was no conceptual clarity about what a gas actually was.

Robert Boyle, in 1662, was the first to establish a rigorous quantitative relation: by compressing and expanding air trapped in a U-tube with mercury, he measured that the product [[P]] × [[V]] remains constant at fixed temperature. This was the first law of a gas, but it described only the isotherm.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the lack of an absolute temperature scale and the confusion between heat and temperature. Without a scale with an absolute zero, it was impossible to formulate the direct proportionality between [[V]] and [[T]], because that proportionality is exact only in kelvin. Eighteenth-century scientists worked with arbitrary scales (Fahrenheit, Réaumur, Celsius) without suspecting that a natural zero existed.

Moreover, the very notion of "gas" was confused: the Flemish Jan Baptist van Helmont coined the word in the seventeenth century, but it remained a matter of debate whether air was a simple element or a mixture. Without correctly identifying what substance was being studied, generalising the laws was difficult.

## What changed

In 1787, Jacques Charles found experimentally that at constant [[P]] the [[V]] of a gas varies linearly with temperature measured in Celsius — and that by extrapolation [[V]] reaches zero at −273 °C. Gay-Lussac published more systematic results in 1802 and was the first to mention explicitly that absolute zero as the natural origin of the gas temperature scale.

In 1811, Amedeo Avogadro formulated the hypothesis that equal volumes of different gases, at the same [[P]] and [[T]], contain the same number of molecules. This hypothesis (ignored for decades) was the missing piece to combine Boyle's, Charles's and Gay-Lussac's laws into a single relation with [[n]] as an additional variable.

The final synthesis came with the work of Rudolf Clausius and James Clerk Maxwell in the mid-nineteenth century: the kinetic theory of gases provided the microscopic derivation of the ideal gas law, showing that [[R]] is the product of Avogadro's number and Boltzmann's constant.

## Impact on physics

The ideal gas law was the first example of an **equation of state** connecting measurable macroscopic variables to a universal constant. It opened the way to thermodynamics as an autonomous discipline: Carnot, Clausius and Kelvin built upon it the laws of thermodynamics. The existence of absolute zero — predicted by extrapolating the gas laws — motivated the kelvin scale, which later proved to be the natural scale for all quantum and statistical physics.

Avogadro's synthesis was also the first step toward modern quantitative chemistry: it allowed the determination of relative molecular masses by comparing gas densities, and established the concept of the mole as the unit of amount of substance.

## Connection with modern physics

The ideal gas equation of state is today one of the starting points of statistical mechanics: it follows directly from the partition function of a gas of non-interacting particles, and its corrections (the virial equation) are the first terms of a systematic expansion in powers of density. The constant [[R]] is directly related to Boltzmann's constant (the ratio of [[R]] to Avogadro's number), which since 2019 has an exactly defined value in the International System of Units, simultaneously fixing the exact value of [[R]] as 8.314 462 618 J/(mol·K).

In astrophysics, the ideal gas equation of state (with quantum degeneracy corrections) describes the behaviour of plasma in stellar interiors. In cosmology, the cosmic microwave background is essentially radiation in thermal equilibrium described by Planck's law, which is itself derived from the quantum generalisation of the photon gas equation of state.
