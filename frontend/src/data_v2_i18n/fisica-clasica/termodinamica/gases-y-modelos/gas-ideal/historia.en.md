## Historical problem

In the seventeenth century, natural philosophers knew that air has weight and can be compressed, but no quantitative relationship systematically connecting [[P]], [[V]], and [[T]] yet existed. The problem was twofold: the measuring instruments — the thermometer and the manometer — were in their infancy, and the concept of "gas" as a distinct state of matter had not yet been precisely defined. There was no clarity about whether air was a single element or a mixture, nor about what "temperature" meant as a quantitative quantity.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The deepest obstacle was the absence of an absolute temperature scale. Without a natural zero, it was impossible to formulate the direct proportionality between [[V]] and [[T]] as a law, because that proportionality is exact only when [[T]] is measured in kelvin. Scientists of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries worked with arbitrary scales (Réaumur, Fahrenheit, Celsius) with no suspicion that there was a natural zero defined by the behaviour of gases.

Moreover, the confusion between heat and temperature persisted well into the eighteenth century. Without distinguishing between the amount of heat transferred and the temperature of the system, correctly interpreting gas compression and expansion experiments was impossible.

## What changed

In 1662, Robert Boyle compressed and expanded air trapped in a U-tube with mercury and demonstrated that the product [[P]]·[[V]] remains constant at a fixed temperature. This was the first quantitative law of a gas, but it described only the isothermal process.

In 1787, Jacques Charles discovered that at constant [[P]] the volume [[V]] varies linearly with temperature in Celsius and that, on extrapolating, the volume would be zero at −273 °C — the first experimental hint of absolute zero. Gay-Lussac published more systematic results in 1802 and was the first to mention that zero as the natural origin of the temperature scale for gases.

In 1811, Amedeo Avogadro proposed that equal volumes of different gases at the same [[P]] and [[T]] contain the same number of molecules. This hypothesis — ignored for decades — was the missing piece needed to include [[n]] as a variable and unify the laws of Boyle, Charles and Gay-Lussac in a single equation.

The microscopic synthesis came with Clausius and Maxwell in the mid-nineteenth century: kinetic theory showed that [[P]] emerges from the statistical average of molecular collisions, that [[T]] is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy of the molecules, and that [[R]] is the product of Avogadro's number and Boltzmann's constant.

## Impact on physics

The ideal gas law was the first example of an equation of state connecting measurable macroscopic variables with a universal constant, identical for all gases. That universality of [[R]] was the first demonstration that gas behaviour in the ideal limit depends only on the number of molecules, not on their chemical nature.

The existence of absolute zero — predicted by extrapolating the gas laws — motivated the kelvin scale, which later turned out to be the natural scale of all quantum and statistical physics. Carnot, Clausius and Kelvin built upon it the laws of thermodynamics. Avogadro's hypothesis was also the first step towards modern quantitative chemistry: it allowed relative molecular masses to be determined by comparing gas densities, and established the concept of the mole as the unit of amount of substance.

## Connection with modern physics

The ideal gas equation of state is derived directly from the partition function of a gas of non-interacting quantum particles, both in the classical limit (Boltzmann gas) and in the extreme quantum limits (degenerate Fermi gas, Bose–Einstein condensate). In astrophysics, the ideal gas equation of state — with quantum degeneracy corrections — describes the behaviour of plasma in stellar interiors.

The constant [[R]] has had an exactly defined value in the International System of Units since 2019: 8.314 462 618 J/(mol·K). That exact value is derived from the exactly fixed values of Boltzmann's constant (k_B = 1.380 649 × 10⁻²³ J/K) and Avogadro's number (N_A = 6.022 140 76 × 10²³ mol⁻¹), whose product is exactly [[R]]. This definition connects the macroscopic scale of thermodynamics with the atomic scale of quantum mechanics directly and without experimental uncertainty.
