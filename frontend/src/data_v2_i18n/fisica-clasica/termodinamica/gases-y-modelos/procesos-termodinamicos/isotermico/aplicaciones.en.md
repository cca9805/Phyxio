## 1. Oil-flooded screw compressors with oil cooling

Modern screw compressors inject oil into the compression chamber to absorb the heat generated during air compression. This technique keeps the air temperature very close to ambient throughout the process, approximating the isothermal behaviour of the gas. The result is lower electricity consumption than an equivalent adiabatic compressor, because the area under the isothermal curve in the pV diagram is smaller than the area under the adiabatic curve for the same compression ratio.

The key to performance is that oil, having a high specific heat capacity, absorbs heat in the compression zone and transfers it to the external radiator without significantly raising its own temperature. The dominant variable is [[T]]: the lower the gas temperature during compression, the less work is needed to compress the same volume of air to the same pressure.

Dominant variable: the gas temperature during compression; keeping it close to ambient [[T]] determines the energy efficiency of the compressor.
Validity limit: the isothermal model is valid when the screw rotation speed is below 3000 rpm and the oil flow rate is sufficient to keep the gas temperature below 80 °C.

## 2. Breathing of marine mammals underwater

When a whale dives to great depths, the air contained in its lungs and connected cavities is compressed by the increasing hydrostatic pressure of the water. At depths of 200 metres, the external pressure is about 20 atm. The soft lung tissues yield to the outside and air volume decreases according to Boyle's Law, because the animal's body temperature (approximately 37 °C) is maintained relatively constant thanks to the high heat capacity of biological tissue.

The residual lung volume at 200 metres depth is approximately one-twentieth of the surface volume. This controlled collapse is possible because cetacean alveoli are very flexible and because heat exchange with surrounding tissues keeps temperature within the normal range. The animal must ascend slowly so that the gas re-expands quasi-statically and the lung volume returns to its initial value without generating damaging pressures.

Dominant variable: the hydrostatic pressure [[p1]], which increases linearly with depth and determines the volume reduction [[V2]] according to Boyle's Law.
Validity limit: the pulmonary isothermal model fails at depths greater than 300 metres, where pressure exceeds 30 atm and biological compression effects generate additional heat that raises gas temperature.

## 3. Boyle manometers in pressure metrology

Boyle manometers are laboratory instruments that determine unknown absolute pressures by measuring the volume change of a reference gas at controlled temperature. The principle is direct: if the initial state of the reference gas is known and the volume after equilibrating with the unknown pressure is measured, Boyle's Law allows that pressure to be calculated with high precision.

These instruments are used in vacuum metrology, in the calibration of industrial pressure sensors, and in the experimental determination of the gas constant. The reference gas temperature is controlled by a thermostat bath whose temperature is measured with millikelvin precision thermometers. The measurement process is slow and quasi-static to ensure the isotherm is followed faithfully.

Dominant variable: the ratio [[V1]] over [[V2]], whose measurement precision directly determines the uncertainty in the calculated pressure.
Validity limit: the isothermal measurement is valid for pressures between 0.1 Pa and 10 000 Pa; above this, corrections for gas imperfection are significant and require higher-order equations of state.

## 4. Compressed gas cylinders for scuba diving

A 12-litre diving cylinder charged to 200 bar contains compressed air whose amount of moles is calculated using the ideal gas equation of state. As the diver breathes, pressure in the cylinder decreases almost isothermally because the cylinder metal has high heat capacity and keeps the gas temperature very close to the water temperature (5 to 25 °C depending on location).

The pressure regulator of the diving equipment reduces gas pressure from the cylinder level to the diver's ambient pressure. This expansion occurs in two stages, the second expansion being approximately isothermal when gas flow is low (calm diving). Boyle's Law predicts with good accuracy the number of breaths available given the initial pressure, water temperature, and volume of each breath.

Dominant variable: pressure [[p1]] inside the cylinder, which directly determines the total volume of gas available for breathing at ambient pressure.
Validity limit: the isothermal approximation fails during high-intensity physical activity where gas flow is fast; in those cases, expansion is partially adiabatic and gas temperature drops, slightly reducing the pressure delivered by the regulator.

## 5. Langmuir-Blodgett troughs for thin film deposition

In the Langmuir-Blodgett technique for depositing monomolecular films onto solid substrates, a monolayer of amphiphilic molecules floats on the water surface in a trough equipped with a movable barrier. When the barrier is displaced, the available surface area decreases and the surface pressure of the monolayer increases. This process is the two-dimensional analogue of the isothermal process: water temperature is maintained constant by thermostatized circulation and the "two-dimensional Boyle's Law" relates surface pressure to area per molecule.

The surface pressure versus area-per-molecule diagram is the two-dimensional analogue of the pV diagram of the isotherm. Inflections in this curve reveal phase transitions of the monolayer (analogous to gas-liquid-solid transitions in three dimensions). Precise control of [[T]] via the thermostat is essential to obtain reproducible isotherms and deposit high-quality films.

Dominant variable: the water temperature [[T]], which determines the shape of the isotherm and the pressure at which monolayer phase transitions occur.
Validity limit: the two-dimensional isothermal model fails when the barrier compression speed exceeds the monolayer relaxation rate, generating out-of-equilibrium states that produce defective films.
