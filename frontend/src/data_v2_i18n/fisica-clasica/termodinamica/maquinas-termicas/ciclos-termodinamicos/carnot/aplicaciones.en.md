# Applications — Carnot Cycle

## 1. Performance evaluation of steam thermal power plants

Coal, natural gas, and biomass thermal power plants operate with Rankine cycles whose real efficiency lies between 33 and 45 %. The Carnot efficiency between the superheated steam temperature (typically 810–870 K) and the condenser temperature (305–320 K) provides the thermodynamic ceiling against which design quality is measured. An engineer uses the ratio of real efficiency to Carnot efficiency (called second-law efficiency) to identify how much irreversibility remains to be eliminated. Ultra-supercritical plants operate with [[T_H]] up to 900 K, raising the Carnot to 66 % and justifying the investment in high-temperature alloys.

Dominant variable: [[T_H]] of superheated steam at the turbine inlet.
Validity limit: when steam temperature exceeds the metallurgical resistance of boiler materials (above 650 °C) or when pressure losses in piping invalidate the isothermal assumption.

## 2. Design of industrial refrigeration systems

An inverted Carnot cycle defines the maximum coefficient of performance (COP) of any refrigeration machine: [[T_C]] divided by the difference [[T_H]] minus [[T_C]]. In industrial cold rooms maintaining product at 253 K (minus 20 °C) with heat rejection to the environment at 308 K, the Carnot COP is 4.6. Real systems with screw compressors achieve COP of 2.5 to 3.5, quantifying the improvement margin in heat exchangers and compression. Investment in two-stage expansion systems is justified when the ratio of real to Carnot efficiency falls below 0.55.

Dominant variable: [[T_C]] of the refrigerated space and the temperature difference between reservoirs.
Validity limit: when the refrigerant exhibits non-ideal phase changes with subcooling or superheating that distance the real cycle from any reversible reference.

## 3. Thermodynamic analysis of internal combustion engines

Otto and Diesel engines operate with cycles that are not strictly between two isothermal reservoirs, but the Carnot between maximum combustion temperature (2000–2800 K) and exhaust temperature (700–900 K) offers a comparison reference. A high-efficiency Otto engine with a compression ratio of 12 achieves a theoretical efficiency of 63 %, while the Carnot between its extreme temperatures would be 70 %. The 7-point difference shows that even an ideal Otto cycle loses thermodynamic potential by operating with isochoric processes instead of isothermal ones. This comparison guides research in pressure-controlled combustion engines.

Dominant variable: maximum combustion temperature, controlled by compression ratio and fuel type.
Validity limit: when peak temperatures cause molecular dissociation of air and the working gas composition changes, invalidating the ideal gas assumption with constant properties.

## 4. Sizing of geothermal heat pumps

Geothermal heat pumps extract heat from the subsurface at 283–288 K and raise it to 318–328 K for heating. The Carnot COP for heating is [[T_H]] divided by [[T_H]] minus [[T_C]], yielding values between 8 and 12 for temperature differences of 30 to 45 K. Real equipment achieves COP of 3.5 to 5.5. Comparison with Carnot determines that buried heat exchangers are the main source of irreversibility when operating with temperature differences greater than 8 K between the fluid and the ground. This guides the sizing of geothermal probe lengths.

Dominant variable: temperature difference between the ground and the heated space, which determines the theoretical Carnot COP.
Validity limit: when prolonged heat extraction reduces ground temperature until it approaches that of the heated space, eliminating the thermodynamic advantage and reducing real COP below 2.

## 5. Thermodynamic certification of combined gas-steam cycles

Combined cycle gas turbine (CCGT) plants operate with a high-temperature Brayton cycle (1500–1800 K) coupled to a heat recovery Rankine cycle with exhaust at 308–320 K. The Carnot between the combined system's extreme temperatures reaches 80–83 %. The most advanced real CCGT plants achieve efficiencies of 62–64 %, equivalent to 77–79 % of Carnot. This ratio certifies that CCGT technology approaches the thermodynamic limit more closely than any other thermal conversion technology.

Comparison with Carnot justifies that remaining marginal improvements require disproportionate investments in superalloy materials and blade cooling systems. Triple-pressure plants with reheat represent the state of the art, where each additional degree in [[T_H]] translates into approximately 0.04 percentage points of additional efficiency. The economic implication is significant: raising turbine inlet temperature from 1500 K to 1600 K increases [[eta_C]] by roughly 2.5 percentage points, but requires ceramic matrix composites or thermal barrier coatings costing millions per unit. The Carnot framework quantifies whether the thermodynamic gain justifies the material science investment, providing a rigorous basis for engineering decisions in power plant development.

Dominant variable: [[T_H]] at the gas turbine inlet, limited by the metallurgy of single-crystal superalloys and thermal barrier coating durability.
Validity limit: when thermal degradation of blades or nitrogen oxide formation at high temperature imposes a practical ceiling on [[T_H]] regardless of the Carnot limit, or when the exhaust temperature falls below the minimum required for effective steam generation in the bottoming Rankine cycle.
