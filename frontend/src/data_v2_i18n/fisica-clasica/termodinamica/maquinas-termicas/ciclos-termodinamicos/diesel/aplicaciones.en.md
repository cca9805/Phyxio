# Applications — Diesel Cycle

## 1. Efficiency evaluation of long-distance truck engines

Heavy truck Diesel engines operate with compression ratios [[r]] of 16 to 20 and cutoff ratios [[r_c]] of 2 to 3, achieving theoretical efficiencies of 55 to 65 %. Modern real engines with electronic common-rail injection achieve efficiencies of 42 to 46 %, representing 70 to 75 % of the theoretical limit. The fleet engineer uses the comparison with the ideal Diesel cycle to quantify the improvement margin available through technologies such as exhaust gas recirculation, two-stage supercharging, and fuel injection optimization. A 12-liter engine with [[r]] of 18 and [[r_c]] of 2.5 has a theoretical Diesel efficiency of 57.4 %, and each percentage point closer to the ideal saves approximately 2500 liters of diesel fuel annually on long-distance routes.

Dominant variable: [[r]] of the engine, determined by the geometric cylinder design and limited by the mechanical strength of the block.
Validity limit: when combustion temperatures exceed 2200 K and nitrogen oxide emissions force compression ratio reduction to meet emission regulations.

## 2. Design of two-stroke marine Diesel engines

Two-stroke marine Diesel engines are the most efficient heat engines in the world, with real efficiencies of 50 to 55 % and compression ratios [[r]] of 18 to 22. They operate with low cutoff ratios [[r_c]] (1.8 to 2.5) because injection is slow and controlled in large-bore cylinders (up to 960 mm stroke). The ideal Diesel cycle provides the thermodynamic reference against which each incremental improvement in injector design, combustion chamber geometry, and scavenging system is evaluated. A Wärtsilä-Sulzer engine of 80 MW with [[r]] of 20 and [[r_c]] of 2 has a theoretical efficiency of 64.7 %, and its real efficiency of 52 % represents 80 % of the ideal, placing these engines among the closest to the thermodynamic limit.

Dominant variable: injection [[r_c]], electronically controlled to optimize the balance between power and emissions.
Validity limit: when heavy residual fuel oil HFO produces incomplete combustion with particle formation that invalidates the ideal isobaric model.

## 3. Sizing of emergency Diesel generators

Hospitals, data centers, and industrial plants use Diesel generators as electrical backup. These engines operate with [[r]] of 14 to 18 and [[r_c]] of 2 to 3, with real efficiencies of 35 to 40 % at partial load. The ideal Diesel cycle allows calculating the minimum theoretical specific fuel consumption and sizing the storage tank for the required autonomy. For a 500 kW electrical generator with theoretical Diesel efficiency of 58 % and real efficiency of 37 %, the engineer calculates that 1351 kW thermal input is needed, equivalent to 135 liters per hour of diesel fuel. The cycle analysis determines that improving injection and turbocharging can raise efficiency to 40 %, reducing consumption to 125 liters per hour and extending tank autonomy by 8 %.

Dominant variable: [[r]] of the engine, which determines the efficiency baseline and conditions the choice between high-speed engines (lower r) and medium-speed engines (higher r).
Validity limit: when the generator operates below 30 % load and the cutoff ratio becomes so small that combustion resembles an isochoric process more closely, invalidating the pure Diesel model.

## 4. Optimization of Diesel engines for construction machinery

Excavators, bulldozers, and construction cranes use Diesel engines that operate under extreme transient conditions, with rapid load and speed variations. Typical engines have [[r]] of 16 to 19 and variable [[r_c]] between 1.5 and 4 depending on instantaneous power demand. The ideal Diesel cycle analysis at different [[r_c]] allows mapping the efficiency curve as a function of load and determining optimal operating regimes. A 7-liter excavator engine with [[r]] of 17 and average [[r_c]] of 2.8 has a theoretical efficiency of 55.3 %. The electronic management system continuously adjusts [[r_c]] to maintain real efficiency above 38 %, prioritizing efficiency under partial-load conditions that represent 60 % of operating time.

Dominant variable: instantaneous [[r_c]], controlled by the pressure and duration of injection from the common-rail system.
Validity limit: when load transitions are so rapid that the engine does not reach quasi-static conditions and combustion is significantly non-uniform within the cylinder.

## 5. Thermodynamic analysis of Diesel-electric locomotives

Diesel-electric locomotives use a Diesel engine coupled to an electric generator that powers traction motors. Typical engines have [[r]] of 14 to 16 and [[r_c]] of 2.5 to 3.5, with real efficiencies of 38 to 43 %. The ideal Diesel cycle provides the reference for evaluating the complete energy conversion chain: from fuel heat to engine mechanical work, from this to electricity in the generator, and from electricity to wheel torque. A 3000 kW locomotive engine with [[r]] of 15 and [[r_c]] of 3 has a theoretical Diesel efficiency of 53.2 %. The real engine efficiency is 41 %, to which a generator efficiency of 96 % and traction motor efficiency of 94 % are applied, giving an overall efficiency of 37 %. Comparing with the theoretical limit of each stage allows identifying where to invest to maximize the efficiency of the complete chain.

Dominant variable: [[r_c]] at each engine speed regime, which defines the efficiency map as a function of revolutions and load.
Validity limit: when the locomotive operates at prolonged idle regimes where combustion is so poor that the isobaric heat addition model is no longer representative.
