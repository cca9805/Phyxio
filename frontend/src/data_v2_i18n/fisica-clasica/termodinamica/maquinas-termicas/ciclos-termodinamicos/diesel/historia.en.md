# History — Diesel Cycle

## Historical problem

In the late 19th century, internal combustion engines based on the Otto cycle dominated industry, but their efficiency was limited by premature fuel detonation, which prevented using compression ratios above 10 or 12. The central question was whether an operating mode existed that would allow much higher compression of the working gas without risk of destructive autoignition, thereby better exploiting fuel energy.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The thermodynamics of the era, consolidated by Carnot, Clausius, and Thomson, established that higher compression meant higher efficiency. However, practice showed that Otto engines could not increase their compression ratio without causing detonation. The problem was that in the Otto cycle fuel mixes with air before compression, so the mixture can autoignite prematurely at high pressure and temperature.

Rudolf Diesel, a German engineer trained at the Technische Hochschule in Munich under the influence of Carl von Linde, proposed a radical solution in 1893: compress pure air alone until reaching temperatures above the fuel autoignition point, then inject fuel directly into the hot compressed air. Since there was no premixed mixture, there was no detonation risk, and the compression ratio could be significantly raised.

## What changed

Diesel patented his concept in 1893 and built the first functional prototype in 1897 at the Maschinenfabrik Augsburg-Nürnberg (MAN) factory. The first engine achieved an efficiency of 26 %, compared to the 15 to 20 % typical of the best Otto engines of the era. This improvement demonstrated that the compression-ignition principle was viable and thermodynamically superior for applications where efficiency was the priority.

The idealized thermodynamic cycle describing this operation bears his name: adiabatic air compression, constant-pressure heat addition during fuel injection, adiabatic expansion, and constant-volume heat rejection. The key difference from Otto (constant-volume heat addition) is constant-pressure heat addition, which introduces the cutoff ratio as an additional parameter.

## Impact on physics

The Diesel engine experimentally demonstrated that classical thermodynamics predictions about the effect of compression ratio on efficiency were correct. The ability to operate with compression ratios of 14 to 25, much higher than Otto, confirmed that thermal efficiency is an increasing function of compression, limited only by mechanical material properties and combustion kinetics.

The analysis of the Diesel cycle also drove the development of isobaric process thermodynamics in closed systems and the understanding of entropy generated during non-isothermal heat addition, connecting directly to the second law formulation and the Gouy-Stodola theorem.

## Connection with modern physics

In contemporary engineering, Diesel engines are the dominant technology for heavy transport, backup power generation, and naval propulsion, with real efficiencies of 40 to 55 %. Computational modeling of real Diesel cycles uses computational fluid dynamics (CFD) coupled with detailed combustion chemical kinetics, integrating the Navier-Stokes equations with turbulence and spray models. Diesel's original concept of separating compression from injection remains the fundamental principle of these engines, and the idealized cycle bearing his name remains the thermodynamic reference for evaluating the efficiency of any compression-ignition engine.
