# Applications — Otto Cycle

## 1. Automobile gasoline engines

Automobile gasoline engines operate on the Otto cycle principle with compression ratios [[r]] between 9 and 12. The theoretical efficiency for [[r]] of 10 and [[gamma]] of 1.4 is 0.602, while actual efficiency ranges from 0.30 to 0.38 due to losses from non-instantaneous combustion, heat transfer to cylinder walls, and mechanical friction of the piston and rings. A modern 2.0-liter four-cylinder direct-injection engine achieves efficiencies of 36 to 38 %, representing 60 to 63 % of the theoretical Otto value. Direct injection and variable valve timing technologies allow closer approach to the theoretical limit by optimizing cylinder filling and reducing pumping losses. The comparison between actual and theoretical efficiency quantifies the technological improvement margin available for each engine generation.

Dominant variable: [[r]], limited to 9 to 12 by the detonation resistance of commercial gasoline (RON 95-98).
Validity limit: when combustion temperatures exceed 2500 K or when the engine operates with stratified lean mixtures that invalidate the assumption of homogeneous gas with constant properties.

## 2. High-performance motorcycle engines

Sports motorcycle engines use compression ratios [[r]] of 11 to 13, higher than typical automobiles, thanks to higher-octane fuels and more efficient cooling systems from direct air exposure. The theoretical efficiency for [[r]] of 12 and [[gamma]] of 1.4 is 0.630. A 1000 cc four-cylinder in-line motorcycle engine achieves actual efficiencies of 32 to 36 % at its maximum power regime (12000 to 14000 rpm). At extreme regimes above 15000 rpm, combustion duration represents a significant fraction of cycle time, distancing actual behavior from the ideal isochoric model. The comparison with the theoretical Otto cycle allows evaluating the impact of high revolutions on engine efficiency and justifies using dual cycle models for more accurate predictions.

Dominant variable: [[r]], combined with engine speed which affects the relative duration of combustion.
Validity limit: when extreme revolutions (15000 to 18000 rpm) make combustion unable to be considered instantaneous at constant volume, and the isochoric model departs significantly from reality.

## 3. Light aviation engines

Light aviation engines (Lycoming or Continental type) use Otto cycles with [[r]] between 7 and 8.5, deliberately low to ensure reliability and avoid detonation at different altitudes where atmospheric pressure varies. The theoretical efficiency for [[r]] of 8 and [[gamma]] of 1.4 is 0.565. A typical Lycoming O-360 light aviation engine achieves actual efficiencies of 25 to 30 %, representing 44 to 53 % of the theoretical value. The low compression ratio is a design decision that prioritizes absolute reliability over efficiency: an engine failure in flight is an emergency, while in a car it is merely an inconvenience. The comparison with the theoretical Otto cycle quantifies the thermodynamic price of safety in aviation.

Dominant variable: [[r]], restricted to 7 to 8.5 by extreme reliability requirements and multi-altitude operation.
Validity limit: at high altitudes (above 3000 meters) intake pressure drops significantly and cylinder filling is incomplete, invalidating the assumption of constant gas mass per cycle.

## 4. Stationary natural gas engines

Stationary natural gas engines for distributed power generation operate with Otto cycles using [[r]] between 10 and 14, taking advantage of natural gas having higher detonation resistance than gasoline (methane number equivalent to RON 120-130). The theoretical efficiency for [[r]] of 12 and [[gamma]] of 1.4 is 0.630, with actual efficiencies of 35 to 42 %. Stationary operating conditions allow precise control of temperatures, pressures, and air-fuel mixture that brings actual efficiency closer to theoretical. Cogeneration systems that recover rejected heat [[Q_C]] for heating or industrial processes achieve overall efficiencies of 80 to 90 %, although pure electrical efficiency remains limited by the Otto cycle.

Dominant variable: [[r]], which can be higher than in gasoline thanks to the high detonation resistance of natural gas.
Validity limit: when natural gas composition varies (presence of propane or butane) and detonation resistance changes, forcing a reduction in [[r]] to avoid autoignition.

## 5. Competition engines with direct injection

Modern Formula 1 engines (1.6-liter V6 turbo hybrid) operate with compression ratios [[r]] of 14 to 17, made possible by special ultra-high-octane fuels, precise direct injection, and advanced cycle-by-cycle electronic detonation control. The theoretical efficiency for [[r]] of 15 and [[gamma]] of 1.4 is 0.661. Actual efficiencies reach 50 % thanks to turbocharger energy recovery (MGU-H) and the electric hybrid system (MGU-K), which capture part of the energy that in a conventional engine would be lost as exhaust heat and braking. The difference between the 66 % theoretical and the 50 % actual measures the irreversibilities that the world's most advanced technology has yet to eliminate.

Dominant variable: [[r]], maximized through extreme-octane fuels and real-time electronic detonation control.
Validity limit: the cold-air standard model loses accuracy because combustion temperatures widely exceed 2000 K, [[gamma]] varies significantly throughout the cycle, and combustion is not purely isochoric, requiring variable-property models and dual cycle analysis.
