# History — Coefficient of Performance

## Historical problem

In the early nineteenth century, steam engines dominated industry but no theory explained the limits of mechanical refrigeration. The first attempts at artificial cooling relied on ice and salt mixtures, without a thermodynamic basis indicating how much work is needed to extract a given amount of heat from a cold space. The central question was whether a fundamental limit to refrigeration efficiency existed and what it depended on.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the confusion between heat and work. Without the second law of thermodynamics formulated, there was no way to demonstrate that transferring heat from cold to hot necessarily requires external work. Caloric theory suggested that heat flowed spontaneously from higher to lower temperature, but offered no quantitative relationship between the required work and reservoir temperatures. Furthermore, the notion that a machine can have efficiency greater than one was counterintuitive without distinguishing between thermal efficiency and coefficient of performance.

## What changed

Kelvin demonstrated in 1851 that no cyclic device can transfer heat from cold to hot without consuming work, establishing the need for COP as a quantitative measure. Clausius formalized the inequality that limits COP to the Carnot value. Jacob Perkins patented the first vapor-compression system in 1834, and Carl von Linde perfected it in the 1870s, providing the first real machine where COP could be measured. Max Planck explicitly formalized the Carnot COP as a theoretical limit in the early twentieth century.

## Impact on physics

COP as a concept transformed refrigeration and heating engineering from an empirical practice into a quantitative discipline based on universal thermodynamic principles. The European energy label, introduced in 1994, uses COP as the primary efficiency metric for appliances. The 1987 Montreal Protocol forced the replacement of CFC refrigerants, and COP was the key metric for evaluating whether substitutes maintained efficiency.

## Connection with modern physics

In modern thermodynamics, COP appears in the analysis of laser cooling, magnetic refrigeration, and quantum heat engines. The Carnot limits for COP remain the fundamental reference even in these nanoscale systems. Research on high-temperature heat pumps for district heating networks and the integration of renewable energy sources depends directly on maximizing COP to reduce primary energy consumption.
