# History — Practical Examples

## Historical problem

Until the mid-nineteenth century, food preservation depended on natural ice cut in winter and stored in insulated cellars. In warm regions, ice was a luxury product imported at great expense. The central question was whether it was possible to build a machine that could produce cold artificially in a continuous and economically viable way, and how to size its power for a given application.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main difficulty was the lack of a quantitative theory connecting the required mechanical power with the amount of heat that could be extracted. Without the concept of COP, engineers could not predict how much fuel or electricity a refrigeration machine would need to cool a given amount of product. The first systems were designed by trial and error, with massive oversizing and very low efficiencies.

## What changed

Jacob Perkins patented the first functional vapor-compression system in 1834 using ether as refrigerant. Carl von Linde perfected the compression cycle with ammonia in the 1870s and developed the first industrial refrigerators for breweries. Willis Carrier invented modern air conditioning in 1902 to control humidity in a printing plant, accidentally discovering that temperature control was equally valuable. The development of synthetic CFC refrigerants by Thomas Midgley in 1930 enabled safe and affordable domestic refrigeration.

## Impact on physics

Practical refrigeration examples transformed the food industry, medicine and urban habitability. The cold chain enabled global transport of perishable foods. The energy classification of appliances, started in Europe in 1994 with the A to G label, is directly based on measured COP. The 1987 Montreal Protocol eliminated CFCs and forced the redesign of all systems with alternative refrigerants, using COP as the evaluation metric.

## Connection with modern physics

Today, heat pumps are a key technology in the energy transition. The European Union Energy Efficiency Directive promotes their mass adoption to reduce carbon emissions. Research into low global warming potential refrigerants, variable-speed compressors and transcritical carbon dioxide cycles seeks to maximize real COP. In fundamental physics, the same principles apply to magnetic refrigeration and laser cooling of atoms.
