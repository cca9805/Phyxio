## 1. Haber process: industrial ammonia synthesis

The synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen is one of the most important chemical processes in modern history: it provides the fixed nitrogen that feeds the fertilizers sustaining global agriculture. Gibbs free energy is the thermodynamic arbiter that determines under which conditions of temperature and pressure ammonia production is favorable and under which conditions the reaction direction reverses.

The process has negative [[DeltaH]] (exothermic) and negative [[DeltaS]] (reduction in the number of gaseous moles). The result is that [[DeltaG]] is negative at temperatures below the inversion temperature (around 465 K). At industrial operating temperatures (673–773 K), [[DeltaG]] is positive according to the standard model, which requires working at high pressure to shift the thermodynamic equilibrium toward products. The dominant variable in process design is [[T_inv]]: the higher it is, the wider the temperature window where the process is spontaneous, facilitating kinetics without sacrificing thermodynamics. Industrial optimization seeks the point where catalytic reaction rate and Gibbs equilibrium yield mutually compensate.

Dominant variable: the inversion temperature [[T_inv]], which determines the operating range where synthesis is thermodynamically favorable without requiring extreme pressures.

Validity limit: the standard Gibbs model with constant [[DeltaH]] and [[DeltaS]] is valid at low temperatures; at industrial temperatures (above 400 °C) the Kirchhoff correction and the effect of elevated pressure are indispensable for predicting real yield.

## 2. Electrochemical cells and rechargeable batteries

In an electrochemical cell, the spontaneous redox reaction converts chemical energy into electrical energy. The change in free energy [[DeltaG]] is directly proportional to the maximum cell voltage: the standard cell potential multiplied by the transferred charge equals exactly minus [[DeltaG]]. This principle governs the design of lithium batteries, hydrogen fuel cells, and supercapacitors.

In lithium-ion batteries, the intercalation reaction of lithium into the graphite electrode has negative [[DeltaG]] during discharge (spontaneous process that releases electrical work) and positive [[DeltaG]] during charging (non-spontaneous process requiring external electrical work). Analysis of [[DeltaG]] as a function of state of charge allows estimation of the safe operating voltage window and prediction of battery degradation when the [[DeltaG]] of secondary reactions begins to become comparable to that of the main reaction.

Dominant variable: the variation of [[DeltaG]] during the charge-discharge cycle, which determines the operating voltage and the energy efficiency of the device.

Validity limit: the constant-[[DeltaG]] model with state of charge is valid only in the linear operating range; at the extremes of charge and discharge, activity effects and polarization modify the effective [[DeltaG]] in a non-linear manner.

## 3. Protein folding and biological stability

The spontaneous folding of a protein from its unfolded form to its native conformation is one of the most complex phenomena in molecular biology. Thermodynamically, the process is spontaneous (negative [[DeltaG]]) under physiological conditions because the conformational entropy loss of the polypeptide chain (unfavorable entropy term) is more than compensated by the gain of non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, Van der Waals forces, hydrophobic effect) that stabilize the native structure.

The dependence of [[DeltaG]] on temperature is critical for protein stability. At physiological temperature (310 K), the [[DeltaG]] of folding is slightly negative, with typical values between 20 and 60 kJ/mol depending on the protein. As temperature increases, the [[T]]·[[DeltaS]] term increases and the [[DeltaG]] of folding tends toward zero and then becomes positive: the protein denatures. The denaturation temperature is essentially [[T_inv]] of the folding process. This prediction guides the design of thermostable proteins for industrial use (enzymes in detergents, biocatalysts in high-temperature synthesis).

Dominant variable: the magnitude of [[DeltaG]] of folding at physiological temperature, which determines the kinetic and thermodynamic stability of the protein against thermal or pH perturbations.

Validity limit: the standard Gibbs model with constant [[DeltaH]] and [[DeltaS]] is only a first approximation; real folding involves significant heat capacity changes and solvent effects that make [[DeltaH]] and [[DeltaS]] markedly temperature-dependent.

## 4. Extractive metallurgy: reduction of metal oxides

The production of pure metals from their ores (oxides, sulfides) requires the reduction reaction to be thermodynamically favorable. The Ellingham diagram represents [[DeltaG]] of formation of different metal oxides as a function of temperature: each metal traces a line with a slope proportional to its entropy change, and the metal whose line lies lower "wins" thermodynamically in competing for oxygen.

For iron production in the blast furnace, carbon (as coke) reduces iron oxides because at high temperatures (above approximately 1000 K) the [[DeltaG]] line for carbon dioxide formation falls below that of iron oxide: the oxidation of carbon is thermodynamically more favorable than that of iron. The critical variable is the temperature at which that line crossing occurs (the intersection of the [[T_inv]] values of the two competing reactions). The furnace operator controls the temperature precisely to remain in the regime where [[DeltaG]] of the overall reduction process is negative.

Dominant variable: the crossing temperature of the Ellingham diagram, which determines above which temperature the metal reduction is thermodynamically spontaneous.

Validity limit: the linear Gibbs model is valid when there are no phase changes in the studied temperature range; in the Ellingham diagram, phase changes produce abrupt breaks in line slopes that the simple model does not capture.

## 5. Corrosion and cathodic protection of metallic structures

Corrosion of metallic structures in contact with electrolytes (salt water, moist soil) is an electrochemical process controlled by [[DeltaG]]. The corrosion cell operates spontaneously because the [[DeltaG]] of the anodic reaction (metal dissolution) is negative under environmental conditions. Analysis of [[DeltaG]] allows calculation of which metals corrode spontaneously, in what priority order, and at what maximum thermodynamic rate.

Cathodic protection reverses the spontaneity of corrosion: by connecting the structure to a sacrificial anode of zinc or aluminum (metals with more negative [[DeltaG]] of oxidation), the system is forced to operate in the regime where corrosion of the structural metal has positive [[DeltaG]] (non-spontaneous process). Zinc or aluminum preferentially oxidize because their [[T_inv]] for reversing oxidation is much higher than that of iron: they are thermodynamically more "sacrificeable." The design of cathodic protection requires calculating the difference in [[DeltaG]] between the two electrodes to guarantee sufficient protection potential throughout the entire service life of the sacrificial anode.

Dominant variable: the difference in [[DeltaG]] of oxidation between the sacrificial anode and the structural metal, which determines whether cathodic protection is thermodynamically effective.

Validity limit: the standard Gibbs model is valid for moderate temperature and electrolyte concentration conditions; in extreme environments (high temperature, high salinity, acidic conditions) the real electrochemical potentials diverge from the standard value and the analysis requires activities and ionic strength corrections.
