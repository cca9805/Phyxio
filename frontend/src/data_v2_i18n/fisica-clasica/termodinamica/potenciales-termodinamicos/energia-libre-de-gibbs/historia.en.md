## Historical problem

In the mid-nineteenth century, thermodynamics had established its first two principles with relative solidity: conservation of energy and the increase of entropy in the universe. However, a central unsolved problem persisted: **how to predict whether a chemical reaction or physical process will occur spontaneously under laboratory conditions?**

Scientists of the era knew that spontaneous processes released heat in most observed cases, and the Berthelot-Thomsen rule —which identified exothermic processes with spontaneous ones— was the dominant guide in the early 1870s. But this rule failed systematically for spontaneous endothermic processes, such as the dissolution of many salts or the melting of ice above its melting point. The scientific community needed a single criterion unifying all cases.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The central intellectual obstacle was the **separation between internal energy and the capacity to do work**. The first principle guaranteed conservation of total energy, but said nothing about what fraction of that energy could be converted into useful work. The second principle indicated that the entropy of the universe always increases, but operating with the entropy of the entire universe was operationally impossible in laboratory experiments where only the system and its immediate surroundings could be measured.

The confusion between "energy that is released" and "energy available for work" was at the core of the problem. European physicists, particularly those of the German tradition (Clausius, Helmholtz, Kirchhoff), were constructing the thermodynamic edifice from the side of energy and entropy, but the connection with practical conditions of constant pressure and temperature —the typical laboratory conditions— had not been formalized.

Furthermore, the notion that entropy could act as a "driving force" opposed to heat was deeply counterintuitive: how could disorder be a cause of spontaneity as powerful as or more powerful than energy release?

## What changed

Josiah Willard Gibbs, a professor of mathematical physics at Yale University virtually unknown in Europe at the time, published between 1875 and 1878 his monumental two-part work: *On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances*. In it he formulated the thermodynamic potential that now bears his name, along with the concepts of chemical potential, the phase rule, and Gibbs free energy.

What changed was not just an equation: it was the **entire conceptual framework**. Gibbs demonstrated that for systems at constant pressure and temperature, the spontaneity criterion is neither the change in internal energy nor the entropy of the universe separately, but a combination of both —the free energy— referenced exclusively to the system. This reformulation converted a problem that required measuring the universe into a problem that required measuring only the system.

Gibbs's work was initially ignored in Europe because it was published in the *Transactions of the Connecticut Academy*, a journal of very limited reach. It was Wilhelm Ostwald who, upon translating it into German in 1892, popularized Gibbs's work in the international scientific community, triggering an immediate impact on physical chemistry and applied thermodynamics.

## Impact on physics

The introduction of Gibbs free energy transformed several fields simultaneously. In chemistry, it provided the rigorous spontaneity criterion that had been missing and made it possible to connect the macroscopic properties of reactions with the equilibrium constant through the thermodynamic relation between [[DeltaG]] and the reaction equilibrium constant.

In physics, it laid the foundations of the **thermodynamics of open systems and mixtures**: the chemical potential —partial derivative of Gibbs energy with respect to amount of substance— made it possible to quantitatively treat matter transfer between phases, solubility, osmotic pressure, and mixtures. All of modern physical chemistry rests on this edifice.

The subsequent connection between [[DeltaG]] and statistical mechanics —developed by Boltzmann and extended by Planck and Einstein— made Gibbs free energy the bridge between the macroscopic world of classical thermodynamics and the microscopic world of atoms and molecules. The relation between [[DeltaG]] and the statistical partition function is one of the deepest results of late nineteenth-century theoretical physics.

## Connection with modern physics

The concept of Gibbs free energy is a living pillar of contemporary science. In **biophysics and biochemistry**, [[DeltaG]] determines the direction of every metabolic reaction, the stability of protein structures, and the functioning of molecular motors. ATP hydrolysis —the cell's "energy currency"— releases Gibbs free energy that couples to endergonic reactions to sustain life.

In **nanotechnology and materials science**, the calculation of [[DeltaG]] guides the design of new energy materials, heterogeneous catalysts, and electrodes for next-generation batteries. Ab initio calculation methods based on density functional theory (DFT) allow computation of [[DeltaH]] and [[DeltaS]] for reactions at the atomic scale, making computational materials design possible before synthesis.

In **cosmology and extreme-state physics**, Gibbs thermodynamics extends to contexts where pressure and temperature conditions are extraordinary: the formation of exotic phases of nuclear matter inside neutron stars is analyzed through generalized thermodynamic potentials whose conceptual root is directly Gibbs free energy.
