## 1. Bomb calorimetry: combustion in a sealed vessel

Bomb calorimetry measures the heat of combustion of a substance in a fixed-volume steel vessel, completely sealed and immersed in a thermostatted bath. Under these conditions, volume does not change and the appropriate thermodynamic criterion is Helmholtz free energy, not Gibbs. The [[DeltaA]] change determines whether combustion is spontaneous at the experiment temperature and how much maximum non-expansion work could be extracted in a reversible process.

For sodium benzoate combustion in a bomb calorimeter at 298 K, [[DeltaU]] is very negative (about minus 26 MJ/kg) and [[DeltaS]] is slightly positive due to increased gaseous product moles. The result is enormously negative [[DeltaA]]: combustion is thermodynamically inevitable in the confined vessel. The dominant variable is [[DeltaU]], which sets the magnitude of heat measured directly by the calorimeter.

Dominant variable: the internal energy change [[DeltaU]], which directly determines the heat released measured by the bomb calorimeter.

Validity limit: the Helmholtz model with constant [[DeltaU]] and [[DeltaS]] is valid for complete combustions at ambient temperature; above 800 K, incomplete combustion products may form that alter [[DeltaU]] and [[DeltaS]].

## 2. Cryogenics: gas liquefaction in rigid tanks

Liquefaction of gases such as nitrogen or helium in fixed-volume cryogenic tanks requires cooling the gas until the Helmholtz change of the gas-liquid transition is negative. In a sealed tank, internal pressure changes during cooling but volume remains constant, making [[DeltaA]] the correct operational criterion for determining spontaneous liquefaction temperature.

For nitrogen, the gas-liquid transition has negative [[DeltaU]] and negative [[DeltaS]], with [[T_inv]] around 77 K. Below this temperature, [[DeltaA]] is negative and liquefaction is spontaneous in the confined tank. Cryogenic engineers design cooling systems to reach temperatures below the stored gas [[T_inv]].

Dominant variable: the inversion temperature [[T_inv]], which marks the cryogenic threshold below which the liquid phase is thermodynamically preferred in the rigid tank.

Validity limit: the model is valid for pure gases without impurities; impurity presence modifies [[T_inv]] and may induce solidification before complete liquefaction, a phenomenon not captured by the standard model.

## 3. Elastomers and rubber: elastic retraction at apparently constant volume

Elastomers such as natural rubber store elastic energy when stretched and release it when contracting. Thermodynamically, the elastic retraction process at ambient temperature can be analyzed via [[DeltaA]] when the body's macroscopic volume change is small compared to internal conformational changes of polymer chains.

Rubber retraction after stretching has negative [[DeltaU]] (polymer chains release conformational energy returning to coiled state) and negative [[DeltaS]] (greater chain ordering). At ambient temperature, [[DeltaA]] is negative: retraction is spontaneous. At very high temperatures (above [[T_inv]]), the entropic term dominates and rubber loses elasticity: it degrades thermodynamically.

Dominant variable: the [[DeltaA]] change at ambient temperature, which determines elastic retraction force and thermodynamic stability of the retracted state.

Validity limit: the Helmholtz model with constant [[DeltaU]] and [[DeltaS]] is a first approximation; real retraction involves significant heat capacity changes and crystallization effects making [[DeltaU]] and [[DeltaS]] depend on extension and temperature.

## 4. Sealed batteries: electrochemical equilibrium in a closed cell

Sealed batteries (sealed lead-acid, lithium batteries in rigid enclosure) operate at practically constant volume. The discharge electrochemical reaction has a Helmholtz change that determines maximum cell voltage and redox reaction spontaneity in the confined vessel.

In a sealed lead-acid battery, the discharge reaction has negative [[DeltaU]] and negative [[DeltaS]] (solid lead sulfate formation from liquids). At ambient temperature, [[DeltaA]] is negative: discharge is spontaneous. At elevated temperatures (above [[T_inv]], around 350 K for this reaction), discharge ceases to be thermodynamically favorable and the battery self-discharges via parallel processes.

Dominant variable: the [[DeltaA]] change during the charge-discharge cycle, which determines operating voltage and energy efficiency in the sealed vessel.

Validity limit: the constant [[DeltaA]] model with state of charge is valid only in the linear operating range; at charge and discharge extremes, activity and polarization effects modify effective [[DeltaA]] nonlinearly.

## 5. Hydrogen storage in metal hydrides

Metal hydrides such as LaNi₅H₆ store hydrogen in a rigid vessel via absorption in the metal crystal lattice. Hydride formation is a constant-volume process (the tank does not change size) at controlled temperature, where [[DeltaA]] determines hydrogen equilibrium pressure over the hydride.

For LaNi₅H₆ formation from LaNi₅ and H₂, [[DeltaU]] is negative (metal-hydrogen bonds release energy) and [[DeltaS]] is negative (gas converts to hydrogen atoms in the lattice). At ambient temperature, [[DeltaA]] is negative: hydrogen absorption is spontaneous. Desorption temperature (hydride regeneration) corresponds to [[T_inv]]: above this threshold, [[DeltaA]] becomes positive and hydrogen releases spontaneously from the rigid vessel.

Dominant variable: the inversion temperature [[T_inv]], which determines operating temperature of the hydrogen absorption-desorption cycle in the storage tank.

Validity limit: the standard Helmholtz model is valid for stable hydrides in the 273–373 K range; above these temperatures, base metal decomposition and material sintering irreversibly alter [[DeltaU]] and [[DeltaS]].
