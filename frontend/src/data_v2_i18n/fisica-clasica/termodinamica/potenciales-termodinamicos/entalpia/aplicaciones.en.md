## 1. Domestic heating by natural gas combustion

In domestic heating systems, the burner operates at constant pressure and converts the chemical enthalpy of methane into heat transferred to the radiator water. The dominant variable is [[DeltaH]] of methane combustion, which determines the heat delivered per cubic metre of gas burned.

The dominant hypothesis is that pressure is constant during combustion and that combustion products exit at the exhaust gas temperature. The model validity limit is that combustion must be complete; with incomplete combustion, the effective [[DeltaH]] is lower and burner efficiency drops. In condensing boilers, the enthalpy of vaporisation of water in the exhaust gases is also recovered, significantly increasing efficiency.

**Dominant variable:** [[DeltaH]] of methane combustion (approximately minus 890 kJ/mol).

**Validity limit:** complete combustion and constant burner pressure.

In practice, this reading makes it possible to compare lower and higher heating values without changing the physical language: both are enthalpy balances, but one ignores condensation of the water formed and the other recovers it. For teaching, this case links an abstract thermodynamic quantity with energy bills, boiler efficiency and emissions.

## 2. Industrial chemical reactor design

In designing reactors for industrial synthesis (ammonia, methanol, sulfuric acid), [[DeltaH]] of reaction determines whether the reactor needs external cooling or heating for controlled operation. Exothermic reactors require heat extraction systems; endothermic reactors require continuous energy input.

The enthalpic balance of the reactor is computed by summing the [[DeltaH]] values of all simultaneous reactions, weighted by their relative rates. The model hypothesis is that the process is quasi-static and that heat capacities are constant over the operating range. The limit is that if operating temperatures differ by more than 200 K from the table reference temperature, Kirchhoff's correction must be applied.

**Dominant variable:** [[DeltaH]] of the main synthesis reaction.

**Validity limit:** operating temperatures in the range where [[DeltaH]] is approximately constant.

The didactic value is that the sign of [[DeltaH]] does more than classify the reaction: it determines reactor architecture. A strongly exothermic reaction requires thermal control to avoid runaway, whereas an endothermic reaction requires distributed heat input so conversion does not stop.

## 3. Differential scanning calorimetry of materials

Differential scanning calorimetry measures [[DeltaH]] of phase transitions (melting, crystallisation, glass transition) in polymeric materials, alloys and pharmaceutical compounds. The technique compares the heat flow of the sample with that of an inert reference while varying [[T]] at a controlled rate.

The area under the heat-flow versus temperature peak gives [[DeltaH]] of the transition. The dominant variable is the difference in [[DeltaH]] between the high-temperature and low-temperature phases. The model is valid when the heating rate is slow enough for the process to be quasi-static; at high rates, peaks shift and broaden, reducing accuracy.

**Dominant variable:** [[DeltaH]] of the studied phase transition.

**Validity limit:** heating rates below 20 °C/min for most polymers.

This application is useful because the measured area is not just a graphical feature; it is the amount of enthalpy absorbed or released by the sample. A broadened peak therefore signals both a kinetic delay and a possible loss of equilibrium interpretation, which is why scan rate must be reported with the result.

## 4. Biochemistry: energetics of metabolic reactions

In biochemistry, [[DeltaH]] of ATP hydrolysis (approximately minus 30 kJ/mol) quantifies the enthalpic energy available to drive biosynthetic reactions, active transport and mechanical muscle work. Metabolic pathways are designed to couple exothermic reactions with endothermic reactions so that the overall balance is thermodynamically favourable.

The biochemical model hypothesis is that reactions occur in aqueous solution at constant pressure (atmospheric pressure in the cytoplasm). The validity limit is that reactant and product concentrations must not deviate too far from standard physiological conditions (pH 7, millimolar concentrations), because [[DeltaH]] depends on solution conditions.

**Dominant variable:** [[DeltaH]] of hydrolysis of high-energy bonds.

**Validity limit:** physiological conditions of pH, temperature and ionic concentration.

This case also shows the limit of treating [[DeltaH]] as the only criterion for feasibility. Metabolism depends on entropy and Gibbs free energy as well; nevertheless, [[DeltaH]] tells the thermal load that a cell, tissue or bioreactor must dissipate during the pathway.

## 5. Cryogenic engineering: liquefaction of industrial gases

In producing liquid oxygen, nitrogen and argon for industrial and medical use, the liquefaction cycle is designed using the enthalpies of vaporisation of the gases. [[DeltaH]] of vaporisation of nitrogen (approximately 5.6 kJ/mol) and oxygen (approximately 6.8 kJ/mol) determine the compression work and heat exchange in the Linde cycle heat exchangers.

The model assumes quasi-static constant-pressure processes at each cycle stage. The dominant variable is [[DeltaH]] of phase change at the boiling temperature at the operating pressure. The validity limit is that operating pressures must be in the range where the gas-phase behaviour approximates ideal gas behaviour; near the critical point, deviations are significant and a real equation of state is required.

**Dominant variable:** [[DeltaH]] of vaporisation at the cycle operating pressure.

**Validity limit:** pressures away from the critical point of the liquefied gas.

For engineering work, the key point is that phase-change enthalpy sets the minimum heat duty of the exchangers. Real compressors and throttling valves add irreversibilities, but the enthalpy balance remains the backbone used to size stages, estimate losses and compare alternative cryogenic cycles.
