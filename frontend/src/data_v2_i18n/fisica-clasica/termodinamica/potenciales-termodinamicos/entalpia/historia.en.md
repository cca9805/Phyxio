## Historical problem

In the mid-nineteenth century, chemists needed to predict the heat generated by industrial reactions such as coal combustion or acid neutralisation, but had no coherent theoretical framework for systematically summing and subtracting heats of reaction. Calorimetry experiments were costly and produced results that were difficult to generalise, because the measured heat depended on the path followed by the process.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was the belief that the heat exchanged in a reaction necessarily depended on the path: if carbon was oxidised directly to carbon dioxide or in two stages through carbon monoxide, chemists were not certain that the total heat would be the same. Without a thermodynamic state potential to unify heats of reaction, predictive calculation was impossible.

## What changed

In 1840, Germain Henri Hess demonstrated experimentally that the total heat of a chemical reaction is independent of path: it depends only on the initial and final states. This result, known as **Hess's law**, was the first experimental evidence that an additive state quantity associated with thermal processes exists. Decades later, with the formal development of thermodynamics by Clausius and Kelvin, this quantity was identified with the state function [[H]], named **enthalpy** by Kamerlingh Onnes in 1909.

## Impact on physics

The consolidation of enthalpy [[H]] as a state function allowed construction of the first systematic tables of heats of formation and combustion, transforming thermochemistry into a predictive science. The chemical industry could design reactors and processes from tabulated data, without measuring every possible combination of reactants and products. Hess's law became the thermochemical equivalent of the conservation of energy principle for constant-pressure processes.

## Connection with modern physics

Enthalpy [[H]] is one of the four thermodynamic potentials in the Legendre formalism, alongside internal energy, Helmholtz free energy, and Gibbs free energy. In statistical thermodynamics, [[H]] appears in the partition function of systems in contact with a pressure reservoir (isothermal-isobaric ensemble), establishing the bridge between the microscopic scale and the macroscopic scale measurable in the laboratory. In biochemistry, enthalpies of protein binding and denaturation are measured by differential scanning calorimetry at constant pressure, making [[DeltaH]] the central experimental quantity of molecular biophysics.
