## 1. Phase diagrams and transition points

Phase diagrams of a pure substance (water, carbon dioxide, nitrogen) are constructed from the conditions under which [[mu]] of two or more phases is identical. Each curve of the diagram (melting, vaporisation, sublimation curve) corresponds to the locus of T-p pairs at which [[mu]] of the two adjacent phases are equal. The triple point is the only point where the three curves intersect and three phases coexist with identical [[mu]].

The dominant variable is [[mu]] of each phase as a function of T and p. The model hypothesis is that each phase is homogeneous and internally in equilibrium. The validity limit is that the system is sufficiently far from the critical region where phase differences vanish and [[mu]] becomes indistinguishable between liquid and gas.

**Dominant variable:** [[mu]] of each phase as a function of T and p.

**Validity limit:** system far from the critical point and from higher-order transitions.

## 2. Osmotic pressure across semipermeable membranes

When two solutions of different concentration are separated by a membrane permeable only to the solvent, [[mu]] of the solvent differs on the two sides. The solvent flows spontaneously from the side of higher [[mu]] (lower solute concentration, higher solvent activity) to the side of lower [[mu]] (higher concentration, lower activity) until the generated hydrostatic pressure equalises the [[mu]] difference. That equilibrium pressure is the osmotic pressure.

The dominant hypothesis is the ideal dilute solution approximation. The dominant variable is the [[mu]] difference of the solvent between the two solutions. The validity limit is dilute solution; for concentrated solutions or high-molecular-weight polymers, the actual osmotic pressure differs significantly from the Van't Hoff prediction due to non-ideality effects.

**Dominant variable:** [[mu]] difference of the solvent between the two chambers.

**Validity limit:** dilute solutions with solvent activity coefficient close to 1.

## 3. Electrochemistry: cell potential and equilibrium constant

In an electrochemical cell, the electric potential difference between the electrodes is directly related to the difference in [[mu]] between reactants and products of the redox reaction at the electrodes. The electromotive force of the cell is proportional to the total [[mu]] change of the process; when [[mu]] of reactants and products are equal (equilibrium), the electromotive force is zero.

This connection allows computing the equilibrium constant of the redox reaction from electric potential measurements, and vice versa. The dominant variable is the [[mu]] difference weighted by the stoichiometric coefficients of the reaction. The validity limit is the ideal solution model for ions in solution; for concentrated electrolytes, activity coefficient corrections are needed.

**Dominant variable:** [[mu]] difference between reactants and products of the redox reaction.

**Validity limit:** dilute electrolyte solutions where the Debye-Hückel model is applicable.

## 4. Biophysics: neural membrane potential

Neurons maintain a concentration difference of K⁺, Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions between the cell interior and exterior thanks to active ion pumps. This concentration difference creates an electrochemical [[mu]] difference (sum of the chemical [[mu]] and the electrical term) for each ion across the membrane. When ion channels open during the action potential, ions flow spontaneously toward the side of lower electrochemical [[mu]], generating the current that propagates the nerve signal.

The dominant variable is the electrochemical [[mu]] difference of each ion across the membrane. The hypothesis is the ideal dilute solution model for ions in the cytoplasm and extracellular fluid. The validity limit is that ion concentrations be low enough for the ideal solution model to apply (physiological K⁺ concentrations of order 100 mmol/L, within the validity range).

**Dominant variable:** electrochemical [[mu]] difference of each ion across the membrane.

**Validity limit:** physiological ion concentrations within the Debye-Hückel model validity range.

## 5. Process engineering: distillation and mixture separation

Distillation separates components of a liquid mixture by exploiting differences in [[mu]] of each component between the liquid and vapour phases. The more volatile component (higher [[mu]] in the liquid relative to the vapour) transfers preferentially to the vapour phase, enriching it. The equilibrium condition between the ascending vapour and the descending liquid on each distillation tray is equality of [[mu]] of each component in both phases.

Design of distillation columns requires computing liquid-vapour equilibrium compositions on each tray, which reduces to equating [[mu]] of each component in both phases. For non-ideal mixtures, activity coefficients shift the equilibrium position and determine whether simple distillation can separate the components or whether an azeotrope forms that prevents complete separation.

**Dominant variable:** [[mu]] difference of each component between the liquid and vapour phase on each tray.

**Validity limit:** mixtures without azeotropes or with azeotropes at compositions outside the desired operating range.

This application is especially useful for teaching because it turns an abstract potential into a design rule: a tray works only while it maintains a chemical-potential gradient strong enough to transfer matter in the intended direction. When that gradient vanishes, adding more trays no longer improves separation.
