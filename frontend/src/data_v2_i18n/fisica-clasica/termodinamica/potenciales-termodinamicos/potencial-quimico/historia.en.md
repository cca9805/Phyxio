## Historical problem

In the mid-nineteenth century, the classical thermodynamics of Clausius and Kelvin perfectly described heat and work exchanges in closed systems of fixed composition, but could not predict the equilibrium of multicomponent systems or the direction of mixing, dissolution or chemical reaction processes. The central problem was the absence of a thermodynamic potential that explicitly included composition as a state variable.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was that physicists of the era thought of equilibrium exclusively in terms of uniform temperature and pressure. Extending that framework to composition required recognising that matter, like heat, possesses a kind of "potential" whose uniformity defines equilibrium. This idea was conceptually novel and did not fit into the mental frameworks of thermodynamics at the time.

## What changed

Josiah Willard Gibbs published between 1875 and 1878 his monumental article "On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances", in which he introduced the concept of chemical potential [[mu]] as the quantity that completes the thermodynamic formalism for systems of variable composition. Gibbs demonstrated that complete thermodynamic equilibrium of a heterogeneous system requires simultaneous uniformity of temperature, pressure and [[mu]] of each component in all phases.

## Impact on physics

Gibbs's work transformed thermodynamics from a science of simple systems into a universal tool for chemistry, geology, metallurgy and biology. The **Gibbs phase rule**, derived directly from the criterion of equal [[mu]], allowed prediction of the topology of phase diagrams without extensive experimentation. Tables of standard Gibbs free energies, which tabulate [[mu_std]] for thousands of compounds, are the most direct practical legacy of that work.

## Connection with modern physics

Chemical potential [[mu]] appears in statistical mechanics as the derivative of the logarithm of the grand canonical partition function with respect to the number of particles. In solid-state physics, [[mu]] of electrons is the Fermi level: the energy of the last occupied band at zero temperature, whose position relative to the conduction band determines whether a material is a conductor, semiconductor or insulator. In biophysics, gradients of [[mu]] of ions across cell membranes are the driving force of the neural membrane potential and of ATP synthesis by ATP synthase.
