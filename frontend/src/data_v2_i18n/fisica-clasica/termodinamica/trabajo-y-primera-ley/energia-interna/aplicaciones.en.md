# Applications

## 1. Design of cooling systems in internal combustion engines

In the combustion chamber of a car engine cylinder, the fuel-air mixture experiences drastic variations in pressure and temperature in hundredths of a second. When the spark fires and the explosion occurs, the absolute temperature rises sharply, instantaneously increasing the absolute molecular internal energy of the trapped gas. To prevent the piston and the metallic block walls of the engine from suffering metallurgical damage due to overheating, liquid cooling jackets are designed around the cylinders.

The coolant flow must be capable of continuously absorbing and carrying away the heat necessary to limit the heating of the materials, based on the rate of increase of the gas's internal energy in each work cycle.

Dominant variable: the absolute temperature change experienced by the gas in each ignition cycle.
Validity limit: the ideal gas approximation for the combustion gas fails if incomplete combustion occurs with solid residue or if the injection pressure exceeds the super-critical limits of the fuel.

## 2. Management of compressed gas in industrial pneumatic systems

Industrial compressors accumulate air at high pressures within metallic storage tanks for later use in factory machinery. When compressing air continuously, the mechanical work introduced by the rotors increases the internal energy of the gas sample at an accelerated rate. If the tank does not have efficient heat dissipation systems, the internal temperature of the compressed air rises rapidly, which increases the risks of structural thermal fatigue in the tank and valve failures.

Engineers calculate the absolute internal energy of the stored air to design thermal exchangers and predict the pressure drops the gas will undergo as it progressively cools back to the temperature of the industrial environment.

Dominant variable: the constant-volume molar heat capacity of the gaseous mixture of diatomic air.
Validity limit: the linear energy change calculation is incorrect if the intake air contains a high concentration of water vapor that undergoes physical condensation inside the tank.

## 3. Safety in cryogenic liquid nitrogen tanks

Storage tanks for liquid nitrogen at cryogenic temperatures experience an inevitable heat inflow from the surroundings through the vacuum-insulated walls. While liquid and gaseous nitrogen coexist in equilibrium, this heat does not increase the temperature of the vessel, but is spent on evaporating the liquid nitrogen, modifying the internal energy associated with the phase change. Once evaporated, the gaseous nitrogen continues to absorb heat from the outside, increasing its absolute temperature and internal pressure due to the increase in its agitation energy.

The physical design of pressure relief and venting valves is calculated based on the rate of heat entering and the maximum internal energy the gaseous nitrogen can accumulate before exceeding the structural limits of the tank.

Dominant variable: the latent heat of vaporization that controls the change of internal energy in the two-phase system.
Validity limit: the ideal gas approximation for the evaporated nitrogen ceases to be met at high pressures near the rupture limit of the cryogenic tank.

## 4. Functioning of gas shock absorbers in heavy vehicles

Gas shock absorbers for trucks and heavy vehicles use a sealed piston that compresses nitrogen to mitigate road vibrations. When the truck crosses a pothole or road imperfection, the shock absorber rod performs extremely fast mechanical compression work on the gas. As this is a very rapid deformation, the process can be considered practically adiabatic, so all the absorbed mechanical energy is converted immediately into an increase in the internal energy of the nitrogen, raising its temperature.

The metallic body of the shock absorber must be designed to dissipate this thermal increase accumulated after continuous vibrations on long-distance trips, preventing the adjacent hydraulic oil from overheating and losing its design viscosity.

Dominant variable: the work of mechanical compression from the piston acting on the nitrogen.
Validity limit: the ideal gas model fails if extreme deformation compresses the gas to a volume so small that the physical covolume of the molecules is representative.

## 5. Climatological modeling of convective currents in the atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere can be interpreted on a large scale as a gaseous mixture subject to continuous cycles of solar radiation absorption and infrared radiation emission back toward outer space. The internal energy of warm air masses changes constantly due to this radiative energy transfer and surface water evaporation. This increase in molecular internal energy decreases the local density of the air and generates ascending thermal convection currents that originate local winds, pressure fronts, and storms.

Meteorologists use numerical models that calculate the internal energy balance of air masses in each atmospheric sector to predict wind direction and speed and the evolution of global climate phenomena.

Dominant variable: the net thermal radiation absorbed that alters the temperature of the air mass.
Validity limit: classical thermal analysis is insufficient in areas with high humidity concentrations where phase changes of vapor condensation or ice sublimation occur in clouds.
