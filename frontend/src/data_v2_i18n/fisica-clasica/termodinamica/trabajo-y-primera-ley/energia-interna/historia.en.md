## Historical problem

During the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, physics lacked a coherent explanation of the nature of heat and its relationship to mechanical work. The great question that scientists of the era could not answer was whether heat constituted a material, fluid, and indestructible substance, or whether, on the contrary, it was a manifestation of the internal motion of the particles that make up matter. This conceptual gap prevented understanding of how the newly invented steam engines converted fire into useful motion.

---

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main intellectual obstacle was the **caloric theory**, championed by chemists as prestigious as Antoine Lavoisier. Under this paradigm, heat was interpreted as an imponderable, indestructible, and self-repulsive fluid called caloric. It was assumed that bodies contained a fixed quantity of this fluid, which simply flowed from hot bodies to cold ones. This conservative conception prevented accepting that heat could be created from nothing by friction, and it blocked the definition of internal energy as a property that changes dynamically.

---

## What changed

The conceptual breakthrough began when Benjamin Thompson (Count Rumford) observed that the boring of bronze cannons produced heat without limit through friction, contradicting the hypothesis of caloric conservation. Years later, in the 1840s, **James Prescott Joule** carried out a series of high-precision mechanical experiments, most notably the famous paddle-wheel device suspended in water and driven by falling weights. Joule demonstrated the constant quantitative equivalence between the mechanical work performed and the heat dissipated, thereby determining the mechanical equivalent of heat.

---

## Impact on physics

Joule's investigations definitively destroyed the caloric theory. Rudolf Clausius and William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) took these results and formulated the first law of thermodynamics in mathematical form, formally introducing the concept of **internal energy** as a state function. Thermal physics was unified with classical mechanics, establishing that the total energy of the universe remains constant and that heat and work are merely ways of transferring energy to the internal reservoir of matter.

---

## Connection with modern physics

Today, internal energy is studied through **statistical mechanics**, which connects the microscopic quantum states of atoms with the thermodynamic variables of pressure and [[temperatura]]. This theoretical framework allows the analysis of complex modern systems that deviate from classical laws, such as the electron gas in semiconductors, the behaviour of white dwarfs in astrophysics, or the design of quantum refrigerators capable of approaching absolute zero in order to maintain the stability of qubits in quantum computers.
