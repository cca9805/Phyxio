## Historical problem

In the early nineteenth century, engineers building steam engines needed a way to quantify the useful effect of their machines: how much motion a given amount of coal could produce. The intuitive concept of "work" existed in mechanics, but its extension to fluids at variable pressure lacked a precise formulation. The question was: how to measure the "force" of a steam engine in a reproducible and comparable way?

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was distinguishing between the energy stored in the steam (related to pressure and temperature) and the energy transferred to the piston as mechanical work. Engineers empirically used the concept of "mean pressure" multiplied by the piston stroke, but without a theoretical framework to justify that relationship or extend it to variable pressures.

## What changed

Sadi Carnot (1824) and James Prescott Joule (1840s–1850s) formalised the relationship between heat and work, laying the foundations for defining [[trabajo_termodinamico]] as ∫p dV. The introduction of the p-V diagram by James Watt and his partner Boulton (the steam indicator) was the practical instrument that allowed [[trabajo_termodinamico]] to be measured directly as the area under the curve traced by the indicator during each engine cycle.

## Impact on physics

The precise formulation of [[trabajo_termodinamico]] = ∫p dV was essential for stating the first law of thermodynamics and for establishing that [[trabajo_termodinamico]] and heat Q are the two forms of energy transfer between a system and its surroundings. Recognising that [[trabajo_termodinamico]] is path-dependent while internal energy ΔU is a state function was one of the great conceptual advances of nineteenth-century physics.

## Connection with modern physics

Expansion work [[trabajo_termodinamico]] = ∫p dV generalises in statistical thermodynamics as the work done by the particle ensemble when changing the container volume. In quantum mechanics, the analogue of work in finite systems is the change in energy of quantum levels when external parameters of the Hamiltonian (such as the potential well volume) are varied. In cosmology, the work done by the expansion of the universe is described by an analogous term involving the pressure of the cosmic fluid.
