# Applications

## 1. Home insulation
Dominant variable: [[resistencia_termica]]
Validity limit: [[diferencia_temperatura]] < 50 K
In a home, conduction appears through walls, roofs, floors, and windows. The purpose is not to eliminate thermal passage completely, but to reduce the [[potencia_termica]] escaping during the heating or cooling [[tiempo]]. The most useful design variable is [[resistencia_termica]], because it combines [[conductividad_termica]], [[area]], and [[espesor]] into one opposition reading. A wall with low [[conductividad_termica]] and large [[espesor]] has lower [[flujo_calor]] for the same [[diferencia_temperatura]] between indoor [[temperatura_caliente]] and outdoor [[temperatura_fria]]. The [[gradiente_temperatura]] helps visualize how temperature falls across the wall. If insulation is interrupted by thermal bridges, the effective loss [[area]] changes and the simple model becomes incomplete. Estimating [[calor_transferido]] over a night translates the physics into energy use. In renovation work, adding a few centimeters of insulation may save more energy than changing the thermostat slightly. Joints, shutter boxes, and structural columns also matter because a narrow strip with poor thermal opposition can dominate losses. This is why thermal cameras often reveal narrow cold bands rather than uniform wall behavior, and those bands guide the next insulation decision.

## 2. Electronic heat sinks
Dominant variable: [[conductividad_termica]]
Validity limit: [[potencia_termica]] < 200 W
In electronics, conduction decides whether a processor, battery, or power module can operate without overheating. The component generates [[potencia_termica]] and must transfer it toward a heat sink with high [[conductividad_termica]]. The aim is to reduce [[resistencia_termica]] between the [[temperatura_caliente]] region and the environment associated with [[temperatura_fria]]. A small base [[espesor]] and a large contact [[area]] reduce opposition, while poorly applied thermal paste adds contact resistance beyond the elementary model. The [[flujo_calor]] can be very high near the chip, so the design spreads it over a wider surface. During long [[tiempo]], small design differences change the [[calor_transferido]] toward the sink and the stability of the device. The [[gradiente_temperatura]] helps locate critical zones. Real designs add fins, airflow, and interface materials, yet the first conductive question remains simple: how can energy leave the hot spot and reach a larger region before temperature rises too far. A compact device can fail not because total energy is huge, but because the local pathway is too narrow for the generated rate.

## 3. Cooking vessels
Dominant variable: [[flujo_calor]]
Validity limit: [[gradiente_temperatura]] < 5000 K/m
A pan performs well when it distributes heat rapidly from its base to the food. The metal [[conductividad_termica]] supports high [[potencia_termica]] through a broad [[area]], while [[espesor]] reduces deformation and smooths local differences. The [[temperatura_caliente]] is near the flame or electric heater, and [[temperatura_fria]] can be associated with the upper region touching the food. The [[diferencia_temperatura]] drives energy passage and [[flujo_calor]] indicates how intensely each part of the surface receives energy. If the material conducts poorly, hot and cold spots appear. During short cooking [[tiempo]], accumulated [[calor_transferido]] affects texture, evaporation, and browning. The base [[resistencia_termica]] should not be so high that the pan responds slowly to temperature changes. Layered pans exploit the model: steel gives durability, aluminum spreads heat, and the base adapts to induction. Thickness slows the first response but improves uniformity. That tradeoff explains why thin pans react quickly but may burn food locally, while heavier bases store and spread the incoming energy more evenly.

## 4. Cryogenic thermal links
Dominant variable: [[espesor]]
Validity limit: [[calor_transferido]] < 100000 J
In cryogenic containers, conduction is a threat: any solid support between the exterior and the cold interior may transport unwanted energy. Ambient [[temperatura_caliente]] and cryogenic [[temperatura_fria]] create a large [[diferencia_temperatura]]. To reduce [[potencia_termica]], engineers choose low [[conductividad_termica]] materials, increase effective path [[espesor]], or reduce contact [[area]]. The [[gradiente_temperatura]] can be extreme, so thermal stress also matters. The [[flujo_calor]] through small supports may look modest, but over long [[tiempo]] it can produce enough [[calor_transferido]] to evaporate part of the liquid. The [[resistencia_termica]] is a protection metric. The flat model is only an initial estimate because real designs include complex geometry and radiation. Space instruments and low-temperature laboratories often use thin rods, composite supports, and intermediate thermal stages. Every solid bridge is inspected as a possible energy leak. Even a screw, sensor wire, or support bracket can become a thermal shortcut when the temperature separation is enormous. Designers therefore count every solid connection and ask whether its geometry creates an avoidable conductive bridge.

## 5. Laboratory measurements
Dominant variable: [[potencia_termica]]
Validity limit: [[area]] > 0.0001 m²
In the laboratory, a conduction experiment can measure [[conductividad_termica]] by comparing [[potencia_termica]], [[diferencia_temperatura]], [[area]], and [[espesor]]. A sample is placed between a plate associated with [[temperatura_caliente]] and another linked to [[temperatura_fria]]. When the system reaches steady state, [[gradiente_temperatura]] can be considered approximately uniform. The [[flujo_calor]] helps compare samples of different size, and [[resistencia_termica]] ranks materials by insulating ability. If the test lasts enough [[tiempo]], accumulated [[calor_transferido]] can be compared with the electrical input of the heater. Main limits include lateral losses, poorly calibrated sensors, and imperfect contacts. Even so, the practice is valuable because it turns an invisible idea into measurements: temperatures, geometry, power, and accumulated energy become one experimental story. Repeating the test with different thicknesses isolates geometry, while repeating it with equal geometry reveals material behavior. The experiment also teaches uncertainty: a small thermometer offset can distort the inferred material property if the temperature difference is small.
