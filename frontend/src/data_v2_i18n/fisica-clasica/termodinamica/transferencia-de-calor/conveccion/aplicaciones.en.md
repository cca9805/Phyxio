# Applications

## 1. Electronic cooling with heat sinks
Dominant variable: [[coeficiente_conveccion]]
Validity limit: [[coeficiente_conveccion]] < 200 W m⁻² K⁻¹
In a computer, a power converter, or an industrial control board, convection determines whether the heat-sink [[temperatura_superficie]] stays safe. The chip generates [[potencia_termica]], that energy reaches a metal base, and air with lower [[temperatura_fluido]] removes it. Design is not just adding metal: fins increase [[area]] and ventilation raises [[coeficiente_conveccion]]. [[flujo_calor]] helps compare different fin regions, while [[calor_transferido]] over a long [[tiempo]] gives the total removed energy. If a correlation is used, [[numero_nusselt]], [[conductividad_termica_fluido]], and [[longitud_caracteristica]] help estimate the real thermal contact. The allowed [[diferencia_temperatura]] is limited by electronics, and [[resistencia_termica_conveccion]] becomes a design tool: the smaller it is, the easier it is to keep the device within its safe thermal window without oversizing the fan or wasting electrical power. In the laboratory, [[temperatura_superficie]] should be measured at several points because one sensor can hide regions with uneven [[flujo_calor]]. The inlet and outlet [[temperatura_fluido]] should also be recorded to check whether the air warms too much inside the enclosure. This prevents a misleading design in which the base is safe but fin tips carry little useful exchange.

## 2. Classroom and laboratory air conditioning
Dominant variable: [[flujo_calor]]
Validity limit: [[diferencia_temperatura]] < 15 K
In an occupied room, convection appears at walls, windows, radiators, air-supply grilles, and human bodies. Air moves with a [[temperatura_fluido]] that does not always match the room average, while each wall has its own [[temperatura_superficie]]. Comfort depends on the exchanged [[potencia_termica]] and on how [[flujo_calor]] is distributed over bodies and surfaces. A strong draft can increase [[coeficiente_conveccion]] and make people feel cold even when the thermometer barely changes. Energy use is estimated by integrating [[calor_transferido]] over the operating [[tiempo]]. In real equipment, the [[area]] of coils, radiators, and ducts is designed to lower [[resistencia_termica_conveccion]]. Correlations with [[numero_nusselt]] use air [[conductividad_termica_fluido]] and [[longitud_caracteristica]], but measurements are needed because recirculation can alter the effective [[diferencia_temperatura]]. A careful reading separates thermal sensation from energy balance: the user feels local changes in [[flujo_calor]], while the building pays for the sum of [[calor_transferido]] over the whole operating [[tiempo]]. Air distribution matters because a poorly placed diffuser can increase local discomfort while reducing useful room-scale transport.

## 3. Cooling of food and drinks
Dominant variable: [[tiempo]]
Validity limit: [[temperatura_superficie]] > [[temperatura_fluido]]
Soup, coffee, or a hot tray lose energy by convection to air. The food or container [[temperatura_superficie]] is usually higher than ambient [[temperatura_fluido]], so [[diferencia_temperatura]] drives cooling. Blowing or moving the air increases [[coeficiente_conveccion]], lowers [[resistencia_termica_conveccion]], and raises [[flujo_calor]]. Exposed [[area]] also matters: soup in a wide bowl cools faster than in a narrow mug because it offers more boundary. During the waiting [[tiempo]], [[potencia_termica]] decreases as the food approaches the surroundings, so [[calor_transferido]] does not grow at a constant rate. For simple estimates, [[numero_nusselt]], [[conductividad_termica_fluido]], and [[longitud_caracteristica]] can guide the order of magnitude, although evaporation and radiation may require a more complete model when precision matters. The container adds another layer of interpretation because its material can redistribute [[temperatura_superficie]] and change the active [[area]]. Thus two cups with the same liquid may cool at very different rates. Stirring the drink changes the internal surface temperature pattern, so the boundary model becomes closer to the observed cooling.

## 4. Industrial heat exchangers
Dominant variable: [[area]]
Validity limit: [[numero_nusselt]] > 10
In a heat exchanger, two fluids separated by a wall transfer energy in a controlled way. Convection appears on both sides: each stream has its own [[temperatura_fluido]], [[coeficiente_conveccion]], and flow regime. The wall [[temperatura_superficie]] is not always measured directly, but it controls the local [[diferencia_temperatura]]. Design seeks high [[potencia_termica]] without excessive pressure losses. Engineers therefore increase [[area]] through tubes, plates, or fins, and lower [[resistencia_termica_conveccion]] by improving mixing. [[flujo_calor]] helps detect overloaded zones that could foul or degrade. [[calor_transferido]] during operating [[tiempo]] connects to production, efficiency, and cost. [[numero_nusselt]] correlations depend on [[conductividad_termica_fluido]] and [[longitud_caracteristica]], so changing the fluid or geometry requires recalculating the design rather than reusing old coefficients. During continuous operation, fouling must be monitored because a deposited film increases global opposition and shifts [[temperatura_superficie]]. The same flow rate may then deliver less useful [[potencia_termica]]. Maintenance data are therefore part of the physical model, not just an operational detail after the calculation.

## 5. Thermal protection in vehicles and spacecraft
Dominant variable: [[resistencia_termica_conveccion]]
Validity limit: [[flujo_calor]] > 0 W m⁻²
In cars, trains, aircraft, and spacecraft, convection can be either ally or threat. A radiator tries to remove [[potencia_termica]] from an engine, while a thermal shield tries to limit [[flujo_calor]] toward sensitive regions. The exposed [[temperatura_superficie]] is compared with external [[temperatura_fluido]] to obtain [[diferencia_temperatura]]. At high speed, [[coeficiente_conveccion]] can rise strongly, reducing [[resistencia_termica_conveccion]] and increasing [[calor_transferido]] during the exposure [[tiempo]]. Frontal [[area]], roughness, and shape determine the relevant [[longitud_caracteristica]]. Models use [[numero_nusselt]] and [[conductividad_termica_fluido]], but they must be combined with internal conduction and radiation when temperatures are high. This application shows that more convection is not automatically better: it depends on whether the goal is to dissipate energy or prevent it from entering. During atmospheric entry or intense braking, the environment can vary within seconds. Therefore exposure [[tiempo]] and the evolution of [[temperatura_fluido]] are as important as the maximum instantaneous value. Designers compare short peaks with accumulated exposure because material damage may depend on both rate and total energy.
