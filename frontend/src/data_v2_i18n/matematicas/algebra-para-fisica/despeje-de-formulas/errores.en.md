# Common errors

## Error 1: choosing the wrong operation

This happens when a term is moved across the equation without identifying the operation that connects it to the unknown. It feels reasonable because students often hear shortcuts such as move it to the other side, but that shortcut is safe only when the structure is understood.

For example, in `F = m a`, solving for `a` requires division by `m`, not subtracting mass. In `E = P t`, solving for `t` requires energy divided by power. Detect the error by asking which inverse operation would undo the original expression.

## Error 2: losing the meaning of the variable

The same letter may represent different quantities in different topics, and subscripts often matter. Confusing `v`, `v_0`, and `v_f` in motion is not a notation detail; it changes the physical question. The algebra may look neat while answering the wrong question.

Detect this by returning to the statement and writing a phrase: `v_0` is initial velocity, `t` is the time interval, `F` is the net force. If the variable cannot be named, it should not yet be rearranged.

## Error 3: ignoring units

This error seems small because many school formulas are written only with letters. In physics, units are part of the information. If solving for acceleration gives metres per second instead of metres per second squared, a step or the chosen formula is wrong.

In energy, work, and power, ignoring units leads to mixing joules, watts, and seconds as if they were untyped numbers. Detect the problem with dimensional analysis after each important step, not only at the end.

## Error 4: confusing sign and magnitude

A negative sign does not always mean that the result is impossible. In one-dimensional motion, negative velocity can mean motion opposite to the positive axis. Negative work can mean that a force removes mechanical energy from a system.

Detect this error when a numerical result is reasonable but its direction or energy meaning is being erased. Correct it by asking whether the requested quantity is a scalar, a vector component, a magnitude, or an oriented result.

## Error 5: applying a rule outside its domain

Some operations have conditions. You cannot divide by zero mass, take the logarithm of a dimensional quantity without forming a dimensionless ratio, or take a square root and forget possible algebraic branches. Physics adds model conditions such as constant speed, uniform field, or negligible friction.

Detect this by checking denominators, roots, even powers, and special functions. Correct it by writing the condition next to the rearrangement: nonzero mass, positive time, dimensionless argument, or solution compatible with the described motion.

## Quick detection rule

If a rearrangement gives the wrong unit, an absurd dependence, or a result that does not answer the problem, it is wrong even if the algebra looks familiar. The quick rule is: isolated variable, correct unit, interpretable sign, valid model.

When in doubt, test an extreme case. If doubling distance reduces time at constant speed, the formula has been inverted. If increasing mass reduces the force required for the same acceleration, multiplication and division have been confused.

## Quick diagnosis

Input error: using a datum without unit, scale, or a decision about whether it is constant. In formula rearrangement, an isolated number rarely contains all the physical information.

Structure error: applying a correct operation to the wrong relation. The arithmetic may be right while the physics is wrong if the selected pattern does not represent the phenomenon.

Output error: accepting a number without comparing it with an estimate. A result must have a reasonable unit, order of magnitude, sign, and dependence.
