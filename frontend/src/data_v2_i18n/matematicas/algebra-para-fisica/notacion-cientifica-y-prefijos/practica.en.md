# Practice

## Level 1: recognition

Identify the prefix and its power of ten: kilo, milli, micro, nano, and mega. Write whether the number becomes larger or smaller when converted to the base unit.

Exercise: mentally convert `3 km`, `4 ms`, `7 microC`, and `2 MW` into a number times a power of ten with a base unit. Do not perform compound calculations yet.

After writing your answer, use Phyxio's unit converter to check only one of the four. If you need to check all of them, you probably do not yet recognise the prefixes securely enough.

## Level 2: direct application

Convert one prefixed quantity to a base unit. For example, `12 mm = 12 x 10^-3 m = 1.2 x 10^-2 m`. Notice that the final step normalizes the coefficient.

Exercise: convert `0.5 km` to metres, `250 mA` to amperes, `8 ns` to seconds, and `3.6 kJ` to joules. Check that each physical scale is reasonable.

Correction criterion: the results should be `5.0 x 10^2 m`, `2.50 x 10^-1 A`, `8 x 10^-9 s`, and `3.6 x 10^3 J`. If an exponent appears with the opposite sign, check whether large and small prefixes were confused.

## Level 3: choosing the tool

Decide whether conversion is needed before operating. If a formula uses metres and seconds, a distance in centimetres and a time in milliseconds should be converted first. If all quantities share a coherent unit system, that system may be kept.

Exercise: to calculate speed with `d = 40 cm` and `t = 0.20 s`, decide whether to use centimetres per second or convert to metres per second. Explain which form communicates the result better.

Advanced exercise: convert `9 cm^2` to `m^2`, `3 mm^3` to `m^3`, `90 km/h` to `m/s`, and `0.8 g/cm^3` to `kg/m^3`. State which cases raise the prefix to a power and which use a quotient.

Correction criterion: `9 cm^2 = 9 x 10^-4 m^2`, `3 mm^3 = 3 x 10^-9 m^3`, `90 km/h = 25 m/s`, and `0.8 g/cm^3 = 8.0 x 10^2 kg/m^3`.

Tool check: verify these four results with the converter. If one value disagrees, write whether the mistake was in area, volume, quotient, or prefix. Then use the calculator only to repeat one numerical operation, not to decide the conversion.

## Level 4: physical interpretation

Read exponents as scales. `10^-9 m` suggests nanometres; `10^6 W` suggests megawatts; `10^-3 s` suggests milliseconds. Link each scale to a plausible physical phenomenon.

Exercise: classify `5 x 10^-6 s`, `2 x 10^3 m`, `9 x 10^-3 N`, and `1.5 x 10^6 J` as micro, kilo, milli, or mega scale when appropriate.

Advanced exercise: decide how to communicate these results: `4.2 x 10^-7 m`, `6.5 x 10^4 W`, `3.0 x 10^-3 C`, and `1.1 x 10^9 Hz`. Choose between base unit, SI prefix, or scientific notation and justify which is easier to read.

Correction criterion: expressions such as `420 nm`, `65 kW`, `3.0 mC`, and `1.1 GHz` are reasonable, provided that the unit is preserved and significant figures are not lost.

If you are unsure whether to keep scientific notation or use a prefix, use the converter to explore both forms and decide which communicates the phenomenon better. For problems with constants, also open the constants table and check that the value used is in SI before combining it with your data.

## Level 5: intentional error

Analyse the error: someone converts `4 mm` into `4 x 10^3 m`. Explain which prefix was confused, give the correct conversion, and explain why the resulting scale is absurd for a small object.

Analyse another error: someone states that `1 cm^2 = 10^-2 m^2`. Correct the conversion by remembering that the centimetre factor is squared, and explain why the correct result is `10^-4 m^2`.

Analyse a third error: someone converts `60 km/h` into `60 x 10^3 m/s`. Explain that kilometres were converted to metres but hours were not converted to seconds. Correct the result and state why `60 km/h` should be on the scale of tens of `m/s`, not thousands.

Analyse a fourth error: someone converts `1 g/cm^3` into `10^-3 kg/m^3`. Detect that mass was converted but the denominator volume was not. Correct the conversion and explain why the correct SI result is `10^3 kg/m^3`.

## Mastery path

Basic level: you should recognize common prefixes, convert an isolated quantity to the base unit, and read whether an exponent represents a large or small scale. At this stage, the main criterion is not reversing the sign of the exponent.

Intermediate level: you should operate with quantities that have compound units, such as areas, volumes, speeds, and densities. The critical point is remembering that the conversion factor is also squared or cubed when the unit is squared or cubed.

Advanced level: you should decide how to communicate a result: SI prefix, base unit, or scientific notation. The best form is not always the shortest one; it is the form that preserves precision, avoids ambiguity, and lets the physical scale be compared with other data in the problem.

## Deepening practice

Basic level: invent a simple numerical example related to scientific notation and SI prefixes, solve it, and write one sentence with unit and meaning.

Intermediate level: change one datum by a factor `10` and predict the new result before calculating. Then verify whether the prediction matches.

Advanced level: write a limiting case where the usual procedure may fail. Explain which physical or mathematical condition is no longer satisfied.
