# Practice

## Level 1: recognition

Associate quantities with scales. Decide whether `0.004 m`, `3000 m`, `2 x 10^-6 s`, and `5 x 10^6 J` belong to milli, kilo, micro, or mega scale when appropriate.

Exercise: write the approximate order of magnitude of `7 m`, `80 m`, `900 m`, `0.02 s`, and `0.0004 C`. Do not seek precision; seek scale.

## Level 2: direct application

Estimate with powers of ten. If a distance is `600 m` and a time is `30 s`, approximate them as `10^3 m` and `10^1 s`, then estimate speed as `10^2 m/s`.

Exercise: estimate the force if a mass of `80 kg` accelerates at `3 m/s^2`. Then compare with the detailed calculation and decide whether the order of magnitude agrees.

## Level 3: choosing the tool

Decide when estimation is enough and when detailed calculation is required. To detect a unit error, order of magnitude is often enough. To compare two close measurements, it is not enough.

Exercise: state whether you would use estimation or detailed calculation to find a table height, calibrate a sensor, check an absurd speed, or compare two nearly equal resistances.

## Level 4: physical interpretation

Turn exponents into physical language. `10^-9 m` suggests nanometre scale; `10^3 m` suggests kilometre scale; `10^5 Pa` is close to atmospheric pressure.

Exercise: interpret `10^1 m/s`, `10^-3 kg`, `10^6 W`, and `10^-6 C` by naming a kind of phenomenon that could have that scale.

## Level 5: intentional error

Analyse this incorrect estimate: a person travels `1000 m` in `100 s`, and someone estimates `10^5 m/s`. Explain which exponent operation was wrong and what the correct scale is.

Analyse another case: a length of `2 mm` is estimated as `10^3 m`. Correct the prefix and explain why the order of magnitude reveals the error before any formula is used.

## Mastery path

Basic level: you should turn numbers into approximate powers of ten and connect them with familiar scales. The goal is to detect whether something belongs to human, microscopic, astronomical, slow, fast, weak, or intense scales.

Intermediate level: you should combine orders of magnitude in simple formulas and use the result to check whether a detailed calculation makes sense. Here you are not seeking decimal precision; you are looking for an early warning against wrong unit conversions or impossible results.

Advanced level: you should justify which terms can be neglected, compare dominant mechanisms, and separate physical estimation from arbitrary rounding. A strong advanced estimate states assumptions, dominant scale, and expected error range.

## Deepening practice

Basic level: invent a simple numerical example related to order of magnitude, solve it, and write one sentence with unit and meaning.

Intermediate level: change one datum by a factor `10` and predict the new result before calculating. Then verify whether the prediction matches.

Advanced level: write a limiting case where the usual procedure may fail. Explain which physical or mathematical condition is no longer satisfied.
