# Physical applications

## 1. Sound

It appears in decibels. A ratio of intensities relative to a reference is calculated, usually through a logarithm with base `10`. The result means how many times intensity changes, not a linear addition. The dangerous error is believing that `60 dB` is twice `30 dB`.

## 2. Physical chemistry and pH

It appears when measuring acidity through `pH`. A negative logarithmic scale of `H+` ion concentration is calculated. The result means concentration factor. The dangerous error is forgetting that lowering pH by one unit multiplies concentration by `10`.

## 3. Waves and spectra

It appears when representing frequencies and wavelengths spanning many ranges, from radio waves to gamma radiation. Comparison by orders of magnitude is calculated. The result shows full regions without crushing data. The dangerous error is reading logarithmic separations as linear differences.

## 4. Fields and power laws

It appears in log-log graphs of intensity, field, or flux versus distance. The scaling exponent is calculated. The result means how the quantity changes when distance is multiplied. The dangerous error is taking logarithms without subtracting experimental backgrounds.

## 5. Experimental data

It appears when measurements range from very small to very large values. A stable representation is calculated to compare patterns. The result helps detect scaling laws. The dangerous error is hiding relative uncertainties or zeros incompatible with logarithms.
