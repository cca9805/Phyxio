# Procedure

## Before calculating

Read which quantity is being optimized or interpreted. Maximum height, minimum energy, maximum power, and equilibrium point are not the same. Each case changes the physical reading of the extremum.

Mark the allowed domain. A maximum inside one interval may disappear if the domain is enlarged; a physical minimum may occur at a boundary and not at a zero-slope point.

## Step 1: identify the mathematical object

The object may be a graph, table, or function. Identify independent variable, dependent quantity, and unit. Then locate increasing, decreasing, and flat regions.

If you have a formula and advanced level, compute derivative or reason with slope. If you only have a graph, use trend changes and compare nearby values.

## Step 2: choose the operation

To recognize a visual extremum, look for change from increasing to decreasing or the reverse. For an analytic critical point, solve `f'(x) = 0` and check points where the derivative does not exist.

For stability, locating the point is not enough. Look around it: if energy rises on both sides when displaced, the minimum is stable; if it falls when moving away, the maximum is unstable.

## Step 3: preserve units and signs

The location of the extremum has units of the horizontal variable; the extreme value has units of the vertical quantity. Do not mix them. A maximum at `t = 4 s` and height `20 m` contains two different data.

The sign of slope before and after classifies the point: `+` to `-` suggests maximum; `-` to `+` suggests minimum. If it does not change, it may be a stationary point without extremum.

## Step 4: calculate

On a graph, read coordinates of the extremum and compare with neighboring points. In a function, calculate candidates with `f'(x) = 0`, evaluate the function at candidates and domain boundaries, and compare values.

To classify, use slope sign change or curvature. If the second derivative is positive at a critical point, there is usually a local minimum; if negative, there is usually a local maximum.

## Step 5: interpret the result

Write a physical sentence: "minimum energy indicates stable equilibrium", "maximum amplitude indicates resonance", "maximum range appears for this condition", or "the critical point is not stable".

Include domain and limitations. If the extremum is obtained inside a simplified model, state that it may not hold outside the interval or with omitted effects.

## Self-check checklist

Optimized quantity, domain, coordinates with units, local or global, classification, stability or physical meaning, and model limits. If domain is missing, the extremum may be misinterpreted.
