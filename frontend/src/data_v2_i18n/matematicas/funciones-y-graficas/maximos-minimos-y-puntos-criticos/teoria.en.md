# Maxima, minima, and critical points

## What it is

A maximum is a point where a quantity reaches a value greater than nearby values. A minimum is a point where it reaches a value smaller than nearby values. A critical point is a place where trend changes, slope becomes zero, or the function stops behaving regularly.

In physics, these points often mark equilibrium, stability, threshold, saturation, or optimal condition. They are not just drawing features: they indicate where the system changes behavior or where a quantity stops increasing.

## Why it appears in physics

It appears in potential energy, motion, thermodynamics, optics, resonance, and experimental optimization. A minimum of energy may represent stable equilibrium; a maximum may represent unstable equilibrium or a barrier; a maximum amplitude may indicate resonance.

It also appears when searching for optimal conditions: maximum range, maximum power, minimum cost, minimum resistance, or operating point. Reading extrema avoids trying values at random.

## Visual or geometric idea

The visual image is a curve with peaks, valleys, and trend changes. At a peak, the curve rises before and falls after. At a valley, it falls before and rises after. At an inflection point, curvature changes even if there is no maximum or minimum.

If the curve represents potential energy, a valley resembles a ball that returns to the center when displaced slightly: stable equilibrium. A peak resembles a ball that moves away when displaced: unstable equilibrium.

## Minimal symbolic language

At advanced level, critical points are often found with zero slope: `f'(x) = 0`, or at points where the derivative does not exist. To classify them, inspect the sign change of slope or curvature.

In basic graph reading, it is enough to observe whether the function changes from increasing to decreasing, decreasing to increasing, or changes curvature. The symbol must preserve the quantity: maximizing height is not the same as maximizing energy or power.

## What the result means

A maximum may mean limit value, resonance, optimum performance, or energy barrier. A minimum may mean stable state, lowest energy, minimum time, or lowest-cost configuration.

The result must include variable, unit, and domain. Saying "the maximum is at `x = 3`" is incomplete; say which quantity is optimized, what value it reaches, and inside which interval.

## When not to use it

Do not call a local maximum global without checking the whole domain. Do not confuse critical point with stable equilibrium: some critical points are unstable or do not represent physical equilibrium.

Do not use a smoothed curve to invent extrema if experimental data are very noisy. In the lab, an apparent maximum may be a fluctuation unless repetition and uncertainty are controlled.

## Operational summary

First identify the vertical quantity and horizontal domain. Then locate changes in increase, decrease, and curvature. Then classify maximum, minimum, critical point, or inflection and translate the result into equilibrium, stability, threshold, or optimum.

Basic level: recognize peaks and valleys. Intermediate level: decide local/global and read units. Advanced level: use derivatives, curvature, stability, and model limits.
