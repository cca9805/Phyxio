# Practice

## Level 1: recognition

Classify six schematic graphs as linear, quadratic, inverse, exponential, logarithmic, or sinusoidal. Write one visual clue for each: constant slope, curvature, flattening, repetition, or fast fall.

Identify whether the quantity grows, decreases, oscillates, or approaches a limit. Do not calculate parameters yet; the goal is recognizing behavior.

## Level 2: direct application

Calculate the slope of a linear `F-x` relation and write the unit of the constant. Then measure period and amplitude in a simple sinusoidal signal.

For an inverse relation, compare expected values when distance doubles. Explain whether the result falls to one half, one quarter, or another amount depending on the chosen model.

## Level 3: choosing the tool

Decide which function family you would try for: a spring, cooling, light intensity with distance, oscillator position, and projectile height. Justify each choice with one physical property.

Compare two possible models for scarce data: a line and a parabola. Write which additional measurement you would request to decide whether there is acceleration or constant velocity.

## Level 4: physical interpretation

Interpret a decreasing exponential with factor `0.5` every `10 s`. Explain what half-life means and why the same amount is not always subtracted.

Interpret a phase-shifted sinusoid. State what changes in the drawing, which parameter represents it, and what it could mean in waves or harmonic motion.

## Level 5: intentional error

A learner sees that a graph grows and claims it must be linear. Locate the error, explain that other functions also grow, and correct it by proposing tests: constant slope, constant ratio, or period.

Another learner fits an inverse law outside the measured interval and predicts an infinite value at the source. Detect the domain problem, explain which assumption fails, and write a limited physical conclusion.
