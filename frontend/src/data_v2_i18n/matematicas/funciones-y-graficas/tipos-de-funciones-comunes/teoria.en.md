# Common function types

## What it is

Recognizing function types means reading the general shape of a relation before calculating. A line, parabola, inverse curve, exponential, logarithm, or sinusoid does not tell the same physical story.

The tool is not memorizing isolated drawings, but linking shape, equation, units, and behavior. This lets you anticipate constant-rate growth, acceleration, weakening with distance, decay, compressed scale, or periodic repetition.

## Why it appears in physics

It appears because physical models produce recognizable graph families. Uniform motion gives lines, accelerated motion gives parabolas, many ideal fields follow inverse laws, decays use exponentials, and waves use sinusoidal functions.

It also appears in laboratory work. Before fitting data, check whether the expected shape is linear, quadratic, inverse, or periodic. If the shape does not fit, the model, scale, interval, or measurement may be wrong.

## Visual or geometric idea

The basic image is a gallery of behaviors. A line keeps constant slope; a quadratic curve bends more and more; an inverse curve falls fast and then flattens; an exponential multiplies changes; a logarithm grows more slowly; a sinusoid repeats cycles.

Each shape answers a different question. A line asks about proportionality, a parabola about changing rate, an inverse relation about weakening with distance, an exponential about relative growth or decay, and a sinusoid about phase, period, and amplitude.

## Minimal symbolic language

Basic forms are written as `y = ax + b`, `y = ax^2 + bx + c`, `y = k/x`, `y = A e^(kt)`, `y = a log(x) + b`, and `y = A sin(omega t + phi)`. The letters are not decoration: they represent physical parameters.

The parameter `a` may be slope, curvature, or scale; `b` may be initial value; `A` often means amplitude; `k` measures growth or decay strength; `omega` measures angular frequency; `phi` shifts phase.

## What the result means

Identifying the function helps interpret the model. A line in force-extension suggests a spring constant. A decreasing exponential in radioactive activity suggests half-life. A sinusoid in position-time indicates oscillation with period and amplitude.

The result is not only "it looks curved". It must say which quantity controls the change, which parameter can be calculated, and which prediction is valid inside the observed domain.

## When not to use it

Do not classify a function only by appearance if axes are distorted or if the scale is logarithmic. An exponential may look linear on semilog paper, and a power law may look linear on log-log paper.

Do not force a function family with too few data. Two points always define a line, but they do not prove that the phenomenon is linear. Interval, uncertainty, and physical criterion are needed.

## Operational summary

First read axes and units. Then describe the trend: constant, curved, inverse, multiplicative, compressed, or periodic. Then connect that shape with a candidate function and with the physical parameter that might be extracted.

Basic level: recognize visual shapes. Intermediate level: connect shape with equation and units. Advanced level: decide whether the model is valid, whether a scale transform is needed, and whether parameters have physical meaning.
