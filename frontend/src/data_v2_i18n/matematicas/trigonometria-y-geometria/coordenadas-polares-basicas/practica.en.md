# Practice

## Level 1: recognition

Identify in a drawing the angle, reference, positive sense, legs, hypotenuse, radius, or components. Classify whether the problem asks for length, component, angle, ratio, or position. Explain the expected unit before calculating.

## Level 2: direct application

Use the relation `x = r cos(theta), y = r sin(theta), r = sqrt(x^2+y^2)` with simple numerical data. Calculate the result, preserve the unit, and compare whether the scale is reasonable. Repeat the exercise with angles 30, 45, or 60 degrees to use notable values.

## Level 3: choosing the tool

Choose between sine, cosine, tangent, Pythagoras, radians, similarity, or polar conversion in three different situations. Justify the choice with a geometric sentence: what each side represents, which axis is used, and which datum is missing.

## Level 4: physical interpretation

Interpret the result as a force component, velocity, height, arc, direction, or position. Compare what happens if the angle increases, radius changes, axis is reversed, or another quadrant is used. Detect whether the sign makes sense.

## Level 5: intentional error

Correct a setup where opposite and adjacent have been confused, degrees and radians mixed, sign confused with magnitude, or a trigonometric ratio treated as a physical quantity. Write the corrected version and explain the physical consequence of the error.

## Intensive closing practice

Basic level: calculate a simple component and draw the triangle. Intermediate level: estimate before calculating. Advanced level: use an inverse function and check the quadrant. Very advanced level: decide whether the geometric model remains valid when the angle or basis changes with time.

## Cumulative training

Exercise 1: identify the geometry without calculating and explain which datum is missing. Exercise 2: estimate the result using a notable angle. Exercise 3: calculate with the symbolic relation and preserve the unit. Exercise 4: change the quadrant or reference axis and predict the sign. Exercise 5: write a complete physical interpretation. Exercise 6: invent a plausible error and correct it with justification.
