# Basic vector fields

## What it is

A vector field assigns a vector to each point of space. It does not describe a single arrow, but a spatial rule: at each position there is magnitude, direction, and sense that may change from point to point.

## Why it appears in physics

It appears in gravity, electric field, magnetic field, fluid velocity, and wind. It supports the study of distributed forces, possible trajectories, flow, circulation, and regions where the effect increases or decreases.

## Visual or geometric idea

The visual image is a map full of arrows. Long arrows indicate larger magnitude, arrow orientation shows local direction, and changes in direction reveal sources, sinks, vortices, or symmetries.

## Minimal symbolic language

It is written `vec F(x, y, z)` or `vec E(vec r)`. Each input point returns components, for example `(F_x(x,y), F_y(x,y))`. The field is not a trajectory: a trajectory may follow or cross the field depending on dynamics.

## What the result means

The result indicates which vector a particle or fluid element would feel at each position. A uniform field has the same vector everywhere; a radial field changes direction with position.

## When not to use it

Do not confuse field with field line or with a particle moving. Do not read drawn arrows as exact values if they are only qualitative representation.

## Operational summary

Identify domain, vector at each point, spatial variation, and symmetry. Basic level: read arrows. Intermediate: evaluate components at points. Advanced: connect with flux, circulation, divergence, and curl.
