# Vector operations

## What it is

Operating with vectors means combining arrows while respecting direction, sense, and magnitude. Addition produces a resultant vector; subtraction compares two vectors; multiplying by a scalar changes magnitude and, if the scalar is negative, reverses sense.

## Why it appears in physics

It appears when several forces act on a body, when net displacement is calculated, when fields are added, or when velocity is decomposed. Physics uses resultants and components, not isolated lists of magnitudes.

## Visual or geometric idea

Addition is seen with head-to-tail or parallelogram rules. Subtraction is seen by adding the opposite. Decomposition replaces one arrow by two perpendicular arrows that together produce the same effect along chosen axes.

## Minimal symbolic language

If `vec A = (A_x, A_y)` and `vec B = (B_x, B_y)`, then `vec A + vec B = (A_x + B_x, A_y + B_y)`. Multiplying by `k` gives `k vec A = (k A_x, k A_y)`. Units are preserved unless the scalar has units.

## What the result means

The result is a new directed quantity. Zero net force means equilibrium after summing components, not absence of individual forces. A velocity subtraction may represent relative velocity, not a loss of speed.

## When not to use it

Do not add magnitudes when directions differ. Do not forget component signs or mix vectors expressed in different bases. Before operating, all vectors must be in the same reference.

## Operational summary

Draw, choose axes, express components, operate component by component, and reconstruct magnitude and direction if needed. Basic level: graphical addition. Intermediate: components. Advanced: resultants, equilibrium, and reference changes.
