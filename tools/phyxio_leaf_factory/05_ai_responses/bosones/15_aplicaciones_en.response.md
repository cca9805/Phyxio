# Applications
## 1. Ultracold atomic gases
Dominant variable: [[temperatura]]
Validity limit: [[temperatura]] > 0 K and a sufficiently dilute gas for interactions to remain small

In ultracold atomic gases, bosons allow direct study of how the [[ocupacion_media]] of low states changes when [[temperatura]] is reduced. The measured object is often the spatial and velocity distribution of a cloud, but the predicted quantity is the redistribution of population among states of different [[energia]]. As [[temperatura]] decreases, [[longitud_onda_termica]] increases and matter waves start to overlap. If [[densidad_particulas]] approaches [[densidad_critica]], occupation of the ground state becomes macroscopic. The ideal model works as a guide for deciding whether the cloud is classical, quantum-degenerate, or close to condensation. Its limit is the neglect of interactions and detailed trap geometry. In practice, the model helps decide whether to keep cooling, compress the cloud, change [[densidad_particulas]], or move to an interacting description. It also guides the interpretation of expansion images, because a narrow distribution can indicate that many atoms occupy low-[[energia]] states.

## 2. Photons in cavities and thermal radiation
Dominant variable: [[energia]]
Validity limit: well-defined modes and approximate thermal equilibrium between radiation and environment

Electromagnetic modes in a cavity can be treated as bosonic. In this context, the [[ocupacion_media]] of a mode depends on its [[energia]] and on the [[temperatura]] of the environment. The prediction is how many radiation quanta occupy a mode of given frequency. Low-[[energia]] modes tend to be more populated than high-[[energia]] modes, and [[constante_boltzmann]] fixes the relevant thermal energy scale. This application connects with blackbody radiation: one is not counting material particles with [[masa_particula]], but field excitations. The main limit is that [[potencial_quimico]] for a material gas cannot be copied blindly to thermal photons. The application measures intensity per mode and predicts how the spectrum changes with [[temperatura]]. Heating the cavity mainly increases [[ocupacion_media]] in accessible modes, while very high-[[energia]] modes remain weakly populated unless the thermal scale is comparable.

## 3. Phonons in solids
Dominant variable: [[ocupacion_media]]
Validity limit: small vibrations around equilibrium and a stable crystalline lattice

In a solid, collective lattice vibrations are described by phonons, which are bosonic excitations. The [[ocupacion_media]] of a vibrational mode tells how many quanta of vibration occupy a state of given [[energia]] on average. This helps predict heat capacity, thermal transport, and scattering of light or neutrons. [[temperatura]] controls which modes are activated: at low [[temperatura]], only low-[[energia]] modes have significant population; at higher [[temperatura]], occupation spreads across more modes. The model fails when vibrations are too large, when defects dominate, or when the harmonic approximation is no longer valid. It measures collective motion rather than individual atoms. A macroscopic quantity such as heat capacity is interpreted as a sum over microscopic mode occupations. This explains why a cold solid does not activate every vibrational mode: many states have [[energia]] too high for the available [[temperatura]].

## 4. Design of quantum traps
Dominant variable: [[potencial_quimico]]
Validity limit: known [[energia]] levels and equilibrium between cloud and trap

In magnetic or optical traps, confinement changes the set of [[energia]] levels available to bosons. The [[ocupacion_media]] of each level depends on [[potencial_quimico]], [[temperatura]], and level spacing. The prediction is which states become populated first and under what conditions collective accumulation appears. If [[potencial_quimico]] approaches the lowest allowed level, that level can gain population rapidly. This application helps plan evaporative cooling and detect when a continuum gas description should be replaced by a discrete-level reading. The key limit is not to use the density of states of a free gas when the trap dominates the physics. The application predicts the order in which levels fill and constrains experimental design: level spacing that is too large changes the [[ocupacion_media]] curve, while a very dense cloud requires checking [[densidad_particulas]], [[densidad_critica]], and interactions before trusting the ideal prediction.

## 5. Condensed matter and quasiparticles
Dominant variable: [[fugacidad]]
Validity limit: well-defined quasiparticles lasting longer than the relaxation time

Many collective excitations in condensed matter are treated as effective bosons. Magnons, polaritons, and related modes can show high occupation when mode [[energia]], [[temperatura]], and an effective parameter analogous to [[potencial_quimico]] allow it. [[fugacidad]] is useful as a compact language for the statistical weight of such modes, although its interpretation depends on the system. The measured signal is often spectral intensity, emission, linewidth, or response to an external field; the inferred quantity is the [[ocupacion_media]] of collective modes. The ideal model must be used carefully because losses, external pumping, and interactions can prevent strict thermal equilibrium. Even so, the bosonic idea remains useful: high occupation of selected modes can amplify the material response. The application predicts emission thresholds, low-mode accumulation, and collective changes that would not be explained by counting independent particles. It is especially useful when the measured signal grows faster than expected from a classical population model.
