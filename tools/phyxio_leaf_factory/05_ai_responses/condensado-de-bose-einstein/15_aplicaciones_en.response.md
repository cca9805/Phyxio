# Applications
## 1. Ultracold atomic clouds
Dominant variable: [[fraccion_condensada]]
Validity limit: [[temperatura]] < [[temperatura_critica]] and a sufficiently dilute gas with weak interactions

In ultracold-atom laboratories, the condensate is used to measure how much of the gas has entered a common quantum state. [[temperatura]] is controlled through evaporative cooling, and [[densidad_particulas]] is estimated from cloud images. [[temperatura_critica]] predicts where a narrow peak associated with [[numero_condensado]] appears. What is measured is not simply coldness, but a statistical redistribution of [[numero_particulas]]. If [[fraccion_condensada]] increases during further cooling, the reading confirms that [[longitud_onda_termica]] has become comparable with the spacing between bosons. This application validates the ideal model and reveals when trap geometry or interactions require an extended model. In a teaching laboratory, it also links images to theory: a broad thermal distribution can be compared with a narrow central peak. That visual change connects the [[fraccion_condensada]] curve with an actual observable and lets students discuss thermal noise, imaging resolution, and uncertainty in the inferred threshold and final condensate estimate in practice during analysis.

## 2. Matter-wave interferometry
Dominant variable: [[numero_condensado]]
Validity limit: coherence maintained during measurement and losses small compared with [[numero_particulas]]

A condensate can act as a coherent matter-wave source. In interferometry, a large [[fraccion_condensada]] supports clean interference patterns because many bosons share one macroscopic phase. [[temperatura]] must stay sufficiently below [[temperatura_critica]] so that the thermal cloud does not hide the signal. The application predicts what part of [[numero_particulas]] contributes to the coherent pattern and what part remains as thermal background. If [[densidad_particulas]] is too low, the source loses intensity; if it is too high, interactions can distort the phase. Measured quantities include fringe contrast, peak width, and phase stability. If the condensed population is small, the coherent signal is buried in a thermal background; if it is large, the pattern becomes more robust and repeatable. Thus [[numero_condensado]] is a practical quality measure, not just a calculated extra, and it constrains how long the device can integrate before decoherence spoils the phase information.

## 3. Quantum simulation with bosonic gases
Dominant variable: [[temperatura_critica]]
Validity limit: approximate thermal equilibrium and lattice scale larger than the effective interaction length

In optical lattices, condensates are used to simulate collective quantum phases. [[temperatura_critica]] provides a first scale for deciding whether the sample can begin the experiment with enough condensed population. [[densidad_particulas]], effective [[volumen]], and confinement are adjusted to prepare a controlled [[fraccion_condensada]]. What is predicted is the thermal window in which collective behavior is not destroyed by excitations. Although ideal formulas do not describe the full lattice, they provide a compass: higher [[densidad_particulas]] and lower effective [[masa_particula]] make condensation easier. The limit is reached when lattice structure, collisions, or disorder dominate. At that point the condensate may still be the useful initial state, but the later evolution can no longer be explained only with {{f:fraccion_condensada_bose}}. The application separates preparation of the gas from the dynamics imposed afterward, which is essential when comparing ideal threshold estimates with many-body evolution in the lattice.

## 4. Calibration of ultracold thermometry
Dominant variable: [[temperatura]]
Validity limit: resolved imaging and [[fraccion_condensada]] inside the physical interval 0 <= f_0 <= 1

At nanokelvin scales, direct measurement of [[temperatura]] is difficult. The cloud shape after expansion and the estimated [[fraccion_condensada]] serve as an indirect thermometer. If [[temperatura_critica]] is known, the condensed proportion indicates how far below the threshold the gas is. This application measures and constrains [[temperatura]], but it depends on reliable estimates of [[densidad_particulas]], [[numero_particulas]], and [[volumen]]. Poor calibration can produce negative fractions or values above one, a signal that the model, image analysis, or unit conversion has failed. The comparison with {{f:fraccion_condensada_bose}} acts as an internal control. When a sequence of images gives a fraction that rises smoothly during cooling, the thermometry is coherent; when it jumps without reason or exceeds the physical range, exposure, spatial calibration, and unit conversion must be checked before claiming a change in the condensate itself.

## 5. Design of phase-transition experiments
Dominant variable: [[densidad_particulas]]
Validity limit: changes slow compared with equilibration time and [[numero_particulas]] sufficiently large

To observe the transition, an experimental path is designed by changing [[temperatura]] or [[densidad_particulas]] in a controlled way. The question is what minimum density is needed to condense at an accessible temperature. [[densidad_critica]] translates that requirement into a practical condition on [[numero_particulas]] and [[volumen]]. If the apparatus cannot cool further, increasing [[densidad_particulas]] can raise [[temperatura_critica]] into a measurable range. This application predicts the threshold, constrains trap design, and helps decide whether the experimental resources are enough. The limit is that excessive compression increases losses and interactions, precisely where the ideal model becomes less faithful. Experimental design therefore searches for a sweet spot: enough [[densidad_particulas]] to raise the threshold, but not so much that the assumptions behind the first calculation collapse and the measured transition becomes a property of the apparatus as much as of the gas.
