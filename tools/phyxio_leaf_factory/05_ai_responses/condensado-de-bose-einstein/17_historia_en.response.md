# Bose-Einstein Condensate
## Historical problem
In the early twentieth century, physics was trying to understand why matter and radiation did not always follow classical statistics. The problem was not merely to calculate a [[temperatura]], but to explain how many indistinguishable particles could be distributed among quantum states. The idea of a condensate emerged when Bose statistics were applied to material particles: if bosons could share a state, then at low [[temperatura]] a macroscopic population could accumulate in the ground level.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
Classical intuition pictured particles as distinguishable little objects spread through space. In that picture, [[fraccion_condensada]] is not natural, because nothing suggests that many particles should behave as one quantum entity. It was also difficult to accept that [[densidad_particulas]] and [[longitud_onda_termica]] were decisive: having many atoms was not enough; their matter waves had to overlap.

## What changed
Bose introduced a new way of counting states for photons, and Einstein extended the idea to material gases. That change turned indistinguishability into a physical rule with measurable consequences. [[temperatura_critica]] became a threshold: below it, excited states could not contain all bosons, so [[numero_condensado]] appeared in the ground state.

## Impact on physics
For decades the prediction was a hard-to-see jewel because it required extreme cooling. When atomic condensates were observed in 1995, the phenomenon opened an experimental path toward coherent matter waves, quantum simulation, and controlled ultracold gases. [[fraccion_condensada]] became an observable quantity, not just a statistical curiosity.

## Connection with modern physics
Today the condensate connects quantum statistics, thermodynamics, atomic optics, and many-body physics. The question of this leaf, how [[fraccion_condensada]] depends on [[temperatura_critica]], [[temperatura]], and [[densidad_particulas]], captures the historical lesson: a macroscopic transition can emerge from a microscopic quantum counting rule.
