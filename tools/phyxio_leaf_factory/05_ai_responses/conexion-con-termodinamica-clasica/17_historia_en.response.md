# Connection with Classical Thermodynamics
## Historical problem
Classical thermodynamics was developed before the microscopic structure of matter was firmly accepted. Its laws worked very well for heat engines, gases, and phase changes, but they left a deeper question open: why variables such as [[entropia]] and [[temperatura]] describe so many different systems.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The main obstacle was understanding irreversibility and equilibrium without directly observing molecules. [[energia_interna]] could be measured through energy balance, but its connection to microscopic configurations was unclear. The idea of [[numero_microestados]] changed the question: one does not need to follow every particle, but to count how many configurations produce the same macrostate.

## What changed
Boltzmann introduced a statistical reading: [[entropia]] is connected to microscopic multiplicity through [[constante_boltzmann]]. Later, [[temperatura]] could be understood as a property linked to the sensitivity of entropy to energy. [[probabilidad_relativa]] explained why some microscopic states carry greater weight at equilibrium.

## Impact on physics
This connection justified classical laws from a microscopic basis. It also gave meaning to fluctuations, equilibrium distributions, and potentials such as [[energia_libre]]. Thermodynamics became not only a science of engines but also a bridge between scales.

## Connection with modern physics
Today this connection appears in molecular simulation, solid-state physics, statistical chemistry, and information theory. Whenever a classical variable is compared with a distribution of microstates, the same central idea is used: macroscopic behavior emerges from many organized microscopic weights.

The modern importance of this history is that it lets a graph or formula be read without artificially separating the microscopic world from the macroscopic one. When [[probabilidad_relativa]] appears, a statistical weight is being measured; when [[energia_libre]] appears, stability under constraints is being summarized. That view remains active in laboratories and simulations.
