# Velocity Distribution
## Historical problem
The historical problem was to understand how a gas could have stable measurable properties while being made of invisible molecules moving and colliding continuously. Pressure and [[temperatura_absoluta]] could be measured, but the [[velocidad]] of each particle was not accessible. Physics needed a bridge from disordered microscopic motion to reproducible macroscopic laws.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
Before the statistical view became standard, it was tempting to imagine that all molecules in a gas at one [[temperatura_absoluta]] had nearly the same speed. That picture could not explain evaporation, diffusion, or the existence of unusually fast molecules. The conceptual step was to accept that a thermal state fixes a whole distribution, not a single [[velocidad]].

## What changed
Maxwell introduced a probabilistic description of molecular speeds, and Boltzmann deepened the connection between probability, energy, and equilibrium. The [[densidad_probabilidad_velocidad]] became a physical tool rather than a sign of ignorance. It allowed consistent definitions of [[velocidad_mas_probable]], [[velocidad_media]], [[velocidad_rms]], and [[energia_cinetica_media]].

## Impact on physics
The velocity distribution connected thermodynamics with microscopic mechanics. It explained how pressure arises from molecular collisions and why thermal energy is statistically distributed. It also made it possible to compute [[probabilidad_intervalo]] for speed ranges, which is essential in transport, diffusion, and chemical kinetics. Physics moved beyond individual trajectories and learned to describe populations.

## Connection with modern physics
Today the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution remains the starting point for classical gases, near-equilibrium plasmas, and transport models. It also provides a contrast with quantum distributions, where state occupation changes because of particle identity. In this leaf, its modern role is to show that [[temperatura_absoluta]] shapes a speed curve, not one universal molecular speed.
