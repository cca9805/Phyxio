# Statistical Equilibrium
## Historical problem
The historical problem was to understand how systems made of enormous numbers of particles could display stable macroscopic properties. Microscopic mechanics described individual motions, but by itself it did not explain why pressure, temperature, or state occupation could remain steady.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The conceptual obstacle was the confusion between equilibrium and rest. In gases, solids, and molecular systems, particles keep moving and changing state. Physics needed a statistical reading in which [[probabilidad_estado]] and [[flujo_probabilidad]] replaced the individual trajectory as central objects.

## What changed
Statistical physics introduced the ensemble, probability normalization, and balance among transitions. Equilibrium could then be interpreted as a stationary distribution. Later, detailed balance made it possible to distinguish true equilibrium from steady states with internal currents.

## Impact on physics
This change connected [[entropia]], [[temperatura]], [[energia_estado]], and [[peso_boltzmann]] with macroscopic observations. Equilibrium stopped being a picture of stillness and became a condition on compensated statistical flows.

## Connection with modern physics
Today the same idea appears in Monte Carlo simulations, condensed matter, physical chemistry, biological systems, and nonequilibrium processes. This leaf keeps that legacy: recognizing equilibrium requires asking whether probabilities stop changing and whether net [[flujo_probabilidad]] vanishes.
