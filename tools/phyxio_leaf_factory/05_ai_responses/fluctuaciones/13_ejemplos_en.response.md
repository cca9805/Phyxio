# Exam-type example

## Problem statement
A small canonical system has a [[funcion_particion]] that depends on [[beta]]. Near a given [[temperatura]], the slope of ln(Z) with respect to [[beta]] gives [[energia_media]], and the curvature gives [[varianza_energia]]. For a simplified model, the estimated [[energia_media]] is 4.0 x 10^-21 J and the estimated [[varianza_energia]] is 1.6 x 10^-42 J^2. Find [[fluctuacion_energia]], decide whether it is comparable with the mean energy, and relate the result to [[capacidad_calorifica]].

## Data
- [[energia_media]]: 4.0 x 10^-21 J
- [[varianza_energia]]: 1.6 x 10^-42 J^2
- [[temperatura]]: 300 K
- [[constante_boltzmann]]: 1.38 x 10^-23 J/K
- System: canonical, fixed volume, discrete microstates

## System definition
The system is small and in thermal contact with a reservoir that fixes [[temperatura]]. Its microstates have different [[energia_microestado]], and their relative occupation is encoded by the [[funcion_particion]]. The goal is not to know which microstate the system occupies at every instant, but to describe the statistical energy distribution around [[energia_media]].

## Physical model
We use the canonical ensemble. [[beta]] measures how strongly thermal selection penalizes high-energy microstates. [[derivada_log_particion_beta]] is associated with [[energia_media]], while [[derivada_segunda_log_particion_beta]] is associated with [[varianza_energia]]. [[capacidad_calorifica]] is not computed numerically here, but it is interpreted as a macroscopic response connected to that microscopic spread.

## Model justification
The problem states fixed [[temperatura]] and equilibrium with an environment, so the canonical ensemble is appropriate. The given [[varianza_energia]] is positive and has units of J^2, a necessary signal for physical interpretation as energy spread. Because the system is small, [[fluctuacion_energia]] can be a noticeable fraction of [[energia_media]]. The model would be doubtful if the system were isolated, if temperature were not measured in kelvin, or if the curvature of ln(Z) had been extracted by mixing different physical configurations.

Therefore, the system should not be pictured as physically carrying exactly 4.0 x 10^-21 J at all times. Its behavior depends on a distribution generated by the [[funcion_particion]]. Physically, a large [[capacidad_calorifica]] in this range would indicate that small thermal changes redistribute probabilities strongly.

# Real-world example

## Context
Consider a nanosystem, such as a molecule adsorbed on a surface, exchanging energy with a thermal substrate. At macroscopic scale, the temperature may look almost constant, but at microscopic scale the molecule energy can wander among accessible states. The [[funcion_particion]] summarizes those energy visits without tracking every microstate trajectory.

## Physical estimation
At 300 K, the product of [[constante_boltzmann]] and [[temperatura]] is of order 10^-21 J. If several molecular configurations have energy separations on that scale, an estimate of [[varianza_energia]] can be significant and [[fluctuacion_energia]] may not be negligible compared with [[energia_media]]. An approximate relative fluctuation between 0.1 and 1 can be a reasonable value for a small nanosystem, depending on how many states are thermally accessible. For a macroscopic material, the total fluctuation magnitude may grow, but the relative scale usually shrinks because many weakly correlated contributions average out.

## Interpretation
The key reading is that fluctuations are a fingerprint of microscopic accessibility. When [[temperatura]] opens more [[energia_microestado]] options, the curvature of ln(Z) can increase and so can [[varianza_energia]]. [[capacidad_calorifica]] translates this microscopic redistribution into an observable response: when the system can absorb energy into many internal possibilities, its mean energy changes with temperature in a structured way. The partition function therefore acts as a compact map linking microscopic spread with macroscopic thermal behavior.
