# Fluctuations

## Historical problem
Classical thermodynamics described stable quantities: energy, temperature, and heat capacity looked like smooth properties of large systems. Microscopic physics forced a deeper question: what happens when a system can occupy many microstates with different [[energia_microestado]]? The challenge was to explain how a macroscopic [[energia_media]] could emerge from changing microscopic values, and also how to quantify the spread around that average.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
Before statistical mechanics, a fluctuation could look like experimental imperfection or external disturbance. There was no clear way to understand that even in equilibrium the energy of a subsystem can vary when it exchanges energy with a thermal environment. There was also no simple connection between that spread and a measurable quantity such as [[capacidad_calorifica]].

## What changed
The introduction of the [[funcion_particion]] changed the language. Instead of following every microstate, canonical weights are summed and derivatives of ln(Z) are studied. The slope with respect to [[beta]] gives [[energia_media]], while the curvature gives [[varianza_energia]]. Fluctuation stopped being structureless noise and became a calculable property of the equilibrium distribution.

## Impact on physics
This idea showed that macroscopic responses are not independent of the microscopic world. [[capacidad_calorifica]] can be understood as a signal of how much energy fluctuates. Peaks, abrupt changes, and thermal anomalies began to be read as traces of internal reorganizations, not merely isolated laboratory data.

## Connection with modern physics
Today fluctuations are central in nanosystems, Monte Carlo simulations, transition physics, condensed matter, and statistical information. [[fluctuacion_energia]] helps decide when an average is representative and when the spread is an essential part of the phenomenon. This leaf connects that modern idea with a compact tool: the curvature of the [[funcion_particion]].
