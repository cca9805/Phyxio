## 1. Simple metals and conduction scale
Dominant variable: [[densidad_electronica]]
Validity limit: 10²⁶ m⁻³ ≤ [[densidad_electronica]] ≤ 10²⁹ m⁻³ and [[temperatura_absoluta]] << [[temperatura_fermi]]

In metals such as sodium, copper, or aluminum, the electron gas estimates why electrical conduction is tied to states near [[energia_fermi]]. What is measured or estimated is the energetic scale available to conduction electrons, not ordinary thermal energy. When [[densidad_electronica]] increases, the filling boundary rises because Pauli exclusion forces higher-momentum states to be filled. This predicts [[temperatura_fermi]] far above room conditions and explains why only states near [[potencial_quimico]] noticeably change [[ocupacion_estado]] under small perturbations. The application is useful for a first reading of simple metals, provided that detailed bands and effective mass are not required.


It also explains why moderate heating does not empty the deep filled region: [[temperatura_absoluta]] changes only a narrow band compared with the very large [[temperatura_fermi]]. In classroom use, the application compares tabulated densities and predicts whether [[energia_fermi]] should sit far above the room-temperature thermal scale.
## 2. Comparing conducting materials
Dominant variable: [[energia_fermi]]
Validity limit: use only as an order-of-magnitude comparison unless effective mass is specified

Comparing materials through [[energia_fermi]] helps distinguish electronic degeneracy scales. Two materials with different free [[numero_electrones]] per [[volumen]] have different [[densidad_electronica]] and therefore different occupation edges. What is predicted is whether the quantum scale clearly dominates over [[temperatura_absoluta]]. If [[temperatura_fermi]] is tens of thousands of kelvin, occupation is fermionic even in ordinary laboratory conditions. This constrains a common misunderstanding: high conductivity does not mean all electrons move freely with the same energy. It means many deep states are filled and the active region lies near [[energia_fermi]].


This comparison also separates density from mobility: a material can have high [[densidad_electronica]] and still scatter carriers strongly through impurities. The electron gas does not predict full resistance by itself, but it constrains the quantum energy scale available to occupied states.
## 3. Doped semiconductors
Dominant variable: [[potencial_quimico]]
Validity limit: valid as an estimate only if carriers are nearly free and effective mass is declared

In doped semiconductors, electron-gas language helps explain how doping shifts [[potencial_quimico]] and modifies [[ocupacion_estado]]. The measured quantity is often carrier density, while the predicted quantity is occupation probability near allowed bands. Here the free-gas formula with [[masa_electron]] may be insufficient because the crystal lattice changes the relation between [[energia_estado]] and momentum. Even so, the conceptual reading remains: greater available [[densidad_electronica]] raises the filling scale and changes the relative position of [[energia_estado]] with respect to [[potencial_quimico]]. The validity limit is not to confuse effective carriers with ideal free electrons.


The application is especially useful for seeing how [[ocupacion_estado]] changes sharply when dopants move [[potencial_quimico]]. If temperature rises, [[temperatura_absoluta]] broadens the transition and the reading is no longer a rigid filling picture.
## 4. Nanodevices and confinement
Dominant variable: [[volumen]]
Validity limit: the 3D model fails if one dimension approaches the effective electronic wavelength

In nanodevices, reducing size changes the accessible [[volumen]] and can alter state counting completely. The 3D electron gas predicts how [[densidad_electronica]] raises [[energia_fermi]], but under strong confinement volume density is no longer the only relevant variable. What is constrained is the validity of using a smooth curve versus density. In quantum wells, wires, or dots, discrete levels may matter more than a continuous Fermi boundary. This application acts as a warning: if the graph assumes a continuous relation between [[energia_fermi]] and [[densidad_electronica]], one must confirm that the system is not dimensionally confined.


In small transistors, two-dimensional layers, or mesoscopic contacts, this warning prevents extrapolating volume formulas beyond their territory. The measured quantity may be a surface density, even though a student may mistakenly convert it into volumetric [[densidad_electronica]].
## 5. Dense matter and degeneracy pressure
Dominant variable: [[velocidad_fermi]]
Validity limit: a relativistic model is required if [[velocidad_fermi]] is no longer much smaller than 3·10⁸ m/s

In very dense matter, such as idealized stellar interiors, [[densidad_electronica]] can become so high that [[energia_fermi]] and [[velocidad_fermi]] grow dramatically. What is predicted is not only occupation in a metal, but the degeneracy pressure associated with forced filling of states. The same Pauli idea remains active: many electrons in a small [[volumen]] must occupy high-energy states. The validity limit appears when [[velocidad_fermi]] becomes relativistic; then the nonrelativistic model of this leaf is no longer enough. This application shows that the density-Fermi-occupation question is relevant beyond the laboratory, reaching astrophysical matter.


Here an estimate of [[energia_fermi]] decides whether electrons remain nonrelativistic or whether different physics is needed. The nearly complete occupation of low states shows that pressure can arise from quantum exclusion rather than ordinary thermal motion.

Across all five contexts, the same diagnostic survives: [[densidad_electronica]] fixes the filled-state scale, [[energia_fermi]] expresses that scale, and [[ocupacion_estado]] tells which states can actually respond near [[potencial_quimico]]. This is the compact bridge from material density to microscopic electronic behavior.
