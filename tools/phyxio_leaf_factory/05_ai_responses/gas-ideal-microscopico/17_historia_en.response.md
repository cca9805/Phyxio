# Microscopic Ideal Gas

## Historical problem

For a long time, gases were described through macroscopic relations among [[presion]], [[volumen]], and [[temperatura]], without a clear picture of what happened inside. The experimental laws worked, but they left a deep question: why should a thermometer reading be related to the push exerted on container walls?

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The main obstacle was accepting that a seemingly continuous substance could be made of invisible moving particles. The [[temperatura]] looked like a property of the whole body, not a measure of microscopic [[energia_cinetica_media]]. Without statistical reasoning, individual collisions seemed too chaotic to generate simple laws.

## What changed

Kinetic theory reinterpreted the gas as many particles with [[masa_particula]] moving inside a [[volumen]]. The [[presion]] became the average momentum transfer from wall collisions, and [[temperatura]] became linked to [[energia_cinetica_media]]. The [[constante_boltzmann]] made the bridge explicit by converting kelvin into energy per particle.

## Impact on physics

The microscopic ideal gas showed that a macroscopic law can be a statistical average. It was not necessary to know every trajectory; distributions, averages, and scales such as [[velocidad_rms]] could explain observable behavior. This shift helped build statistical mechanics and gave thermodynamics a molecular foundation.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the model remains a gateway to molecular simulations, plasma physics, atmospheric models, and materials science. Its modern value is conceptual as much as computational: [[temperatura]] summarizes agitation, [[energia_cinetica_media]] measures that agitation per particle, and [[presion]] reveals the collective effect of invisible impacts.
