# Fundamental Hypothesis
## Historical problem
Nineteenth-century physics needed to connect macroscopic behavior of gases and solids with microscopic motion. The obstacle was severe: positions and velocities of all particles could not be known, yet thermodynamics gave reproducible predictions. The fundamental hypothesis answered this tension with a statistical rule for isolated systems: if microstates are accessible under the same constraints, they receive the same [[probabilidad_microestado]].

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The hard step was accepting that a physical law could rely on counting and probability without following every trajectory. It was also unclear why one macrostate should be more likely than another. The key distinction was microstate versus macrostate: a macrostate can dominate because it has large [[multiplicidad_macroestado]], even though each individual microstate has the same weight.

## What changed
With Boltzmann and Gibbs, the count of [[numero_microestados_accesibles]] became a physical tool. [[entropia]] stopped being only an empirical thermal quantity and became connected to microscopic multiplicity through [[constante_boltzmann]]. Equilibrium was reinterpreted as the statistically dominant condition under constraints such as [[energia_total]], [[volumen]], and [[numero_particulas]].

## Impact on physics
The hypothesis made it possible to derive macroscopic properties from sets of microstates. It also prepared the route toward more general ensembles, where microcanonical equiprobability becomes non-uniform weighting when a system exchanges energy. Its impact was to turn microscopic ignorance into a precise rule, not into surrender.

## Connection with modern physics
Today the idea appears in simulations, information theory, material models, and small-system thermodynamics. When [[desbalance_probabilidad]] is measured, the question is whether the microcanonical ideal is valid or whether richer dynamics are needed. The hypothesis remains a starting point: before complex distributions, one must understand unbiased probability over accessible microstates.
