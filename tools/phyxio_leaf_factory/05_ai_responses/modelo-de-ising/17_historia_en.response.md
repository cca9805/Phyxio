# Ising Model

## Historical problem

In the early twentieth century, magnetism raised a difficult question: materials could display collective order even though their microscopic constituents were thermally agitated. Physics needed a model that could connect local interaction, [[temperatura]], and observable magnetization without reproducing every real atomic detail.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The obstacle was explaining how many microscopic variables could create a macroscopic transition. Looking at one [[espin]] alone does not produce a magnetic phase. The key had to be cooperation: [[acoplamiento]] between neighbors, thermal competition, and the effect of a [[campo_externo]].

## What changed

The Ising model introduced a radical simplification: replace complex moments by binary [[espin]] variables on a lattice. With that reduction, [[energia_ising]], [[magnetizacion_total]], and [[magnetizacion_media]] could be calculated and compared through statistical weights. Although the one-dimensional version did not show a finite-temperature transition, later extensions revealed a rich physical structure.

## Impact on physics

The model became a central tool in statistical physics. It helped clarify phase transitions, broken symmetry, domains, critical phenomena, and computational methods. Its value is not complete realism, but the ability to show how a minimal microscopic rule can produce collective behavior.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the Ising model appears in magnetism, networks, statistical computing, Monte Carlo methods, complex systems, and optimization. The modern reading connects [[probabilidad_configuracion]] with thermal equilibrium and uses [[beta_termica]] to understand whether energy or disorder dominates.
