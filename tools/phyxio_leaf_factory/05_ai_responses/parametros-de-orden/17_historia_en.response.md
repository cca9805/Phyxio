# Order Parameters
## Historical problem
For a long time, phase transitions were described mainly through visible changes: solid, liquid, gas, magnetic, or nonmagnetic. That language was useful, but it did not explain what very different systems had in common. The problem was to find a quantity that could describe internal organization without relying only on appearance or [[temperatura]].

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The relevant change was often not an everyday quantity. In a magnet, order is related to collective orientation; in a mixture, to composition; in a crystal, to geometric symmetry. Physics needed a flexible idea: the [[parametro_de_orden]]. Another difficulty was that near [[temperatura_critica]], fluctuations make the boundary between phases less like a clean line and more like a delicate region.

## What changed
Order parameters made symmetry breaking measurable. A phase could be recognized because the [[parametro_de_orden]] was zero, finite, or changed in a characteristic way. With [[temperatura_reducida]], different systems could be compared near their own [[temperatura_critica]]. The use of [[exponente_critico_beta]], [[amplitud_critica]], and [[susceptibilidad]] turned phase change into a quantitative reading rather than only a visual classification.

## Impact on physics
The concept unified magnetism, superconductivity, mixtures, and structural transitions. The key issue was no longer only the substance, but the symmetry pattern that is broken. It also helped distinguish continuous transitions from changes with a [[salto_parametro_de_orden]], opening the way to Landau theory, critical phenomena, and universality.

## Connection with modern physics
Today the [[parametro_de_orden]] remains central. It connects experimental data with physical interpretation: which phase exists, how it responds to [[campo_conjugado]], what [[susceptibilidad]] means, and how the system approaches [[temperatura_critica]]. In modern physics, phases are understood through organization, symmetry, and collective response.
