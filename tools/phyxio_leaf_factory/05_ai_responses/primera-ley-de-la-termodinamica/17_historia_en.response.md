# First Law of Thermodynamics

## Historical problem

The historical problem behind the first law was understanding the relation between heat, mechanical work, and energy. For a long time, heat was interpreted as a material fluid, caloric, passing from one body to another. That picture explained some everyday phenomena, but it failed for steam engines, friction, and gas compression. Engines produced work from heat, while friction produced warming from work. Physics needed one common principle.

## Prior conceptual difficulty

The difficulty was that [[calor_intercambiado]] and [[trabajo_sobre_sistema]] looked like different kinds of phenomena. One was associated with temperature and thermal contact; the other with force and motion. The modern idea required treating both as energy transfers, not as substances. It also required separating those transfers from internal energy stored in the state of the system.

## What changed

Joule's experiments and the mechanical theory of heat showed that work could be quantitatively transformed into heating. This made a single accounting rule possible: what enters as heat or work changes [[variacion_energia_interna]]. Internal energy became a state property connected with microscopic structure, even when the microscopic details were not needed to apply the macroscopic balance.

## Impact on physics

The first law unified calorimetry, heat engines, gas expansion, and mechanical processes under conservation of energy. It allowed engines to be analyzed without imagining that they created energy from nothing, and it gave a quantitative basis for comparing [[energia_interna_inicial]] and [[energia_interna_final]]. It also made clear that energy conservation alone was not enough to determine efficiency or natural direction; the second law was needed for irreversibility.

## Connection with modern physics

Today the first law remains the entry point to thermodynamics, statistical physics, and energy engineering. In microscopic systems, materials, atmospheres, and reactors, the central question is still what crosses the boundary and what remains accumulated inside. The modern interpretation of [[variacion_energia_interna]] connects the macroscopic balance to molecular energy that is not directly visible.
