# Probability and Ensembles
## Historical problem
The historical problem was to connect macroscopic thermodynamic laws with systems made of enormous numbers of microscopic parts. Measuring every [[estado_microscopico]] was impossible, yet macroscopic quantities were reproducible. Physics needed a tool for speaking about statistical populations without knowing every detail.

## Prior conceptual difficulty
The conceptual difficulty was the meaning of probability in physics. It was not enough to call it ignorance: a [[probabilidad_estado]] had to be connected with equivalent preparations, ideal [[conteo_sistemas]], and physical constraints. A single microscopic trajectory could fail to represent collective behavior.

## What changed
The [[ensemble]] concept made it possible to imagine many equally prepared copies under the same [[restriccion_macroscopica]]. Then the average of a quantity no longer depended on one isolated copy, but on [[valor_medio_ensemble]]. Spread entered through [[fluctuacion_ensemble]], giving a richer reading than the average alone.

## Impact on physics
This idea made microcanonical, canonical, and grand canonical ensembles possible. It also connected statistical weights, normalization, and observables. Statistical physics stopped looking for a complete microscopic photograph and began studying controlled distributions.

## Connection with modern physics
Today ensembles appear in molecular simulation, condensed matter, statistical information, and fluctuation models. The central point remains the same: an [[ensemble]] uses probabilities to represent many compatible systems and extract testable macroscopic predictions.
