# Connection with Classical Entropy

## Conceptual context
[[entropia]] can be introduced through two languages that first look unrelated. Classical thermodynamics describes it through [[calor_reversible]] exchanged between equilibrium states. Statistical physics reads it through how many microscopic configurations match the same macroscopic description. This leaf joins both views. A single physical change can be described as reversible thermal spreading or as a change in microscopic multiplicity. The central question is how a quantity measured with heat and [[temperatura]] can match a quantity built from [[numero_microestados]].

## 🟢 Essential level
The link between classical and statistical [[entropia]] says that a thermal change also changes how many microscopic ways the system can realize its macroscopic state. When a system receives [[calor_reversible]] at a given [[temperatura]], it is not only receiving energy; that energy becomes compatible with a different set of internal possibilities. [[delta_entropia]] measures that change in accessible spreading. From the microscopic view, the system moves from an initial multiplicity to a final multiplicity, and [[ratio_microestados]] tells how much that accessible set grows or shrinks. If the final state has many more compatible configurations, [[entropia]] increases. If it has fewer, it decreases. [[k_boltzmann]] acts as the conversion factor between statistical counting and ordinary thermodynamic units. The comparison must always describe the same physical change.

The formal message is that the same [[delta_entropia]] can be read as a classical thermal quotient or as a logarithmic change in multiplicity. [[k_boltzmann]] turns a dimensionless statistical reading into a quantity with units of energy per [[temperatura]]. Therefore sign, units, and system identity must agree in both routes. Any rearrangement must also preserve that [[delta_entropia]] is a state quantity: the reversible path supplies a calculable value, while microscopic counting supplies the interpretation. If the two routes give opposite signs, the issue is not physics but an error in system identity, scale, or domain.

## 🔴 Structural level
The model rests on a careful distinction between state, path, and microscopic description. [[entropia]] is a state quantity: its change between two equilibrium states does not depend on the real path followed. However, the classical calculation using [[calor_reversible]] requires a reversible path, actual or imagined. This avoids a common trap: an irreversible process may have a well-defined [[delta_entropia]], but it is not computed by dividing arbitrary real heat by [[temperatura]]. The reversible path is a calculation device, not necessarily the physical story.

The main assumptions are initial and final equilibrium, positive absolute [[temperatura]], a system large enough for [[numero_microestados]] to have statistical meaning, and compatibility between the macroscopic state and the microscopic count used. The [[calor_reversible]] must refer to the same system and the same state change that is being compared statistically. If external constraints, available energy, volume, or particle number change without being included in the count, the connection becomes inconsistent.

Several invariants keep the interpretation stable. [[k_boltzmann]] is universal; the sign of [[delta_entropia]] must match the growth or reduction of [[ratio_microestados]]; and the final units must be energy per [[temperatura]]. A [[ratio_microestados]] greater than one corresponds to increasing [[entropia]], while a value below one corresponds to decreasing accessible multiplicity. No multiplicity change gives zero entropy change, both in the model and in a graph reading.

The model breaks down when equilibrium states cannot be identified, when [[temperatura]] is not well defined, when non-reversible heat is treated as reversible heat, or when microstates from different systems are compared. In the graph reading, the key shape is not linear growth with absolute multiplicity, but logarithmic sensitivity to microscopic ratios. This visual structure helps detect the mistaken idea that enormous microstate counts automatically imply proportionally enormous [[delta_entropia]]. It also forces checks of domain, units, and sign before accepting a result. In terms of domain, the graph should reinforce this reading: equal-looking changes in [[ratio_microestados]] do not always become linear jumps in [[delta_entropia]]. The model therefore requires slope, sign, and curvature to be interpreted together with the assumptions, not as decorative features.

## Deep physical interpretation
The connection does not say that [[entropia]] is vague “disorder.” It says something sharper: [[entropia]] measures how many microscopic realizations are compatible with a macroscopic description and how that multiplicity changes. [[calor_reversible]] represents a carefully controlled energy transfer; [[temperatura]] gives the thermal scale that determines how significant that transfer is. From below, the same change appears as growth or reduction in [[numero_microestados]]. Thus [[delta_entropia]] is a bridge: it translates a macroscopic energy reading into a statistical reading. [[k_boltzmann]] is the unit adapter between the two physical languages.

## Order of magnitude
For macroscopic systems, [[numero_microestados]] can be astronomically large, often exponential in particle number. That is why logarithms and [[ratio_microestados]] are used instead of direct lists of configurations. A [[delta_entropia]] of a few J/K may already represent a gigantic microscopic change. In ordinary gases or solids, values from about 1 J/K to 100 J/K are plausible laboratory-scale entropy changes when [[temperatura]] is in kelvin and [[calor_reversible]] in joules.

## Personalized resolution method
First identify the two equilibrium states and decide whether the data are classical, statistical, or both. Then locate [[calor_reversible]], [[temperatura]], [[numero_microestados]], or [[ratio_microestados]]. Use the reversible formula when a reversible path is defined; use the Boltzmann connection when multiplicities or ratios are given. Check the sign of [[delta_entropia]]: it must increase when the final state has more accessible microstates. Finally, validate units, system consistency, and physical meaning.

## Special cases and extended example
If [[ratio_microestados]] is one, the two states have the same multiplicity and [[delta_entropia]] is zero, even if other variables not included in the comparison differ. If a system receives [[calor_reversible]] at high [[temperatura]], the same heat gives a smaller entropy change than at low [[temperatura]], because the added energy changes the relative spreading less. In a reversible gas expansion, accessible volume usually grows and so does [[numero_microestados]]. The classical view records the change through reversible heat; the statistical view reads it as more configurations available to the particles.

## Real student questions
Are classical and statistical [[entropia]] different quantities? No. They are compatible readings of the same state quantity when applied to the same system and state.

Why does [[temperatura]] appear in the classical formula? Because it sets the thermal scale of the exchange: the same [[calor_reversible]] does not change energy spreading equally at all temperatures.

Can I use any heat? No. The direct relation uses [[calor_reversible]]. For irreversible processes, compute [[delta_entropia]] through an equivalent reversible path.

Why is there a logarithm statistically? Because [[entropia]] must add for independent systems while their microstates multiply.

## Final synthesis
[[delta_entropia]] links two worlds: reversible energy exchange and microscopic counting of possibilities. [[k_boltzmann]] lets both languages measure the same [[entropia]].
