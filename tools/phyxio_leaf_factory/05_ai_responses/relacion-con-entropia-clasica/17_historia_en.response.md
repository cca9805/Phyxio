# Connection with Classical Entropy

## Historical problem
[[entropia]] entered classical thermodynamics as a quantity needed to describe the limits of heat engines and reversible processes. Its operational definition was tied to [[calor_reversible]] and [[temperatura]], but that reading left a deeper question open: what does this quantity physically represent inside matter?

## Prior conceptual difficulty
Before the statistical interpretation, [[entropia]] looked like an abstract macroscopic tool. One could calculate [[delta_entropia]], but it was not obvious why it should increase in natural processes or why it depended on thermal scale. The idea of [[numero_microestados]] clarified the issue by showing that macroscopic states are not single microscopic arrangements. Each macrostate can be realized in many microscopic ways.

## What changed
Boltzmann and statistical mechanics introduced a microscopic reading: [[entropia]] is related to the multiplicity of compatible configurations. The classical quotient involving [[calor_reversible]] and [[temperatura]] then stopped being only a calculation rule and became the macroscopic reflection of a change in [[ratio_microestados]]. [[k_boltzmann]] fixed the scale between both descriptions.

## Impact on physics
The connection explained why spontaneous processes tend toward more probable macrostates without discarding classical thermodynamics. It also linked heat, equilibrium, probability, and physical information. [[delta_entropia]] became a bridge quantity: measurable in the laboratory and explainable through microscopic multiplicity.

## Connection with modern physics
Today this relation appears in gases, solids, computational physics, cosmology, and information theory. Modern physics does not replace the classical definition; it deepens it. When the system is at equilibrium and the microscopic count is coherent, classical [[entropia]] measured through [[calor_reversible]] and statistical entropy based on [[numero_microestados]] describe the same physical fabric at different scales.
